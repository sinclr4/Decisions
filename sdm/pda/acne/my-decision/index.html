Our Decision Support telephone service is here to assist and guide you through the Shared Decision Making process.

The Decision Support team is made of professional Health Coaches who are clinicians, commonly nurses, who have undertaken additional training to become a Health Coach.

The Health Coaches will assist you by:

  • Providing you with information
  • Helping you to understand your condition
  • Recognise what is important to you regarding the outcome of treatment
  • Identifying potential solutions
  • Encourage you in discussing options with your family
  • Transferring skills which will assist you in using the information and resources available to you
  • Support you in building confidence in discussing your choice with your doctor
  • You may find that this can be achieved in one telephone call with a Health Coach however; if further calls are required to support you in reaching your decision the Health Coach will schedule these with you.

Important Things To Note

  • A Health Coach does not know which treatment option is best for you.
  • The Health Coach does not have information about your medical history.
  • You will still need to talk over your decision and reasons for your decision with your doctor or nurse.

MORE INFORMATION ABOUT ACNE

What is acne?

Acne is another word for spots. People with severe acne have lots of spots, or spots that are large, red, and sore.

Acne spots typically appear on the face and upper body. In these areas of your body your skin has thousands of tiny hair follicles. These hair follicles are different from the ones on your scalp because they have a very large gland (sebaceous gland) that produces an oily substance called sebum, and a very small hair. Sebum is a protective substance that flows through the pore onto the surface of the skin.

Visible acne spots start when pores become blocked. Blocked pores stop sebum flowing onto the surface of the skin and they trap acne causing bacteria. We don't know what causes pores to become blocked.

Spots that aren't inflamed are called whiteheads, when the pore is closed, and blackheads, when the pore is open.

Inflamed or red spots also start off as blocked pores (microcomedones). Spots on the surface of the skin are called papules, if they don't contain pus, or pustules, if they do contain pus. Deep, inflamed spots that are hard to the touch are called nodules.

What are the signs of acne?

If you have acne, you get spots on your skin. They usually appear on your face, neck, back, chest, or arms.

The main signs of acne are:

  • Blackheads (small black spots) or whiteheads (white bumps) on your skin
  • Greasy skin
  • Red, inflamed spots
  • Spots with pus in them
  • Painful spots that go deep into your skin.
Why has this happened to me?

We don't know exactly what causes acne. Some people say that diet, not enough sunlight, or poor hygiene is to blame, but there is no proof that this is the case.

The main reason teenagers get acne is probably the hormonal changes that happen during adolescence. Both boys and girls have increased levels of the hormone testosterone during puberty. The increase in testosterone can cause your skin to make more sebum, which can encourage acne.

Although acne usually starts to appear during adolescence, it can also start for the first time when people are older.

Some people get acne because of tight clothes, or exposure to chemicals at work, or greasy make-up. We don't cover these types of acne in this decision aid.

You are more likely to have acne if other people in your family have it too.[1]Williams HC, Dellavalle RP, Garner S. Acne vulgaris. Lancet. 2012; 379: 361-372.

Some things that can make acne worse:

  • Rubbing your skin
  • Picking or squeezing spots
  • Tight clothes, bicycle helmets, or bag straps that rub the skin
  • Pollution and high humidity
  • For women, the hormonal changes during menstrual periods, and when people start or stop taking the contraceptive pill
  • Stress[1]Williams HC, Dellavalle RP, Garner S. Acne vulgaris. Lancet. 2012; 379: 361-372.
  • Smoking.[1]Williams HC, Dellavalle RP, Garner S. Acne vulgaris. Lancet. 2012; 379: 361-372.
How do I find out I have acne?

You don't usually need to see your GP to find out if you have acne. Spots on the face and upper body, especially in a teenager or young adult, are almost certainly acne.

Spots in just one part of the body (other than the face or upper body) may not be acne. Also, spots that appear suddenly are not likely to be acne because acne appears quite gradually.

If you are not sure if your spots are acne or some other skin condition, a pharmacist, school nurse (if you attend school), or your GP can usually tell you.

When is acne bad enough to see a doctor?

Acne can last for several years. Some people have more severe acne. You may want to see a doctor about acne if:

  • Your acne started before the age of 12, particularly with blackheads in the middle of the face. This can be a sign that severe acne may follow.
  • You have very oily skin
  • Lots of people in your family have acne
  • You have a lot of spots on the chest, back, neck or arms
  • Your acne is causing scars
  • You have inflammatory acne (red, swollen acne that is tender to the touch)
  • Your spots are making you so unhappy that you feel less able to enjoy life
  • Your spots are suddenly getting worse.

Even if none of these things is the case, you might still want to have treatment for your acne. Acne is not something you have to put up with. It can be treated. There are also many treatments available to buy without a prescription.

If acne is left untreated, especially in the first three years of getting spots, it can leave permanent scars.

How many people have acne?
  • About 85 in 100 people between the ages of 12 and 24 years get acne (which could be mild, moderate or severe) at some point
  • About 8 in 100 adults aged 25 to 34 years have some degree of acne at some point
  • About 3 in 100 adults aged 35 to 44 years have some degree of acne at some point.[4]Naftanel M. Acne vulgaris. October 2012. Available at http://bestpractice.bmj.com/best-practice/monograph/101/basics/epidemiology.html (accessed on 15 January 2013).

More teenage boys than girls have acne. During adulthood, more women have acne than men.[4]Naftanel M. Acne vulgaris. October 2012. Available at http://bestpractice.bmj.com/best-practice/monograph/101/basics/epidemiology.html (accessed on 15 January 2013).

What will happen?

People tend to get acne first during adolescence. It often goes away as you grow older, but sometimes only after several years. In 70 in 100 adolescents, it stops about five years after it starts.[6]National Institute for Clinical Excellence. Referral advice. December 2001. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/media/94d/be/referraladvice.pdf (accessed on 15 January 2013).

We don't know whether any individual person's adolescent acne will last into adulthood.[1]Williams HC, Dellavalle RP, Garner S. Acne vulgaris. Lancet. 2012; 379: 361-372.

Treatments can help control acne. It can take six to nine weeks before you can tell if treatment is working. Some treatments need to be taken for six months or longer. If one treatment doesn't help you, ask if another treatment might work better.

Most people are treated by their GP. He or she may suggest seeing a doctor who specialises in skin conditions (a dermatologist) if:

  • Your acne is severe
  • You're at risk of scarring
  • You've tried several treatments without finding one that works
  • Your acne is upsetting you a lot.

Here are some things people can try to keep acne-prone skin comfortable:

  • Cleaning skin gently. Some people try to get rid of grease and spots by scrubbing their skin, but this can make acne worse.
  • Avoiding touching spots. Squeezing or picking your spots can leave marks and make your acne worse.
  • Using oil-free make-up, moisturisers, and sun-creams. Some types of make-up can block pores and cause spots.

Spots themselves don't cause serious physical harm, but if they aren't treated early, they can leave scars. 

The main harm acne causes is emotional. Many people with acne don't like the way it looks. Some people find it embarrassing, and even become upset or depressed. If you're concerned about your acne, make sure your doctor understands how you feel.

What are the consequences of acne?

If acne is left untreated, it can lead to scars, changes in skin pigment (patches of different coloured skin), and emotional problems.

Emotional problems

Acne is often at its worst during teenage years and early adulthood. This is when people often have trouble with self-confidence and self-esteem. Some people with acne feel anxious, embarrassed, ashamed, or depressed. They may avoid socialising and going out.[1]Williams HC, Dellavalle RP, Garner S. Acne vulgaris. Lancet. 2012; 379: 361-372.

If your acne is having a big effect on your life, you may want to speak to your doctor. He or she can arrange for you to see a counsellor. There are also a number of acne support groups on the internet that you can join. There are currently no acne support groups in the UK.

Scarring

Facial scarring from acne affects up to 20 in 100 teenagers.[1]Williams HC, Dellavalle RP, Garner S. Acne vulgaris. Lancet. 2012; 379: 361-372.

It may be possible to avoid scarring by having treatment as soon as acne appears. People are more likely to have scarring if acne is left untreated for a long time. Even mild acne that is left untreated can cause scars.

There are two main types of acne scar: 

  • Atrophic. These are sunken scars caused by loss of tissue.
  • Hypertrophic. These are raised scars caused by an increase in tissue.

There are a number of treatments to reduce acne scarring but none of them completely removes the scars. Preventing scarring by treating acne effectively is the best course of action.

If your acne scars are not severe, and you are young, your scars may fade with time.

Pigment changes

When acne clears up it can leave a change in the skin causing an increase (hyperpigmentation) or decrease (hypopigmentation) in the pigment in the skin. This is when the colour of your skin changes in the area where the acne used to be. These changes are more obvious in skin of colour. Unlike other acne scars, hyper- and hypopigmentation leave a flat scar that usually disappears within two years.

Where can I find support and advice?

You can find useful information and advice at The Acne Academy (www.acneacademy.org), a UK-based charitable organisation supported by doctors, nurses and other healthcare workers.

Some acne advice websites sell acne treatment. These groups might not provide unbiased information. Also, be careful about accepting advice about acne treatments from people in online support groups. This information may be biased, incorrect, or even harmful. It is best to speak to a pharmacist, your GP, or, if you are still at school, your school nurse.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT NO TREATMENT

What is no treatment?

Not everyone with acne decides to have treatment. Some people prefer to wait and see if their acne clears up on its own.

Who can have no treatment?

Anyone can decide not to have treatment. People are usually advised to consider treatment if their acne is more severe, or likely to become more severe. This might be true if: 

  • Their acne started before the age of 12, particularly if they have blackheads in the middle of their face
  • They have very oily skin associated with acne
  • Lots of people in their family have acne
  • They have lots of acne, especially on the chest, back, neck or arms ('truncal acne')
  • They have scars from acne 
  • They have inflammatory acne (red, swollen acne that is tender to the touch)
  • Their acne has suddenly got worse
  • Their spots are making them so unhappy that they feel less able to enjoy life.
What are the main effects of not having treatment?

Untreated acne may stay the same, or get worse, or get better. It's impossible to tell in advance whether your acne will get better or worse over time.[1]Williams HC, Dellavalle RP, Garner S. Acne vulgaris. Lancet. 2012; 379: 361-372.

If acne is left untreated, especially in the first three years of developing spots, it can leave permanent scars. Untreated acne can also result in hyper- or hypopigmentation (skin colour changes), which can be particularly troublesome for people with skin of colour.

What are the main side effects of not having treatment?

If acne is left untreated, especially in the first three years of developing spots, it can leave permanent scars.

How might not having treatment affect my life?

We don't know how not having treatment might affect your life. That may depend on whether your acne gets worse, gets better or stays the same.

Some people with acne have  emotional problems, including embarrassment, poor self-image, low self-esteem, self-consciousness, frustration, and anger.[11]Magin P, Adams J, Heading G, et al. Psychological sequelae of acne vulgaris: Results of a qualitative study. Canadian Family Physician. 2006; 52:978-979.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT LIFESTYLE CHANGES

What are lifestyle changes?

In this decision aid, we're looking at three lifestyle changes: diet, complementary therapies, and cosmetics (to mask, rather than treat, acne).

Diet

Some people believe that certain foods and drinks, such as chocolate, milk, or coffee, causes acne or makes acne worse. We don't know if this is the case. You need to ensure your diet is healthy and balanced if you decide to avoid certain foods. You may want to talk to your GP before you try this.

Complementary therapies

Complementary therapies to treat acne include acupuncture, Ayurvedic treatments (oral and topical), homeopathy, essential oils, and Chinese herbal remedies. Some complementary therapies are said to help reduce acne inflammation and others are believed to help kill bacteria.

Cosmetics

These include concealers, correctors, foundation, and other 'camouflaging' cosmetics. Cosmetics don't aim to treat acne, but rather to mask it. You can use cosmetics even if you are having treatment. If you are using topical therapies, though, you must remember to remove the cosmetics before applying the topical therapy.

Who can try lifestyle changes?

Anyone can try lifestyle changes, whether their acne is mild, moderate, or severe.

What are the main effects of lifestyle changes on acne?

Diet

Many different foods, including sugary foods, greasy foods, and milk (particularly skimmed milk) have been said to cause acne. We don’t know for certain that this is the case. We do know that some groups of people that don’t have a Western-diet, such as the Inuit (indigenous people of the Arctic region) and Ache (native hunter-gatherers from Paraguay), don’t have acne, but when these groups are exposed to Western diets, they are prone to acne.[12]Smith RN, Mann NJ, Baue A, et al. A low-glycemic load diet improves symptoms in acne vulgaris patients: a randomized controlled trial. American Journal of Clinical Nutrition. 2007; 86: 107-115.

Western diets are high in foods that convert quickly into energy. These so-called 'high glycaemic-index' (high GI) foods include corn flakes, white rice, white bread, biscuits, and ice cream. Your body converts low glycaemic-index foods into energy much more slowly. Low glycaemic-index (low GI) foods include beans, seeds, vegetables, and fruits.

In a small group of young men with mild to moderate facial acne (43 people), those put on a low GI diet (23 men) for 12 weeks had almost twice as much acne clear up as those on a high GI diet (20 men).[12]Smith RN, Mann NJ, Baue A, et al. A low-glycemic load diet improves symptoms in acne vulgaris patients: a randomized controlled trial. American Journal of Clinical Nutrition. 2007; 86: 107-115. Because it was only a small group, it is not possible to say for certain that low GI diets will help improve your acne. This is one of a number of studies that suggest that diet may have an effect on acne. We need more proof before we can be sure of the effect of diet on acne.

Complementary therapies

There is a huge variety of complementary therapies to treat acne. We don’t know if any of them work because there is not enough research to say.[14]Magin PJ, Adams J, Pond CD, et al. Topical and oral CAM in acne: A review of the empirical evidence and consideration of its context. Complementary Therapies in Medicine. 2006; 14: 62-67.

If you are considering seeing a complementary medicine practitioner, you may want to check their credentials first and ensure that they are registered with a professional body. 

Cosmetics to mask acne

Camouflaging your acne with concealers, correctors and foundations may give you more confidence when socialising. Cosmetics are not just for women. There are concealers marketed specifically for men.

What are the main side effects of lifestyle changes?

Diet

As long as you don’t make extreme changes to your diet, you are unlikely to experience any side effects.

Complementary therapies

There are too many complementary therapies to list the side effects.

Cosmetics to mask acne

There are no side effects from using cosmetics to mask acne. You may want to look for products labelled 'non-comedogenic', which means they are unlikely to make acne worse or cause skin irritation (irritant contact dermatitis). Thick make-up and make-up that contains oil may block the pores and cause a different type of acne known as pomade acne.

It is important to remember to remove cosmetics with a gentle cleanser at night, especially before applying topical therapies.

How might lifestyle changes affect my life?

Diet

Changing your diet might mean you have to buy and prepare special meals. If you are dependent on your parents and school for meals, you may find sticking to a diet particularly difficult.

Complementary therapies

There are too many complementary therapies to comment on how each will affect your life. As with any therapy, you will need to apply the cream, take the supplement or have the treatment, at regular intervals.

Cosmetics

Cosmetics that effectively mask acne can increase your self esteem and confidence in social situations. You will need to learn how to use cosmetics so they look natural.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT TREATMENTS USED ON THE SKIN (TOPICAL TREATMENTS)

What are treatments used on the skin?

Treatments that are applied to the skin are called topical therapies. They come in a number of forms including creams, gels, foams, and washes. Some can be bought without a prescription at pharmacies and shops. Others need a prescription from your doctor.

There are many treatments used on the skin for acne. These are some of the better known ones:

Retinoids work by stopping dead skin cells from clogging pores. They can also help reduce inflammation (redness and swelling). Topical retinoids are usually used alongside other topical therapies, such as benzoyl peroxide and topical antibiotics. Because they unplug pores, they allow other therapies to enter the pores and work more effectively. Topical retinoids are usually used for moderate to severe acne. They are usually applied to the skin once a day, at night time, about half an hour after the skin has been washed. They can only be bought with a prescription. Topical retinoids include adapalene, isotretinoin, and tretinoin.

Benzoyl peroxide helps to kill bacteria and unblock pores. You use the cream or gel once or twice a day, usually after you wash. Benzoyl peroxide can be bought without a prescription. It is sometimes used alongside antibiotics to prevent bacteria becoming resistant to antibiotics.

Antibiotics are medicines that kill bacteria. Using an antibiotic gel or cream helps to get rid of the bacteria that make your spots red and inflamed. Topical antibiotics only treat inflammatory acne. They do not treat blackheads or whiteheads. You'll need to use your treatment once or twice a day for two months. You will need a prescription for topical antibiotics. Topical antibiotics include clindamycin and erythromycin.

Azelaic acid stops bacteria multiplying on the surface of the skin. It also helps to keep skin pores clear. If benzoyl peroxide irritates your skin too much, you may wish to try azelaic acid as it tends to cause less irritation. Azelaic acid is applied to the skin once or twice a day. It can only be bought with a prescription.

Nicotinamide reduces the inflammation (redness and swelling) in inflammatory acne. It is used to treat mild to moderate acne. Nicotinamide needs to be applied to the skin twice a day. Gels containing nicotinamide can be bought without a prescription.

Salicylic acid works by stopping dead skin cells from clogging pores. It is used to treat blackheads and whiteheads. If retinoids irritate your skin too much, you may wish to try salicylic acid as it tends to cause less irritation. Gels and washes containing salicylic acid can be bought without a prescription.

Some of these treatments contain more than one of the above substances. Gels that contain retinoids or benzoyl peroxide also often contain an antibiotic. These combination topical therapies usually work better than a single topical therapy alone.[15]Strauss JS, Krowchuk DP, Leyden JJ. Guidelines of care for acne vulgaris management. Journal of the American Academy of Dermatology. 2007; 56: 651-663. Some contain zinc. We don't know if zinc makes them better at treating acne, but we do know that zinc, on its own, is not effective at treating acne.[15]Strauss JS, Krowchuk DP, Leyden JJ. Guidelines of care for acne vulgaris management. Journal of the American Academy of Dermatology. 2007; 56: 651-663.

Who can have treatments used on the skin?

Most people with acne can choose treatments used on the skin. People with mild or moderate acne are more likely to be advised to try these treatments. They are also sometimes used alongside oral medicines, for people who have more severe acne. For example, if oral antibiotics are used for more than two months, doctors recommend using benzoyl peroxide for at least five to seven days between antibiotic courses to kill any surviving bacteria on the skin.[1]Williams HC, Dellavalle RP, Garner S. Acne vulgaris. Lancet. 2012; 379: 361-372.

Topical retinoids must not be taken by pregnant or breastfeeding women or women who are trying to conceive.

What are the main effects of treatments used on the skin on acne?

These treatments, used individually or in combination, improve mild or moderate acne.

Topical retinoids can be used to treat blackheads, whiteheads, and inflammatory acne. Benzoyl peroxide combined with a retinoid or antibiotic works when used on mild or moderate acne.[18]Nast A, Dréno B, Bettoli V, et al. Guidelines for the treatment of acne (ICD L70.0). European evidence based (S3). Journal of the European Academy of Dermatology and Venereology. 2012; 26 (supplement 1): S1-S29. There are many other treatments used on the skin, and combinations of treatments, that improve mild or moderate acne. Your pharmacist or GP can give you some more suggestions if these don't work for you.

Treatments used on the skin help in one or more of the following ways:

  • They reduce the number of bacteria that live on the skin
  • They help the skin shed cells more effectively (which helps to unclog blocked pores)
  • They reduce inflammation (redness and swelling).

These treatments only work on the area of skin where you put them. Oral medicines treat the whole body.

 

What are the main side effects of treatments used on the skin?

These treatments can irritate the skin. This may cause dry skin, redness, stinging, itching, and burning. Some treatments, such as azelaic acid and antibiotics, can make your skin sensitive to sunlight. Many of these treatments make the skin feel tight.

There are too many topical therapies to list all of the side effects here. You can ask your pharmacist about the potential side effects of individual treatments, or read the label. 

If you have any mild side effects (such as skin irritation), you may want to reduce the amount of the treatment you use and apply it less often. These treatments are made up in different substances to make them easy to apply to the skin. Some, such as alcohol, are harsher on the skin than others. If you are using an alcohol-solution treatment that is irritating your skin, you could try it in a different form, maybe in a moisturising cream. If the irritation doesn’t go away after a few weeks, even when you use less or use it less often, you might want to consider a different treatment.

If you have any severe side effects or are worried about the side effects of your treatment, see your GP.

Side effects tend to improve with time.

Benzoyl peroxide can bleach your clothes, bedding, and any other textiles your treated skin comes into contact with.

How might treatments used on the skin affect my life?

The instructions on topical treatments usually advise that the treatment is applied once or sometimes twice a day. Once the spots are better the number of times the treatment need to be applied can be reduced. It may not be necessary to apply the treatment every day.

Topical treatments need to be applied to the entire affected area (face, back, shoulders, etc.), not just the spots you can see at the time. If the treatment irritates the skin, try using it for short periods and gradually build up the time it is in contact with your skin.

It can take six to eight weeks before you see the benefits of these treatments. Many people give up before the treatment has had a chance to work. If you start on a treatment, it is important to continue with it for at least four weeks to give it a chance to show an effect.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT ORAL MEDICINES

What are oral medicines?

Oral medicines are taken by mouth (tablets or capsules). They treat your whole body rather than just the affected area of skin.

There are three main types of oral medicine for treating acne:

  • Oral antibiotics reduce the number of bacteria on the skin. They are usually used alongside a topical treatment, such as benzoyl peroxide, to kill bacteria that survive the antibiotics. There are three main types ("classes") of oral antibiotic used to treat acne: tetracyclines such as tetracycline, doxycycline, lymecycline, and minocycline; macrolides such as erythromycin and azithromycin; and trimethoprim.
  • Hormone therapies, especially combined oral contraceptive pills that contain the 'female' hormones oestrogen and progestin, work by reducing the effect of 'male' hormones (androgens). Androgens (such as testosterone) increase the activity of the glands that produce sebum. Hormone therapies can be used by some girls who have started their periods or some women.
  • Isotretinoin is an oral retinoid (a substance related to vitamin A) that has many effects on acne, including reducing the amount of sebum the skin produces.

Another treatment sometimes prescribed to treat acne is spironolactone, which works by blocking the effects of hormones called androgens.

Oral medicines are only available with a doctor's prescription. Isotretinoin is used for severe acne that has not cleared with other treatments. It can only be prescribed by a specialist skin doctor (a dermatologist).

Who can have oral medicines?

Oral medicines are usually used to treat moderate to severe acne. People are more likely to have oral medicines if they have:

  • Severe acne
  • Acne that is on the torso (chest, back, shoulders)
  • Acne that hasn't responded to topical treatments, or oral antibiotics, or both
  • Acne that is likely to form scars
  • Acne that is causing emotional upset.

Oral antibiotics

People with kidney or liver disease may not be able to take certain oral antibiotics. Also, doctors are not keen to prescribe some antibiotics (especially macrolides and trimethoprim) because they are becoming less effective.[1]Williams HC, Dellavalle RP, Garner S. Acne vulgaris. Lancet. 2012; 379: 361-372.

Children under 12 years of age are advised against taking tetracycline antibiotics because it can permanently discolour tooth enamel and affect their bone development.

If oral antibiotics are used for more than two months, it is recommended that benzoyl peroxide (a topical therapy) is used alongside antibiotics or for at least five to seven days between antibiotic courses to reduce surviving bacteria on the skin.

Hormone therapies

Hormone therapies can be used by girls who have started their periods or some women. The combined oral contraceptive pill can be suitable for women who have acne flare-ups before their period starts, and who need contraception.

People with one or more of the following 'risk factors' (things that can make taking the drug dangerous) may be advised against taking the combined oral contraceptive pill:

  • High blood pressure
  • A personal or family history of developing blood clots (thrombosis)
  • Heart problems, including an irregular heartbeat
  • Obesity
  • Age over 40
  • Smoker.

Isotretinoin

Isotretinoin is prescribed for people with moderate or severe acne. It can have serious side effects and is only recommended when all other therapies (including oral antibiotics) have failed. People need to be referred to a skin specialist (dermatologist) before they are prescribed isotretinoin. Isotretinoin can only be prescribed by a dermatologist.

Isotretinoin must not be taken by pregnant or breastfeeding women, women who are trying to conceive, girls under 12 years old, or people taking tetracycline antibiotics.

Buying prescription medicine on the internet

Some people may be tempted to bypass their GP or dermatologist and buy medicines over the internet without a prescription. This is a very dangerous thing to do. Most medicines bought on the internet are fake (counterfeit). Some contain the drug they claim to be selling, but in the wrong amount (which can have cause side effects) or they contain none of the drug they claim to be selling, only harmful substances.

What are the main effects of oral medicines on acne?

Oral antibiotics

Bacteria called Propionibacterium acnes live naturally in hair follicles where they live on sebum. They don't cause any trouble, unless they become trapped in hair follicles. When this happens, the area around the hair follicles becomes inflamed (red and swollen) as the body's immune system tries to fight the bacteria. Oral antibiotics reduce these bacteria. They work well at treating inflammatory acne, but they cannot clear acne completely.[1]Williams HC, Dellavalle RP, Garner S. Acne vulgaris. Lancet. 2012; 379: 361-372. 

Hormone therapies

The combined oral contraceptive pill and other hormone therapies work well at reducing inflammatory and non-inflammatory acne. We don’t know if combined oral contraceptives work better or worse than oral antibiotics in treating acne.[21]Arowojolu AO, Gallo MF, Lopez LM, et al. Combined oral contraceptive pills for treatment of acne (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

Isotretinoin

This is the most effective treatment for severe acne. Most cases of acne will be clear by the end of a course of isotretinoin, and 85 out of every 100 people who take isotretinoin will remain clear.[1]Williams HC, Dellavalle RP, Garner S. Acne vulgaris. Lancet. 2012; 379: 361-372.  Isotretinoin can reduce the amount of scarring if given early in the course of the disease.

What are the main side effects of oral medicines?

The possible side effects of oral antibiotics depend on the type of antibiotic you are taking.

How might oral medicines affect my life?

Antibiotics

You will need to take antibiotic pills once or twice a day (depending on the antibiotic), every day, for at least six months.

Some people become sensitive to sunlight when taking tetracylines. If you spend a lot of time outdoors you should wear sunscreen. You will also need to avoid using sunbeds.

Hormone therapies

The way you take hormone treatment varies between different types. For some common types, you have to remember to take a pill every day for three weeks, followed by a week off, for as long as you need treatment. Other types are taken continuously.

Isotretinoin

Isotretinoin needs to be taken once or twice a day, every day, for at least six months.[18]Nast A, Dréno B, Bettoli V, et al. Guidelines for the treatment of acne (ICD L70.0). European evidence based (S3). Journal of the European Academy of Dermatology and Venereology. 2012; 26 (supplement 1): S1-S29.

If you are a woman and you are prescribed isotretinoin, you will need to sign a form saying that you understand the risk of birth defects and you are willing to use contraceptives to avoid becoming pregnant, even if you are not currently sexually active. Contraceptives need to be used at least one month before treatment, throughout treatment, and at least one month after stopping treatment. You may be advised to use more than one form of contraception.

Some people become sensitive to sunlight when taking isotretinoin. If you spend a lot of time outdoors you should wear sunscreen. You will also need to avoid using sunbeds.

You will need to avoid having hair waxed, laser treatment, and any other treatment that is rough to your skin while taking isotretinoin, and for six months after stopping treatment. These procedures could cause scarring.

Isotretinoin can cause dry eyes. If you wear contact lenses, you may need to switch to glasses if your eyes become dry.

Isotretinoin may also affect your night vision. If this is the case, it may make driving at night difficult or impossible.

You must not donate blood while on isotretinoin.

Before you take any vitamins, supplements, or medicine that can be bought without a prescription, you need to check with your doctor to ensure they are safe to take. Some medicines and supplements (such as vitamin A), react with isotretinoin and can make side effects worse.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT LIGHT THERAPY

What is light therapy?

Light therapy (also called 'photo-therapy') involves using machines to focus certain types of light at the skin. Light therapy includes many different types of treatment, for example using light at different wavelengths.

In photodynamic therapy, a special light-sensitive lotion is applied to the affected area before light is directed at the skin. The lotion helps the light therapy work more effectively.

All light therapies work in one of three ways:

  • They kill bacteria
  • They reduce inflammation
  • They shrink the glands that make sebum.

Some light therapies also work for acne scars but only those that are helpful for active acne are discussed in this decision aid.

Who can have light therapy?

If you have mild or moderate acne that hasn't responded to topical therapies, you might want to consider light therapy.

You should avoid light therapy if you are taking oral antibiotics or isotretinoin as these medications may make your skin more sensitive to light.

Light therapy to treat acne is generally not available through the NHS. Many private clinics offer light therapy and some pharmacies and online retailers sell light therapy devices that can be used at home.

What are the main effects of light therapy on acne?

Light therapy can improve acne in the short term, but we don't know if it has long-term benefits.[1]Williams HC, Dellavalle RP, Garner S. Acne vulgaris. Lancet. 2012; 379: 361-372.

Photodynamic therapy seems to work better than other light therapies at treating acne, but the harsh side effects mean that many people don't complete their treatment.[30]Hamilton FL, Car J, Lyons C, et al. Laser and other light therapies for the treatment of acne vulgaris: systematic review. British Journal of Dermatology. 2009; 160: 1273-1285.

We don't know if light therapies are better than other acne treatments.

Light therapy doesn't work for everyone. It is impossible to tell in advance if it will work for you.

What are the main side effects of light therapy?

Possible side effects of light therapy are:

  • Pain 
  • Redness of the skin
  • Swelling 
  • Itching
  • Darkening of the skin.

Pain, redness, swelling, and itching usually go away a few hours after treatment, but skin darkening can take up to three months to clear.[30]Hamilton FL, Car J, Lyons C, et al. Laser and other light therapies for the treatment of acne vulgaris: systematic review. British Journal of Dermatology. 2009; 160: 1273-1285.

Photodynamic therapy (which involves applying light-sensitive cream to the skin) tends to be more painful than therapies that rely on light alone.[30]Hamilton FL, Car J, Lyons C, et al. Laser and other light therapies for the treatment of acne vulgaris: systematic review. British Journal of Dermatology. 2009; 160: 1273-1285.

Side effects of photodynamic therapy include:

  • Pain
  • Redness of the skin
  • Severe folliculitis (inflammation of the hair follicles).

The side effects of photodynamic therapy can be bad enough that people decide to stay off work or college after treatment.[30]Hamilton FL, Car J, Lyons C, et al. Laser and other light therapies for the treatment of acne vulgaris: systematic review. British Journal of Dermatology. 2009; 160: 1273-1285.

How might light therapy affect my life?

The number of treatments and the time between them will be determined by how bad your acne is and the type of machine used.

If you don't have a light device at home, you will need to go to a clinic for your treatment.

Sometimes, light therapy can make your acne worse for the first few days after therapy. If you are already feeling self-conscious about your acne, you may want to bear this in mind.
