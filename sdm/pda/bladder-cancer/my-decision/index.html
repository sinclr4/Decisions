Our Decision Support telephone service is here to assist and guide you through the Shared Decision Making process.

The Decision Support team is made of professional Health Coaches who are clinicians, commonly nurses, who have undertaken additional training to become a Health Coach.

The Health Coaches will assist you by:

  • Providing you with information
  • Helping you to understand your condition
  • Recognise what is important to you regarding the outcome of treatment
  • Identifying potential solutions
  • Encourage you in discussing options with your family
  • Transferring skills which will assist you in using the information and resources available to you
  • Support you in building confidence in discussing your choice with your doctor
  • You may find that this can be achieved in one telephone call with a Health Coach however; if further calls are required to support you in reaching your decision the Health Coach will schedule these with you.

Important Things To Note

  • A Health Coach does not know which treatment option is best for you.
  • The Health Coach does not have information about your medical history.
  • You will still need to talk over your decision and reasons for your decision with your doctor or nurse.

MORE INFORMATION ABOUT BLADDER CANCER

What does the bladder do?

The bladder is an organ that stores urine. Urine is a mixture of waste products and water that comes from the kidneys. When the bladder is full of urine, it passes it out of the body through a tube called the urethra.

What is high-risk non-muscle-invasive bladder cancer?

Cancer is abnormal and uncontrolled cell growth in the body. Non-muscle-invasive bladder cancer is when these abnormal cells are found within the inner layers of the bladder, but not in the main muscle coat. It is a common type of bladder cancer.

Specialist doctors (urologists) and nurses are able to provide exact details about the different forms of bladder cancer.

Urologists may talk about the 'stage' of bladder cancer (Ta, T1, Tis), which is a way of describing the cancer based on where it is in the bladder, and if it has spread.

They may also talk about the 'grade' of bladder cancer, which is what the cancer cells look like under the microscope. Cancers that are called low grade (Grade 1) look like normal cells and tend to grow slowly and are unlikely to spread. Cancers that are called intermediate grade (Grade 2) look somewhat abnormal, and some grow slowly with little chance of spreading, and others grow more quickly with a higher chance of spreading. Cancers that are called high grade (Grade 3) look abnormal and grow more quickly and are likely to spread.

High-risk non-muscle-invasive bladder cancer involves the following forms of bladder cancer, either singly or in combination:

  • pTaG3 (cancer only in the surface lining of the bladder)
  • pT1G2 (cancer involving the tissue layer between the surface lining and the main muscle coat of the bladder)
  • pT1G3 (cancer involving the tissue layer between the surface lining and the main muscle coat of the bladder)
  • pTis (called carcinoma in situ, meaning cancer spreading in flat patches within the surface lining of the bladder)

It is called high-risk non-muscle-invasive bladder cancer for two reasons:

  • There is a risk that there is already cancer in the muscle wall of the bladder which has not been spotted yet (this is called understaging). This may be the case for between 20 in 100 to 30 in 100 people.[1]Kulkarni GS, Hakenberg OW, Gschwend JE, et al. An updated critical analysis of the treatment strategy for newly diagnosed high-grade T1 (previously T1G3) bladder cancer. European Urology. 2010; 57: 60-70. [2]Burger M, Witjes JA. Second international consultation on bladder cancer. 2012. Available at http://www.icud.info/bladdercancer2nd.html (accessed on 23 January 2013).
  • There is a risk of the cancer returning in a more advanced form (this is called progression). This means cancer spreading into, or beyond, the main muscle coat of the bladder. This can happen to between 15 in 100 and more than 70 in 100 people, depending on the exact type of high-risk non-muscle-invasive bladder cancer.[2]Burger M, Witjes JA. Second international consultation on bladder cancer. 2012. Available at http://www.icud.info/bladdercancer2nd.html (accessed on 23 January 2013).

When bladder cancer involves the muscle coat of the bladder, between 20 in 100 and 25 in 100 people will have their cancer spread into the lymph glands around the bladder. This sharply reduces the chance that it can be cured.[4]Efstathiou J, Quinn DI, Stenzl A. Muscle-invasive, presumably regional tumor. Second international consultation on bladder cancer. 2012. Available at http://www.icud.info/bladdercancer2nd.html (accessed on 23 January 2013). So most people want treatment to reduce the risk of cancer spreading to the muscle.

What are the symptoms of bladder cancer?

The most common symptom of bladder cancer is blood in the urine. Most people with bladder cancer see blood in their urine. The blood is usually bright red in colour, but it is sometimes dark brown, and there may be visible blood clots. If there is a small amount of blood in your urine, you may not be able to see this. A urine test can be carried out by your doctor, which will tell you if there is any blood in your urine even if it's a small amount which is not visible.

Other symptoms of bladder cancer include feeling pain when passing urine, feeling that you need to pass urine more than usual, and feeling that you need to pass urine very suddenly, although these symptoms are more commonly due to conditions other than cancer.

Why has this happened to me?

Bladder cancer becomes more common in people as they get older. It is also more common in men than women.

Some other things can also increase the chance of someone getting bladder cancer, such as smoking tobacco. Smoking may increase the chance of getting bladder cancer by two to three times.[5]Letasiova S, Medveova A, Sovcikova A, et al. Bladder cancer, a review of the environmental risk factors. Environmental health: a global access science source. 2012; 11 (supplement 1): S11. [6]Scottish Intercollegiate Guidelines Network. Management of transitional cell carcinoma of the bladder. December 2005. Guideline 85. Available at http://www.sign.ac.uk/guidelines/fulltext/85/index.html (accessed on 23 January 2013). Some jobs are also linked with bladder cancer, because it can be triggered by being in contact with some types of chemicals, such as certain dyes. Radiotherapy treatment of the pelvis for other cancers may also increase the chance of bladder cancer.

How do I find out whether I have bladder cancer?

Most people who have bladder cancer started by seeing their GP about their symptoms. The GP will ask about the symptoms and may ask for a urine test.

The GP will then make a decision on who needs to go to hospital to be seen by a specialist (a urologist), who may carry out more checks, such as blood tests and a scan.

The urologist may want to look inside the bladder. The procedure to do this is called a cystoscopy and it lasts for three to four minutes. This first cystoscopy is usually done using a local anaesthetic which numbs the area so it is not painful. A first cystoscopy involves passing a thin tube with a small telescopic camera into the opening of the urethra through to the bladder to see if there is anything abnormal in the bladder. Some people will need a second cystoscopy under general anaesthetic (where you are put to sleep) so that a small sample of the bladder tissue, called a biopsy, can be taken and examined under a microscope.

If the tests show that you have bladder cancer, you may be asked to go back to hospital for more scans to see how far the cancer extends, and to discuss how best to treat it.

How many people have bladder cancer?

More than 10,500 people find out they have bladder cancer each year in the UK.[7]Cancer Research UK. Bladder cancer risks and causes. August 2012. Available at http://www.cancerresearchuk.org/cancer-help/type/bladder-cancer/about/bladder-cancer-risks-and-causes (accessed on 24 January 2013).

What will happen if I have bladder cancer?

If you have bladder cancer, you will go into hospital to have a telescopic operation to remove the cancer from your bladder. This operation is called a transurethral resection of bladder tumour (or TURBT). After the operation your urologist will know more about your cancer.

There are several treatment options that your urologist will discuss with you after your operation, depending on what form of bladder cancer you have.

If you have high-risk non-muscle-invasive bladder cancer, the treatment options are:

  • Putting the Bacillus Calmette-Guérin (BCG) vaccine into the bladder
  • Surgery to remove the bladder completely (this is called a cystectomy)
  • Watchful waiting, where your urologist will keep monitoring you, with telescopic bladder checks (cystoscopy), to check whether the cancer has come back in the bladder.

Two of these three options, BCG and surgery to remove the bladder, reduce the risk of the cancer getting worse, but watchful waiting does not. Watchful waiting will simply identify whether the cancer has come back in the bladder, and what type it is.

The risk of your cancer getting worse with watchful waiting is high (between 15 in 100 and more than 70 in 100).[2]Burger M, Witjes JA. Second international consultation on bladder cancer. 2012. Available at http://www.icud.info/bladdercancer2nd.html (accessed on 23 January 2013). If the cancer gets worse, it may become difficult or impossible to cure.

If your general health is reasonable or good, you are likely to be advised to have an active treatment (BCG or surgery). If your general health is poor, and other medical conditions present a higher risk to your life than your bladder cancer, you might feel that watchful waiting is an option for you to consider, because you avoid the problems and side effects of treating your bladder cancer.

Your urologist will discuss how these treatments might affect your daily life, as well as the chances of the cancer returning or spreading. Whichever option you choose, you should discuss it carefully with your urologist, your specialist nurse, and those close to you. Your urologist should be someone who specialises in high-risk non-muscle-invasive bladder cancer.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT BCG VACCINE TREATMENT

What is BCG vaccine treatment for bladder cancer?

The Bacillus Calmette-Guérin (BCG) vaccine is used to treat bladder cancer. We do not know how the BCG vaccine works. It seems to make the body's immune system destroy cancer cells. The BCG vaccine is also used to prevent tuberculosis (TB) in other patients.

BCG vaccine treatment is given in a hospital. Your doctor or nurse will put a liquid form of the BCG vaccine into your bladder using a thin tube (called a catheter). The liquid BCG vaccine is then left in your bladder for two hours.[9]Gontero P, Bohle A, Malmstrom PU, et al. The role of bacillus Calmette-Guerin in the treatment of non-muscle-invasive bladder cancer. European Urology. 2010; 57: 410-429. [10]MacMillan Cancer Support. BCG treatment. Available at http://www.macmillan.org.uk/Cancerinformation/Cancertypes/Bladder/Treatingearly/BCGtreatment.aspx (accessed on 23 January 2013). You should not pass urine during this time. The whole treatment takes around three hours and you can usually go home as soon as it's finished.

Who can have BCG vaccine treatment?

BCG vaccine treatment is used for people with bladder cancer that is aggressive but has not extended into the main muscle of the bladder. This is called high-risk non-muscle-invasive bladder cancer. BCG vaccine treatment is not suitable for everyone. For example, it is not suitable for people with:

  • Cancer in the muscle wall of the bladder
  • Tuberculosis
  • Allergy to the BCG vaccine
  • A weak immune system (immunosuppression).
What are the main effects of BCG vaccine treatment on high-risk non-muscle-invasive bladder cancer?

The main effect of the BCG vaccine treatment is to reduce the chance of cancer coming back, or worsening by spreading into or beyond the muscle wall of the bladder. Bladder cancer comes back after BCG vaccine treatment in between 25 in 100 and 75 in 100 people.[1]Kulkarni GS, Hakenberg OW, Gschwend JE, et al. An updated critical analysis of the treatment strategy for newly diagnosed high-grade T1 (previously T1G3) bladder cancer. European Urology. 2010; 57: 60-70. The different figures are because the results vary a lot between different groups of people and between different studies of treatment.

Having BCG vaccine treatment may help you live longer than if you did not have treatment. In some groups of people, between 60 in 100 and 70 in 100 people having BCG vaccine treatment will live for at least another five years.[2]Burger M, Witjes JA. Second international consultation on bladder cancer. 2012. Available at http://www.icud.info/bladdercancer2nd.html (accessed on 23 January 2013). [13]Shahin O, Thalmann GN, Rentsch C, et al. A retrospective analysis of 153 patients treated with or without intravesical bacillus Calmette-Guerin for primary stage T1 grade 3 bladder cancer: recurrence, progression and survival. Journal of Urology. 2003; 169: 96-100. [14]Griffiths TR, Charlton M, Neal DE, et al. Treatment of carcinoma in situ with intravesical bacillus Calmette-Guerin without maintenance. Journal of Urology. 2002; 167: 2408-2412. However, up to half of those people will have needed to have had their bladder removed because the cancer came back after BCG treatment.

What are the main effects of BCG vaccine treatment on what you can do and how you feel?

There is not much information about how having BCG vaccine treatment affects what you can do and how you feel. You may feel relieved that you are having a treatment that cuts your chances of your cancer coming back.

What are the main side effects of BCG vaccine treatment in bladder cancer?

Fewer than 5 in 100 people get serious side effects with BCG vaccine treatment.[2]Burger M, Witjes JA. Second international consultation on bladder cancer. 2012. Available at http://www.icud.info/bladdercancer2nd.html (accessed on 23 January 2013). [9]Gontero P, Bohle A, Malmstrom PU, et al. The role of bacillus Calmette-Guerin in the treatment of non-muscle-invasive bladder cancer. European Urology. 2010; 57: 410-429. [17]Shelley MD, Court JB, Kynaston H, et al. Intravesical bacillus Calmette-Guerin versus mitomycin C for Ta and T1 bladder cancer (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. The most common side effect is bladder irritation. 

After having BCG vaccine treatment:[2]Burger M, Witjes JA. Second international consultation on bladder cancer. 2012. Available at http://www.icud.info/bladdercancer2nd.html (accessed on 23 January 2013). [9]Gontero P, Bohle A, Malmstrom PU, et al. The role of bacillus Calmette-Guerin in the treatment of non-muscle-invasive bladder cancer. European Urology. 2010; 57: 410-429. [17]Shelley MD, Court JB, Kynaston H, et al.   • About 70 to 80 out of 100 people want to pass urine more often than usual or have an irritated bladder, which feels a bit like having a urine infection (cystitis). This usually goes away within two to four weeks following treatment.
  • About 25 in 100 people have blood in their urine.
  • About 50 out of 100 people have flu-like symptoms for 24 to 48 hours after each BCG vaccine treatment. This usually goes away within a couple of days after treatment.

Other side effects after BCG vaccine treatment include reactions in other parts of the body, probably due to inflammation caused by the immune response to BCG.

Very rarely the BCG bacteria produces a generalised infection that needs urgent treatment, with the same drugs (antibiotics) that are used to treat TB.

How might BCG vaccine treatment affect my life?

You will need to go to hospital regularly for BCG vaccine treatment. The BCG vaccine is usually given once a week for six weeks, for the first round of treatment. If the treatment is working well for you, you may have it every three to six months, once a week for three weeks. You can continue having treatment for up to three years.[10]MacMillan Cancer Support. BCG treatment. Available at http://www.macmillan.org.uk/Cancerinformation/Cancertypes/Bladder/Treatingearly/BCGtreatment.aspx (accessed on 23 January 2013). [22]Lamm DL. You will be able to go about your daily life in more or less the same way as you would normally, fitting work and social life around your appointments.

When you go home after your BCG vaccine treatment, you will need to be careful not to splash your urine when you pass urine for about six hours after your treatment. That's because it will contain live bacteria. If you are a man, it may be easier to sit on the toilet to pass urine.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT SURGERY TO REMOVE THE BLADDER (CYSTECTOMY)

What is a cystectomy?

Cystectomy is an operation that removes the bladder. Some of the tissues around the bladder, such as the lymph nodes (lymph glands), will also be taken out. The tube that takes urine from the bladder to the outside of the body (the urethra) may be taken out. For women who have been through the menopause, the womb and ovaries will be removed if they have not already been removed at a previous operation (hysterectomy). For men, the prostate gland is taken out.
Cystectomy is carried out in a hospital under general anaesthetic, which means you will be asleep throughout the operation. The bladder is removed through a cut (incision) in the abdomen. The cut usually runs from just above the belly button to the pubic bone. In some hospitals, the bladder can be removed by keyhole surgery (laparoscopic or robotic surgery), where the operation is done with telescopic surgery via a few small cuts in the abdomen instead of one large cut.[4]Efstathiou J, Quinn DI, Stenzl A. Muscle-invasive, presumably regional tumor. Second international consultation on bladder cancer. 2012. Available at http://www.icud.info/bladdercancer2nd.html (accessed on 23 January 2013).

After your bladder is removed, you will need another way to drain urine from your body. There are several different options.

  • Urinary stoma: this is when urine is collected in a bag that sticks to the skin of your abdomen. Urine from your kidneys goes to a short piece of bowel (intestine) that passes out of the abdomen through a small opening (called a stoma) and allows urine to drain continually into a bag.
  • Bladder substitute (or 'new bladder', sometimes called a neobladder): a large length of bowel is used to make a replacement for your bladder, by forming a pouch (reservoir). The tubes that usually pass urine from the kidneys to the bladder (ureters) are attached directly to this pouch. With careful instruction and a good deal of effort, most people can learn to hold and pass urine in a more or less normal way.
  • Cutaneous catheterisable reservoir (a urine pouch sometimes described as a Mitrofanoff procedure): a urine pouch (reservoir) is made inside your abdomen from part of your intestine. The ureters are attached directly to the pouch. The pouch is connected to the surface using a channel made from bowel and attached to the skin of the abdominal wall, often at the navel. There is a valve mechanism that prevents continual leakage of urine. The pouch is emptied by passing a catheter into the pouch via the navel.
Who can have a cystectomy?

Cystectomy may be offered to many people with bladder cancer that is aggressive but has not extended into the main muscle coat of the bladder. This is known as high-risk non-muscle-invasive bladder cancer.

Cystectomy may not be suitable for everyone. It is a big operation and has important consequences. You need to understand what's involved before you decide whether it is right for you.

The complex nature of the surgery and what is involved, together with the need to be able to fully understand the issues and the procedures involved, means that cystectomy may not be in the best interests of all people. It may not be suitable for some people who:

  • Have poor general health
  • Are frail
  • Have memory difficulties
  • Have learning difficulties.
What are the main effects of cystectomy on high-risk non-muscle-invasive bladder cancer?

The main effect of cystectomy is to reduce the chances of this kind of bladder cancer coming back and to help you live longer. Some studies have found that overall, between 75 and 85 in 100 people with high-risk non-muscle-invasive bladder cancer who have cystectomy will live for at least another five years.[24]Stein JP, Lieskovsky G, Cote R, et al. Radical cystectomy in the treatment of invasive bladder cancer: long-term results in 1,054 patients. Journal of Clinical Oncology. 2001; 19: 666-675. [2]Burger M, Witjes JA. Second international consultation on bladder cancer. 2012. Available at http://www.icud.info/bladdercancer2nd.html (accessed on 23 January 2013). 

What are the main effects of cystectomy on what you can do and how you feel?

There is not much information about the effect of cystectomy on what you can do and how you feel. It is a big operation with important consequences for how you manage urination afterwards. 

You may feel relieved to have had a treatment that reduces the chances of your cancer coming back. Some people prefer to have their bladder taken out, because it gives them the best chance of cure from their bladder cancer. 

What are the main complications of cystectomy?

Some people with bladder cancer die from surgery to remove the bladder. This happens to between 1 and 4 in 100 people.[4]Efstathiou J, Quinn DI, Stenzl A. Muscle-invasive, presumably regional tumor. Second international consultation on bladder cancer. 2012. Available at http://www.icud.info/bladdercancer2nd.html (accessed on 23 January 2013). These numbers are from international studies, and hospitals in England and Wales are only allowed to do cystectomies if they can show that no more than 3.5 in 100 people die from this surgery in their hospital. Most hospitals should have lower death rates than that. You can ask your specialist what the rates of complications are in your hospital.

Between 25 in 100 and 50 in 100 people having surgery to remove their bladder will suffer a major complication. Complications can include:

  • Chest infection
  • Wound infection or breakdown
  • A blood clot (deep vein thrombosis or pulmonary embolus)
  • An abnormal heart rhythm
  • Angina
  • A heart attack
  • Stroke
  • Blockage or leakage from the surgical joins where the bowel is rejoined, or where the tubes from the kidneys (ureters) are joined to the bowel that makes the urinary drainage mechanism.

These complications might result in a longer hospital stay than planned.

Cystectomy is likely to affect your sex life. The nerves that control erections may be damaged during surgery. This means that almost all men who have cystecomy will either not get any erections after their surgery, or will not get erections firm enough for sex. Men almost always have their prostate gland taken out during the operation. This will mean that they cannot ejaculate after surgery.

In women, surgery to remove the bladder can make the vagina shorter and narrower. This may make having sex painful or difficult. The womb and ovaries may also be taken out during surgery. This will mean they will not be able to have children.

For some patients, surgery can be done in a way that attempts to preserve nerves involved with sensation during sex. If this sort of surgery is possible and safe for your particular cancer, this may reduce the impact of surgery on your sex life.

How might cystectomy affect my life?

You will usually need to stay in hospital for seven to 14 days after surgery. You will have a scar on your abdomen where the surgeon made the cut. You will need to see your urologist regularly for check-ups after surgery. This is to check the cancer has not come back, and to check that urinary drainage is working well.

You will have either a urinary stoma (a bag on your abdomen into which urine drains) or an internal pouch made from bowel, which replaces the bladder, depending on the type of surgery you have. The internal pouch is either connected to the urethra, so that you hold and pass urine more or less normally (bladder substitute) or connected to the abdominal wall, so that you empty the pouch by passing a plastic tube (catheter) into it several times each day (catheterisable reservoir).

Whichever of these forms of surgery is done, you will need to get used to the way that they work. The one that most people find simplest and easiest to use is a stoma (external bag). Either of the internal pouches (bladder substitute or catheterisable reservoir) requires more time and effort to get used to how they work and to learn to use them effectively. In the first few months after surgery, people with an internal pouch need more frequent follow-up visits to hospital.

Some people find it hard to come to terms with the stoma, or to manage it. This could affect your relationships with your partner, family, friends, or work colleagues. It may also affect how you feel when meeting new people. Managing a stoma affects people in different ways. 

These changes don't bother some people. Some people may become anxious, less confident, or depressed. Other people may not be worried by the changes.

Some people are bothered by having to get used to managing their urinary flow with a pouch, or by having a stoma. The research does not show that people's quality of life is better with one type of urinary drainage compared to another.[27]Gerharz EW, Mansson A, Hunt S, et al. Quality of life after cystectomy and urinary diversion: an evidence based analysis. Journal of Urology. 2005; 174: 1729-1736. [28]Mansson A, Davidsson T, Hunt S, et al. The quality of life in men after radical cystectomy with a continent cutaneous diversion or orthotopic bladder substitution: is there a difference? BJU International. 2002; 90: 386-390.

Unless you do heavy physical work, your surgery should not make any difference to your job once you have recovered from surgery. You should also be able to do exercise, such as swimming, running, horse riding, or golf, even if you have a stoma (external bag).

Many people find problems with their sex lives distressing. This can affect your relationship and your confidence.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT WATCHFUL WAITING

What is watchful waiting?

Watchful waiting is where you see your urologist regularly, to check your symptoms and to check that your cancer has not come back or spread.

Who can choose watchful waiting?

Anyone with bladder cancer can choose to have watchful waiting. You may not want to have any further treatment, or you may want to wait before starting any further treatment. If your general health is poor, the risk to your life from other health conditions may be greater than the risk from the bladder cancer. If that is the case, you may want to consider watchful waiting.

What are the main effects of watchful waiting on bladder cancer?

If you decide to have watchful waiting, your condition may stay the same or may get worse. Your bladder cancer won't get better.

It's hard to know how likely it is that your cancer will get worse, because it varies depending on the exact type of cancer. Different studies show different results. Between 15 in 100 and 75 in 100 people with high-risk non-muscle-invasive bladder cancer who decide not to have any further treatment after telescopic removal of the tumour, will find that their bladder cancer spreads into or beyond the muscle coat of the bladder within three to five years.[2]Burger M, Witjes JA. Second international consultation on bladder cancer. 2012. Available at http://www.icud.info/bladdercancer2nd.html (accessed on 23 January 2013). [13]Shahin O, Thalmann GN, Rentsch C, et al. A retrospective analysis of 153 patients treated with or without intravesical bacillus Calmette-Guerin for primary stage T1 grade 3 bladder cancer: recurrence, progression and survival. Journal of Urology. 2003; 169: 96-100. [14]Griffiths TR, Charlton M, Neal DE, et al. Treatment of carcinoma in situ with intravesical bacillus Calmette-Guerin without maintenance. Journal of Urology. 2002; 167: 2408-2412. 

If the cancer does come back, it may be removed by telescopic surgery, or removal of the bladder. This is not always possible.

What are the main effects of watchful waiting on what you can do and how you feel?

There is not much information about the effects of watchful waiting on what you can do and how you feel. You are likely to be able to do the same as you can do now, unless your cancer comes back or spreads. 

Some people may find it stressful to live with worrying about cancer coming back. Other people do not worry about this. Some people may prefer watchful waiting because it may mean they can avoid side effects and complications. 

What are the main side effects of watchful waiting?

There are no direct side effects from watchful waiting. Your urologist will keep checking your symptoms and checking your cancer to see if it has spread. If your cancer grows and spreads then your bladder cancer symptoms may get worse or you may get new symptoms.

How might watchful waiting affect my life?

You will need to have regular appointments with your urologist so that they can check your symptoms. Your doctor will also check that your cancer has not spread. You will need to have regular blood tests, examinations, and scans.

You will be able to go about your daily life in the same way as you would normally, fitting work and social life around your appointments.
