Our Decision Support telephone service is here to assist and guide you through the Shared Decision Making process.

The Decision Support team is made of professional Health Coaches who are clinicians, commonly nurses, who have undertaken additional training to become a Health Coach.

The Health Coaches will assist you by:

  • Providing you with information
  • Helping you to understand your condition
  • Recognise what is important to you regarding the outcome of treatment
  • Identifying potential solutions
  • Encourage you in discussing options with your family
  • Transferring skills which will assist you in using the information and resources available to you
  • Support you in building confidence in discussing your choice with your doctor
  • You may find that this can be achieved in one telephone call with a Health Coach however; if further calls are required to support you in reaching your decision the Health Coach will schedule these with you.

Important Things To Note

  • A Health Coach does not know which treatment option is best for you.
  • The Health Coach does not have information about your medical history.

MORE INFORMATION ABOUT CARPAL TUNNEL SYNDROME

What does the carpal tunnel do?

The carpal tunnel is a narrow gap running through the bones and ligaments in the wrist. A nerve that gives feeling in the hand (the median nerve) passes through this tunnel, along with the tendons (cords of tissue) that bend the fingers and wrist.

What is carpal tunnel syndrome?

The carpal tunnel is quite narrow and rigid. It's easy for the nerve inside the tunnel to get squeezed. This interferes with the messages that the nerve is sending to the brain, and this can cause carpal tunnel syndrome.

What are the symptoms of carpal tunnel syndrome?

The main symptoms of carpal tunnel syndrome include:[1]Quality Standards Subcommittee of the American Academy of Neurology. Practice parameter for carpal tunnel syndrome (summary statement). Neurology. 1993; 43: 2406-2409.

  • A dull ache in the hand, wrist, or arm
  • Tingling, pins and needles, or numbness in the hand
  • A weak or clumsy hand
  • Dry skin, swelling, or colour changes in the fingers and hand.

People who have symptoms that are severe and come on very suddenly (this is called acute severe pain) should see a doctor as soon as possible.

The symptoms may come and go, but they will probably get worse over time. More than 33 in 100 people with carpal tunnel syndrome recover completely within six months without treatment, although symptoms could return later.[2]Futami T, Kobayashi A, Utika T, et al. Carpal tunnel syndrome; its natural history. Hand Surgery. 1997; 2: 129-130.

Some people with carpal tunnel syndrome might drop things as their grip becomes weaker. Some people can't use their hands to feel whether things are hot or cold. If the nerve damage becomes severe, they may lose the feeling in their hand. They might not be able to use that hand much anymore.

People with carpal tunnel syndrome may find that their symptoms are worse after certain activities, such as painting, knitting, or driving. They might find that repetitive movements of the hand and wrist, or holding the arm or hand in one position, bring on the symptoms.[1]Quality Standards Subcommittee of the American Academy of Neurology. Practice parameter for carpal tunnel syndrome (summary statement). Neurology. 1993; 43: 2406-2409. They may wake up at night because their hand tingles or their wrist aches. Shaking the hand or changing its position often makes it feel better.

People who have symptoms that are very bad for three to four weeks at a time should speak to a doctor as soon as possible. Early treatment can help.

If carpal tunnel syndrome affects both of the hands, the dominant hand (the right one for someone who is right-handed) is usually affected first and more severely.

What causes carpal tunnel syndrome?

Carpal tunnel syndrome can sometimes be what’s called ‘idiopathic’ – this means it’s not possible to say what caused it. Although it's not always easy to say exactly what causes carpal tunnel syndrome,[4]Von Schroeder H, Botte MJ. Carpal tunnel syndrome. Hand Clinics. 1996; 12: 643-655. we know that certain things make someone more likely to get it. These things are called risk factors. They include:[4]Von Schroeder H, Botte MJ. Carpal tunnel syndrome. Hand Clinics. 1996; 12: 643-655.[6]De Krom MCTF, Kester A, Knipschild P, et al. Risk factors for carpal tunnel syndrome. American Journal of Epidemiology. 1990; 132: 1102-1110.

  • Being born with a small carpal tunnel.
  • Getting an injury to the wrist that causes swelling, such as a fracture or sprain.
  • Regularly doing things that put a lot of pressure on the wrist, and repeating a movement. There are many activities like this. Examples include using power tools, cleaning, sewing, and working on an assembly line.
  • Having fluid retention (extra fluid in the body) during pregnancy or the menopause.
  • Having a cyst (a swelling full of fluid) or tumour (a growth) in the carpal tunnel.
  • Having certain medical conditions, such as diabetes, hypothyroidism, or rheumatoid arthritis.[7]McCartan B, Ashby E, Taylor EJ, et al. Carpal tunnel syndrome. British Journal of Hospital Medicine. 2012; 73: 199-202.[8]Rempel D, Evanoff B, Amadio PC, et al. Consensus criteria for the classification of carpal tunnel syndrome in epidemiologic studies. American Journal of Public Health. 1998; 88: 1447-1451.
  • Being overweight, or losing weight quickly, which may cause swelling around the median nerve.
How do I find out if I have carpal tunnel syndrome?

A doctor may suspect that someone has carpal tunnel syndrome if they have symptoms like numbness, tingling, burning, or aching pains in their wrist, hand, and fingers.

A test where the doctor taps or puts pressure on the nerve at the wrist might make their fingers tingle (this is called Tinel's sign).[1]Quality Standards Subcommittee of the American Academy of Neurology. Practice parameter for carpal tunnel syndrome (summary statement). Neurology. 1993; 43: 2406-2409. Another test involves pressing the backs of the hands together and pointing the fingers down for a minute. This may cause tingling or numbness in the fingers (Phalen's test).

Further tests may be needed if: 

  • the doctor is not certain whether or not someone has carpal tunnel syndrome
  • the doctor suspects they may have another condition called neuropathy, or
  • they are still having pain, numbness, or tingling after surgery.[10]British Society for Surgery of the Hand. Evidence for surgical treatment 1: Carpal Tunnel Syndrome (CTS). 2011. Available at: http://www.bssh.ac.uk/education/guidelines/carpal_tunnel_syndrome.pdf (accessed on 23 October 2012).

These tests measure the electrical activity of the nerves and muscles and will show whether someone has carpal tunnel syndrome and, if so, how bad it is.[8]Rempel D, Evanoff B, Amadio PC, et al. Consensus criteria for the classification of carpal tunnel syndrome in epidemiologic studies. American Journal of Public Health. 1998; 88: 1447-1451.

How many people have carpal tunnel syndrome?

About 3 in 100 men and 5 in 100 women have carpal tunnel syndrome.[12]Atroshi I, Gummesson C, Johnsson R, et al. Prevalence of carpal tunnel syndrome in a general population. Journal of the American Medical Association. 1999; 282: 153-158. Women are more likely to have it than men.

Men are more likely to get carpal tunnel syndrome as they get older.[4]Von Schroeder H, Botte MJ. Carpal tunnel syndrome. Hand Clinics. 1996; 12: 643-655.[14]Stevens JC, Sun S, Beard CM, et al. Carpal tunnel syndrome in Rochester, Minnesota, 1961 to 1980. Neurology. 1988; 38: 134-138. Women are more likely to get carpal tunnel syndrome between the ages of 45 and 54. Carpal tunnel syndrome is common in pregnant women. Up to about 60 in 100 pregnant women get symptoms of carpal tunnel syndrome.[15]Pazzaglia C, Caliandro P, Aprile I, et al. Multicenter study on carpal tunnel syndrome and pregnancy incidence and natural course. Acta Neurochirurgica. 2005; 92: 35-39.

What will happen?

Most people with carpal tunnel syndrome don’t seek medical attention for several months or years. Most people find it affects some aspect of how they perform their daily activities.[16]Jerosch-Herold C, Mason R, Chojnowski AJ. A qualitative study of the experiences and expectations of surgery in patients with carpal tunnel syndrome. J HAND THER. 2008;21:54–62. Some people find they have to change the way they do things to work around the problem.[17]Khu KJ, Bernstein M, Midha R. Patients' perceptions of carpal tunnel and ulnar nerve decompression surgery. Canadian Journal of Neurological Sciences. 2011; 38: 268-273.

More than 33 in 100 people with carpal tunnel syndrome recover completely within six months without treatment, although symptoms could return at a later date.[2]Futami T, Kobayashi A, Utika T, et al. Carpal tunnel syndrome; its natural history. Hand Surgery. 1997; 2: 129-130. The symptoms are more likely to go away after pregnancy in people who are pregnant when the symptoms develop or who are young and haven't had carpal tunnel syndrome for long.[19]Marshall S, Tardif G, Ashworth N. Local corticosteroid injection for carpal tunnel syndrome (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. The symptoms are less likely to go away without treatment if the pain is in both wrists and if the damage to the nerve is more severe.[19]Marshall S, Tardif G, Ashworth N. Local corticosteroid injection for carpal tunnel syndrome (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. Most pregnant women find the symptoms go after they've given birth, although some still have symptoms a year later.[15]Pazzaglia C, Caliandro P, Aprile I, et al. Multicenter study on carpal tunnel syndrome and pregnancy incidence and natural course. Acta Neurochirurgica. 2005; 92: 35-39.

Carpal tunnel syndrome can get worse over time.[2]Futami T, Kobayashi A, Utika T, et al. Carpal tunnel syndrome; its natural history. Hand Surgery. 1997; 2: 129-130. Some people who have severe carpal tunnel syndrome permanently lose most of the use of their hand.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT LIFESTYLE CHANGES

What are lifestyle changes?

People with carpal tunnel syndrome can choose to have no medical treatments, such as medications or surgery, for the moment. But that doesn't mean that they can't do anything about their symptoms. Some people choose to self-manage their condition. That just means they find out about the condition themselves, and do things to try to improve it.

People with carpal tunnel syndrome can try making changes to their workplace or their daily lifestyle, activities, and hobbies that may help improve their symptoms. Examples include:

  • Changes to lifestyle and activities: if someone with carpal tunnel syndrome has a hobby or activity that causes them pain, they could try doing it less often, or stopping it altogether for a while. They could try changing the hand's position from time to time when doing things that cause them pain.
  • Changes to the work environment: this means adjusting desks, chairs, and computer monitors, and using specially designed keyboards (sometimes called ergonomic keyboards) or equipment to achieve the best posture for the hand and the forearm. There are different types of computer mouse designed for people with hand pain, such as a trackball or joystick mouse. People may choose to do their own research (perhaps on the internet) on things like good posture, stretching exercises, and the best equipment to use.
  • Using aids: such as special scissors and shears.

It may be possible for someone with carpal tunnel syndrome to see an occupational therapist where they work, who can provide advice on how to change activities that cause them difficulty.

Who can change their lifestyle?

Anyone with carpal tunnel syndrome can choose to self-manage their symptoms. People may be more likely to want to do this if their symptoms come and go and are not severe.

If someone chooses to change their lifestyle, their doctor may suggest some lifestyle and activity changes and give advice about how they can self-manage their condition. Or the doctor may refer them to an occupational therapist. People can also see an occupational therapist privately, without a GP referral.

What are the main effects of lifestyle changes on carpal tunnel syndrome?

Some people say that making changes to their lifestyle, work equipment, and activities helps them with their symptoms. There has not been enough research for us to be certain of the effects of lifestyle changes on carpal tunnel syndrome.[23]O'Connor D, Page MJ, Marshall SC, et al. Ergonomic positioning or equipment for treating carpal tunnel syndrome (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

What are the main effects of lifestyle changes on what people can do and how they feel?

If making changes to their lifestyle and activities improves their symptoms, people may find it easier to do things such as typing, writing, driving, or picking up objects. There has not been enough research for us to be certain of the effects of lifestyle changes on carpal tunnel syndrome.[23]O'Connor D, Page MJ, Marshall SC, et al. Ergonomic positioning or equipment for treating carpal tunnel syndrome (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

What are the main side effects of lifestyle changes?

Done sensibly, self-managing symptoms is unlikely to have side effects. People who choose to self-manage their symptoms can check with their doctor or occupational therapist before buying special equipment or aids, to make sure they make the best choices.

How might lifestyle changes affect your life?

People who have occupational therapy will need to attend a number of sessions, and set aside time for these. Their doctor should be able to tell them how many sessions they are likely to need, and how often they might need to go to the hospital or clinic. Some occupational therapists visit people at home.

People with carpal tunnel syndrome can ask their employer for an assessment of their workplace. Specially designed equipment such as keyboards, arm rests, and computer mouse devices can be expensive. People can seek advice about what equipment will work best for them.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT PAIN RELIEF MEDICATION

What are pain medications?

Pain medications are drug treatments designed to reduce pain. Some of these tablets can be bought from a pharmacy without a prescription, while others need to be prescribed by a doctor.

  • Over the counter medicines: medicines that can be bought without a prescription include paracetamol, paracetamol combined with low-strength codeine, and some non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) such as ibuprofen and aspirin.
  • Prescription medicines: these include stronger NSAIDs, cyclooxygenase 2 (COX-2) inhibitors, codeine in stronger doses, or other medicines.
Who can have pain medications?

Most people can take some types of pain medication. People who have certain medical conditions, including asthma or a stomach ulcer, may not be able to take medications such as NSAIDs because they might worsen the condition.

People with carpal tunnel syndrome who also have chronic kidney disease should take care when taking NSAIDs. People with both of these conditions may be advised not to take NSAIDs at all, or only now and then.

Older people with heart problems may be advised not to take NSAIDs.

What are the main effects of pain medications on carpal tunnel syndrome?

There hasn't been enough research to say whether NSAIDs relieve pain in carpal tunnel syndrome. In one group of people, pain was no better in people taking NSAIDs than in people who were not taking the drugs.[25]O'Connor D, Marshall S, Massy-Westropp N. Non-surgical treatment (other than steroid injection) for carpal tunnel syndrome (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

We don't know whether paracetamol can help reduce pain in carpal tunnel syndrome because there has not been any research in this area.

What are the main effects of pain medications on what people can do and how they feel?

There hasn't been enough research to say whether NSAIDs alone improve hand and wrist movement in carpal tunnel syndrome.[25]O'Connor D, Marshall S, Massy-Westropp N. Non-surgical treatment (other than steroid injection) for carpal tunnel syndrome (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

Someone with carpal tunnel syndrome may find it easier to move their hand and wrist if they wear a splint as well as taking NSAIDs.[27]Gurcay E, Unlu E, Gurcay AG, et al. Evaluation of the effect of local corticosteroid injection and anti-inflammatory medication in carpal tunnel syndrome. Scottish Medical Journal. 2009; 54: 4-6.

What are the main side effects of pain medications?

Most pain medications have side effects. Someone is more likely to have side effects if they have other existing health problems, such as heart disease. It is important that people who are taking any other medications, including herbal medicines and vitamin supplements, check with their doctor or pharmacist that these will not react with their pain medicine.

Paracetamol is unlikely to cause side effects at the correct dose. Taking too much paracetamol can cause liver damage.

NSAIDs such as aspirin and ibuprofen can cause bleeding in the stomach when taken over a long period. They may also damage the kidneys. NSAIDs may raise the risk of heart attacks and stroke. NSAIDs may not be suitable for some people with certain conditions, such as asthma, a peptic ulcer, or angina, or in people who have had a heart attack or stroke.

How might pain medications affect my life?

Some people find that to control their pain they need to take pain medications on most days. Some people dislike taking medicines every day.

It's a good idea for people to check with their doctor whether their pain medicines interact with other medicines they might be taking, including herbal medicines, vitamin supplements, and certain foods. If the medicines do interact, people may need to change how they use these.

People who take some types of pain relief medication over a long time may also need to take medicines to protect their stomach lining.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT PHYSICAL MANAGEMENT

What is physical management?

Physical management of carpal tunnel syndrome usually means two things:

  • Hand exercises: these are exercises that people with carpal tunnel syndrome can do to try to relieve the trapped nerve in the wrist. These involve stretching the fingers and wrist into different positions to see if this can release the pressure on the nerve. The exercises are known as nerve and tendon gliding exercises.
  • Splinting: wrist splints keep the hand and wrist straight and reduce the pressure on the trapped nerve. It is important to wear the right kind of splint to make sure the wrist is kept in a neutral position.
Who can have physical management?

If someone's symptoms come and go and are not severe, their doctor may suggest they do hand exercises, wear a wrist splint, or both.

A physiotherapist or a hand therapist can offer advice about nerve and tendon exercises that may help with the symptoms.

What are the main effects of physical management on carpal tunnel syndrome?

Nerve and tendon exercises may help pain in some people, but there hasn't been enough research for us to be sure about how much they might help.[28]Page MJ, O'Connor D, Pitt V, et al. Exercise and mobilisation interventions for carpal tunnel syndrome (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

Splints prevent the wrist bending. Wearing a wrist splint at night can help reduce pain in the short term. In a small group of people with carpal tunnel syndrome, about 50 in 100 people said their symptoms had improved three months after starting to use a wrist splint.[29]Gerritsen AA, de Vet HC, Scholten RJ, et al. Splinting versus surgery in the treatment of carpal tunnel syndrome. Journal of the American Medical Association. 2002; 288: 1245-1251.

We don't know whether one splint design works better than another, or whether wrist splints work better if they are worn all the time, just during the day, or just at night. There hasn't been much research.[30]Page MJ, Massy-Westropp N, O'Connor D, et al. Splinting for carpal tunnel syndrome (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

What are the main effects of physical management on what people can do and how they feel?

If nerve and tendon exercises improve symptoms, people may find it easier to move their hands and wrists, and get on with their daily activities and hobbies. We're not sure how much these exercises help, because there hasn't been much research.[28]Page MJ, O'Connor D, Pitt V, et al. Exercise and mobilisation interventions for carpal tunnel syndrome (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

Wearing a wrist splint at night can make it easier for someone with carpal tunnel syndrome to move their hand or wrist.[30]Page MJ, Massy-Westropp N, O'Connor D, et al. Splinting for carpal tunnel syndrome (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

What are the main side effects of physical management?

It's possible that some nerve and tendon exercises could make the condition worse. It's important for people with carpal tunnel syndrome to discuss exercises with a physiotherapist or another health professional, to make sure they do the right exercises and that they do them properly.[28]Page MJ, O'Connor D, Pitt V, et al. Exercise and mobilisation interventions for carpal tunnel syndrome (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

When wearing a splint at night some people may have:

  • discomfort
  • swelling of the hands and wrist
  • trouble falling asleep, or
  • pins and needles for a short while when taking the splint off in the morning.[30]Page MJ, Massy-Westropp N, O'Connor D, et al. Splinting for carpal tunnel syndrome (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.[35]Shi Q, MacDermid JC. Is surgical intervention more effective than non-surgical treatment for carpal tunnel syndrome? A systematic review. Journal of Orthopaedic Surgery. 2011; 6: 17.
How might physical management affect my life?

People who choose physical management will need to set aside time to do nerve and tendon exercises, and be consistent with the exercise programme that their doctor or physiotherapist recommends.

People who choose physical management may need to go to hospital for a number of physiotherapy sessions. They will have to set aside time for these. Their doctor can advise how many sessions they are likely to need, and how often they would need to go to the hospital or clinic.

Wearing a splint may get in the way when doing daily activities.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT STEROID INJECTION

What are steroid injections?

Having a steroid injection (the full name of this type of medication is corticosteroid) means having an injection into the carpal tunnel. This is done by a doctor.

Corticosteroids are sometimes called steroids for short, but they are not the same as the anabolic steroids used by some bodybuilders and athletes. Corticosteroids are similar to the natural chemicals the body makes to reduce swelling.

Corticosteroids are also available as tablets. Doctors don't often use corticosteroid tablets to treat carpal tunnel syndrome, because injections work better, and because the tablets may be more likely than the injections to cause side effects.[19]Marshall S, Tardif G, Ashworth N. Local corticosteroid injection for carpal tunnel syndrome (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

What are the main effects of steroid injections on carpal tunnel syndrome?

A corticosteroid injection into the carpal tunnel can make the hand, arm, and wrist less painful and swollen. People may feel improvement for up to one month after the injection.[19]Marshall S, Tardif G, Ashworth N. Local corticosteroid injection for carpal tunnel syndrome (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

What are the main effects of steroid injections on what people can do and how they feel?

Someone with carpal tunnel syndrome may be able to move their hand and wrist better after a corticosteroid injection into the carpal tunnel.[19]Marshall S, Tardif G, Ashworth N. Local corticosteroid injection for carpal tunnel syndrome (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. This means they might find it easier to do certain activities such as sleeping, typing, writing, driving, or picking up objects. The injection can cause discomfort at first, but they should be able to use their hand again as they did before within a day or two.

What are the main side effects of steroid injections?

Corticosteroid injections can sometimes cause an infection in the wrist, and there is a chance that the nerve could be damaged by the injection.[7]McCartan B, Ashby E, Taylor EJ, et al. Carpal tunnel syndrome. British Journal of Hospital Medicine. 2012; 73: 199-202. Other side effects can include discomfort or swelling at the injection site, and pain for a couple of days after the injection.

Doctors will keep a close eye on the number of corticosteroids that people have. This is because having repeated injections can cause serious side effects, such as weakened bones and tendons. Doctors usually make sure that people don't have more than a few injections per year, and they may limit the number of injections people can have in one area of the body.

How might steroid injections affect my life?

People who have a corticosteroid injection will need to visit their GP surgery or a special clinic. They may find that in their area all the corticosteroid injections are done in one surgery or clinic, so they may have to travel further than they normally would to see their doctor.

To start with, doctors usually recommend one injection. They may suggest more injections if someone needs them. We don’t know if there's a certain number of corticosteroid injections that would relieve the symptoms of carpal tunnel syndrome the most. We do know that having two corticosteroid injections in the space of eight weeks doesn't work better than having just one injection.[19]Marshall S, Tardif G, Ashworth N. Local corticosteroid injection for carpal tunnel syndrome (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT SURGERY

What is surgery?

Surgery involves an operation on the wrist. The surgeon cuts free a band of tissue called the carpal ligament from the median nerve. The operation is usually done under local anaesthetic. The anaesthetic numbs the arm and hand but doesn't send the patient to sleep.

This operation can be done by either open or keyhole (also called endoscopic) surgery. In open surgery, the surgeon makes a cut in the wrist up to 5 centimetres (2 inches) long. With keyhole surgery, the surgeon makes a small cut in the wrist and puts in a tube called an endoscope. The surgeon then uses a tiny camera and instruments on the end of the tube.

A lot of research has compared open and keyhole surgery. The two types of surgery tend to be as good as each other in treating carpal tunnel syndrome.[41]Huisstede BM, Randsdorp MS, Coert JH, et al. Carpal tunnel syndrome. Part II: effectiveness of surgical treatments - a systematic review. Archives of Physical Medicine and Rehabilitation. 2010; 91: 1005-1024.[42]Scholten RJ, Mink van der Molen A, Uitdehaag BM, et al. Surgical treatment options for carpal tunnel syndrome (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. Most carpal tunnel surgery done in the UK is open surgery.

Who can have surgery?

Surgery is usually only recommended for people who have severe symptoms and have tried other treatments first.

Some people with carpal tunnel syndrome often decide to have surgery if their pain and numbness doesn’t go away after several months or years, or if it gets gradually worse.[17]Khu KJ, Bernstein M, Midha R. Patients' perceptions of carpal tunnel and ulnar nerve decompression surgery. Canadian Journal of Neurological Sciences. 2011; 38: 268-273.

What are the main effects of surgery on carpal tunnel syndrome?

The effects of surgery can last a lifetime. Many people who have surgery never have symptoms again.

Having surgery to release the pressure on the nerve will probably help reduce pain.[29]Gerritsen AA, de Vet HC, Scholten RJ, et al. Splinting versus surgery in the treatment of carpal tunnel syndrome. Journal of the American Medical Association. 2002; 288: 1245-1251.[35]Shi Q, MacDermid JC. Is surgical intervention more effective than non-surgical treatment for carpal tunnel syndrome? A systematic review. Journal of Orthopaedic Surgery. 2011; 6: 17.

In a group of people with carpal tunnel syndrome, about 80 in 100 people said their symptoms were better three months after surgery.[29]Gerritsen AA, de Vet HC, Scholten RJ, et al. Splinting versus surgery in the treatment of carpal tunnel syndrome. Journal of the American Medical Association. 2002; 288: 1245-1251.

People who have surgery may feel the benefits for at least 2 years after the operation.[47]Ly-Pen D, Andréu JL, Millán I, et al. Comparison of surgical decompression and local steroid injection in the treatment of carpal tunnel syndrome: 2-year clinical results from a randomized trial. Rheumatology. 2012; 51: 1447-1454. The benefits may last a lot longer.

What are the main effects of surgery on what people can do and how they feel?

Releasing the trapped nerve using carpal tunnel surgery usually makes it easier to move the hand and wrist, compared with how easy they were to move before the operation. This may mean that people who have had surgery can resume their daily activities and hobbies.[48]Jarvik JG, Comstock BA, Kliot M, et al. Surgery versus non-surgical therapy for carpal tunnel syndrome: a randomised parallel-group trial. Lancet. 2009; 374: 1074-1081. This can also help people to sleep better.

Most people who have surgery are happy with the result because their symptoms improve.[17]Khu KJ, Bernstein M, Midha R. Patients' perceptions of carpal tunnel and ulnar nerve decompression surgery. Canadian Journal of Neurological Sciences. 2011; 38: 268-273.


What are the main side effects of surgery?

The most common complications of carpal tunnel surgery are skin irritation, scarring, infection, and blood collecting under the skin at the site of the wound (a haematoma).[35]Shi Q, MacDermid JC. Is surgical intervention more effective than non-surgical treatment for carpal tunnel syndrome? A systematic review. Journal of Orthopaedic Surgery. 2011; 6: 17. The scar from surgery can be tender for two to three months after the operation.

Keyhole surgery may cause temporary nerve problems such as numbness and tingling. Open surgery may lead to wound infection or haematoma.

With both types of surgery, it's possible that the pain from the scar or the stiffness in the wrist may be worse than the symptoms of carpal tunnel syndrome. The wrist may also lose strength because the carpal ligament is cut. These side effects are rare.

How might surgery affect my life?

People who have surgery for carpal tunnel syndrome should be able to go home the same day. They will usually be able to dress and eat without any help a day or two after surgery.

Most people can start to drive again 10 to 14 days after the operation.

The time it takes for someone to be able to return to work will depend on the type of job they have. People who work a desk-based job in an office will usually be able to go back to work two to three weeks after the operation. It can take up to six weeks to return to work if the job is more physically demanding.

Sometimes doctors recommend wearing a wrist splint or doing exercises after surgery, although research shows that these probably won't speed up recovery.[41]Huisstede BM, Randsdorp MS, Coert JH, et al. Carpal tunnel syndrome. Part II: effectiveness of surgical treatments - a systematic review. Archives of Physical Medicine and Rehabilitation. 2010; 91: 1005-1024. A splint may cause more temporary pain and make the scar tender. People should be able to return to work sooner if they don't wear a splint after their operation.

People who have complications from the operation may need to delay their return to work. For some people the surgery will not work and they will need a second operation.[52]Verdugo RJ, Salinas RA, Castillo JL, et al. Surgical versus non-surgical treatment for carpal tunnel syndrome (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. This rarely happens.

We don't know how likely other problems from the surgery are later on.

Based on how you feel about the consequences of each option, this page explains the trade-offs that you should consider before deciding which option is best, or worst, for you.

If your symptoms get worse you can think about other treatments. Carpal tunnel syndrome can get worse over time if left untreated.
