Our Decision Support telephone service is here to assist and guide you through the Shared Decision Making process.

The Decision Support team is made of professional Health Coaches who are clinicians, commonly nurses, who have undertaken additional training to become a Health Coach.

The Health Coaches will assist you by:

  • Providing you with information
  • Helping you to understand your condition
  • Recognise what is important to you regarding the outcome of treatment
  • Identifying potential solutions
  • Encourage you in discussing options with your family
  • Transferring skills which will assist you in using the information and resources available to you
  • Support you in building confidence in discussing your choice with your doctor

  • A Health Coach does not know which treatment option is best for you.
  • The Health Coach does not have information about your medical history.

MORE INFORMATION ABOUT CATARACTS

How do the eyes work?

This is how your vision works, from when you open your eyes to when your brain tells you what you are looking at: 

  1. Light enters your eye through the clear layer at the front (the cornea).
  2. The light passes through the pupil, the dark gap in the centre of the coloured part (iris) of your eye.
  3. The light goes through your lens, which helps to focus light.
  4. The light is focused onto the back of your eye (your retina). The retina has nerve cells that pick up light.
  5. The nerve cells in your retina send a pattern of signals to your brain. These signals make up a picture.
  6. Your brain interprets the signals and tells you what you are seeing.

Your lens must be clear to focus the light sharply on the retina.

What happens if you have a cataract?

If you have a cataract, it means the lens in your eye is not clear. [1]The Royal College of Ophthalmologists. Understanding cataracts. November 2011. Available at http://www.rcophth.ac.uk/core/core_picker/download.asp?id=765 (accessed on 02 May 2012). This makes the picture on the back of your eye blurry. It's like looking through a dirty windscreen or frosted glass. You can get cataracts in just one eye or in both eyes. Most people with cataracts end up having them in both eyes.

As you get older, your lens can change in two ways:[2]National Eye Institute. Cataract: What you should know. September 2003. Available at http://www.nei.nih.gov/health/cataract/webcataract.pdf (accessed on 02 May 2012).

  • Your lens might get clumps in it. Your lens is made up mostly of water and protein. As you get older, the protein can clump up and cloud your lens. Then less light is focused on the back of your eye. Your vision might get blurry.
  • Your lens might change colour. When you are younger, your lens doesn't have any colour. As you get older, it slowly turns yellow-brown. You might find it harder to see some colours, such as blues and purples. This yellowing of the lens might blur your vision by making your eye more short sighted. You may be able to correct this blurriness with a new glasses prescription.
What are the symptoms of cataracts?

Cataracts can affect your eyesight in different ways. These changes may bother you a little or a lot.

The symptoms of cataracts include:[2]National Eye Institute. Cataract: What you should know. September 2003. Available at http://www.nei.nih.gov/health/cataract/webcataract.pdf (accessed on 02 May 2012).[1]The Royal College of Ophthalmologists. Understanding cataracts. November 2011. Available at http://www.rcophth.ac.uk/core/core_picker/download.asp?id=765 (accessed on 02 May 2012).

  • Things look cloudy or blurry
  • You keep needing new glasses
  • Colours seem faded. You may find it hard to tell between some colours, especially shades of blue
  • You get a glare from lights. Headlights, lamps, or sunlight may seem too bright
  • You may find it harder than usual to see at night
  • You may have double vision.
Why has this happened to me?

Most people get cataracts because of changes to the lens as they get older. You may hear doctors call these senile cataracts or age-related cataracts. You can get a cataract in just one eye, although most people get them in both eyes. Cataracts don't spread from one eye to the other. You don't catch them like an infection.

There are other causes of cataracts. For example:[2]National Eye Institute. Cataract: What you should know. September 2003. Available at http://www.nei.nih.gov/health/cataract/webcataract.pdf (accessed on 02 May 2012).

Eye injury. You can get cataracts if you hurt your eye. The cataracts might not show up until years later. These are sometimes called traumatic cataracts.

Radiation. Rarely, people get cataracts if they come into contact with certain types of radiation, such as the kind of radiation that comes from radiotherapy. Being near intense heat may also increase your chances of getting cataracts.

Having surgery for other eye problems. Some people get cataracts after surgery for a different eye problem called glaucoma. 

Having diabetes. People who have diabetes often get cataracts, and at a younger age than people who don't have diabetes.[6]Klein BE, Klein R, Lee KE, et al. Diabetes, cardiovascular disease, selected cardiovascular disease risk factors, and the 5-year incidence of age-related cataract and progression of lens opacities: The Beaver Dam Eye study. American Journal of Ophthalmology. 1998; 126: 782-790.

Taking steroids. Getting cataracts has been linked to taking drugs called steroids for a long time. This includes steroids used as creams, as drops to treat eye problems, and steroids that you breathe in to treat asthma.[7]Jobling AI, Augusteyn RC. What causes steroid cataracts? A review of steroid-induced posterior subcapsular cataracts. Clinical and Experimental Optometry. 2002; 85: 2.

Smoking. People who smoke heavily are more likely to get cataracts in middle age than other people.[Flaye DE, Sullivan KN, Cullinan TR, et al. Cataracts and cigarette smoking: the City Eye Study. Eye. 1989; 3: 379-384.] 

 

How do I know I have cataracts?

You may find out that you might have cataracts during a routine eye test.[8]NHS Executive. Action on cataracts: good practice guidance. February 2000. Available at http://www.dh.gov.uk/en/Publicationsandstatistics/Publications/PublicationsPolicyAndGuidance/DH_4005637 (accessed on 02 May 2012).

Your optometrist (a healthcare professional who can test your eyes) or ophthalmologist (a medical doctor who specialises in eye problems) can find out for sure by doing a more thorough eye exam. Here is what you can expect.[9]National Eye Institute. Cataract: what you should know. September 2003. Available at http://www.nei.nih.gov/health/cataract/webcataract.pdf (accessed on 02 May 2012).

  • An eye test (also called a visual acuity test). You are asked to read letters from different rows of a chart. This measures how well you see at certain distances.
  • An examination to look inside your eye. In this test, drops are put in your eye to make your pupil wider so that the doctor can see more of the inside of your eye. Your ophthalmologist will look inside your eye to see if you have a cataract.

If your cataract has been there for some time, the centre of your eye (your pupil) will be grey or white. By this stage your eyesight will be severely affected. Some people will need more tests or a more detailed examination.

Your doctor will ask you how much your cataract bothers you. The doctor will also try to find out how much your vision affects what you can do in your daily life. They will ask if you are able to do things like read a newspaper, see traffic signs or watch television.

They will also ask about your health and any other eye problems you have had or have. You might have other tests to find out more about the health of your eyes.

How many people have cataracts?

In the UK, about 33 in 100 people over 65 have cataracts in one or both eyes.[10]The Royal College of Ophthalmologists. Cataract surgery guidelines. 2011. Available at  http://www.rcophth.ac.uk/core/core_picker/download.asp?id=765 (accessed on 02 May 2012). About 300,000 people in the UK have surgery to take out their cataracts each year.[11]Hospital Episode Statistics. Main procedures and interventions: 3 character 2009-10. Available at http://www.hesonline.nhs.uk/ (accessed on 02 May 2012).

What will happen?

Cataracts usually get slowly worse over time, unless they are removed by an operation. Without treatment, the cataract may make the whole lens of the eye cloudy.

You may find ways to adapt in the early stages. As your eyesight gets worse it may feel like trying to see through a frosted window or a heavy net curtain or fog. The time may come when you can’t see any detail at all. You will still be able to tell light from darkness.

We can't say how fast this will happen because it varies enormously between people.

It does not become more difficult to remove a cataract if you decide to wait before having surgery.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT VISUAL AIDS AND ADAPTATIONS

What are visual aids and adaptations?

Visual aids are things you can use to help you see better or make the most of your vision, without actually treating the cataract. Visual aids include:

  • Prescription glasses
  • Magnifying lenses to help you see up close
  • Adjusted lighting
  • Sunglasses to prevent glare
  • Wide-brimmed hats to block sunlight.

Adaptations means making changes to make the most of the vision you have. You can try using large print books or adjusting computer screens to make text appear larger, or adjusting the colour on televisions and other screens to make the picture brighter and easier to see.

Your ophthalmologist, optician or GP can refer you to your local low vision service. The low vision service can give you advice about adapting to changes in your vision and information about visual aids that make everyday tasks easier. They can also tell you about support that is available for people with poor vision.

Visual aids can help you adjust to a loss of vision and make the most of the vision you have. They will not restore your vision or prevent any further loss of vision.

Who can choose visual aids and adaptations?

Anyone who has a cataract can choose to use visual aids and make adaptations. Some people, including people who are over 60, are entitled to free eye tests and help with the costs of some visual aids, such as glasses. Your optician or ophthalmologist will be able to tell you what you are entitled to. Other visual aids, such as magnifying glasses or audiobooks, you will have to pay for. Charities such as the RNIB can provide more information about visual aids and what they cost.

If you want to try visual aids and adaptations, before deciding whether or not to have a cataract operation, it is safe to do so. It does not become more difficult to remove a cataract should you decide to wait before having surgery.[12]Hodge W, Horsley T, Albiani D, et al. The consequences of waiting for cataract surgery: a systematic review. Canadian Medical Association Journal. 2007; 176: 1285-1290. [13]NHS Choices. Cataract surgery – when it is used. April 2012. Available at http://www.nhs.uk/Conditions/Cataract-surgery/Pages/When-it-should-be-done.aspx (Accessed 09 May 2012). 

What are the effects of visual aids on your eyesight?

Visual aids may help your eyesight, but we don't know for certain because there has been little research on this.

In the early stages of a cataract, you may be able to improve your vision by using visual aids. But the improvement may not last long.

Here's what we know about the vision of a group of women over 70 who were waiting to have a cataract operation for six months:

  • People's eyesight got worse over the six months they were on the waiting list.
  • About 63 in 100 people had vision good enough for them to drive legally (a score of 6/12 or less).[14]Harwood RH, Foss AJ, Osborn F, et al. Falls and health status in elderly women following first-eye cataract surgery: a randomized controlled trial. British Journal of Ophthalmology. 2005; 89: 53-59.
What are the effects of visual aids on what you can do?

Visual aids are intended to help you do more with the level of vision you have. We don't know how well they work, because there hasn't been much research looking at this.

We know that people waiting to have a cataracts operation tend to see their ability to do things get worse over time. One group of women aged over 70 waiting to have a cataracts operation filled in a questionnaire that measured what they could do in daily life. The questionnaire asked if they were able to do things like read a newspaper, see traffic signs, or watch television. On a scale of 1 to 100, people's scores got worse during the six months they waited, from 80 in 100 to 74 in 100.[14]Harwood RH, Foss AJ, Osborn F, et al. Falls and health status in elderly women following first-eye cataract surgery: a randomized controlled trial. British Journal of Ophthalmology. 2005; 89: 53-59.

Some people with a cataract are still legally able to drive. If you have cataracts in just one eye you will not need to tell the Driver and Vehicle Licensing Agency (DVLA) about your medical condition provided you have no other medical condition in the other eye. If you have cataracts in both eyes you will need to tell the DVLA about your medical condition.

About 63 in 100 people waiting to have a cataracts operation have vision good enough for them to drive legally (a score of 6/12 or less).[14]Harwood RH, Foss AJ, Osborn F, et al. Falls and health status in elderly women following first-eye cataract surgery: a randomized controlled trial. British Journal of Ophthalmology. 2005; 89: 53-59.

Cataracts can make it difficult to drive at night or in bright sunlight because of the glare. You can try visual aids such as eyeshades to help with this, if your eyesight (with glasses) is good enough for you to legally drive. If your eyesight is not good enough to drive, using visual aids won't help. People who have cataracts are more likely to have accidents or say they have problems driving than people who have had cataracts removed.

What are the effects of visual aids on your quality of life?

You may find that visual aids can help you continue to enjoy the things in life that are important to you, such as reading or getting out and about. This will depend on how bad your cataracts are, and what sort of things you enjoy doing.

We don't know whether visual aids have any effect on your quality of life, because there has been little research on this.

How much time will I have to spend in hospital or having treatment?

You won’t have to spend any time in hospital if you use visual aids. You should have regular eye tests - at least every two years - in case you need new glasses.

What are the unwanted side effects of visual aids?

Some people may find it difficult to adapt to using visual aids.

It is unlikely that most visual aids will cause unwanted side effects. There has not been much research looking at this question.

How will visual aids affect my life?

We don't know much about how visual aids can affect your life, because there's not much research looking at this.

Having a cataract that isn't removed may increase your chance of falling or breaking a bone. In a group of women aged over 70 who were waiting to have cataract surgery, about 25 in 100 people fell twice or more. Eight in 100 of these women broke a bone.[14]Harwood RH, Foss AJ, Osborn F, et al. Falls and health status in elderly women following first-eye cataract surgery: a randomized controlled trial. British Journal of Ophthalmology. 2005; 89: 53-59.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT CATARACT SURGERY

What is cataract surgery?

Cataract surgery is an operation to remove the cataract. The operation involves removing the cloudy lens and replacing it with an artificial lens.[1]The Royal College of Ophthalmologists. Understanding cataracts. November 2011. Available at http://www.rcophth.ac.uk/core/core_picker/download.asp?id=765 (accessed on 02 May 2012). The eye surgeon (ophthalmic surgeon) will usually be your ophthalmologist (a medical doctor who specialises in eye problems).

We've included some information about what happens before and during the operation. You can ask your doctors for more information about how to prepare for surgery and what you need to do afterwards.

Before The Operation

You usually have an appointment with the eye surgeon a week or two before your operation. This is to measure your eye, to prepare for the best artificial lens to replace your natural lens. 

On the day of the operation a nurse will help you get ready for surgery. You will be given anaesthetic eye drops or gel to numb your eye to stop you feeling any pain. You won’t be able to feel the eye that is being operated on, but you’ll be awake. It may be possible for you to have other types of anaesthetic if you are feeling very anxious.

During The Operation

You will probably see the light of the microscope your eye surgeon uses. You may also see flashes of light, or you may catch a glimpse of the surgical instruments.

Most people don't find this uncomfortable. Around 2 in 100 people find the light from the microscope uncomfortably bright. A nurse will explain how to signal if you feel uncomfortable.[19]Newman DK. Visual experience during phacoemulsification cataract surgery under topical anaesthesia. British Journal of Ophthalmology. 2000; 84: 13-15.

There are two ways to remove the lens which has the cataract. In both, your eye surgeon will make a cut in the clear layer (cornea) that covers the front of your eye and then remove your cloudy lens through the cut.

The usual way of doing the operation is called phacoemulsification (or 'phaco' for short). The surgeon makes a small cut, usually about 3 millimetres long, in the cornea. He or she puts a tool with a fine needle through the cut to reach the lens. The tool gives off high-energy ultrasound waves. These sound waves break down the lens into small soft pieces which are then sucked out through the centre of the needle. The surgeon will then put a lens made of a flexible plastic into the eye. This lens unfolds once it's in the right place inside the capsule in the eye. The cut on the eye often won’t need stitches and heals on its own.

The second way of doing the operation is called extracapsular extraction. The surgeon makes a longer cut across the eye. The cloudy lens is removed in one piece. A rigid plastic lens is placed inside the eye. The cut is closed with stitches. These stitches usually need to be taken out later, once the cut has healed. 

Extracapsular extraction isn't done very much any more. Around 3 in 100 people who have cataracts removed have it done by extracapsular extraction.[20]Hospital Episode Statistics. Main procedures and interventions: 3 character 2010-11. Available at http://www.hesonline.nhs.uk (accessed on 10 May 2012). It's only used if you have another problem that would make phacoemulsification too hard to do.[21]Asbell PA, Dualan I, Mindel J, et al. Age-related cataract. Lancet. 2005; 365: 599-609. 

After the operation

If you are in pain once your anaesthetic wears off you can have painkillers. You usually need an antibiotic eye drop to prevent infection and a steroid eye drop to reduce any swelling. You’ll have a dressing over your eye for a few hours. Most people go home from hospital the same day.

The artificial lenses come in different strengths, like the lenses in glasses. Your eye specialist chooses a strength of lens that matches your eye. The artificial lens that your surgeon puts in will become part of your eye and won't feel any different from your natural lens.

Different types of replacement lens are available:[22]NHS Choices. Treating age-related cataracts. April 2012. Available at http://www.nhs.uk/Conditions/Cataracts-age-related/Pages/Treatment.aspx (Accessed 14 May 2012).

  • Monofocal (fixed-strength), which are set for one level of vision, usually distance vision. You’ll need reading glasses for tasks where you have to focus on a short distance.
  • Multifocal lenses, which allow two or more different strengths, such as near and distance vision.
  • Accommodating lenses, which allow the eye to focus on both near and distant objects, in a similar way to the natural human lens.

You can discuss with your ophthalmologist which lens is best for you. Multifocal and accommodating lenses are not usually free on the NHS.


Who can choose cataract surgery?

Anyone with a cataract causing vision problems can choose cataract surgery, provided they are able to have an anaesthetic.

There is no specific level of poor vision that you have to reach before having surgery for a first cataract, and no need to wait for your cataract to get worse. Some people can read letters on an eye test chart, but can't do daily activities. You should be offered the choice of a cataract operation if your vision stops you coping with everyday tasks.

Because there is always a risk of problems with operations, doctors usually recommend that you wait until you have difficulty doing everyday tasks such as reading or driving.[1]The Royal College of Ophthalmologists. Understanding cataracts. November 2011. Available at http://www.rcophth.ac.uk/core/core_picker/download.asp?id=765 (accessed on 02 May 2012).

Your ophthalmologist may ask how much your cataract affects you. They may ask if you have problems:[24]Steinberg EP, Tielsch JM, Schein OD, et al. The VF-14. An index of functional impairment in patients with cataract. Archives of Ophthalmology. 1994; 112: 630-638.

  • Reading small print such as labels on medicine bottles, or a telephone book
  • Reading a newspaper or book
  • Seeing numbers on a telephone
  • Recognising people when they are close to you
  • Seeing steps, stairs, or kerbs
  • Reading traffic, street, or shop signs
  • Writing cheques or filling out forms
  • Playing games or doing quizzes
  • Cooking
  • Watching television
  • Daytime driving
  • Night-time driving.

If you have poor vision for another reason (for example, if you have another eye problem) the operation is less likely to help you.[25]Desai P, Minassian DC, Reidy A. National cataract surgery survey 1997-8: a report of the results of the clinical outcomes. British Journal of Ophthalmology. 1999; 83: 1336-1340. But you can still have a cataract removed.

What are the effects of cataract surgery on your eyesight?

After your cataract surgery, if you don't have other eye diseases, you should be able to:

  • See things in focus
  • Look into bright light with less glare
  • Tell the difference between colours.

Most people who have the operation can see much better afterwards.[25]Desai P, Minassian DC, Reidy A. National cataract surgery survey 1997-8: a report of the results of the clinical outcomes. British Journal of Ophthalmology. 1999; 83: 1336-1340. Here’s what we know from one study:

  • Around 85 in 100 people who had cataracts (and no other eye diseases) had vision good enough to drive (6/12 vision) shortly after the operation.
  • Three months after their operation, 92 in 100 people had vision that was good enough to drive. Around 82 in 100 people could see better than this (6/9 vision).
  • People who had cataracts along with other eye problems, such as macular degeneration (a lack of blood to the retina), diabetic retinopathy (when blood and fluid leaks into the eye), or glaucoma (pressure in the eye), probably won't see as well after their operation as people who have just cataracts.[25]Desai P, Minassian DC, Reidy A. National cataract surgery survey 1997-8: a report of the results of the clinical outcomes. British Journal of Ophthalmology. 1999; 83: 1336-1340.
  • About 65 in 100 people with other eye problems have vision good enough to drive shortly after a cataract operation. And after three months, 77 in 100 people with other eye diseases have vision good enough to drive. Around 61 people in 100 have 6/9 vision or better.[25]Desai P, Minassian DC, Reidy A. National cataract surgery survey 1997-8: a report of the results of the clinical outcomes. British Journal of Ophthalmology. 1999; 83: 1336-1340.
What are the effects of cataract surgery on what you can do?

Most people who have cataract surgery find it easier to do everyday tasks after their operation.[29]Desai P, Reidy A, Minassian DC, et al. Gains from cataract surgery: visual function and quality of life. British Journal of Ophthalmology. 1996; 80: 868-873.

People who have a cataracts operation have higher scores on a questionnaire that measures what they could do in daily life, compared to people who are still waiting for their operation. The questionnaire asks if people were able to do things like read a newspaper, see traffic signs, or watch television. In one group of women aged over 70 with cataracts, on a scale of 1 to 100, people improved from an average score of 79 in 100 before the operation to an average score of 90 in 100 by six months after their operation.[14]Harwood RH, Foss AJ, Osborn F, et al. Falls and health status in elderly women following first-eye cataract surgery: a randomized controlled trial. British Journal of Ophthalmology. 2005; 89: 53-59.

Most people (92 out of 100) have vision good enough to drive after having their cataract operation.[25]Desai P, Minassian DC, Reidy A. National cataract surgery survey 1997-8: a report of the results of the clinical outcomes. British Journal of Ophthalmology. 1999; 83: 1336-1340. People are less likely to have difficulty driving, or to have accidents, after surgery.[32]Suzwari A, Desapriya E, Scime G et al. Effectiveness of cataract surgery in reducing driving-related difficulties: a systematic review and meta-analysis. Injury Prevention. 2008; 14: 324-328.

What are the effects of cataract surgery on your quality of life?

People who have cataract surgery say they are more likely to be able to look after themselves, and to get around and have a social life with family and friends, after surgery.[33]Lamoureux EL, Fenwick E, Pesudovs K et al. The impact of cataract surgery on quality of life. Current Opinion in Ophthalmology. 2011, 22: 19-27.

How much time will I have to spend in hospital or having treatment?

Most people are able to go home from hospital the same day that they have the operation.[Hospital Episode Statistics. Main procedures and interventions: 3 character 2009-10. Available at http://www.hesonline.nhs.uk (accessed on 23 May 2011).] 

People only stay in hospital if they have problems after their operation, or other eye diseases (such as glaucoma).

Most people have no problems after cataract surgery and are able to get up and around as normal the next day.[34]The Royal College of Ophthalmologists. November 2011. Understanding cataracts. Available at http://www.rcophth.ac.uk/core/core_picker/download.asp?id=765 (accessed on 02 May 2012).

It usually takes about two to six weeks to make a full recovery. You will need to use eye drops – probably an antibiotic eye drop to avoid infection and a steroid eye drop to reduce swelling.

You will usually have a check-up with your ophthalmologist or eye care team after surgery. This will happen between one and six weeks after your operation. Sometimes you may need to have a check-up on the day after surgery.

You may need new reading glasses after the operation. You’ll need an appointment for an eye test to get the right strength glasses.[34]The Royal College of Ophthalmologists. November 2011. Understanding cataracts. Available at http://www.rcophth.ac.uk/core/core_picker/download.asp?id=765 (accessed on 02 May 2012).

What are the unwanted side effects of cataract surgery?

About 10 in 100 people have complications (problems) during cataract surgery.[36]Snellingen T, Evans JR, Ravilla T, et al. Surgical interventions for age related cataract (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. Many problems can be treated at the time to prevent further damage to your vision. The majority will not cause long-term problems.

About 7 in 100 people have a problem if their lens was removed in pieces (by phacoemulsification). About 21 in 100 people who have their lens removed in one piece (by extracapsular extraction) had a problem.[37]The Royal College of Ophthalmologists. Cataract surgery guidelines. 2010. Available at http://www.rcophth.ac.uk/core/core_picker/download.asp?id=544&filetitle=Cataract+Surgery+Guidelines+2010 (accessed on 09May 2012).

Problems that can make your vision much worse than before surgery happen in 1 in 1,000 operations.[25]Desai P, Minassian DC, Reidy A. National cataract surgery survey 1997-8: a report of the results of the clinical outcomes. British Journal of Ophthalmology. 1999; 83: 1336-1340.

Problems during the operation

Possible problems during the operation include:

Bleeding: Heavy bleeding inside the eye (haemorrhage) happens to less than 1 in 1,000 people.[25]Desai P, Minassian DC, Reidy A. National cataract surgery survey 1997-8: a report of the results of the clinical outcomes. British Journal of Ophthalmology. 1999; 83: 1336-1340. Heavy bleeding is serious and can damage your sight. You are more likely to have a little bleeding which does not cause long-term problems.

Damaged capsule: The capsule that supports the lens inside your eye can tear or break during the operation. This happens to about 4 in 100 people. If it happens, your sight may be worse than before the operation.[25]Desai P, Minassian DC, Reidy A. National cataract surgery survey 1997-8: a report of the results of the clinical outcomes. British Journal of Ophthalmology. 1999; 83: 1336-1340.

Piece of lens left behind: A bit of the lens with the cataract can break off and become lost in the eye. This happens to less than 1 in 100 people. You may need another operation to remove the lost piece of lens.[25]Desai P, Minassian DC, Reidy A. National cataract surgery survey 1997-8: a report of the results of the clinical outcomes. British Journal of Ophthalmology. 1999; 83: 1336-1340.

Problems after the operation

About 25 in 100 people have a problem within a few days of their operation.[25]Desai P, Minassian DC, Reidy A. National cataract surgery survey 1997-8: a report of the results of the clinical outcomes. British Journal of Ophthalmology. 1999; 83: 1336-1340.[37]The Royal College of Ophthalmologists. Cataract surgery guidelines. 2010. Available at http://www.rcophth.ac.uk/core/core_picker/download.asp?id=544&filetitle=Cataract+Surgery+Guidelines+2010 (accessed on 09May 2012).These include:

Swelling of the cornea: The cornea is the clear, thin film that covers the front of your eye. It can swell after the operation. This happens to about 10 in 100 people. This can be painful. In some cases, the cornea may turn cloudy. Usually, the swelling is temporary and will go away by itself. If it doesn’t improve, you might need another operation to transplant a cornea so you can see better again. This happens to less than 1 in 100 people who have cataract surgery.[44]Bourne RR, Minassian DC, Dart JK, et al. Effect of cataract surgery on the corneal endothelium: modern phacoemulsification compared with extracapsular cataract surgery. Ophthalmology. 2004; 111: 679-685.

Increased pressure in the eye: This is uncomfortable and happens to about 8 in 100 people. Your doctor may give you eye drops to reduce the swelling.[25]Desai P, Minassian DC, Reidy A. National cataract surgery survey 1997-8: a report of the results of the clinical outcomes. British Journal of Ophthalmology. 1999; 83: 1336-1340.

Inflammation of the front part of the eye: This is called uveitis. It happens to about 6 in 100 people.[46]Powe NR, Schein OD, Gieser SC, et al. Synthesis of the literature on visual acuity and complications following cataract extraction with intraocular lens implantation. Archives of Ophthalmology. 1994; 112: 239-252.

An infection inside the eye: This is called endophthalmitis and can cause sight loss in the infected eye. The infection should be treated with antibiotics straight away. About 2 in 1,000 people will get an infection after surgery.[47]Taban M, Behrens A, Newcomb RL, et al. Acute endophthalmitis following cataract surgery: a systematic review of the literature. Archives of Ophthalmology. 2005; 123: 613-620. About 1 in 1,000 people lose some or all of the sight in the affected eye.[46]Powe NR, Schein OD, Gieser SC, et al. Synthesis of the literature on visual acuity and complications following cataract extraction with intraocular lens implantation. Archives of Ophthalmology. 1994; 112: 239-252.

Bruising: Your eye or eyelid may be bruised after the operation.[37]The Royal College of Ophthalmologists. Cataract surgery guidelines. 2010. Available at http://www.rcophth.ac.uk/core/core_picker/download.asp?id=544&filetitle=Cataract+Surgery+Guidelines+2010 (accessed on 09May 2012). This will go away in time.

Swelling of the retina: Fluid may build up in the retina between two weeks and 12 weeks after the operation. If this happens, your sight may become cloudy. Doctors call this cystoid macular oedema. It usually goes away by itself, but can cause some worsening of vision. Between 2 in 100 and 3 in 100 people get this after a phacoemulsification operation.[46]Powe NR, Schein OD, Gieser SC, et al. Synthesis of the literature on visual acuity and complications following cataract extraction with intraocular lens implantation. Archives of Ophthalmology. 1994; 112: 239-252.

Injury to the iris: Less than 1 in 100 people have an injury to their iris (the coloured part of the eye) if they have their cataracts removed by phacoemulsification. It's more likely if you have manual extracapsular extraction.[46]Powe NR, Schein OD, Gieser SC, et al. Synthesis of the literature on visual acuity and complications following cataract extraction with intraocular lens implantation. Archives of Ophthalmology. 1994; 112: 239-252.

Problems that may happen later

Some problems can happen months or even years after your operation. These include:

Cloudy lens capsule: This is called posterior capsular opacification (PCO), or posterior capsular thickening. It happens when the thin capsule that is left in place to support the artificial lens becomes cloudy. As light from the lens has to pass through the capsule, it needs to be clear for you to see sharply. This problem can be treated with an operation using a laser beam. Around 25 in 100 people get PCO within five years of having a cataract operation.[52]Schaumberg DA, Dana MR, Christen WG et al. A systematic overview of the incidence of posterior capsule opacification. Ophthalmology. 1998; 105 :1213-1221.

Detached retina: This is where the light-sensitive area at the back of your eye (the retina) comes away from the back of your eyeball or is torn. It happens to around 7 in 1,000 people after a cataract operation.[46]Powe NR, Schein OD, Gieser SC, et al. Synthesis of the literature on visual acuity and complications following cataract extraction with intraocular lens implantation. Archives of Ophthalmology. 1994; 112: 239-252. A detached retina is a medical emergency and needs immediate treatment. You'll need another operation straight away to put the retina back in place with a laser.

How will cataract surgery affect my life?

You can return quickly to many everyday activities. Your vision may be blurry at first. The healing eye needs time to adjust so that it can focus properly with the other eye.[2]National Eye Institute. Cataract: What you should know. September 2003. Available at http://www.nei.nih.gov/health/cataract/webcataract.pdf (accessed on 02 May 2012).

You may have to avoid some things for a week to ten days:[34]The Royal College of Ophthalmologists. November 2011. Understanding cataracts. Available at http://www.rcophth.ac.uk/core/core_picker/download.asp?id=765 (accessed on 02 May 2012).

  • Rubbing or pressing your eye. You may have to wear an eye patch while you are asleep to help protect your eye.
  • Swimming, until you have seen a specialist to check your eye has healed. You will need to avoid contact with dirty water while your eye is healing. You should also take care to avoid getting water in your eye when washing your hair.
  • Strenuous exercise, contact sports or heavy lifting. You should also avoid bending from the waist down to pick up objects from the floor. You can walk, climb stairs and perform normal tasks like light household chores and shopping.
  • Wearing eye make-up until your eye has healed.

If you are in a windy or dusty place, you should take care that nothing blows into your eye. 

You may notice that colours are very bright. This is because the intraocular lens is clear, unlike your natural lens that may have had a brown or yellow tint because of the cataract. You may need new glasses or contact lenses once your eye has healed.[2]National Eye Institute. Cataract: What you should know. September 2003. Available at http://www.nei.nih.gov/health/cataract/webcataract.pdf (accessed on 02 May 2012).
