MORE INFORMATION ABOUT CHRONIC OBSTRUCTIVE PULMONARY DISEASE

What do the lungs do?

To understand what COPD is and how it can be treated, it may help to know more about how your lungs work.

Your lungs are in the centre of your chest, behind your ribs. They fill up with air when you breathe in and they empty when you breathe out. This is what happens when you take a breath:

  • Air travels from your mouth down your windpipe and into your lungs.
  • The air passes into your lungs through a network of tubes (your airways).
  • These tubes have fine hairs inside them, called cilia. These help to sweep tiny specks of dust, or dirt and germs, out of your lungs.
  • Your lungs make small amounts of a thick fluid, called mucus (or phlegm). Mucus keeps your airways moist and also helps get rid of dirt and germs.
  • The air you breathe in ends up in lots of tiny air sacs (called alveoli) inside your lungs.
  • Each tiny air sac is covered with blood vessels. Oxygen that you breathe in goes through the wall of the tiny air sacs and into your blood vessels.
  • Your blood takes the oxygen around your body. Oxygen is released into your body's tissues to be used as food.
  • After your body uses the oxygen, it creates waste gases – mostly carbon dioxide. The waste gases go from the tissues back into your blood.
  • Your blood returns to your lungs with these waste gases. When you breathe out you get rid of these waste gases.
What is chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD)?

COPD is the name for a group of lung diseases that cause problems with breathing. These lung diseases include chronic bronchitis, emphysema, and chronic obstructive lung disease.

If you have COPD, your lungs are probably damaged. Cigarette smoke is usually the cause. Some people get COPD from breathing in harmful chemicals at work or from air pollution. Some people get COPD because they have a genetic problem that is passed down from their parents. This is called alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency.

Cigarette smoke and other harmful chemicals can irritate the airways in your lungs, making them produce more mucus (phlegm). This stops the fine hairs (cilia) in your airways working properly. This means mucus stays trapped in your lungs instead of being forced out when you cough.

Too much mucus can make the walls of your airways swell and get thicker. When this happens, your airways get narrower. This can make it harder for you to breathe.

Cigarette smoke and harmful chemicals can also damage the walls of the tiny air sacs (alveoli) in your lungs. This means less of the oxygen you breathe in can get into your blood. Not having enough oxygen in your blood will make you get out of breath and tired quickly.

What are the symptoms of COPD?

The main symptoms of COPD are coughing, getting out of breath easily, and feeling very tired.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease: management of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease in adults in primary and secondary care (partial update). Clinical guideline 101. June 2010. Available at: http://www.nice.org.uk/cg101 (accessed on 22 October 2012).

People who have COPD often get a deep chesty cough that brings up lots of mucus (phlegm). This can have a thin or thick texture, and is either clear or a yellow-green colour. The cough is usually worse in the morning or after doing exercise or other physical activities. It may get worse after you smoke a cigarette. If you have COPD you will have a cough that won't go away.

Your symptoms may get worse when the weather is cold.

If you have COPD you may also get:[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease: management of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease in adults in primary and secondary care (partial update). Clinical guideline 101. June 2010. Available at: http://www.nice.org.uk/cg101 (accessed on 22 October 2012).

  • Chest infections, especially when the weather is cold
  • Wheezing (making a whistling sound when you breathe)
  • Swollen ankles, particularly if your heart isn't very strong
  • Weight loss, because your body has to use more energy to get air into and out of your lungs
  • Bones that break easily (this can be caused by not doing enough exercise so your bones get weaker)
  • Problems sleeping. 
What are flare-ups of COPD?

There may be times when your COPD symptoms suddenly get worse. This is called a flare-up. Doctors sometimes call this an exacerbation.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease: management of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease in adults in primary and secondary care (partial update). Clinical guideline 101. June 2010. Available at: http://www.nice.org.uk/cg101 (accessed on 22 October 2012).

If you have a flare-up of your COPD, you may need different treatments until the flare-up goes away. You will usually need to take oral steroids for a short amount of time. If your flare-up is caused by bacteria you may also need to take antibiotics to treat the infection. Doctors call these treatments rescue medications. During a flare-up, you might:

  • Find it harder than usual to breathe.
  • Cough more and bring up more mucus (phlegm). Your mucus will probably be thicker than usual and have more of a yellow-green colour.

Lots of people with COPD have flare-ups after they've caught a cold or a chest infection. Flare-ups of COPD aren't always caused by an infection. Some people get flare-ups without having an infection.

Why has this happened to me?

Smoking 

People who smoke, or used to smoke, are more likely to have COPD.[4]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Stable angina. July 2011. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG126 (accessed on 06 December 2012). The chemicals in cigarette smoke harm the lungs. If you breathe in cigarette smoke from another person (second-hand smoke), this can also increase your chance of getting COPD.

Not all smokers get COPD. A lot depends on your body and whether there is a history of COPD in your family. [5]NHS Choices. Chronic obstructive pulmonary disorder - causes. November 2010. Available at: http://www.nhs.uk/Conditions/Chronic-obstructive-pulmonary-disease/Pages/Causes.aspx (accessed on 22 October 2012).

Air pollution

You have more chance of getting COPD if you live or work in areas with lots of air pollution, such as where there's heavy industry.[6]Honig HG, Ingram RH. Chronic bronchitis, emphysema and airways obstruction. In: Braunwald E, Fauci AS, Kasper DL, et al. Harrison's principles of internal medicine. 15th edition. McGraw-Hill, New York, NY; 2001. These areas will have more harmful particles and gases in the air. 

As well as outdoor air pollution, burning certain fuels inside your home may increase your chance of getting COPD. A study found that people breathing in wood smoke while doing domestic chores inside the house increased their chance of getting COPD compared to other fuels.[7]Kurmi OP, Semple S, Simkhada P, et al. COPD and chronic bronchitis risk of indoor air pollution from solid fuel: a systematic review and meta-analysis. Thorax. 2010; 65: 221-228.

Infections 

You have more chance of getting COPD if you get lots of chest infections, especially if you’re a smoker. If you get an infection caused by a bacteria or virus, your lungs may produce too much mucus (phlegm). This will make it harder for you to breathe.[6]Honig HG, Ingram RH. Chronic bronchitis, emphysema and airways obstruction. In: Braunwald E, Fauci AS, Kasper DL, et al. Harrison's principles of internal medicine. 15th edition. McGraw-Hill, New York, NY; 2001.

Your job

You have more chance of getting COPD if you work near harmful dusts, chemicals, or gases.[6]Honig HG, Ingram RH. Chronic bronchitis, emphysema and airways obstruction. In: Braunwald E, Fauci AS, Kasper DL, et al. Harrison's principles of internal medicine. 15th edition. McGraw-Hill, New York, NY; 2001. Like cigarette smoke, these things can irritate your lungs and make it harder for you to breathe.

Your genetic makeup

Some doctors think that the genes people get from their parents increases their chance of getting COPD. We don't know for certain what these genes are. If you’re a smoker (or if you used to smoke) and have a relative with COPD, you are more likely to get COPD than another smoker who doesn't have a relative with COPD.[5]NHS Choices. Chronic obstructive pulmonary disorder - causes. November 2010. Available at: http://www.nhs.uk/Conditions/Chronic-obstructive-pulmonary-disease/Pages/Causes.aspx (accessed on 22 October 2012).

A rare type of COPD is linked to your genes. It’s called alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency and it’s passed down from your parents. Between 1 in 2,000 and 1 in 7,000 people from Europe have alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency. It’s not as common in people from Asia or Africa.[6]Honig HG, Ingram RH. Chronic bronchitis, emphysema and airways obstruction. In: Braunwald E, Fauci AS, Kasper DL, et al. Harrison's principles of internal medicine. 15th edition. McGraw-Hill, New York, NY; 2001. Usually people with COPD don’t get it from their parents.[5]NHS Choices. Chronic obstructive pulmonary disorder - causes. November 2010. Available at: http://www.nhs.uk/Conditions/Chronic-obstructive-pulmonary-disease/Pages/Causes.aspx (accessed on 22 October 2012).

How do I find out I have COPD?

Many people find out they have COPD after seeing their GP about a cough that won't go away. You will need tests to confirm that your cough is caused by COPD.

The main test that doctors use to find out if you have COPD is called spirometry.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease: management of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease in adults in primary and secondary care (partial update). Clinical guideline 101. June 2010. Available at: http://www.nice.org.uk/cg101 (accessed on 22 October 2012). Spirometry is done using a spirometer. During the test, you will be asked to take the biggest breath you can and then blow out as hard as you can into the spirometer. After you’ve done the test, you will be asked to breathe in a drug called a bronchodilator. You will then be asked to do the spirometry test again to see if your lungs work any better. This will help your doctor rule out other lung disorders such as asthma.

The spirometer takes two measurements. One is the most amount of air you can breathe out in one second (called the forced expiratory volume in one second, or FEV1). The other is the total amount of air you breathe out (called the forced vital capacity, or FVC). These measurements can tell doctors how well your lungs are working. Doctors call this your lung function.

The FEV1 results are given as a percentage of how much lung function you are expected to have compared with normal measurements.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease: management of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease in adults in primary and secondary care (partial update). Clinical guideline 101. June 2010. Available at: http://www.nice.org.uk/cg101 (accessed on 22 October 2012). Knowing your FEV1 results can be useful when choosing which treatments are best for you. You should have your FEV1 tested every 15 months.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease: management of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease in adults in primary and secondary care (partial update). Clinical guideline 101. June 2010. Available at: http://www.nice.org.uk/cg101 (accessed on 22 October 2012).

  • People with COPD who have a FEV1 of 80 percent or more have mild COPD (stage 1 COPD).
  • People with COPD who have a FEV1 between 50 percent and 79 percent have moderate COPD (stage 2 COPD).
  • People with COPD who have a FEV1 between 30 percent and 49 percent have severe COPD (stage 3 COPD).
  • People with COPD who have a FEV1 less than 30 percent have very severe COPD (stage 4 COPD). 

It's important that you have a spirometry test. Some doctors have a spirometer in their surgery, but if not you may have to go to hospital for the test.

You may need to have other tests to rule out other illnesses:

  • Chest x-ray or CT (computer tomography) scan
  • Blood test for anaemia
  • Walking test to measure how fast you get out of breath
  • Electrocardiogram (ECG) or echocardiogram to see if your heart is working properly
  • Tests to find out how much oxygen or carbon dioxide you have in your blood
  • Mucus (phlegm) test to see if you have a chest infection.

You need to have had spirometry, a chest x-ray, and a blood test for anaemia to be sure that you have COPD.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease: management of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease in adults in primary and secondary care (partial update). Clinical guideline 101. June 2010. Available at: http://www.nice.org.uk/cg101 (accessed on 22 October 2012).

COPD is one of the most common lung diseases in the UK.

Around 835,000 people in the UK have been diagnosed with COPD. There are about 2 million people with COPD who do not know they have it.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Available at: http://www.nice.org.uk/cg101 (accessed on 22 October 2012).

COPD usually affects people over the age of 35 years. Most people don’t find out they have COPD until they are in their 50s.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease: management of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease in adults in primary and secondary care (partial update). Clinical guideline 101. June 2010. Available at: http://www.nice.org.uk/cg101 (accessed on 22 October 2012).

COPD affects more men than women, although in recent years the number of women getting COPD has increased.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease: management of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease in adults in primary and secondary care (partial update). Clinical guideline 101. June 2010. It's common to have COPD and other illnesses or health problems that also need treatment.

What will happen to me if I have COPD?

Once your lungs become damaged this cannot be reversed. You can have treatments to help your symptoms, but they don't repair your lungs. Stopping smoking or keeping away from harmful chemicals that can irritate your lungs may slow the rate at which your symptoms get worse.[20]NHS Clinical Knowledge Summaries. Chronic obstructive pulmonary disorder - prevalence and incidence. 2010. Available at: http://www.cks.nhs.uk/chronic_obstructive_pulmonary_disease/background_information/prevalence_and_incidence (accessed on 22 October 2012).

For most people, COPD slowly gets worse over time. It's difficult to predict what will happen to you because everyone is different. Some people's COPD stays the same for years. For others it gets worse more quickly. The right treatment can help to reduce your symptoms, help your lungs work better for longer, and reduce the number of flare-ups you get.

If your COPD is severe, you will find it hard to breathe and it will take a lot of effort to get air in and out of your lungs. If you can't get enough oxygen into your blood, you will get tired easily and you may be unable to walk very far. Some people with COPD need to breathe extra oxygen. If you're finding it very hard to breathe during a flare-up, you may need a ventilator to help you breathe.

COPD causes between 25,000 and 30,000 deaths a year in the UK.[5]NHS Choices. Chronic obstructive pulmonary disorder - causes. November 2010. Available at: http://www.nhs.uk/Conditions/Chronic-obstructive-pulmonary-disease/Pages/Causes.aspx (accessed on 22 October 2012). Around 90 in 100 people in the UK who die of COPD are over 65 years of age.[20]NHS Clinical Knowledge Summaries. Chronic obstructive pulmonary disorder - prevalence and incidence. 2010. Available at: http://www.cks.nhs.uk/chronic_obstructive_pulmonary_disease/background_information/prevalence_and_incidence (accessed on 22 October 2012).

In the UK, about 1 in 8 people who need to go to hospital for an emergency do so because of COPD.[20]NHS Clinical Knowledge Summaries. Chronic obstructive pulmonary disorder - prevalence and incidence. 2010. Available at: http://www.cks.nhs.uk/chronic_obstructive_pulmonary_disease/background_information/prevalence_and_incidence (accessed on 22 October 2012). Around 3 or 4 people in 100 who have to go to hospital because of a flare-up of COPD die while in hospital. Between 11 and 24 in 100 people who need treatment in intensive care because of a flare-up of COPD die while in hospital.[24]NHS Clinical Knowledge Summaries. Chronic obstructive pulmonary disorder - prognosis. Available at: http://www.cks.nhs.uk/chronic_obstructive_pulmonary_disease/background_information/prognosis (accessed on 22 October 2012).

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT STOPPING SMOKING

What can help me to stop smoking?

Most people who smoke find it easier to stop smoking if they get professional help or advice. This can be from a doctor, pharmacist, nurse, or trained counsellor. You can have treatment to help you stop smoking at the same time as having professional help. You can also have treatment to help you stop smoking while following a self-management programme, having pulmonary rehabilitation, or taking COPD drugs.

The main treatments to help people stop smoking are:

  • Nicotine replacement therapy (or NRT). These treatments work by giving your body a very small amount of nicotine to stop you getting cravings or withdrawal symptoms when you’re not smoking. They can come as arm patches, chewing gum, inhalers, tablets that you put under your tongue, lozenges, and nasal sprays. You can buy them from a pharmacy. Your doctor can also prescribe them for you.
  • Drugs to help you stop smoking. Your doctor can prescribe you two tablet drugs that may help you stop smoking. These are called bupropion and varenicline. Bupropion works by increasing the amounts of two chemicals (called neurotransmitters) that carry messages between your brain cells. This seems to make it easier for people to stop smoking, although we’re still not sure exactly how. Varenicline works by blocking the pleasant effects of nicotine in your body. This reduces your craving for cigarettes and can help make it easier for you to stop smoking. You will need to get counselling and advice from a doctor, nurse, pharmacist, or a trained counsellor when you use varenicline.

Other treatments are hypnosis, electronic cigarettes, and self-help guides. These are aids and devices that may help you get over your cigarette cravings. 

To learn about treatments that can help you stop smoking, see the Patient Decision Aid on: 
Smoking Cessation.

Who can have treatment to help them stop smoking?

Anyone who smokes can choose to stop smoking. Most smokers are able to have treatments to help them stop.

Most smokers can use nicotine replacement therapy. If you have heart problems you should talk to your doctor before trying nicotine replacement therapy. If you're pregnant or breastfeeding, having nicotine replacement therapy is probably safer than continuing to smoke.

You can’t take bupropion or varenicline if you’re pregnant or breastfeeding.[25]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Smoking cessation services in primary care, pharmacies, local authorities and workplaces, particularly for manual working groups, pregnant women and hard to reach communities. Public health guidance 10. February 2008. Available at: http://www.nice.org.uk/PH010 (accessed on 22 October 2012).

What are the main effects of stopping smoking on COPD?

The main effects of stopping smoking on COPD are that you are likely to live longer, to have fewer symptoms like coughing and breathlessness, and your lungs are likely to work better. Your lung damage is likely to slow down. 

It doesn't matter which type of treatment you take to stop smoking, so long as you find one that works for you. The treatments that seem most likely to help people with COPD to stop smoking are professional help and advice, drug treatments, and nicotine replacement therapy.

Stopping smoking if you have COPD means:

  • Your lungs are likely to work better and continue working well for longer. This means your breathing will not get worse as quickly as it would if you continued to smoke.[26]Anthonisen NR, Connett JE, Kiley JP, et al. Effects of smoking intervention and the use of an inhaled anticholinergic bronchodilator on the rate of decline of FEV1: the lung health study. Journal of the American Medical Association. 1994; 272: 1497-1505. 
  • You are likely to have fewer symptoms five years after stopping, compared to if you continued to smoke. You are less likely to feel out of breath, to wheeze, cough, or have cough with phlegm, or have chest infections.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease: management of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease in adults in primary and secondary care (partial update). Clinical guideline 101. June 2010. Available at: http://www.nice.org.uk/cg101 (accessed on 22 October 2012).
  • You are likely to live for longer.[28]Strassmann R, Bausch B, Spaar A, et al. Smoking cessation interventions in COPD: a network meta-analysis of randomised trials. European Respiratory Journal. 2009; 34: 634-640.

One group of people with COPD who stopped smoking after using nicotine replacement therapy or counselling were more likely to be alive 15 years later, compared to people with COPD who did not have treatment to stop smoking.[26]Anthonisen NR, Connett JE, Kiley JP, et al. Effects of smoking intervention and the use of an inhaled anticholinergic bronchodilator on the rate of decline of FEV1: the lung health study. Journal of the American Medical Association. 1994; 272: 1497-1505. 

In one group of people with COPD, about 80 in 100 people who had a long-lasting cough no longer had a cough one year after stopping smoking.[30]Kanner RE, Connett JE, Williams DE, et al. Effects of randomized assignment to a smoking cessation intervention and changes in smoking habits on respiratory symptoms in smokers with early chronic obstructive pulmonary disease. American Journal of Medicine. 1999; 106: 410-416.

What are the main effects of stopping smoking on what you can do and how you feel?

Your lungs will work better and you will have fewer COPD symptoms if you stop smoking. This may help you stay active for longer.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease: management of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease in adults in primary and secondary care (partial update). Clinical guideline 101. June 2010. People who have treatment to help them stop smoking are less likely to feel anxious or sad than people who don’t take treatment to help them stop smoking.[33]Tashkin D, Kanner R, Bailey W, et al. Smoking cessation in patients with chronic obstructive pulmonary disease: a double blind, placebo-controlled, randomised trial. Lancet. 2001; 357: 1571-1575.

If you stop smoking you are less likely to need to go into hospital.[34]Godtfredsen NS, Vestbo J, Osler M, Prescott E. Risk of hospital admission for COPD following smoking cessation and reduction: a Danish population studyThorax 2002;57:967–972.

In one group of people with COPD, those who had treatment to help them stop smoking needed fewer visits to the doctor over five years than those who didn’t have treatment.[30]Kanner RE, Connett JE, Williams DE, et al. Will stopping smoking have any unwanted effects?

Sometimes when you try to stop smoking your cough gets worse for a few weeks. This is a sign that your lungs are healing. Your cough should get better over time. You should speak to your doctor if your cough doesn't go away after stopping smoking.

Some people put on weight when they stop smoking because they eat more. In one group of people with COPD, those who gave up smoking put on an average of about 18 pounds (around 8 kilograms) in the next five years.[37]O'Hara P, Connett JE, Lee WW, et al. Early and late weight gain following smoking cessation in the lung health study. American Journal of Epidemiology. 1998; 148: 821-830. Eating healthily can help you control your weight. Your GP or nurse can also give you support and information on ways to help you lose weight, if you wish to do so.

Treatments that can help you stop smoking can cause unwanted effects. Most of these effects are temporary. Not all effects need treatment.

Bupropion

In one group of people taking a 12-week course of bupropion, about 44 in 100 people had unwanted effects.[33]Tashkin D, Kanner R, Bailey W, et al. Smoking cessation in patients with chronic obstructive pulmonary disease: a double blind, placebo-controlled, randomised trial. Lancet. 2001; 357: 1571-1575. The most common unwanted effects with bupropion were difficulty sleeping (insomnia) and dry mouth.

  • About 24 in 100 people who took bupropion had insomnia. 
  • About six in 100 people who took bupropion had dry mouth. 

Varenicline

In one group of people taking a 12-week course of varenicline, about 42 in 100 people had unwanted effects, mainly feeling sick.[39]Tashkin DP, Rennard S, Hays JT. Effects of varenicline on smoking cessation in patients with mild to moderate COPD. A randomized controlled trial. Chest. 2011; 139: 591-599. About 27 in 100 people who took varenicline had nausea.

Other less common unwanted effects included having sleeping problems (difficulty sleeping, abnormal dreams, or nightmares), chest infection, flatulence, and vomiting.

Some people who take varenicline to stop smoking say they have mood changes, such as feeling anxious, agitated, or depressed. Some people said they felt like harming themselves.[40]Medicines and Healthcare products Regulatory Agency. Europe-wide review recommends updates to product information for varenicline (brand name Champix). December 2007. Available at: http://www.mhra.gov.uk/Safetyinformation/Safetywarningsalertsandrecalls/Safetywarningsandmessagesformedicines/CON2033390 (accessed on 22 October 2012).

We don't know if the mood changes are actually caused by varenicline. They might be a result of giving up smoking. [39]Tashkin DP, Rennard S, Hays JT. Effects of varenicline on smoking cessation in patients with mild to moderate COPD. A randomized controlled trial. Chest. 2011; 139: 591-599. 

How might giving up smoking affect my life?

It takes willpower to stop smoking, even if you have support and take treatments. You might need a number of attempts to stop smoking.

Many people don't manage to stop smoking the first time they try. Some people find this can be frustrating and that this can make them feel irritable.

If you choose to use nicotine replacement therapy, you may need to try different types before you find one that suits you.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT DRUG TREATMENTS

What drugs are used to treat COPD?

A range of drug treatments are used to improve COPD symptoms. Some are taken with an inhaler or nebuliser, so you breathe in the drug as a powder or spray. You should be shown how to use your inhaler or nebuliser correctly. This increases the chances the drug will help you. You can take some drugs combined together in the one inhaler.

Other drugs are taken by mouth as a tablet. The right treatment will depend on the symptoms you get, and how much your lungs have been damaged. 

You may have to try different drugs to find out which suits you best. Some people take a combination of drugs for COPD. You may have to take more than one drug combined together in the same inhaler. Some drugs are used only when you have a flare-up of your COPD.

Beta-2 agonists

These are bronchodilator drugs. They relax muscles around your small airways so that more air can get into your lungs. This can make it easier for you to breathe. You need to breathe in these drugs using an inhaler, or sometimes a nebuliser. There are two types of beta-2 agonists: short-acting and long-acting. The short-acting ones work quickly and last for about three to four hours. The long-acting ones last for up to 12 hours.

Beta-2 agonists include salbutamol, formoterol, salmeterol, and terbutaline.

Anticholinergics

These are bronchodilator drugs. They relax muscles around your large airways to help more air get into your lungs. This can make it easier for you to breathe. You need to breathe in these drugs using an inhaler or sometimes a nebuliser. There are two types of anticholinergic: short-acting and long-acting. The short-acting ones work quickly and last for about three to four hours. The long-acting ones don't work very quickly, but they last around a day. You usually only need to take long-acting anticholinegics once or twice a day. You usually use short-acting anticholinergics when you're having trouble breathing, or a few times a day to help keep your airways open.

Anticholinergics include ipratropium and tiotropium.

Inhaled corticosteroids (steroids)

These are drugs that you breathe in to reduce the inflammation in your airways. This should make it easier for you to breathe. You usually take inhaled steroids at least twice a day. They work best if you take them regularly. This will help keep the inflammation in your airways under control. You may need to take these drugs over a long period of time.

Inhaled steroids include beclometasone, budesonide, and fluticasone. You can only take these drugs if they are combined in an inhaler with a long-acting beta-2 agonist.

Mucolytic drugs

These are drugs that make the mucus in your lungs thinner, looser, and less sticky. This should help make it easier for you to breathe. You are less likely to get chest infections if you have less mucus in your lungs. Mucolytic drugs usually come as tablets that you take two to four times a day.

Mucolytic drugs include carbocisteine and mecysteine.

Rescue medication

If you have a flare-up of COPD you will usually need to take oral steroids. Sometimes you will also need to take antibiotics. These are both types of drugs that you swallow.

Oral steroids can help you feel less out of breath. Prednisolone is an oral steroid that is commonly used if you have a flare-up of COPD. You are likely to have to take it for seven to 14 days.

Antibiotics are drugs that can kill off bacteria that cause infections in your body, including lung infections. You are usually only given antibiotics if you have a cough that makes you bring up phlegm, and the phlegm has colour or has pus in it. Antibiotics come as capsules, tablets, or as a liquid. These drugs may need to be taken up to 4 times a day for up to 10 days. Antibiotics include amoxicillin, erythromycin, and tetracycline.

Oxygen

Breathing in extra oxygen is a treatment you can have if you have severe or very severe COPD and have a lot of damage to your lungs that hasn't been helped by other drugs. If the amount of oxygen in your blood is low, breathing in extra oxygen may help you get more oxygen into your blood. With a better supply of oxygen going to your heart, lungs, and other organs, your body should work and feel better. This may help you to live longer. 

If your COPD isn't very severe or you have a healthy amount of oxygen in your blood, breathing in extra oxygen probably won't help you. If you have a flare-up of COPD and you get very out of breath you may be given oxygen for a short time.

Before you have oxygen you may need to have a test called pulse oximetry to check how much oxygen is in your blood. You may be able to have this test at your doctor’s surgery, or your doctor can arrange for you to have the test at a hospital. The test will help your doctor tell if you are likely to benefit from having extra oxygen. Your doctor will give you a prescription for the exact amount of extra oxygen to take, if needed. Having too much extra oxygen can sometimes be harmful. You can have oxygen either in hospital or in your own home.

What is a self-management programme for COPD?

Self management means learning more about your illness, how you can manage your symptoms and when to get help to prevent a flare-up of COPD. You can find out about how to manage your illness from your nurse, GP, or your specialist hospital team. You can do this on your own or as part of a group.

Self-management programmes are courses designed to support you to manage your own condition.

A typical self-management programme for COPD can include learning about COPD, getting advice on improving your diet, learning about the best types of exercises you can do to stay healthy, and learning how to spot when your symptoms are getting worse to help you avoid flare-ups. You may also learn about monitoring your COPD and how and when to take your treatments to prevent flare-ups. You may be given a diary to help you plan how you are going to manage your COPD during the programme and afterwards.

A self-management programme for COPD will focus only on COPD. It should take into account your needs and the severity of your disease. It can be held in hospital, at a GP surgery or local clinic, or you may be given information and advice to follow on your own at home.

You can follow a self-management programme while you are having other treatments for COPD, such as stopping smoking, taking COPD drugs, or having pulmonary rehabilitation.

Will having pulmonary rehabilitation cause any side effects?

Having pulmonary rehabilitation is unlikely to cause unwanted effects. 

How might pulmonary rehabilitation affect your life?

If you decide to have pulmonary rehabilitation in a hospital, you will need to go to hospital a few times a week for around six weeks. This may interfere with your work or normal everyday activities, particularly if you have to travel far to get to hospital.

To get the best out of pulmonary rehabilitation you will need to attend every session. This will require commitment. You may need to make arrangements with your work or make arrangements for child care if you have children.
