STEP 5: MY DECISION

Our Decision Support telephone service is here to assist and guide you through the Shared Decision Making process.

The Decision Support team is made of professional Health Coaches who are clinicians, commonly nurses, who have undertaken additional training to become a Health Coach.

The Health Coaches will assist you by:

  • Providing you with information
  • Helping you to understand your condition
  • Recognise what is important to you regarding the outcome of treatment
  • Identifying potential solutions
  • Encourage you in discussing options with your family
  • Transferring skills which will assist you in using the information and resources available to you
  • Support you in building confidence in discussing your choice with your doctor
  • You may find that this can be achieved in one telephone call with a Health Coach however; if further calls are required to support you in reaching your decision the Health Coach will schedule these with you.

Important Things To Note

  • A Health Coach does not know which treatment option is best for you.
  • The Health Coach does not have information about your medical history.
  • You will still need to talk over your decision and reasons for your decision with your doctor or nurse.

MORE INFORMATION ABOUT DEPRESSION

What is depression?

Depression is the word used to describe feelings of low mood and/or loss of pleasure in everyday activities [1]NICE. Depression. The treatment and management of depression in adults, CG 90. It’s a lot more than feeling sad, upset, bored or fed up. It isn’t just the way a person feels after a bad day at the office or a row with someone they care about.

People with depression feel miserable day after day, and nothing and no one seems able to change this mood. They feel helpless and hopeless, unable to control how they feel or what is happening to them. They can’t see a “light at the end of the tunnel.”

Depression sometimes gets mixed up with anxiety, and they can be hard to tell apart. People with anxiety are excessively worried about a number of different things and find it hard to control their worries [2]NICE. Generalised anxiety disorders and panic disorder (with or without agoraphobia) in adults. CG113. Some people feel both anxious and depressed, and some of the symptoms overlap.

Depression can be mild, moderate or severe, depending on the number of symptoms and how much they affect a person’s everyday life.

What are the main symptoms of depression?

The main symptoms of depression are [3]NICE. Depression. The treatment and management of depression in adults, CG 90; http://www.cks.nhs.uk/depression/management/scenario_detection_assessment_diagnosis#-403459:

  • Low mood or sadness nearly every day
  • Loss of interest or pleasure in most activities

Other symptoms include [4]Depression Alliance UK. What is depression. http://www.depressionalliance.org/help-and-information/what-is-depression.php:

  • Tiredness and lack of energy
  • Continuing sadness
  • Loss of self-confidence and self-esteem
  • Difficulty concentrating
  • Feeling guilt or worthless
  • Feeling helpless and hopeless
  • Sleep problems – finding it hard to go to sleep, or waking up very early
  • Avoiding people, sometimes even close friends
  • Finding it hard to work, study or care for family
  • Loss of appetite
  • Loss of sex drive and/or sexual problems
  • Aches and pains
  • Self-harming and thinking about self-harming in some way

Some groups of people are more likely to have physical symptoms of depression, such as aches and pains and dizziness, than others. For example, bodily symptoms of depression are more common in people of Asian background.

Why has this happened to me?

There are many reasons why people become depressed. Often there is no single cause. Instead, a number of different factors may interact in a way we don’t fully understand. Some of these factors may be to do with the way a person thinks, feels and relates to people. Others may be external triggers.

Possible factors involved in depression include:

Biological factors

An imbalance of chemicals in the brain which carry messages from one nerve cell to another (neurotransmitters). The main neurotransmitters in the brain are serotonin, noradrenaline and dopamine.

  • Family history: although there is no clear genetic cause of depression, it is more common in some families than others.

Stressful life events

  • These may have happened while a person is growing up, or later in life. They include bereavement, break-up of a relationship, loss of a job or home, money problems, alcohol or drug problems.

Relationships with other people

  • Falling out with family, friends or work colleagues can lead to feelings of sadness and isolation
  • Being unable to make friendships or relationships can also result in feelings of loneliness and low self-esteem
  • Drinking alcohol or taking drugs, such as cannabis, may lead to further breakdown of relationships with family and friends, employment problems, homelessness and worsening depression.

Depression may start:

  • After a very upsetting, difficult or stressful event, such as a bereavement, break up of a relationship, loss of a job or home. This is sometimes called reactive depression. Everyone reacts differently to this type of event, and most feel low, anxious, irritable or angry. [5]Depression Alliance. Depression Terms You Might Hear. http://www.depressionalliance.org/help-and-information/depression-terms.php. It is when people have these symptoms for weeks or months after a traumatic event, without showing any improvement, that they may have depression
  • Out of the blue, with no obvious stressful event to trigger it. This is sometimes called endogenous depression [5]Depression Alliance. Depression Terms You Might Hear. http://www.depressionalliance.org/help-and-information/depression-terms.php

Other triggers include: [5]Depression Alliance. Depression Terms You Might Hear. http://www.depressionalliance.org/help-and-information/depression-terms.php

  • Lack of daylight and sunlight during the winter: This is called seasonal affective disorder (SAD).
  • Childbirth: many new mothers have mood swings in the first few days after having a baby, and about 10-20 in every 100 women continue to have symptoms of depression, including panic attacks, sleep problems and feeling unable to cope, for much longer. This is called post-natal depression.
  • Physical illness: serious health problems, such as a stroke, cancer or heart disease, can make people depressed.

This patient decision aid does not go into detail about SAD, post-natal depression or depression linked to a physical illness, but many of the treatments for mild to moderate depression that are discussed are used to treat these types of depression too.

How do I find out if I have depression?

It’s hard to know if a person has depression unless they go to their family doctor (GP). The doctor can diagnose depression based what a person says about how they are feeling and what has been happening to them. Some doctors use questionnaires to help them find out whether a person has depression (eg: the Patient Health Questionnaire [PHQ-9]) (Patient.co.uk/doctor/Patient-health-Questionnaire-(PHQ-9).htm).

The doctor will want to know if a person has had one or both of the core symptoms of depression most days, most of the time, for at least two weeks. These core symptoms are: [8]NHS Evidence. Clinical Knowledge Summaries. Depression. http://www.cks.nhs.uk/depression/management/scenario_detection_assessment_diagnosis/assessment_and_diagnosis#

  • Low mood or sadness nearly every day and/or
  • Loss of interest or pleasure in most activities

The doctor will also ask about any other symptoms of depression which a person may have. He/she is likely to diagnose depression if a person has five out of nine of this list of symptoms, including at least one of the core symptoms [9]American Psychiatric Association. Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, fourth edition. http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK64063/::

  • Depressed mood most of the day.
  • Diminished interest or pleasure in all or most activities.
  • Significant unintentional weight loss or gain.
  • Sleep problems (insomnia) or sleeping too much.
  • Agitation or slowness of thinking or physical activity noticed by other people.
  • Fatigue or loss of energy.
  • Feelings of worthlessness or excessive guilt.
  • Recurrent thoughts of death.

People with mild depression may only have five of the symptoms of depression including one of the core symptoms, and there may be little effect on their ability to carry out everyday activities. [8]NHS Evidence. Clinical Knowledge Summaries. Depression. http://www.cks.nhs.uk/depression/management/scenario_detection_assessment_diagnosis/assessment_and_diagnosis#

People with moderate depression are likely to have at least five symptoms of depression, including both of the core symptoms, and their symptoms may be more severe than with mild depression, and have more impact on their everyday lives. [8]NHS Evidence. Clinical Knowledge Summaries. Depression. http://www.cks.nhs.uk/depression/management/scenario_detection_assessment_diagnosis/assessment_and_diagnosis#

If someone has fewer than five of the symptoms of depression, including one of the core symptoms, this is often called sub-threshold depression.

How many people have depression?

Depression is common. It will interfere with the normal activities of about two-thirds of adults at some time in their lives [12]http://www.cks.nhs.uk/depression/background_information/prevalence# .

More women get depression than men. One in four women and one in 10 men will need treatment at some time in their lives [13]NHS Evidence. Clinical Knowledge Summaries. Depression. http://www.cks.nhs.uk/depression/background_information/prevalence#-402794.

One in three people with depression have moderate or severe illness [13]NHS Evidence. Clinical Knowledge Summaries. Depression. http://www.cks.nhs.uk/depression/background_information/prevalence#-402794.

Depression is more common in people who have physical health problems than those who are otherwise well. People with two or more physical illnesses are about eight times more likely to have depression than those who are physically healthy [13]NHS Evidence. Clinical Knowledge Summaries. Depression. http://www.cks.nhs.uk/depression/background_information/prevalence#-402794.

Depression seems to be more frequent in some families than others, but there is no clear way it is inherited. Biological, psychological and social factors all seem to play a part [16]NHS Evidence. Clinical Knowledge Summaries. Depression. http://www.cks.nhs.uk/depression/background_information/prevalence#-406145

What will happen to me if I have depression?

On average, a bout of depression lasts six to eight months before people get better [17]NHS Evidence. Clinical Knowledge Summaries. Depression. http://www.cks.nhs.uk/depression/background_information/prevalence#-402801.

Mild depression is less likely to be diagnosed by a doctor, and more likely to get better without treatment. Symptoms of moderate depression are more obvious, and likely to have more impact on daily life - [17]NHS Evidence. Clinical Knowledge Summaries. Depression. http://www.cks.nhs.uk/depression/background_information/prevalence#-402801- and to be treated with psychological therapies and/or medication.

About 50 out of 100 people who have depression get it again and the more times people have depression the more likely it is to come back. [17]NHS Evidence. Clinical Knowledge Summaries. Depression. http://www.cks.nhs.uk/depression/background_information/prevalence#-402801. 

People who get depression before they are 20 years of age, or have additional alcohol problems, or get it in old age, recover less well. [17]NHS Evidence. Clinical Knowledge Summaries. Depression. http://www.cks.nhs.uk/depression/background_information/prevalence#-402801.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT WATCHFUL WAITING

How is watchful waiting used to treat depression?

Watchful waiting for depression means that you will meet your GP every few weeks to discuss how you are getting on. You won’t have any medicines for depression and you won’t have formal sessions of talking therapies [21]Meredith LS, Cheng WJY, Hickey SC, Dwight-Johnson M. Factors associated with primary care clinicians’ choice of a watchful waiting approach to managing depression. Psychiatric Services 2007; 58: 72-78.

Watchful waiting doesn’t mean no treatment. When you see your GP you will be able to talk about things that are worrying you and get information about depression [1]NICE. Depression. The treatment and management of depression in adults, CG 90.

There is no fixed format for watchful waiting, and doctors do it in different ways. Some offer more advice and support than others. [23]Moore M, Byng R, Harris SB, Kendrick T. ‘Watchful waiting’ or ‘active monitoring’ in depression management in primary care: Exploring the recalled content of general practitioner consultations. J Affect Disord 2013; 145: 120-125.

Many people find it helpful to talk about problems, such as family upset, drinking, drug or money problems or unemployment, and discuss practical options for solving them in an informal way. Some continue with watchful waiting until they recover completely. If they don’t get any better, they can discuss other types of treatment with their doctor.

Who can use watchful waiting for depression?

Your GP may suggest watchful waiting if [1]NICE. Depression. The treatment and management of depression in adults, CG 90:

  • You are likely to get better without any specific treatment
  • You would prefer not to have another type of treatment
  • You do not have enough symptoms that suggest a diagnosis of depression.

People with mild depression and those with symptoms of depression after a sad event, such as a bereavement, may be offered watchful waiting for depression. If you’ve recently lost someone you cared about, through death or break-up of a relationship, then feeling sad or being unable to sleep well is a natural response. It will take time to get over your sadness, but you may start to feel better within a few months without specific treatment for depression [21]Meredith LS, Cheng WJY, Hickey SC, Dwight-Johnson M. Factors associated with primary care clinicians’ choice of a watchful waiting approach to managing depression. Psychiatric Services 2007; 58: 72-78.

What are the main effects of watchful waiting treatment on depression?

Watchful waiting can be effective in treating mild to moderate depression. It may not be as effective as other treatment.

In a group of people with symptoms of mild to moderate depression for at least eight weeks, where it wasn’t clear whether or not they should have treatment, those who had active monitoring by their GP had slightly worse symptoms of depression after 12 and 26 weeks than those who had additional antidepressant medication with selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs): [26]Kendrick T, Chatwin J, Dowrick C et al. Randomised controlled trial to determine the clinical effectiveness and cost-effectiveness of selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors plus supportive care, versus supportive care alone, for mild to moderate depression with somatic symptoms in primary care: the THREAD (THREshold for AntiDepressant response) study. Health Technol Assess. 2009 Apr;13(22):iii-iv, ix-xi, 1-159

  • After 12 weeks, 29 in 100 people who had active monitoring no longer had depression, compared to 44 in 100 who had an SSRI as well as support from their GP. [26]Kendrick T, Chatwin J, Dowrick C et al. Randomised controlled trial to determine the clinical effectiveness and cost-effectiveness of selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors plus supportive care, versus supportive care alone, for mild to moderate depression with somatic symptoms in primary care: the THREAD (THREshold for AntiDepressant response) study. Health Technol Assess. 2009 Apr;13(22):iii-iv, ix-xi, 1-159
  • At 26 weeks, 36 in 100 people on active monitoring didn’t have depression compared to 54 in 100 of those who had an SSRI as well [26]Kendrick T, Chatwin J, Dowrick C et al. Randomised controlled trial to determine the clinical effectiveness and cost-effectiveness of selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors plus supportive care, versus supportive care alone, for mild to moderate depression with somatic symptoms in primary care: the THREAD (THREshold for AntiDepressant response) study. Health Technol Assess. 2009 Apr;13(22):iii-iv, ix-xi, 1-159

Amongst this same group of people, most recalled talking to their GP about their practical problems and how these might be solved, and quite a lot discussed changing the way they thought, addressing relationships or talking to trusted friends or family. What they talked about with their GP didn’t affect whether or not their depression improved, but those who discussed their problems and possible solutions with their GP felt more satisfied with their care [23]Moore M, Byng R, Harris SB, Kendrick T. ‘Watchful waiting’ or ‘active monitoring’ in depression management in primary care: Exploring the recalled content of general practitioner consultations. J Affect Disord 2013; 145: 120-125


Will watchful waiting for depression cause any unwanted effects?

Watchful waiting is unlikely to cause any unwanted effects in people with mild depression.

If it is used by people with more severe depression who need depression medication or talking therapies, it may delay them getting effective treatment.

How might watchful waiting for depression affect my life?

If your depression is mild, discussing your problems in a general way with your GP may help you to understand them better and find ways to solve them, so they have less impact on your everyday life.

Going to see your GP every few weeks should not take up a lot of your time, but you may want to set time aside between your meetings to think carefully about what you have discussed.

How likely is your depression to come back after watchful waiting?

We don’t know how likely depression is to come back if you have active monitoring/watchful waiting.

In general, people who receive some form of treatment for depression are less likely to relapse when they are on treatment than after treatment stops.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT LIFESTYLE CHANGES AND SELF HELP INTERVENTIONS

What self help treatments are used to treat depression?

A lot of different self help treatments are used to treat depression, on their own or with other types of treatment for depression. They aim to improve your symptoms and enable you to take part in the everyday activities you enjoyed before you became depressed. This is sometimes called improving the quality of your life. It includes feeling well enough to go to work or college, looking after your family and home, getting involved in social and leisure activities and enjoying being with family and friends.

Self help treatments include:

  • Finding out more about depression, eg: by reading booklets or accessing information about depression from websites, such as the Depression Alliance [30]http://www.depressionalliance.org and MIND [31]http://www.mind.org.uk/mental_health_a-z/7980_understanding_depression
  • Talking about your depression with people who also have depression, eg: by joining a self-help group [32]eg: http://www.depressionalternatives.co.uk or through an internet forum
  • Using guided or internet/computer programmes based on cognitive behaviour therapy (CBT) – a type of talking therapy which enables you to reconsider your views about your life, and understand how your actions affect your thoughts and feelings, eg: Beating the Blues – an interactive, multimedia course of eight online sessions, each lasting about one hour, which explains how the way you think affects your feelings and behaviour, and shows you how to cope better in the short and long term [33]http://www.beatingtheblues.co.uk
  • Lifestyle changes, eg: exercise, diet, alcohol, drug or debt management
  • Herbal remedies/complementary therapies, eg: St John’s Wort (Hypericum perforatum), acupuncture, music or dance therapy
Who can use self help treatments for depression?

Anyone with depression can use self help interventions. People with mild depression may find that their symptoms improve with self help interventions alone. Those with moderate depression are likely to need other treatments as well as self help.

You can use self help interventions on your own or in a group, with or without some support from trained specialists.

You can have self-help CBT in three main ways [34]Farrand P, Woodford J. Impact of support on the effectiveness of written cognitive behavioural self-help: A systematic review and meta-analysis of randomised controlled trials. Clin Psychol Rev. 2013 Feb;33(1):182-95:

  • On your own using leaflets, computer or internet programmes, without any help from a doctor or trained specialist
  • Using leaflets, computer or internet programmes, with some help from a doctor or trained specialist
  • Using leaflets and other materials, with regular meetings with a doctor or trained specialist

Medical specialists do not recommend St John’s Wort because of the possibility of unwanted effects and interactions with other medicines for depression (see: Will self help treatment for depression cause unwanted effects?) [1]NICE. Depression. The treatment and management of depression in adults, CG 90.

What are the main effects of self help treatments on depression?

Reading about depression in leaflets or on websites, or joining a self help group can help to improve symptoms of depression and make you feel better [36]Donker T, Griffiths K. M, Cuijpers P et al. Psychoeducation for depression, anxiety and psychological distress: a meta-analysis BMC Medicine 2009; 79; Mead N, Lester H, Chew-Graham C et al. Effects of befriending on depressive symptoms and distress: systematic review and meta-analysis. British Journal of Psychiatry 2010; 196: 96-101.

Getting leaflets or accessing information on the internet is easy and cheap, and some people prefer to find out more about depression in this way as it is private and they don’t have to tell other people about their problems. [37]Donker T, Griffiths K. M, Cuijpers P et al. Psychoeducation for depression, anxiety and psychological distress: a meta-analysis BMC Medicine 2009; 79.

Lifestyle changes, such as losing weight if you are very overweight, and taking regular exercise, can also reduce symptoms of depression. [38]Fabricatore, A. N. W. and Heymsfield, A. M. Intentional weight loss and changes in symptoms: A systematic review and meta-analysis International Journal of Obesity 2011; 35: 1363-1376; Rimer, J., Dwan, K., Lawlor, D. A et al. Exercise for depression. Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews 2012. You don’t need to take very vigorous exercise, for example if you are older; walking, tai chi and dancing can all help to lift your spirits. [39]Robertson, R. Robertson. Walking for depression or depressive symptoms: A systematic review and meta-analysis. Mental Health and Physical Activity 2012; 5: 66-75; Wang, C. and et al Tai Chi on psychological well-being: Systematic review and meta-analysis BMC Complementary and Alternative Medicine 2010; 10: 23; Pinniger, R. Brown Argentine tango dance compared to mindfulness meditation and a waiting-list control: A randomised trial for treating depression Complementary Therapies in Medicine 2012; 20: 377-384.

If you have a problem with alcohol and are depressed, cutting out alcohol may make you feel better within a few weeks [40]Royal College of Psychiatry. Alcohol and depression. http://www.rcpsych.ac.uk/mentalhealthinfoforall/problems/alcoholanddrugs/alcoholdepression.aspx. The safe limits for alcohol drinking are 14 units per week for women and 21 units per week for men [40]Royal College of Psychiatry. Alcohol and depression. http://www.rcpsych.ac.uk/mentalhealthinfoforall/problems/alcoholanddrugs/alcoholdepression.aspx. It is recommended to spread alcohol units across the week, and to have at least two alcohol-free days per week [40]Royal College of Psychiatry. Alcohol and depression. http://www.rcpsych.ac.uk/mentalhealthinfoforall/problems/alcoholanddrugs/alcoholdepression.aspx.

If you use drugs such as cannabis, getting help to stop using them may improve your symptoms of depression [43]Royal College of Psychiatrists. Cannabis and mental health. http://www.rcpsych.ac.uk/expertadvice/problems/alcoholanddrugs/cannabis.aspx.

Using internet based and computerised psychological treatment programmes based on cognitive behaviour therapy (CBT) can improve symptoms of depression, especially if people get additional support from their doctor or someone else who is trained in psychological treatments for depression [44]Andersson G, Cuijpers P, Andersson G, Cuijpers P. Internet-based and other computerized psychological treatments for adult depression: a meta-analysis. Cognitive Behaviour Therapy 2009; 38: 196-205.

Having CBT on the telephone may reduce the likelihood that you will drop out of therapy. It may not be so effective in the long term [45]Mohr D C. Ho J, Duffecy, J et al. Effect of telephone-administered vs face-to-face cognitive behavioral therapy on adherence to therapy and depression outcomes among primary care patients: a randomized trial
JAMA 2012; 307: 2278-2285.

The only herbal medicine which is known to reduce symptoms in mild to moderate depression is St John’s Wort, also called Hypericum perforatum [46]Sarris J., Kavanagh D. Kava and St. John's Wort: current evidence for use in mood and anxiety disorders. Journal of Alternative & Complementary Medicine 2009; 15: 827-836.

Although acupuncture has sometimes seemed helpful in reducing symptoms in mild to moderate depression, its effectiveness is unproven [47]Ernst, E., Lee, M. S., Choi, T. Y. Acupuncture for depression?: A systematic review of systematic reviews. Evaluation & the Health Professions 2011; 34: 403-412.

What are the main effects of self help treatments for depression on what you can do and how you feel?

Some self-help treatments can help you feel less depressed. These include self help-CBT (with leaflets or on the computer), especially with some support from a trained therapist, exercise, losing weight and herbal treatment with St John’s Wort.

In groups of people with depression using written self help CBT materials, those having a small amount of irregular, face to face or telephone contact with their doctor or trained therapist had a greater improvement in their depression than those who had no contact. Both the irregular and no-contact groups had a greater improvement in their depression than those who had regular appointments with their GP or therapist for additional support [34]Farrand P, Woodford J. Impact of support on the effectiveness of written cognitive behavioural self-help: A systematic review and meta-analysis of randomised controlled trials. Clin Psychol Rev. 2013 Feb;33(1):182-95

In a group of people with depression who had online CBT from a trained therapist as well as seeing their GP, nearly four out of 10 had recovered from their depression after four months. This compared with two out of 10 who just went to their GP and didn’t have the extra online CBT. The better effects of online CBT from a trained therapist were still present after eight months [49]Kessler D, Lewis G, Kaur S et al.Therapist-delivered Internet psychotherapy for depression in primary care: a randomised controlled trial. Lancet 2009; 374: 628-634.

A group of people with depression who used the ‘Beat the Blues’ computerised self-help programme were significantly less depressed at the end of two months treatment, and six months later, than those who had standard treatment for depression from their doctor. It didn’t matter how long they had been ill or what medicines they were taking for their depression. On a scale of 0 (no depression) to 63 (severe depression), ‘Beat the Blues’ users scored 2-7 points better after treatment than those who used standard therapies. Their average score was 12 which put them in the minimal depression category, compared to 16 for the other group, putting them in the mild depression category [50]Proudfoot J, Ryden C, Everitt B et al. Clinical efficacy of computerised cognitive-behavioural therapy for anxiety and depression in primary care: randomised controlled trial. Br J Psychiatry. 2004 Jul;185:46-54. They also scored better in tests of their ability to work, manage their home, take part in social and leisure activities, and enjoy relationships than those who had usual treatment. On average they scored 1-6 points better on 8 point scales of these activities than people who didn’t use the programme [50]Proudfoot J, Ryden C, Everitt B et al. Clinical efficacy of computerised cognitive-behavioural therapy for anxiety and depression in primary care: randomised controlled trial. Br J Psychiatry. 2004 Jul;185:46-54.

In a group of people, two out of 10 of those who had telephone CBT dropped out of treatment, compared to over three out of 10 who had treatment with a therapist. At the end of treatment, improvement in depression was the same for both types of treatment. After six months, those who had seen a therapist for their CBT were less depressed than those had treatment on the telephone [52]Mohr D C. Ho J, Duffecy, J et al. Effect of telephone-administered vs face-to-face cognitive behavioral therapy on adherence to therapy and depression outcomes among primary care patients: a randomized trial JAMA 2012; 307: 2278-2285.

In a very large group of people with depression, exercise had a small effect on improving their symptoms. It was similar to that seen with talking therapies, bright light therapy and antidepressant medicines [53]Rimer, J., Dwan, K., Lawlor, D. A et al. Exercise for depression. Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews 2012.

Having a say in the intensity of your exercise may be helpful [54]Callaghan P, Khalil E, Morres I et al. Pragmatic randomised controlled trial of preferred intensity exercise in women living with depression BMC Public Health 2011; 11: 465. When women exercised regularly on a treadmill at a rate they had chosen they reported significantly better improvement in their symptoms of depression, self esteem and quality of life (emotions, social isolation, energy, sleep, physical mobility and pain) than those who exercised as they were told [55]Callaghan P, Khalil E, Morres I et al. Pragmatic randomised controlled trial of preferred intensity exercise in women living with depression. BMC Public Health 2011; 11: 465. After 12 exercise sessions on a treadmill over four weeks, the depression score of women who had chosen their exercise level improved by 8.5 points, compared to less than one point in the other group.

A group of people with mild to moderate depression who did regular strength training in a gym needed less time off work over the next year than those who went on aerobic exercise or relaxation programmes. For every eight days that the people on the relaxation programme needed off work, those doing strength training, needed a day less [56]Krogh, J., Saltin, B., Gluud, C. et al The DEMO trial: a randomized, parallel-group, observer-blinded clinical trial of strength versus aerobic versus relaxation training for patients with mild to moderate depression. Journal of Clinical Psychiatry 2009; 70: 790-800

In a group of almost 8000 people with depression who intentionally lost weight, either by dieting, taking more exercise, or using medicines to help them lose weight, there was a reduction in symptoms of depression. All-round lifestyle change – healthier eating and taking more exercise – was a little more effective than any individual change on its own [57]Fabricatore, A. N. W. and Heymsfield, A. M. Intentional weight loss and changes in symptoms of depression: A systematic review and meta-analysis International Journal of Obesity 2011; 35: 1363-1376.

Will self help treatment for depression cause any unwanted effects?

Most self help treatments don’t have side effects.

It is sometimes difficult to know whether information in leaflets or on websites is accurate, as inaccurate information can be worrying or give bad advice about how to relieve symptoms.

Making lifestyle changes, such as losing weight and exercise, without checking that the methods are safe for you could lead to physical health problems and injuries.

Using any complementary therapies, such as herbal remedies and acupuncture, without checking what is in them, or that the person giving you them is fully trained could also result in physical health problems and injuries.

St John’s Wort (Hypericum perforatum) may cause allergic skin reactions, stomach upset, nerve pain and make people sensitive to sunlight so they get sunburned, especially if they are fair skinned [58]http://www.nhs.uk/medicine-guides/pages/MedicineOverview.aspx?medicine=St.%20Johns%20wort. St John’s Wort interacts with a large number of commonly used medicines, in a way which can make them more or less effective or cause other unwanted effects. These medicines include other medicines for depression, the contraceptive Pill, and some medicines for high blood pressure and high cholesterol and for thinning the blood in people who are prone to blood clots [58]http://www.nhs.uk/medicine-guides/pages/MedicineOverview.aspx?medicine=St.%20Johns%20wort.

If you decide to use St Johns Wort and are already taking medications, you may want to check with your doctor about any interactions.

How might using self help treatments for depression affect my life?

Self help treatments, such as self-help CBT and exercise, may make it easier for you to lead the kind of life you want. They will also take up some of your time.

If you take part in a self-help CBT programme, you’ll need to set time aside every week for about 8-10 weeks. This will include about an hour a week to do the online exercises, and some more time for further reading and possibly discussions with your GP or trained therapist if you are getting some help from them.

If you decide to make lifestyle changes such as losing weight or taking more exercise, you’ll need to take some time to work out the kind of diet that will help you most, and an exercise programme that you can fit into your everyday life. Exercise is most likely to help you if it’s easy to fit into your day, and you enjoy it.

Amongst groups of people who have found exercise helpful in improving their depression, the most commonly used exercise programme was aerobic exercise that increased people’s heart rate to 60-80 percent of their maximum rate, for 30 minutes, three times a week, for a total of eight weeks [60]Perraton LG, Kumar S, Machotka Z. Exercise parameters in the treatment of clinical depression: a systematic review of randomized controlled trials. J Eval Clin Pract. 2010 Jun;16(3):597-604.

How likely is depression to come back after self help treatment?

We don’t know how likely depression is to come back if you have lifestyle changes and self help interventions.

In general, people who receive some form of treatment for depression are less likely to relapse when they are on treatment than after treatment stops.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT PSYCHOLOGICAL THERAPIES DELIVERED BY HEALTHCARE PROFESSIONALS

What talking therapies are used to treat depression?

Several different types of talking therapies are used to treat depression, on their own or with other treatments for depression. They aim to improve your symptoms and enable you to take part in the everyday activities you enjoyed before you became depressed. This is sometimes called improving the quality of your life. It includes feeling well enough to go to work or college, looking after your family and home, getting involved in social and leisure activities and enjoying being with family and friends.

Talking therapies help you to look at all the factors that may be contributing to your depression, including how your feel and think about things, and lifestyle factors, such as diet, exercise, smoking, drinking alcohol, drug taking, gambling, money problems and employment.

The main psychological therapies for depression are [61]IAPT. Which talking therapy for depression?; Beating the blues: http://www.beatingtheblues.co.uk:

Counselling

Counselling sees depression mainly as an emotional problem. It aims to help people understand, express and make sense of the feelings underlying their depression. Trained counsellors focus on how you feel and see you as a ‘whole’ person. They try to understand your ‘world’ as you see it. Counsellors don’t tell you what to do, but they help you explore what you want to talk about, and develop news ways of looking at yourself and the world around you.

Typically people with mild to moderate depression might receive 6-10 counselling sessions over 8-12 weeks, usually once a week for 50-60 minutes. If you need more sessions, you can discuss this with your counsellor.

Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT)

This is a psychological treatment that aims to help people change patterns of thinking and behaviour that are causing problems. It helps you reconsider how you think about yourself, the world and other people, and understand how what you do affects your thoughts and feelings. Changing how you think when you are depressed can help you fight negative thoughts, make healthier decisions and take more positive action.

You can have CBT at regular sessions over a period of three to four months. You can have it face to face, with a trained therapist, one-to-one (usually 16-20 sessions) or with other people in a group (usually 10-12 sessions). Sessions include practical exercises with the therapist, and you will get ‘homework’ to do between session.

‘Self-help’ CBT is another way of having treatment. You can do this at home, on the computer, on your own or with support from a trained therapist by telephone or on the internet. This is called computerised CBT (CCBT).

Interpersonal psychotherapy (IPT)

This type of talking therapy focuses on relationship problems, such as conflicts, life changes, grief and loss, or difficulty in starting or keeping relationships going. IPT helps you find out how you are feeling and behaving in your relationships and, by dealing with problems, you may feel less depressed. You can have IPT on a one-to-one basis with a trained therapist, and agree how many sessions to have (often 16 sessions, sometimes fewer).

Psychodynamic therapy

This is based on the idea that when something is very painful, we try to ignore it or bury it very deeply. Despite this, it can affect the way we feel and how we relate to other people, and lead to depression.

Psychodynamic therapy encourages you to talk about yourself with a trained therapist so you can find out how previous experiences may be affecting the way you feel and act and could be causing your depression. By understanding your feelings, you may be able to change how you respond to certain situations, move on and become better at relating to other people.

You can agree how many sessions of psychodynamic therapy to have with your therapist. Typically, you may have 16 sessions, held once a week for 50 minutes.

Who can have talking therapies for depression?

Anyone who has depression can have talking therapies, free on the NHS. You can arrange this through your GP. Some GPs specialise in using talking therapies themselves. If they don’t, or you’d rather see someone else, your GP can refer you to a trained therapist specialising in the type of talking therapy that you feel will help you most.

What are the main effects of talking therapies on depression?

Talking therapies can be effective in treating symptoms of mild to moderate depression, and making people feel better. 

In a group of people with depression, 41 in 100 of those who had an average six sessions of talking therapies, mainly focused on cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT), in a well organised programme, with a trained therapist recovered from their depression within a year. Another 15 in 100 people on the same programme got a worthwhile improvement in their symptoms. [62]Richards DA, Borglin G. Implementation of psychological therapies for anxiety and depression in routine practice: two year prospective cohort study. J Affect Disord. 2011 Sep;133(1-2):51-60.

Having counselling from your GP or a trained counsellor in the practice can help to control your depression and improve your quality of life over a period of time [63]Carta MGP, Angermeyer MM. Counseling in primary care improves depression and quality of life. Clinical Practice and Epidemiology in Mental Health 2012; 8: 152-157.

In a group of people who had counselling in addition to the usual care provided by their GP, their depression score improved by 12 points on a 63 point scale after six months. This compared with an improvement of 7 points in those who had usual therapy without counselling. The change meant that people who had counselling no longer had mild or moderate depression. Those who didn’t have counselling still had mild depression [63]Carta MGP, Angermeyer MM. Counseling in primary care improves depression and quality of life. Clinical Practice and Epidemiology in Mental Health 2012; 8: 152-157.

In a large number of people with depression, those who had talking therapies in a group setting (counselling, CBT, IPT, psychodynamic or supportive therapy) had a bigger improvement in their symptoms of depression after treatment than those who had care usually provided by their GP. Three months later, depression symptoms were similar, whether or not people had had talking therapies [65]Huntley A. L., Araya R, Salisbury C et al. Group psychological therapies for depression in the community: systematic review and meta-analysis. British Journal of Psychiatry 2012; 200: 184-190.

Combining talking therapy with antidepressant medication is slightly more effective in treating depression than having talking therapy alone. The difference is small, especially when CBT is used, and it is hard to know whether it would make a noticeable difference to someone with depression [66]Cuijpers P, Andersson Psychological treatment of depression: Results of a series of meta-analyses Nordic Journal of Psychiatry 2011; 65: 354-364.

What are the main effects of psychological therapies for depression on what you can do and how you feel?

By improving your symptoms of depression, talking therapies can support you as you become more involved in everyday activities again – going to work, taking part in family and leisure activities, seeing friends, and getting more enjoyment out of life.

In some very large groups of people, talking therapies improved people’s quality of life [67]Ishak W. W. H. and Balayan, K. Bolotaulo. The impact of psychotherapy, pharmacotherapy, and their combination on quality of life in depression. Harvard Review of Psychiatry 2011; 19: 277-289. In some groups, having antidepressant treatment as well as talking therapy improved quality of life more than talking therapy on its own.

In a group of people who had counselling in addition to the usual care provided by their GP, their quality of life score improved by 10 points on a 100-point scale after six months. This compared to an improvement of less than 2 points in those who had usual therapy without counselling. [63]Carta MGP, Angermeyer MM. Counseling in primary care improves depression and quality of life. Clinical Practice and Epidemiology in Mental Health 2012; 8: 152-157.

A group of people who had intensive CBT in hospital for six weeks had improved psychological wellbeing, social relationships, housing, work and education and leisure activities, and they felt more satisfied with life in general. They didn’t have any improvement in their marital relations, general health or finances [69]Lenz G, Demal U. Quality of life in depression and anxiety disorders: an exploratory follow-up study after intensive inpatient cognitive behaviour therapy. Psychopathology 2000;33:297–302.

In a group of people who had IPT, antidepressant treatment, or usual care from their GP, those who had IPT had greater improvement in their quality of life than those who had usual care [70]Lave JR, Frank RG, Schulberg HC, Kamlet MS. Cost effectiveness of treatments for major depression in primary care practice. Arch Gen Psychiatry 1998;55:645–51. Those who had antidepressant treatment had a slightly bigger improvement than those who had IPT.

Will talking therapies cause any unwanted effects?

Most talking therapies don’t have unwanted effects.

At first, you may find it difficult to talk about your feelings and the problems that may be making you depressed. This may get easier as you get to know your therapist. They are trained to listen to and support people with problems like yours.

You may not find it easy to make changes to the way you think and feel. Again, your therapist is trained to support you as you make changes that can improve your symptoms of depression.

How might using talking therapies for depression affect my life?

As talking therapies aim to improve your symptoms and enable you to take part in the everyday activities you enjoyed before you became depressed, they may have quite a big impact on your life.

Some effects will depend on the kinds of things you decide to change in order to treat your depression. If you find that lifestyle changes, such as eating more healthily, drinking less alcohol, giving up smoking, drugs or gambling, or changing the way you spend your money are important for your recovery, these may affect how you shop, socialise and the places you go.

How likely is depression to come back after talking therapies?

About half of people who respond to CBT for depression may have a recurrence within a year after they stop treatment [71]Vittengl JR, Clark LA, Jarrett RB et al.
Moderators of Continuation Phase Cognitive Therapy’s Effects on Relapse, Recurrence, Remission, and Recovery from Depression. Behav Res Ther. 2010 June; 48(6): 449–458.

Depression is more likely to come back after CBT in younger people with depression, those with less energy and enthusiasm, and in those who had a short term worsening of their depression in the later stages of their CBT.

In a group of people with moderate to severe depression, 39 out of 100 people had relapsed in the first year after they responded to CBT treatment and 24 out of 100 in the second year after treatment [72]Dobson KS, Hollon SD, Dimidjian S et al. Randomized Trial of Behavioral Activation, Cognitive Therapy, and Antidepressant Medication in the Prevention of Relapse and Recurrence in Major Depression J Consult Clin Psychol. 2008 June; 76(3): 468–477.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT MEDICATION

What medicines are used to treat depression?

Several different types of medicine, called antidepressants, are used to treat depression, on their own or with other treatments. They aim to improve your symptoms and enable you to take part in the everyday activities you enjoyed before you became depressed. This is sometimes called improving the quality of your life. It includes feeling well enough to go to work or college, looking after your family and home, getting involved in social and leisure activities and enjoying being with family and friends.

Medicines for depression aim to correct the imbalance in chemicals in the brain, called neurotransmitters, which are known to play a part in causing depression [73]Depression Alliance. http://www.depressionalliance.org/help-and-information/treatments-for-depression.php.

There are three main types of antidepressant:

  • Tricyclics such as amitryptiline, clomipramine, dosulepin, doxepin, imipramine, lofepramine, nortriptyline and trimipramine [74]BNF Jan 2013. These are older antidepressants which are less commonly used now.
  • SSRIs (selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors) such as citalopram, escitalopram, fluoxetine, fluvoxamine, paroxetine and sertraline [74]BNF Jan 2013. These are the most commonly used drugs to treat depression, and health advisors recommend them as first choice of antidepressant [1]NICE. Depression. The treatment and management of depression in adults, CG 90
  • SNRIs (serotonin and noradrenaline reuptake inhibitors) such as duloxetine and venlafaxine [74]BNF Jan 2013. These are generally used for more severe depression.
  • MAOIs (monoamine oxidase inhibitors) such as phenelzine, isocarboxazid, tranylcypromine, moclobemide [74]BNF Jan 2013. These are older drugs and tend to be used only if other antidepressant have not worked, as people need a special diet when they take them [73]Depression Alliance. http://www.depressionalliance.org/help-and-information/treatments-for-depression.php
  • NaSSAs (noradrenaline and specific serotonergic antidepressant), such as mirtazapine

Benzodiazepines, such as diazepam, are not recommended for the treatment of depression.

Who can take medicines for depression?

Your doctor may offer you an antidepressant medicine if [1]NICE. Depression. The treatment and management of depression in adults, CG 90:

  • You’ve had moderate or severe depression in the past
  • You’ve had a few symptoms of depression (sub-threshold depression) for a long time (typically at least two years)
  • You have mild depression that hasn’t got better with other treatments, eg: active monitoring/watchful waiting, self help treatments, talking therapies.

If you see no improvement after three or four weeks, your GP may suggest seeing you more often and/or may:

  • Increase the dose of your antidepressant drug
  • Switch you to a different antidepressant drug
  • Suggest you also have a course of talking therapy
What are the main effects of drug treatment on depression?

Antidepressants can be effective in relieving the symptoms of mild to moderate depression and making people feel better. They may be more effective:

  • In people with severe depression than in those with mild to moderate depression [81]Fournier, J. C., DeRubeis, R. J., Hollon, S. D et al, Antidepressant drug effects and depression severity: a patient-level meta-analysis. JAMA 2010; 303: 47-53.
  • When they are used in combination with talking therapies. [82]Khan, A. Faucett. A systematic review of comparative efficacy of treatments and controls for depression. PLoS ONE 2012; 7.

In a group of over 10,000 people with mild, moderate or severe depression, about 51 in 100 people who took antidepressants got a reduction in their symptoms, compared to about 38 in 100 who took a dummy pill (placebo) [82]Khan, A. Faucett. A systematic review of comparative efficacy of treatments and controls for depression. PLoS ONE 2012; 7.

Analysis of a sub-group of these people showed that 52 in 100 people who took an antidepressant and had talking therapies had a reduction in symptoms, compared to 47 in 100 who just took antidepressants and 47 in 100 who had talking therapies alone [82]Khan, A. Faucett. A systematic review of comparative efficacy of treatments and controls for depression. PLoS ONE 2012; 7.

Amongst a group of over 700 people with mild, moderate and severe depression, the effectiveness of antidepressants increased with the severity of depression. For one person to benefit from treatment, 16 people with mild to moderate depression would need to take antidepressants, compared to 11 people with severe depression and 4 people with very severe depression [81]Fournier, J. C., DeRubeis, R. J., Hollon, S. D et al, Antidepressant drug effects and depression severity: a patient-level meta-analysis. JAMA 2010; 303: 47-53.

In a group of nearly 500 people with mild depression, 41 in 100 people who took antidepressants improved, compared to 38 in 100 who took a placebo. This difference was not significant when statistical tests were done [86]Hegerl U, Schonknecht P, Mergl R Are antidepressants useful in the treatment of minor depression: a critical update of the current literature. Current Opinion in Psychiatry 2012; 25: 1-6.

In a large group of people with sub-threshold depression, people responded just as well to a placebo as to antidepressants [87]Barbui, C. Cipriani. Efficacy of antidepressants and benzodiazepines in minor depression: Systematic review and meta-analysis. British Journal of Psychiatry 2011; 198: 11-16.

We couldn’t find any evidence to show that benzodiazepines are effective in treating depression [88]Barbui, C. Cipriani. Efficacy of antidepressants and benzodiazepines in minor depression: Systematic review and meta-analysis. British Journal of Psychiatry 2011; 198: 11-16.

What are the main effects of drugs for depression on what you can do and how you feel?

In some very large groups of people with mild, moderate and severe depression, antidepressant drugs improved people’s quality of life [67]Ishak W. W. H. and Balayan, K. Bolotaulo. The impact of psychotherapy, pharmacotherapy, and their combination on quality of life in depression. Harvard Review of Psychiatry 2011; 19: 277-289. In some groups, having talking therapy as well as antidepressants improved quality of life more than taking antidepressants on their own.

The effects of antidepressants on quality of life may be larger for people with more severe depression than for those with mild depression.

A group of people with moderate to severe depression who took an antidepressant needed less time off work, performed better at work, and enjoyed family and leisure activities more than before they started treatment [90]Fabian Z, Karányi Z, Szádóczky E. The impact of antidepressive therapy on work productivity and life satisfaction--a one-year follow-up study. Neuropsychopharmacol Hung. 2005 Sep;7(3):146-56.

In another group of people with mild depression, taking an antidepressant had no effect on physical or psychological wellbeing [91]Hermens ML, van Hout HP, Terluin B, et al. Clinical effectiveness of usual care with or without antidepressant medication for primary care patients with minor or mild-major depression: a randomized equivalence trial. BMC Med 2007;7:36.

Will taking drugs for depression cause any unwanted effects?

All types of antidepressants cause side effects. They are usually mild and many of them wear off when you have been taking them for a few weeks and your body gets used to them [92]Royal College of Psychiatrists. Antidepressants. http://www.rcpsych.ac.uk/expertadvice/treatments/antidepressants.aspx.

The newer antidepressants, such as the SSRIs, tend to have fewer side effects than the older ones, such as the tricyclics, and they are not so dangerous if people take too much of them [92]Royal College of Psychiatrists. Antidepressants. http://www.rcpsych.ac.uk/expertadvice/treatments/antidepressants.aspx.

Antidepressants are not addictive like tranquillisers, alcohol and nicotine. You don’t need to keep increasing the dose to get the same effect, or get cravings if you stop taking them.

When you stop taking SSRIs or SNRIs, you may get withdrawal effects, such as stomach upsets, flu-like symptoms, anxiety, dizziness, vivid dreams or nightmares, sensations like electric shocks. [92]Royal College of Psychiatrists. Antidepressants. http://www.rcpsych.ac.uk/expertadvice/treatments/antidepressants.aspx.

Up to 33 in 100 people who stop SSRIs or SNRIs get withdrawal symptoms, some of these effects, and they are usually mild [92]Royal College of Psychiatrists. Antidepressants. http://www.rcpsych.ac.uk/expertadvice/treatments/antidepressants.aspx.

Side effects of antidepressants include: [92]Royal College of Psychiatrists. Antidepressants. http://www.rcpsych.ac.uk/expertadvice/treatments/antidepressants.aspx.

Tricyclics: dry mouth, a slight tremor, fast heartbeat, constipation, sleepiness, and weight gain. In older people, they may cause confusion, slowness in starting and stopping when passing water, faintness through low blood pressure, and falls. Men may find it difficult to get or keep erection, or have delayed ejaculation. Tricyclic antidepressants are dangerous in overdose.

SSRIs: nausea and anxiety, especially in the first few weeks, and sexual problems. Indigestion, which may be prevented by taking them with food, and, rarely, aggression.
SNRIs: similar to SSRIs. Venlaxine may increase blood pressure and you should not use it if you have a serious heart problem.

MAOIs: dangerously high blood pressure if eaten with foods containing tyramine.

NASSAs: similar to SSRIs, they cause drowsiness, and weight gain, but are less likely to cause sexual problems than SSRIs..

This may seem a long list. Most side effects are mild, and wear off. The more serious ones - problems with urinating, difficulty in remembering, falls, confusion - are uncommon in healthy, younger or middle-aged people. [92]Royal College of Psychiatrists. Antidepressants. http://www.rcpsych.ac.uk/expertadvice/treatments/antidepressants.aspx.

Sometimes, people with depression think of harming themselves in some way. People with depression under the age of 18 may feel more suicidal if they take SSRIs than if they don’t. They shouldn’t take this type of antidepressant. [92]Royal College of Psychiatrists. Antidepressants. http://www.rcpsych.ac.uk/expertadvice/treatments/antidepressants.aspx.

In a large group of people with mild, moderate or severe depression, about 3 in 1000 of those taking paroxetine felt suicidal compared to fewer than 1 in 1000 of those taking a placebo. The increase of suicidal thoughts with paroxetine was in people aged 18-24, not in older people [99]Carpenter, D. J. F. Meta-analysis of efficacy and treatment-emergent suicidality in adults by psychiatric indication and age subgroup following initiation of paroxetine therapy: A complete set of randomized placebo-controlled trials. Journal of Clinical Psychiatry 2011; 72: 1503-1514.

How might taking drug treatments for depression affect my life?

As antidepressants aim to improve your symptoms and enable you to take part in the everyday activities you enjoyed before you became depressed, they may have quite a big impact on your life.
The side effects of antidepressants may affect you as well, especially in the first few weeks after you start treatment, before your body has got used to them.

If you are taking an antidepressant that makes you feely sleepy or anxious, you may need to avoid driving or using equipment that could hurt someone if you lost control.

If you are using an antidepressant that makes you feel sick, you may be able to relieve this by taking it with food.

If you are taking an antidepressant that makes you put on weight, you may find need to eat carefully and avoid fatty or sugary foods, and take more exercise.

If you are taking an antidepressant, it’s advisable to avoid drinking alcohol.

How likely is depression to come back after medication?

We don’t have a lot of information about relapse rates in people who take antidepressant medicines to treat their depression.

In a group of people with moderate to severe depression who took antidepressants, 59 out of 100 people relapsed in the first year after stopping treatment. Amongst those who continued taking antidepressants, 53 out of 100 people relapsed in the first year after their initial course of treatment [72]Dobson KS, Hollon SD, Dimidjian S et al. Randomized Trial of Behavioral Activation, Cognitive Therapy, and Antidepressant Medication in the Prevention of Relapse and Recurrence in Major Depression J Consult Clin Psychol. 2008 June; 76(3): 468–477.
