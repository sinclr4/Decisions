STEP 5: MY DECISION

Our Decision Support telephone service is here to assist and guide you through the Shared Decision Making process.

The Decision Support team is made of professional Health Coaches who are clinicians, commonly nurses, who have undertaken additional training to become a Health Coach.

The Health Coaches will assist you by:

  • Providing you with information
  • Helping you to understand your condition
  • Recognise what is important to you regarding the outcome of treatment
  • Identifying potential solutions
  • Encourage you in discussing options with your family
  • Transferring skills which will assist you in using the information and resources available to you
  • Support you in building confidence in discussing your choice with your doctor
  • You may find that this can be achieved in one telephone call with a Health Coach however; if further calls are required to support you in reaching your decision the Health Coach will schedule these with you.

Important Things To Note

  • A Health Coach does not know which treatment option is best for you.
  • The Health Coach does not have information about your medical history.
  • You will still need to talk over your decision and reasons for your decision with your doctor or nurse.

MORE INFORMATION ABOUT DIABETES: IMPROVING CONTROL

What is poorly controlled Type 2 diabetes?

Type 2 diabetes is poorly controlled when the treatment a person is taking is not keeping their blood glucose down to safe levels.

Glucose control is indicated by a blood test called an HbA1c level. An HbA1c level over 58mmol/mol (7.5%) may increase the chance of getting complications in future.

If a person's blood glucose levels remain high they may have a higher chance of getting serious health problems in future. These include damage to the eyes, nerves or kidneys, as well as heart disease and stroke.

What are the symptoms of poorly controlled Type 2 diabetes?

Most people with poorly controlled Type 2 diabetes don’t get symptoms, but some do.

The main symptoms of high blood glucose levels are being thirsty, needing to urinate often, feeling tired and have lots of infections, such as thrush. [1]NHS Choices. Hyperglycaemia – symptoms, 2012

How do I know I have poorly controlled Type 2 diabetes?

People with Type 2 diabetes have their blood glucose levels measured every 3-6 months using a test called HbA1C.

This shows how much glucose (sugar) has ‘stuck’ to the red blood cells in the bloodstream and other important parts of the body, such as the eyes, kidneys, nerves and blood vessels.

HbA1c looks at blood glucose levels over the last 8-12 weeks and so is a good measure of overall diabetes control.

People with an HbA1c between 48 and 58mmol/mol have the lowest risk of getting complications in future. These numbers are sometimes given as percentages, when the safest level is between 6.5 and 7.5 per cent.

Levels over 58mmol/mol (7.5%) may increase the chance of getting complications in future. These include damage to the eyes, nerves or kidneys, as well as heart disease and stroke.

Levels under 48mmol/mol (6.5%), which can happen with some medications, may increase the chance of having low blood sugars, known as hypoglycaemic episodes, or hypos. When this happens a person may feel shaky, weak and hungry. A really severe hypo can cause drowsiness and confusion, or even loss of consciousness. Fortunately this is rare.

People with Type 2 diabetes usually agree their own safest HbA1c level with their diabetes care team. Usually this will be between 48 and 58mmol/mol (6.5-7.5%). [2]NICE Quality standard for diabetes in adults, March 2011

What will happen if my diabetes control is poor?

People with Type 2 diabetes have a higher chance of getting other serious health problems in the long term. Having poor control makes these complications more likely.

The main health problems linked with diabetes are [3]NICE Clinical Guideline 66,Type 2 Diabetes, May 2008:

  • Eye damage that can lead to blindness
  • Kidney damage that can be severe enough to need dialysis or a transplant
  • Damage to the neurological system, especially nerves in the legs and feet. In really bad cases this can lead to amputation
  • Heart disease, including angina and heart attacks
  • Poor blood flow to other parts of the body, particularly the legs. This is called peripheral artery disease. In really bad cases it is sometimes necessary to have a leg or foot amputated
  • Stroke, when blood flow to the brain is blocked.

It is common for people to experience a combination of these health problems at the same time. Higher glucose levels make serious health problems more likely. The chance of dying from them is much higher with HbA1c levels over 64-75mmol/mol (8-9%) [4]Yudkin JS, Richter B, Gale EAM. Intensified glucose lowering in type 2 diabetes: time for a reappraisal. Diabetologia 2010; 53: 2079-85.

What will happen if I improve my control?

Keeping blood glucose down to safe levels makes people less likely to get long term health problems like damage to the eyes, kidneys or nerves, as well as heart disease and stroke.

Reducing HbA1c levels by just 10mmol/mol (1%) cuts the chance of getting these problems by 21 per cent (just over a fifth). [5]Stratton IM, Adler AI, Neil HAW, et al. Association of glycaemia with macovascular and microvascular complications of type 2 diabetes (UKPDS 35): prospective observational study. BMJ 2000; 321:405-12 But bringing HbA1c levels down to below 53mmol/mol (7%) may not give any additional benefit and can make hypos more likely. [6]Action to Control Cardiovascular Risk in Diabetes Study Group. Effects of intensive glucose lowering in type 2 diabetes. N Engl J Med 2008;358:2549-59] ADVANCE Collaborative Group. Intensive blood glucose control and vascular outcomes in patients with type 2 diabetes. N Engl J Med 2008; 358: 2560-72 [7]Duckworth W, Albaira C, Moritz T, et al. Glucose control and vascular complications in veterans with type 2 diabetes. N Engl J Med 2008;360:129-39

People with Type 2 diabetes may live longer if they can keep their HbA1c levels to around 58mmol/mol, or 7.5%. Blood glucose levels that are lower or higher than this may reduce life expectancy. [8]Currie CJ, Peters JR, Tynan A. et al. Survival as a function of HbA1c in people with type 2 diabetes: a retrospective cohort study. Lancet 2010;375:481-89

Better diabetes control may improve people’s quality of life in the long term by reducing the chance of complications and other health problems.

How can I improve my diabetes control with medication?

People with Type 2 diabetes can often control their blood glucose levels at first through lifestyle changes like healthy diet and exercise.

Over the years most people find they need more treatments to keep their blood glucose under control. [9]NICE short clinical guideline 87. Type 2 diabetes: newer agents. Issued May 2009

An important way to improve diabetes control is to take medication regularly, at the right times and in the right doses. Many people with Type 2 diabetes don’t do this and so can’t keep good control.

 

How can I improve my diabetes control by making lifestyle changes?

There are some changes people can make to their lifestyle that can improve their diabetes control or reduce the chance of getting complications, or both. [10]Scottish Intercollegiate Guidelines Network. Management of Diabetes, March 2010 [11]International Diabetes Federation. Global Guideline for type 2 diabetes, 2012

  • Becoming more physically active. Taking regular exercise can improve blood glucose control, whether or not people lose weight.[12]Thomas D, Elliott EJ, Naughton GA. Exercise for type 2 diabetes mellitus. Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews 2006, Issue 3. Art. No.: CD002968 Aerobic exercise, such as brisk walking or swimming, and resistance exercise, such as weight training are both helpful, especially in combination. Being active every 2-3 days is best for diabetes control. [10]Scottish Intercollegiate Guidelines Network. Management of Diabetes, March 2010 [11]International Diabetes Federation. Global Guideline for type 2 diabetes, 2012 But some physical activity is better than none and the more people do, the greater the benefit.
  • Reducing weight. Most people with type 2 diabetes are overweight, which makes them even more likely to get heart disease. Losing weight can help with glucose control as well as reducing the chance of heart disease. People can lose weight through a combination of diet and exercise. VLCD (very low calorie diet) meal replacements can be helpful with support from the diabetes team. Special weight loss drugs or surgery are sometimes recommended for very overweight people.
  • Improving diet. Eating healthily can help people lose weight and may also improve their diabetes control. 

People can do this by: [15]Diabetes UK. Evidence-based nutrition guidelines for the prevention and management of diabetes. May 2011

  • Taking in fewer calories overall, such as by cutting down on high-calorie foods and reducing portion sizes 
  • Drinking less alcohol. Alcohol is high in calories and can increase people's chance of getting future health problems
  • Eating less fat, particularly saturated (animal) fat
  • Eating fewer carbohydrate foods
  • Choosing carbohydrates with a low rather than a high glycaemic index (GI). Foods with a high GI tend to release glucose more rapidly into the bloodstream. They include white bread, rice and potatoes. Low GI foods include beans, seeds, grains and most vegetables.

Mediterranean-style diets that are high in fruit and vegetables, whole grains, legumes and nuts and low in butter and red meat have been shown to help with glucose control. [16]Esposito K, Maiorino MI, Di Palo C, et al. Adherence to a Mediterranean diet and glycaemic control in Type 2 diabetes. Diabet Med 2009 Sep;26(9):900-7

Lifestyle change can be hard, but every effort counts and it can be helpful to start with small changes and build up. Support is available from diabetes teams.

What else can I do to avoid long term health problems?

Improving diabetes control can help reduce the chance of complications and other health problems in the future.

For cigarette smokers, trying to give up can also make a difference. Having Type 2 diabetes means a person is more likely to get heart disease or stroke in future. Smoking increases that chance and giving up reduces it.

It may also be necessary to take extra medication to prevent future health problems. This may include:

  • Drugs to reduce blood pressure. High blood pressure increases the chance of getting heart disease, stroke, eye and kidney damage. Taking one or more drugs to reduce blood pressure can reduce the risk of heart disease by 20-25% and stroke by 30-45%. [11]International Diabetes Federation. Global Guideline for type 2 diabetes, 2012
  • Drugs to reduce blood fats. Having high levels of cholesterol and triglyceride fats in the blood makes people more likely to get heart disease. Statins can reduce cholesterol levels and drugs called fibrates can keep triglycerides down. [3]NICE Clinical Guideline 66,Type 2 Diabetes, May 2008
  • Blood-thinning drugs. Low doses of aspirin (75mg a day) may be recommended for people thought to have a high chance of heart disease or stroke. A drug called clopidogrel can be used instead for people who can’t take aspirin. [3]NICE Clinical Guideline 66,Type 2 Diabetes, May 2008

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT NO CHANGE

What is it?

No change means carrying on as usual. There may be good reasons why trying to improve control isn't right at the moment. 

Who can choose it?

Anyone can opt for no change. It is up to the person to decide whether to make further changes to their lifestyle or medication.

How effective is it?

Making no further changes to lifestyle or medication will not improve a person's diabetes control and may make it worse.

Diabetes is a ‘progressive’ disease that tends to get worse with time. It can’t be cured but it can be controlled by lifestyle changes or treatments that keep blood glucose down to safe levels.

Having poor control means a person's blood glucose levels are higher than is healthy for them. If they don’t change what they are doing their diabetes may get worse faster.

People with Type 2 diabetes have a higher chance of getting other health problems in the long term. The main ones are damage to the eyes, kidneys or neurological system, as well as heart disease, stroke and peripheral vascular disease.

The higher your HbA1c, the more likely a person is to get one or more of these complications. People with HbA1c scores over 86mmol/mol (10%) are three times more likely to get complications than people with scores of under 42mmol/mol 6%. [5]Stratton IM, Adler AI, Neil HAW, et al. Association of glycaemia with macovascular and microvascular complications of type 2 diabetes (UKPDS 35): prospective observational study. BMJ 2000; 321:405-12

Keeping blood glucose levels down reduces a person's chances of getting these complications. For every 1% (10mmol/mol) reduction in HbA1c there is a 21% reduction in the chance of getting a complication.

That’s an average. The chance of getting eye, kidney and nerve problems is reduced by 37% (more than a third) with every 1% (10mmol/mol) reduction in HbA1c. The chance of getting a heart attack is reduced by 14% with every 1% (10mmol/mol) reduction in HbA1c.

People with Type 2 diabetes can often control their blood glucose levels at first by changing their diet and being more physically active. Over time this usually stops working and most people need to take one or more glucose-lowering drugs to keep good control. Eventually this may include insulin injections. [9]NICE short clinical guideline 87. Type 2 diabetes: newer agents. Issued May 2009

What are the main effects on what you can do and how you feel?

Making no further changes to lifestyle or medication will not affect what a person can do.

It may affect how a person feels if the symptoms of high blood glucose levels come back or get worse.

The main symptoms are [1]NHS Choices. Hyperglycaemia – symptoms, 2012:

  • Being thirsty
  • Having a dry mouth
  • Needing to urinate often
  • Feeling very tired
  • Having lots of infections, such as thrush.

If control continues to be poor over a long period of time a person may notice:

  • Weight loss as the body removes excess glucose through the urine
  • Blurred vision caused by dryness in the eyes.

Getting long term health problems like eye, kidney or neurological problems, heart disease or stroke, will certainly affect how a person feels and what they can do.

What are the main side effects?

There are no side effects to making no further improvements to a person's lifestyle or medication.

How might it affect your life?

Making no further changes to a person's lifestyle or medication may not affect their life in the short term.

A person may find it more difficult to arrange insurance if their diabetes is not well controlled. If a person is on insulin and their control is poor, they may find it difficult to keep their full driving licence.

In the longer term a person may be more likely to get other health problems that will affect their life and how they feel.

People with Type 2 diabetes who have had heart attacks, angina, heart failure or stroke in the last year say they have worse general health, more problems with mobility and usual activities and less energy than those who haven’t had these complications. [23]UK Prospective Diabetes Study Group. Quality of life in type 2 diabetic patients is affected by complications but not by intensive policies to improve blood glucose or blood pressure control. Diabetes Care 1999 Jul;22(7):1125-36

People with Type 2 diabetes who have had eye damage or kidney failure in the last year say they have more tension and mood disturbance than those who haven’t had these complications.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT FURTHER IMPROVEMENTS TO CURRENT LIFESTYLE AND MEDICATIONS

What is it?

This means eating more healthily, being more physically active and losing weight if a person needs to. It may also mean:

  • Being more careful to take medication as prescribed
  • Changing the dose or timing of a current medication
  • Talking to a doctor about an alternative treatment that might suit a person better.

People can improve their diet by [24]Diabetes UK. Evidence-based nutrition guidelines for the prevention and management of diabetes, May 2011:

  • Taking in fewer calories overall
  • Eating less fat, particularly saturated (animal) fat
  • Eating fewer carbohydrate foods
  • Choosing carbohydrates with a low rather than a high glycaemic index. These include beans, seeds, grains and most vegetables.

People can increase their physical activity through: [10]Scottish Intercollegiate Guidelines Network. Management of Diabetes, March 2010 [11]International Diabetes Federation. Global Guideline for type 2 diabetes, 2012

  • Aerobic activities like brisk walking and running
  • Resistance activities like weight training
  • Being more active in everyday life, by walking rather than using the car, using stairs rather than lifts, and so on
  • Breaking up long periods of sitting with periods of standing and moving around.

Improved diet and increased activity should help people lose weight. People can also follow special weight-loss diets or take weight-loss drugs. Very obese people can benefit from a special kind of surgery called bariatric surgery. For more information see the decision aid on obesity.

Taking medication as prescribed means taking it regularly and at the times and doses set out in the prescription. A person may want to talk to their doctor about changing the dose of a current treatment or switching to an alternative.

Who can choose it?

Anyone can choose to make further improvements to their lifestyle or medication.

The more improvements a person can make, the more benefits they are likely to get. Some people may already be doing all they can to improve their current lifestyle and medications, so adding in extra medication may be their only option for improving control further.

How effective is it

Healthy eating, being more physically active and losing weight can all reduce blood glucose levels and so improve diabetes control. This may reduce the need for medication.

Low-calorie, low-fat, low glycaemic index (GI) and Mediterranean-style diets can all help with diabetes control [15]Diabetes UK. Evidence-based nutrition guidelines for the prevention and management of diabetes. May 2011 [16]Esposito K, Maiorino MI, Di Palo C, et al. Adherence to a Mediterranean diet and glycaemic control in Type 2 diabetes. Diabet Med 2009 Sep;26(9):900-7

Both aerobic and resistance activities improve diabetes control whether or not they lead to weight loss. [29]Thomas D, Elliott EJ, Naughton GA. Exercise for type 2 diabetes mellitus. The Cochrane Library, 2008 

The benefits don't last unless a person continues to be physically active regularly. People probably need to exercise at least every second or third day to keep blood glucose levels down. [10]Scottish Intercollegiate Guidelines Network. Management of Diabetes, March 2010 [11]International Diabetes Federation. Global Guideline for type 2 diabetes, 2012

Losing weight if needed can improve diabetes control. [10]Scottish Intercollegiate Guidelines Network. Management of Diabetes, March 2010 [11]International Diabetes Federation. Global Guideline for type 2 diabetes, 2012 But it is difficult to separate the effects of weight loss from the lifestyle changes that caused it.

If a person is on medication it can only control blood glucose levels properly if taken as prescribed. That means taking it regularly, without stopping, at the times and doses stated on the prescription.

There is a lot of evidence that more than half of all people with Type 2 diabetes do not take their medication exactly as prescribed. Only about a third of people taking metformin or a sulfonylurea tablet do this. [34]Clark M. Adherence to treatment in patient with Type 2 diabetes 2004. Journal of Diabetes Nursing, vol 8, no 10: 386-391 Taking medication regularly and at the times and doses set out in your prescription might mean a person can stay longer on tablets before needing to take insulin. [34]Clark M. Adherence to treatment in patient with Type 2 diabetes 2004. Journal of Diabetes Nursing, vol 8, no 10: 386-391

Changing the dose or switching to an alternative medication may slow the progression of the disease if the current one is not working well enough. [9]NICE short clinical guideline 87. Type 2 diabetes: newer agents. Issued May 2009

What are the main effects on what you can do and how you feel?

Eating healthily, being more physically active and losing weight if needed can help to improve a person's general health, physical fitness and wellbeing as well as their diabetes control.

The fitter a person is and the better they feel, the more able they should be to cope with daily life and their illness. If a person is overweight or obese, losing weight may improve their quality of life. This is particularly true for women. Obese men and women with Type 2 diabetes say they have less energy than normal-weight men and women with the disease. Obese women with Type 2 diabetes find their disease more overwhelming and difficult to handle than obese men with Type 2 diabetes. [37]Svenningsson I, Marklund B, Attvall S, et al. Type 2 diabetes: perceptions of quality of life and attitudes towards diabetes from a gender perspective. Scandinavian Journal of Caring Sciences 2011; vol 25, issue 4: 688-695

Taking medication as prescribed will improve a person's diabetes control and may make them feel better. Not taking enough medication may lead to hyperglycaemia symptoms like feeling tired and thirsty and needing to urinate often.

Taking too much medication could make a person more likely to have a ‘hypo’. Changing the dose of medication or switching to an alternative medication might make them feel better if the current dose is too low or too high or they can’t tolerate the treatment [9]NICE short clinical guideline 87. Type 2 diabetes: newer agents. Issued May 2009

What are the main side effects?

There are no side effects to having a healthier diet or to losing weight gradually.

People taking insulin with no extra carbohydrate have a higher chance of hypoglycaemia during and after exercise. [10]Scottish Intercollegiate Guidelines Network. Management of Diabetes, March 2010 [11]International Diabetes Federation. Global Guideline for type 2 diabetes, 2012 But that depends on the intensity of the exercise and blood glucose control. A short walk to the shop, for example, shouldn't be a problem. A person's diabetes team can advise about how to minimise the chance of having a hypo during exercise.

People on sulfonylurea tablets also have a higher chance of getting hypoglycaemia during exercise or for up to 24 hours afterwards. They may need to reduce the dose before planned exercise. [41]Nagi D, Gallen I. ABCD position statement on physical activity and exercise in diabetes. Pract Diab Int 2010; vol 27, no 4: 158-163

People with diabetes are more likely to get exercise-related stress fractures of the legs and feet and injuries of the arms and shoulders. [41]Nagi D, Gallen I. ABCD position statement on physical activity and exercise in diabetes. Pract Diab Int 2010; vol 27, no 4: 158-163

People with nerve damage and lack of sensitivity in their feet are more prone to foot ulcers and fractures. They are probably better off with non-weight bearing exercises such as cycling, swimming and rowing. [41]Nagi D, Gallen I. ABCD position statement on physical activity and exercise in diabetes. Pract Diab Int 2010; vol 27, no 4: 158-163

People with damage to the nerves controlling bodily functions (autonomic neuropathy) may have problems with blood pressure and fluid loss during and after exercise. They should probably stick to gentle and short exercise sessions. [41]Nagi D, Gallen I. ABCD position statement on physical activity and exercise in diabetes. Pract Diab Int 2010; vol 27, no 4: 158-163

No medications are harmless and a person may find that changing dose or switching to a new treatment causes side effects.

How might it affect your life?

Lifestyle changes may involve:

  • planning food shopping and menus
  • buying healthy foods like fish, fruit and vegetables
  • making time for physical activity and committing to doing it regularly
  • Following a weight-loss programme, which takes planning, commitment and control

Taking medication as prescribed means a person has to be very organised and careful. A person needs to: [34]Clark M. Adherence to treatment in patient with Type 2 diabetes 2004. Journal of Diabetes Nursing, vol 8, no 10: 386-391

  • Remember to pick up their prescription
  • Remember when to take each medicine
  • Be careful not to take more or less than the prescribed dose
  • Always carry medications when travelling
  • Take the treatment continuously, without interruption

Changing dose or switching to an alternative treatment will mean more visits to a doctor to check that the treatment is working and that the side effects are tolerable. There may be some trial and error before a person finds the treatment that is right for them.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT ADDING IN EXTRA MEDICATION

What is it?

This means taking another treatment as well as rather than instead of a treatment a person is already taking.

It could mean:

  • Adding in a second treatment if currently taking just one or
  • Adding in a third treatment if taking two.

The new treatment could be tablets or injections. (If you are already on two or more treatments, see the decision aid on 'Diabetes: additional treatments to improve control'.)

Who can choose it?

Anyone with poor diabetes control can talk to their doctor about whether it is time to take on an additional treatment.

The possible treatments to consider will depend on what a person is already taking and what other treatments and medical problems they have.

Some treatments, such as insulin and GLP-1 analogues, are only used after at least two tablet treatments have been tried. If a person is on two diabetes treatments and would like to know more about the additional treatments available, see the decision aid on 'Diabetes: additional treatments to improve control'. 

How effective is it?

Type 2 diabetes is what’s called a progressive disease. That means it tends to get worse over time. It can’t be cured but it can be controlled. As it progresses, more treatments may be needed to keep it under control. [9]NICE short clinical guideline 87. Type 2 diabetes: newer agents. Issued May 2009

The point of adding in extra medication is to keep a person's HbA1c down to safe levels. This should slow the progression of the disease and reduce the chance of getting complications like kidney and eye damage, as well as heart disease and stroke.

Most people with Type 2 diabetes start with lifestyle changes, like healthy diet and exercise. When this is no longer enough to keep good control of diabetes, they need to start taking glucose-lowering tablets. Usually people start with one sort of tablet and may go on to take two or three.

Eventually most people with type 2 diabetes need to take insulin to keep their glucose levels under control.

What are the main effects on what you can do and how you feel?

Adding in extra treatments shouldn’t make a big difference to what a person can do.

If a person goes on to insulin they will need to think more carefully about what and when to eat. For example they will need to take extra carbohydrate before unplanned exercise to avoid hypoglycaemia. [10]Scottish Intercollegiate Guidelines Network. Management of Diabetes, March 2010 [11]International Diabetes Federation. Global Guideline for type 2 diabetes, 2012 Adding on another tablet treatment may not affect how a person feels.

People taking two or more different tablets for Type 2 diabetes don’t say they have a worse quality of life than people taking just one. [49]Tamir O, Waitsten J, Raz I, et al. Quality of life and patient-perceived difficulties in the treatment of type 2 diabetes. Rev Diabet Stud 2012 Spring; 9 (1): 46-54 Better controlled patients on two types of tablets say they find their diabetes treatment less difficult than poorly controlled patients on one type of tablet. Better controlled patients on three different tablets have the same quality of life as poorly controlled patients on two different tablets.

Going on to insulin may affect how a person feels. People with Type 2 diabetes who have insulin injections say they have a lower quality of life than those taking tablets.

What are the main side effects?

All medications have side effects. These vary from treatment to treatment.

Possible side effects are listed in in the Patient Information Leaflet inside the medicine box. A person may get some or none of these. The more medications a person is taking the more likely they are to have one or more side effects.

The most common side effects of diabetes medications are: [10]Scottish Intercollegiate Guidelines Network. Management of Diabetes, March 2010 [11]International Diabetes Federation. Global Guideline for type 2 diabetes, 2012

  • Hypoglycaemia, with insulin or sulphonylurea tablets
  • Weight gain, with insulin or pioglitazone
  • Abdominal discomfort, including diarrhoea and nausea, with metformin or GLP-1 analogues

Some diabetes medications have more serious side effects: [52]MHRA information

  • Pioglitazone tablets carry a small increased chance of bladder cancer and bone fractures
  • Pioglitazone also increases the chance of heart failure when given with insulin
  • GLP-1 analogues may cause an increased risk of pancreatitis in people who have either had it already, or are already at risk of the condition
  • Metformin can, very rarely, lead to lactic acidosis, a serious condition when the blood becomes too acidic. This is most likely to happen in people with advanced kidney disease.
How might it affect your life?

Adding in an extra tablet treatment will mean more visits to the doctor or diabetes nurse to check that:

  • A person understands how to use the treatment
  • There are no serious side effects
  • The treatment is having an impact on blood glucose levels.

There may be some trial and error before a person finds the combination of treatments and the doses that are right for them.

If a person adds in insulin or another injectable treatment they will need to be educated in how to use it properly. They will also have to learn how to adjust their diet to fit in with the effects of treatment.

If a person drives a car or motorcycle and adds insulin to their existing medication they will need to notify the Driver Vehicle and Licensing Agency (DVLA). Bus, coach or lorry drivers have to notify the DVLA whatever diabetes treatment they are taking. For more information see Gov.UK at www.gov.uk/diabetes-driving.
