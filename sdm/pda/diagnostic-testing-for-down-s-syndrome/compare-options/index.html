Our Decision Support telephone service is here to assist and guide you through the Shared Decision Making process.

The Decision Support team is made of professional Health Coaches who are clinicians, commonly nurses, who have undertaken additional training to become a Health Coach.

The Health Coaches will assist you by:

  • Providing you with information
  • Helping you to understand your condition
  • Recognise what is important to you regarding the outcome of treatment
  • Identifying potential solutions
  • Encourage you in discussing options with your family
  • Transferring skills which will assist you in using the information and resources available to you
  • Support you in building confidence in discussing your choice with your doctor
  • You may find that this can be achieved in one telephone call with a Health Coach however; if further calls are required to support you in reaching your decision the Health Coach will schedule these with you.

Important Things To Note

  • A Health Coach does not know which treatment option is best for you.
  • The Health Coach does not have information about your medical history.
  • You will still need to talk over your decision and reasons for your decision with your doctor or nurse.

MORE INFORMATION ABOUT DIAGNOSTIC TESTING FOR DOWN'S SYNDROME

Why have I been offered a diagnostic test for Down’s syndrome?

People are offered a diagnostic test if they received a screening test result of 1 in 150 chance or more of having a baby with Down's syndrome.

All women are now offered a screening test for Down's syndrome in pregnancy. It is entirely women’s choice whether or not they have this test. The particular test may vary but it will provide an individual risk for the pregnancy taking into account other factors such as age and any previous history of a baby with Down's syndrome.

All women who have a higher chance of a Down’s syndrome pregnancy can choose to have a diagnostic test to know for certain if their baby has Down’s syndrome, or they can choose not to have a test knowing their likelihood of having a baby with Down’s syndrome. They should be offered support from a trained professional to explain the results of their screening test and the risks and benefits of diagnostic procedures.

Women who decide to have a diagnostic test may get a negative result that tells them their baby does not have Down’s syndrome or a positive result that tells them their baby has Down’s syndrome.

Women who have a positive diagnostic test result can decide to continue their pregnancy knowing their baby has Down’s syndrome or can decide to terminate their pregnancy.

What is Down’s syndrome?

People with Down’s syndrome are born with a learning disability. This means it takes children longer to process information and learn new skills like learning to crawl, walk, speak and read. These learning difficulties are permanent. They cannot be cured but there are supportive therapies which enable many children with Down’s syndrome to achieve their potential and lead active, healthy lives. Good healthcare, taking positive action in the first years, access to support in education and good parenting skills are all important in these children’s development.

Down’s syndrome is the most common cause of learning disability. It occurs in 1 in 700 pregnancies.

Most people with Down’s syndrome have mild to moderate learning disabilities. [1]NHS Choices. Down’s syndrome. http://www.nhs.uk/Conditions/Downs-syndrome/

Children with a mild learning disability develop fairly normal language. Most children with Down’s syndrome are sociable and engaging, enjoying the same activities as other children. They usually go to mainstream schools. 

Children with a moderate learning disability may have more problems with language and may need more support for longer in developing skills of daily living (such as washing and dressing). 

Some children with Down’s syndrome have more severe learning disabilities.  This may mean, for example, that they do not use much spoken language. Or they may need support of daily activities such as washing. [2]Department of Work and Pensions. Learning Disability. What are the effects and signs? It is not possible to determine the extent of any learning disability from the diagnostic test done during pregnancy.

However there is no such thing as a typical person with Down’s syndrome. People born with Down’s syndrome develop differently from each other and have different life experiences.

What causes Down’s syndrome?

Down’s syndrome happens as a result of an extra chromosome. Chromosomes are structures inside the body’s cells which carry the genes that determine how we develop.  A baby usually inherits 23 chromosomes from each parent, making a total of 46.

In Down’s syndrome the baby inherits an extra copy of a chromosome called the 21 chromosome. The baby has 47 chromosomes instead of 46. This extra chromosome explains why babies with Down’s syndrome develop differently from babies without this extra chromosome.. It can come from either the mother or the father.

This happens by chance in about 1 in 700 pregnancies. It is not caused by anything the parents have done.

No one knows for certain why some babies have an extra chromosome 21. The chance of this happening increases as a woman gets older. [3]Down’s syndrome. NHS Choices. http://www.nhs.uk/conditions/Downs-syndrome. However most babies with Down’s are born to younger women, because younger women have more babies than older women overall.

Health problems associated with Down's syndrome

Many children with Down’s syndrome have good health but some children’s health is affected. 

A team of paediatricians (doctors specialising in children) and specialist nurses will monitor your child’s health and development after birth if he/she has Down’s syndrome. He or she will have frequent health checks throughout the years of childhood, so that any problems can be picked up and treated early.

About half the babies with Down’s syndrome will be born with some kind of heart defect. [4]Early support. Information for Parents. Down syndrome. http://www.downs-syndrome.org.uk The most common problem is called a septal defect, where a hole develops in one of the walls of the heart and needs an operation to fix it. [5]http://www.intellectualdisability.info/physical-health/key-facts-from-the-downs-heart-group.

About 10 in 100 babies with Down’s syndrome are born with problems with their bowels [4]Early support. Information for Parents. Down syndrome. http://www.downs-syndrome.org.uk. Sometimes the bowel is not developed properly and needs an operation to fix it. This problem can be detected in pregnancy and may need surgery in the first few days after birth.

Over 75 in 100 children with Down’s syndrome have hearing problems at some point. [7]Early support. Information for Parents. Down syndrome. http://www.downs-syndrome.org.uk The hearing loss may be mild. But for a child with learning difficulties, it can cause a delay in learning to speak and in getting on with other children. Newborn babies with Down’s syndrome will be tested for hearing loss. It can be treated in a number of ways including surgery, medication and hearing aids.

Children with Down’s syndrome may also have more difficulties sleeping and more digestive problems (such as constipation) and they may also be more vulnerable to infection, than other children.

About 1 in 5 children with Down’s syndrome will develop a psychological or behavioural condition such as attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD). [8]NHS Choices. Down’s syndrome. http://www.nhs.uk/Conditions/Downs-syndrome/Pages/Complications.aspx These are not the same as having a learning difficulty. There is advice and support available for the parents of children who develop these problems.

Growing up and getting older

People with Down’s syndrome are now living for far longer than in the past. On average they live between 50 and 60 years. [9]http://www.downs-syndrome.org.uk/images/stories/DSA-documents/Publications/health/Ageing_and_Consequences.pdf

There is no longer the expectation that a child with Down’ syndrome will be living with his or her parents for life. With adequate help and support, many adults with Down’s syndrome can live on their own or with someone else. There are a range of supported living services available for adults with learning disabilities.

Many young adults with Down’s syndrome go into further education and some get employment, usually part-time. Many enter relationships although they will need guidance and support around issues such as contraception or having children.

Older people with Down’s syndrome face similar challenges of getting older as the rest of the population. These include retirement (if they have been working), bereavement (losing family and friends) and moving home. These changes can lead to feelings of confusion, disorientation and depression.

People with Down’s syndrome tend to develop Alzheimer’s disease at an earlier age than the rest of the population. Alzheimer’s is the main type of dementia and causes increasing memory loss. [10]Coppus A, Evenhuis H, Verberne GJ. Dementia and mortality in persons with Down's syndrome. Journal of Intellectual Disability Research. 2006;50:768-77

A lot will depend on the quality of support and services which is provided for older people with Down’s syndrome.

What support is available for parents and carers?

There are many professionals involved in supporting children with Down’s syndrome and their families. They include paediatricians (doctors who look after children), physiotherapists, occupational therapists, dietitians, social workers, speech and language therapists, specialist and home teachers for preschool children.

Each local authority has a Special Educational Needs service to provide support for children at home, in nurseries, playgroups and schools. Other parents, charities and voluntary organisations can offer support, advice and information.

Having a child with Down’s syndrome can mean spending extra time visiting hospitals or clinics, going for tests and attending therapy sessions. Families who have a child with Down’s syndrome may be able to claim a range of benefits and tax credits.

What happens next?

Pregnant women can choose to have or not to have a diagnostic test for Down’s syndrome. Women who do not have a diagnostic test will know their chance of having a child with Down's syndrome and will be offered the usual screening and health tests throughout their pregnancy. Women who decide to have a diagnostic test will choose to have either amniocentesis or chorionic villus sampling. Both of these tests increase the chance of women miscarrying their pregnancy.

Women who have a diagnostic test will get either a positive or negative result. A negative result means the baby does not have Down’s syndrome. A positive result means the baby does have Down’s syndrome.

If the baby has Down’s syndrome, women can choose to carry on with the pregnancy and prepare for the birth of their baby, or they can choose to terminate the pregnancy.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT NO TEST

What is it?

Some women choose not to have a diagnostic test to check whether or not the baby has Down’s syndrome. They will continue to be cared for by their midwives and doctors at the antenatal clinic.

Who can choose it?

Any woman who is offered a diagnostic test for Down’s syndrome can choose not to have it.

What happens if I don’t want the test?

Women who decide not to have a diagnostic test will have usual antenatal care. In the weeks leading up to the birth, their blood pressure and iron levels will be regularly checked to ensure a healthy pregnancy. Urine will also be checked for signs of infection and other problems. Other checks are offered for certain inherited conditions and some infections. None of these checks have any risks to the baby. [11]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Antenatal care Routine care for the healthy pregnant woman March 2008

The baby will be measured to check that he or she is growing properly. Women are given information about how to prepare for labour and birth including writing a birth plan, how to recognise active labour and the options for coping with pain. [11]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Antenatal care Routine care for the healthy pregnant woman March 2008

If I refuse a diagnostic test now, what happens if I change my mind?

Once a screening result is known, a woman should be given adequate time to decide about whether or not to have CVS or an amniocentesis. If an initial decision is made there will always be an opportunity to revisit it in conjunction with your midwife or pregnancy healthcare provider.

All pregnant women are offered an ultrasound scan between 18 and 20 weeks, to check for any physical abnormalities in the baby. Sometimes this can raise further suspicions about Down’s syndrome but can't make a definite diagnosis. Any concerns will be discussed with you and there will continue to be an option to have a diagnostic test

Are there any other ways to find out if the baby has Down’s syndrome?

No. Without a diagnostic test, there is no way of knowing for sure if the baby has Down’s syndrome.

What is my risk of miscarriage in this pregnancy?

All pregnancies have a background risk of miscarriage but this risk is low once the pregnancy has progressed beyond 12 weeks.

If the baby has Down’s syndrome, there is a higher risk of a miscarriage or stillbirth than a normal pregnancy . About 10 in 100 unborn babies with Down’s syndrome may die unexpectedly in later pregnancy. [13]Won, R. H., Currier, R. J., Lorey, F., Towner, D. R. The timing of demise in fetuses with trisomy 21 and trisomy 18. Prenatal Diagnosis 2005; 25: 608-611

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT CHORIONIC VILLUS SAMPLING (CVS)

What is chorionic villus sampling and how is it performed?

Chorionic villus sampling (CVS) is a test which can be carried out in pregnancy to check if the baby has Down’s syndrome. Sometimes it is used to check for other conditions. It is called a diagnostic test, which means it can say for certain if the baby has Down’s syndrome or not.

In CVS, a small piece of tissue is removed from the placenta. The sample of tissue is sent to a laboratory, so that the baby’s chromosome can be examined.

CVS can be carried out in two ways. Usually it is done through the abdomen (tummy). This is called “transdominal CVS”.

Immediately before the test, the abdomen will be cleaned with antiseptic. A small amount of local anaesthetic (painkiller) will be injected into the skin, to numb the area.

Using the ultrasound scan for guidance, a long needle is guided through your skin, into the womb and the placenta. A syringe attached to the end of the needle allows a sample of tissue from the placenta to be drawn (sucked) up into the syringe. Afterwards the needle is taken out and your baby is observed on the ultrasound.

Sometimes because of the position of your womb or the position of the placenta, transabdominal CVS may not be possible. CVS can also be performed by passing a fine plastic tube or biopsy forceps through your cervix (the neck of your womb). This is known as "transcervical CVS". This type of CVS may carry more risk of miscarriage than when the needle goes through the abdomen.

The test takes only a few minutes but the preparation can take a little longer so you should prepare to be in the clinic for about an hour.

When and where would I have chorionic villus sampling?

CVS is usually carried out between the 11th and the 14th week of pregnancy. Sometimes it is carried out later. [14]NHS Screening Programme Chorionic Villus Sampling.www.fetalanomalyscreening.nhs.uk

CVS should not be performed before the 10th week of pregnancy because at this stage it is less safe. [15]Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists. Amniocentesis and Chorionic Villus Sampling. Green-top Guideline No.8. June 2010.

CVS is only offered in a specialist centre by a doctor who should have had specialist training in this technique. According to doctors guidelines, doctors who perform a CVS should carry out at least 30 such procedures each year, to maintain their skill level. [15]Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists. Amniocentesis and Chorionic Villus Sampling. Green-top Guideline No.8. June 2010.

It is an outpatient procedure which means going home the same day.

More Information

Are there any precautions to take before the test?

  • Women can eat and drink as normal before CVS.
  • Most hospitals recommend women bring a partner, friend or family member when they have the test. It is natural to feel anxious and having someone there for support may be helpful. Your partner or friend can stay with you while you have it done.
  • Before the procedure, a doctor or nurse should explain it, including its benefits and risks. You will be asked to sign a consent form.

Will I get any pain?

  • Most women say it is uncomfortable rather than painful. Some women say it feels like period pain. There may be cramping for a few hours afterwards. [15]Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists. Amniocentesis and Chorionic Villus Sampling. Green-top Guideline No.8. June 2010.
  • The doctor will usually give an injection of local anaesthetic (painkiller) for transdominal CVS. [15]Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists. Amniocentesis and Chorionic Villus Sampling. Green-top Guideline No.8. June 2010.

Does CVS ever need to be repeated?

  • Sometimes it can be difficult to do the CVS and if this is the case the doctor will discuss it with you and make a plan for how to get the information you need.

What happens afterwards?

After a CVS women are usually advised to take it easy and not do any strenuous activities, for 24 hours. Some cramping discomfort is usually noticed after the procedure and can be helped by simple painkillers such as paracetamol.

Some woman notice blood “spotting” from the vagina afterwards. This is more common after a transcervical CVS. [NHS Choices. Chorionic villus sampling – how it is performed].

Sometimes more worrying symptoms may occur. You should call your doctor or midwife, or the hospital where you had the test to seek advice if you experience the following:

  • Feel shivery
  • Have a high temperature (38C or above)
  • Are bleeding from your vagina
  • Have persistent low back pain or tummy pain
  • Have clear watery fluid coming from your vagina
  • Have contractions (when your tummy tightens then relaxes)
Can CVS affect the baby?

Between 1 and 2 in 100 women miscarry after CVS. This can happen if the baby is normal or if he or she has Down’s syndrome. [15]Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists. Amniocentesis and Chorionic Villus Sampling. Green-top Guideline No.8. June 2010.

Most of these miscarriages happen within two weeks of having a CVS. [20]UK National Screening Committee. Screening tests for you and your baby.2012 version It is not possible to know when this is caused by the test. [21]NHS Screening programmes.Chorionic villus sampling. www.fetalanomaly.screening.nhs.uk

To keep miscarriage rates as low as possible, doctors guidelines suggest that anyone carrying out CVS should be doing at least 30 of these procedures every year, to maintain their skill levels. [15]Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists. Amniocentesis and Chorionic Villus Sampling. Green-top Guideline No.8. June 2010.

Women may wish to ask the doctor how many such procedures he or she carries out annually, to help them make a decision.

Your hospital should also keep a record of their miscarriage rate following CVS, to be sure that it is no higher than expected.

Laboratory tests and results

There are two types of laboratory test that can be used to look at the baby’s chromosomes. These are

  • A test which checks all the baby’s chromosomes. This is called a full karyotype. The results fom this test are usually ready in two or three weeks [15]Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists. Amniocentesis and Chorionic Villus Sampling. Green-top Guideline No.8. June 2010.
  • A “rapid” test which only tests for chromosomes linked to specific disorders. This test can be used to detect Down’s syndrome. It is called a PCR (short for polymerase chain reaction) test. The results of this test are usually ready within three working days.

Where and when you receive the results will be discussed with you by the staff in the unit doing the test. You may wish to think carefully about how you want to hear this information and who you may want to have with you.

There are national guidelines which say you should be informed of the result within one working day of it being received from the laboratory by the hospital. [24]Screening for Down’s syndrome: UK NSC Policy recommendations 2011-2014 Model of Best Practice. You should be told the results by a trained professional such as your midwife or doctor.

For most women, the laboratory test will definitely say whether the baby has Down’s syndrome or not. Sometimes the result is unclear and a repeat test or an amniocentesis is offered.

Very occasionally women who have a CVS for Down’s syndrome find out that their baby has a different abnormality. If this happens to you, your doctor or midwife will give you information on the condition your baby has and the chance to discuss this with a specialist.

The test can only say whether or not the baby has Down’s syndrome. It will not be able to tell you how your baby is affected or how severe his or her condition is.

There are standards laid down to ensure that the results of the laboratory tests are reliable. [25]Association for Clinical Cytogenetics. Prenatal Diagnosis Best Practice Guidelines. December 2009.

Sometimes after a CVS, both normal and abnormal cells are detected in the tissue from the placenta. It is not clear if the baby is affected by the abnormality, or if it is just the placenta. This is called placental mosaicism. It affects between 1 and 2 in every 100 women who have CVS.

If your CVS detects a mosaicism, you may be offered an amniocentesis and possibly other testing to find out if the abnormality affects the baby. In about 10 in every 100 cases the baby is also affected. [26]Phillips OP, Tharapel AT, Lerner JL, Park VM, Wachtel SS, Shulman LP (March 1996). "Risk of fetal mosaicism when placental mosaicism is diagnosed by chorionic villus sampling". American Journal of Obstetrics and Gynecology , March 1996.

Understanding my results

Most women find out that their baby does not have Down’s syndrome. You will continue your pregnancy in the normal way, with usual antenatal care from your midwife and doctor.

In a small number of cases it may be that the CVS identifies a problem in the baby’s chromosomes which is of importance but which is not Down’s Syndrome.  The particular result will be explained to you in more detail by the unit providing you with your result. Bear in mind that a “normal” result does not guarantee the baby will not have other health problems. Not all of these can be detected by the CVS test.

What if my baby has Down's syndrome?

If your baby is found to have Down’s syndrome, there are two options.

  • Some women and their partners will decide to continue with the pregnancy, make plans and prepare for bringing up a child with Down’s syndrome
  • Some women will decide they wish to end the pregnancy and choose a termination.

Before having the test for Down’s syndrome, many women do not know for sure what they would want to do if the diagnosis was positive.

Only you, together with your partner, can decide what is right for you.

What happens if I continue with the pregnancy after a diagnosis of Down's syndrome?

If you decide to continue your pregnancy it is likely you will be referred to an obstetrician who can support you and your baby through the pregnancy. They will advise on the best place to give birth so your baby will have the care he or she needs at birth. [27]Antenatal Results and Choices. Supporting you throughout your pregnancy. A Handbook for Parents After a Prenatal Diagnosis.April 2009. www.arc-uk.org

  • You will be at higher risk of a miscarriage or stillbirth than a normal pregnancy. About 10 in 100 unborn babies with Down’s syndrome die in later pregnancy.
  • There will be many aspects of your pregnancy and birth to think about and to talk through with health professionals. You will need to look after your own wellbeing, both in pregnancy and after the birth.

Talking with other parents who have a child with Down’s syndrome can be a source of help. You may find it helpful to read a booklet from the Down’s Syndrome Association, called Continuing Pregnancy with a Diagnosis of Down’s Syndrome – a Guide for Parents [28]www.downs-syndrome.org.uk/images/stories/DSA-documents/Publications/general/Continuing_Pregnancy.pdf

What happens if I decide to end the pregnancy after a diagnosis of Down's syndrome?

If you decide to end your pregnancy, you and your partner may need support in making this decision. An organisation called Antenatal Results and Choices may be helpful. [29]Antenatal Results and Choices. A Handbook to be given to parents when an anomaly is diagnosed in their unborn baby. www.arc-uk.org

The method of termination available will depend on the stage of the pregnancy and the policy of the hospital. Women less than 13 weeks pregnant will usually be offered a choice of medical or surgical termination. Women beyond 13 weeks may be offered medical management. 

Will I feel more/less anxious if I have this test?

No one can say how the test will affect you individually. You may be anxious about the test being followed by a miscarriage, or you may feel relieved once the test is over. Waiting for the results is for many women an anxious time. Knowing one way or the other whether the baby has Down’s syndrome early in your pregnancy may come as a relief. On the other hand, if the baby does have Down’s syndrome, you and your partner have further decisions to make. You will need to decide whether to continue with the pregnancy or whether to end it.

How do I know I will get the best care for CVS?

There are national standards laid down for the care of women who have CVS. [30]NHS Fetal Anomaly Screening Programme. Amniocentesis and Chorionic Villus Sampling. Policy, Standards and Protocols. April 2008 They are intended to ensure this procedure is carried out as safely as possible. The standards also advise hospitals to make sure you have enough information and time to make a decision about amniocentesis and that you feel supported in making that decision.

We have outlined some of these standards below. This will help you know what should happen and whether you are getting the best care.

For example, the policy documents say

  • The aims, benefits and risks of CVS should be fully explained to you by a trained professional
  • You should also be given written information about amniocentesis and enough time to read it before being asked to consent
  • The test should only go ahead after you have given written and informed consent
  • Your privacy should be respected and the discussion and the test itself should take place in a room where privacy is assured
  • You should be told the result by a trained professional

And:

  • CVS should not be performed before 10 weeks of pregnancy
  • CVS should be performed with continuous ultrasound scanning
  • CVS should only be performed by some after adequate training

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT AMNIOCENTESIS

What is an amniocentesis and how is it performed?

Amniocentesis is a test which can be carried out in pregnancy to check if the baby has Down’s syndrome. It is called a diagnostic test which means it can say for certain if the baby has Down’s syndrome or not.

In amniocentesis a fine needle is used to remove a small amount of the fluid (called amniotic fluid) which surrounds your baby inside the womb (uterus). The genetic information in the amniotic fluid is the same as that in the cells of the unborn baby. The fluid is sent to a laboratory so that the baby’s chromosomes can be examined.

Immediately before the test, the abdomen (tummy) will be cleaned with antiseptic. Ultrasound is used to monitor the position of the baby and the needle throughout the test.

Using the ultrasound scan for guidance, a long needle is guided through your skin, through your tummy into the fluid in the womb. A syringe attached to the other end of the needle is used to draw off a small amount of the fluid around the baby (usually between 10 and 20mls, or about 2-4 teaspoonfuls).

The baby will quickly start to replace the amniotic fluid that is removed during the procedure. Afterwards the needle is taken out and your baby is observed on the ultrasound.

The doctor doing the test will usually try to avoid inserting the needle through the placenta. However, occasionally, this can be the only way to collect the sample of fluid. If the needle does pass through the placenta it does not appear to increase the risk to the baby [15]Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists. Amniocentesis and Chorionic Villus Sampling. Green-top Guideline No.8. June 2010.

The test takes only a few minutes but the preparation can take a little longer so you should prepare to be in the clinic for about an hour.

When and where would I have an amniocentesis?

Amniocentesis is usually carried out between 15 and 18 weeks of pregnancy. Sometimes it is carried out later than this, to look for certain problems in the developing baby. [32]NHS Screening programmes. Amniocentesis. www.fetalanomaly.screening.nhs.uk.

The procedure is only carried out after 15 weeks of pregnancy because this is when it is safest. [15]Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists. Amniocentesis and Chorionic Villus Sampling. Green-top Guideline No.8. June 2010.

Amniocentesis is carried out in a specialist clinic, by a doctor who will have had specialist training in this technique. According to doctors guidelines, doctors who perform an amniocentesis should carry out at least 30 such procedures every year, to maintain their skills. [15]Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists. Amniocentesis and Chorionic Villus Sampling. Green-top Guideline No.8. June 2010.

It is an outpatient procedure which means going home the same day.


More information

Are there any precautions to take before the test?

  • Women can eat and drink as normal before an amniocentesis
  • You may need to come to your appointment with a full bladder. The doctor or nurse who is looking after you should let you know before you come.
  • Most hospitals recommend women bring a partner, friend or family member when they have the test. It is natural to feel anxious and having someone there for support may be helpful. Your partner or friend can stay with you while you have it done.
  • Before the procedure, a doctor or nurse should explain it including its benefits and risks. You will be asked to sign a consent form.

Will I get any pain?

  • Most women say it is uncomfortable rather than painful. There may be cramping for a few hours afterwards. [15]Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists. Amniocentesis and Chorionic Villus Sampling. Green-top Guideline No.8. June 2010.

Does amniocentesis ever need to be repeated?

  • Sometimes it can be difficult to do an amniocentesis and if this is the case the doctor will discuss it with you and make a plan for how to get the information you need.

What happens afterwards?

  • Some cramping discomfort is usually noticed after the procedure and can be helped by simple painkillers such as paracetamol.
  • Between 1 and 2 in 100 women who have an amniocentesis have some leakage of amniotic fluid.

Sometimes more worrying symptoms may occur and you should call your doctor or midwife, or the hospital where you had the test to seek advice if you experience the following:

  • Feel shivery
  • Have a high temperature (38C or above)
  • Are bleeding from your vagina
  • Have persistent low back pain or tummy pain
  • Have clear watery fluid coming from your vagina
  • Have contractions (when your tummy tightens then relaxes)
Can amniocentesis affect the baby?

About 1 in 100 women miscarry after an amniocentesis. This can happen if the baby is normal or if he or she has Down’s syndrome. [15]Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists. Amniocentesis and Chorionic Villus Sampling. Green-top Guideline No.8. June 2010.

Most of these miscarriages happen two to four weeks after the test. It is not possible to know when this is caused by the test. [37]NHS Screening programmes. Amniocentesis test. www.fetalanomaly.screening.nhs.uk

Doctors guidelines suggest that anyone carrying out amniocentesis should be doing at least 30 of these procedures every year, to maintain their skill levels. [15]Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists. Amniocentesis and Chorionic Villus Sampling. Green-top Guideline No.8. June 2010.

Women may wish to ask their doctor how many such procedures he or she carries out annually. The hospital should also keep a record of their miscarriage rate following amniocentesis, to be sure that it is no higher than expected.

Are some women more at risk of miscarriage after an amniocentesis?

It is not known why some women have a miscarriage after an amniocentesis. Some research suggests that women who are over 40 may be more at risk. Other risk factors include previous bleeding in the pregnancy. [39]Papantoniou NE, Daskalakis GJ, Tziotis JG, Kitmirides SJ, Mesogitis SA, Antzaklis AJ. Risks factors predisposing to fetal loss following a second trimester amniocentesis. BJOG: An International Journal of Obstetrics and Gynaecology 2001;108: 1053-56.

Can I do anything to prevent a miscarriage?

There is nothing a woman can do to prevent a miscarriage after an amniocentesis.

Can amniocentesis have other complications?

The risk of developing a serious (systemic) infection after an amniocentesis is less than one in 1000. [15]Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists. Amniocentesis and Chorionic Villus Sampling. Green-top Guideline No.8. June 2010.

During pregnancy you will be offered testing for HIV and hepatitis B. It is important these results are available before having an amniocentesis. In this way the risk of these infections being passed to the baby will be kept as low as possible. [41]NHS Screening Programme. Amniocentesis test. www.fetalanomaly.screening.nhs.uk

Laboratory tests and results

There are two types of laboratory test that can be used to look at the baby’s chromosomes. These are

  • A test which checks all the baby’s chromosomes. This is called a full karyotype. The results fom this test are usually ready in two or three weeks [15]Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists. Amniocentesis and Chorionic Villus Sampling. Green-top Guideline No.8. June 2010.
  • A “rapid” test which only tests for chromosomes linked to specific disorders. This test can be used to detect Down’s syndrome. It is called a PCR (short for polymerase chain reaction) test. The results of this test are usually ready within three working days.

How will I get my results?

Where and when you receive the results will be discussed with you by the staff in the unit doing the test. You may wish to think carefully about how you want to hear this information and who you may want to have with you.

There are national guidelines which say you should be informed of the result within one working day of it being received from the laboratory by the hospital. [24]Screening for Down’s syndrome: UK NSC Policy recommendations 2011-2014 Model of Best Practice You should be told the results by a trained professional such as your midwife or doctor.

What will the results tell me?

For most women, the laboratory test will definitely say whether the baby has Down’s syndrome or not. Sometimes the result is unclear and a repeat test is needed.

Very occasionally women who have an amniocentesis for Down’s syndrome find out that their baby has a different chromosomal abnormality. If this happens to you, your doctor or midwife will give you information on the condition your baby has and the chance to discuss this with a specialist.

Can amniocentesis tell how severely a baby has Down’s syndrome?

The test can only say whether or not the baby has Down’s syndrome. It will not be able to tell you how your baby is affected or how severe his or her condition is.

How can I be sure the test is reliable?

There are standards laid down to ensure that the results of the laboratory tests are reliable. [25]Association for Clinical Cytogenetics. Prenatal Diagnosis Best Practice Guidelines. December 2009.

Understanding my results

Most women find out that their baby does not have Down’s syndrome. You will continue your pregnancy in the normal way, with usual antenatal care from your midwife and doctor.

In a small number of cases it may be that the amniocentesis identifies a problem in the baby’s chromosomes which is of importance but which is not Down’s syndrome.  The particular result will be explained to you in more detail by the unit providing you with your result. Bear in mind that a “normal” result does not guarantee your baby will not have other health problems. Not all of these can be detected by the amniocentesis test.

What if my unborn baby has Down’s syndrome?

If the unborn baby is found to have Down’s syndrome, there are two options.

  • Some women and their partners will decide to continue with the pregnancy, make plans and prepare for bringing up a child with Down’s syndrome
  • Some women will decide they wish to end the pregnancy and choose a termination.

Before having the test for Down’s syndrome, many women do not know for sure what they would want to do if the diagnosis was positive.

Only you, together with your partner, can decide what is right for you.

What happens if I continue with the pregnancy after a diagnosis of Down's syndrome?

If you decide to continue your pregnancy it is likely you will be referred to an obstetrician who can support you and your baby through the pregnancy. They will advise on the best place to give birth so your baby will have the care he or she needs at birth. [27]Antenatal Results and Choices. Supporting you throughout your pregnancy. A Handbook for Parents After a Prenatal Diagnosis.April 2009. www.arc-uk.org

  • You will be at higher risk of a miscarriage or stillbirth than a normal pregnancy . About 10 in 100 unborn babies with Down’s syndrome die in later pregnancy. [46]Won, R. H., Currier, R. J., Lorey, F., Towner, D. R., Won, Rosa H., Currier, Robert J., Lorey, Fred, and Towner, Dena R. The timing of demise in fetuses with trisomy 21 and trisomy 18. Prenatal Diagnosis 2005; 25: 608-611.
  • There will be many aspects of your pregnancy and birth to think about and to talk through with health professionals. You will need to look after your own wellbeing, both in pregnancy and after the birth.

Talking with other parents who have a child with Down’s syndrome can be a source of help. You may find it helpful to read a booklet from the Down’s Syndrome Association, called Continuing Pregnancy with a Diagnosis of Down’s Syndrome – a Guide for Parents [28]www.downs-syndrome.org.uk/images/stories/DSA-documents/Publications/general/Continuing_Pregnancy.pdf.

What happens if I decide to end the pregnancy after a diagnosis of Down's syndrome?

If you decide to end your pregnancy you and your partner may need support in making this decision. An organisation called Antenatal Results and Choices may be helpful [29]Antenatal Results and Choices. A Handbook to be given to parents when an anomaly is diagnosed in their unborn baby. www.arc-uk.org.

The method of termination available will depend on the stage of the pregnancy and the policy of the hospital. Normally after an amniocentesis, labour will be started with the help of drug treatment. 

Will I feel more/less anxious if I have the test?

No one can say how the test will affect you individually. You may be anxious about the test being followed by a miscarriage, or you may feel relieved once the test is over. Waiting for the results is for many women an anxious time. Knowing one way or the other whether the baby has Down’s syndrome may come as a relief. On the other hand, if the baby does have Down’s syndrome, you and your partner have further decisions to make. You will need to decide whether to continue with the pregnancy or whether to end it.

How do I know I will get the best care for an amniocentesis?

There are national standards laid down for the care of women who have an amniocentesis. [30]NHS Fetal Anomaly Screening Programme. Amniocentesis and Chorionic Villus Sampling. Policy, Standards and Protocols. April 2008. They are intended to ensure this procedure is carried out as safely as possible. The standards also advise hospitals to make sure you have enough information and time to make a decision about amniocentesis and that you feel supported in making that decision.

We have outlined some of these standards below. This will help you know what should happen and whether you are getting the best care.

For example, the policy documents say

  • The aims, benefits and risks of amniocentesis should be fully explained to you by a trained professional
  • You should also be given written information about amniocentesis and enough time to read it before being asked to consent
  • The test should only go ahead after you have given written and informed consent
  • Your privacy should be respected and the discussion and the test itself should take place in a room where privacy is assured
  • You should be told the result by a trained professional

And:

  • Amniocentesis should not be performed before 15 weeks of pregnancy
  • Amniocentesis should be performed with continuous ultrasound scanning
  • The doctor should only insert the needle through the placenta if this is the only way to obtain amniotic fluid
  • Amniocentesis should only be carried out by someone after adequate training
