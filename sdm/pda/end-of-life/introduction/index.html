Our Decision Support telephone service is here to assist and guide you through the Shared Decision Making process.

The Decision Support team is made of professional Health Coaches who are clinicians, commonly nurses, who have undertaken additional training to become a Health Coach.

The Health Coaches will assist you by:

  • Providing you with information
  • Helping you to understand your condition
  • Recognise what is important to you regarding the outcome of treatment
  • Identifying potential solutions
  • Encourage you in discussing options with your family
  • Transferring skills which will assist you in using the information and resources available to you
  • Support you in building confidence in discussing your choice with your doctor
  • You may find that this can be achieved in one telephone call with a Health Coach however; if further calls are required to support you in reaching your decision the Health Coach will schedule these with you.

Important Things To Note

  • A Health Coach does not know which treatment option is best for you.
  • The Health Coach does not have information about your medical history.
  • You will still need to talk over your decision and reasons for your decision with your doctor or nurse.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT OWN HOME

What is it?

This is when someone decides, with their family, that they would prefer to be cared for at home towards the end of life. This might be their own home, or the home of a close relative or friend.
Clearly people having end of life care at home will be in a familiar environment, surrounded by their own things, and will be able to see family and friends easily.

Some people who carry on living at home can visit hospices with day centres which people can access, usually on a weekly basis.

Who can have it?

Anyone can choose to have end of life care at home. But if someone is very ill, it is usually only possible if a relative or friend is willing and able to be present most of the time. [1]Grande G and Ewing G. Death at home unlikely if informal carers prefer otherwise: implications for policy. Palliat Med. 2008; 22: 971-2. [2]Gomes B and Higginson IJ. Factors influencing death at home in terminally ill patients with cancer: systematic review. BMJ. 2006; 332: 515-21.

People considering being cared for at home in the last months of life also need to take into account how much professional support and care can be provided. This will vary according to where they live. In many areas, the GP and District Nurse will provide most of the care. They can arrange for community palliative care nurses (such as Macmillan nurses), to visit people’s homes to provide specialist care including advice on how to control symptoms, and practical and emotional support. Some areas have Hospice at Home services. This allows people to receive hospice care in their own home. The District Nurse can also arrange for care from the Marie Curie Nursing Service and support from some social care agencies.

The social services department of the local authority may provide a range of social care services and equipment to help terminally ill people remain at home.

Another factor which many people consider in deciding whether to be cared for at home is whether the home environment is suitable, especially if someone has a progressive illness. For example, the rooms may be thought too small to contain a lot of equipment or there may be stairs which someone who is ill finds difficult to use.

What will my care be like?

There is not much evidence on the quality of professional care provided for people who choose to be cared for at home. It will partly depend on what services are available in the local area. Some research has found that people being cared for at home and their families have a lack of access to professional support. [3]National Audit Office. End of Life Care. The Stationery Office, November 2008 However in recent years, there has been progress in improving support for end of life care at home. [4]Department of Health. End of Life Care Strategy. Providing high quality care for all adults at the end of life. July 2008

One national survey involved nearly 50,000 bereaved relatives of people receiving care in different settings, including their home, in the last three months of life. The survey asked people about the overall quality of the professional care their relatives had received. It found that the overall quality of care given to people who were at home was rated as outstanding, excellent or good by 82 out of 100 relatives. [5]Department of Health. First national VOICES survey of bereaved people: key findings report. July 2012

Below are some other findings from the survey, from the relatives of those who had been cared for at home.

Relief of pain

Bereaved relatives were asked how well pain was relieved in those who had been cared for at home (and who were in pain). Seventeen in 100 said that pain relief was provided all the time. Thirty in 100 said that pain relief was provided some of the time.

Dignity and respect

Bereaved relatives were asked whether the person being cared for at home was shown dignity and respect at all time. Seventy-nine in 100 said that the person had been shown dignity and respect all of the time by district and community nurses. Seventy-two in 100 said the person had been shown dignity and respect all of the time by GPs.

Involvement in decisions about care

Ninety-two in 100 relatives felt that the person being cared for had been involved in decisions about their care as much as they had wanted.

Support in the last few days of life

Over 80 in 100 relatives said that in the last two days of life, the personal and nursing care needs of the person being cared for had been met. Ninety-two in 100 felt that their relative had had adequate privacy in the last stages.

Will relatives looking after me be supported?

How well relatives looking after someone at home are supported will depend on what services are offered locally. In a survey, 66 out of 100 relatives of someone who had been cared for at home felt they had been supported as much as they wanted. Sixty in 100 felt they were supported adequately at the time of the person’s death and 94 in 100 felt they were dealt with in a sensitive manner after the death. [6]Department of Health. First national VOICES survey of bereaved people: key findings report. July 2012

Will I have access to professional/medical care at all times?

Someone being cared for at home will have access to GP cover at all times. In some areas hospice staff are on call 24 hours day and can visit people at home. It is helpful for people and their families considering end of life care to establish how much professional support would be available.

Will I have immediate access to emergency care/life support in a crisis?

If people need emergency care, or if they wish to have access to treatments that may sustain life, they will probably have to be admitted to hospital. They can return home after emergency treatment is completed. But even if their illness suddenly gets worse they may already have decided that they do not wish to go into hospital and for their care to continue at home. Some medical equipment which makes someone more comfortable can be provided at home.

Will I be able to die at home if I wish?

People who want to die at home have their preference met more often than those choose to die in other settings. Research has found that just over half of those who want to die at home, do so. [7]Natalia Calanzani, Irene J Higginson, Barbara Gomes. Current and Future needs for hospice care: an evidence-based report. January 2013

In a large survey of bereaved relatives of those who had been cared for at home, 88 in 100 felt the person had had enough choice about where they might die. And 94 in 100 thought they had died in the right place. [6]Department of Health. First national VOICES survey of bereaved people: key findings report. July 2012

However, lack of access to professional support and to medication at home can lead to people who are approaching the end of life being admitted from home to hospital. [3]National Audit Office. End of Life Care. The Stationery Office, November 2008

Sometimes, people and their families change their minds about where they would like to be cared for in the last days or weeks of life. If symptoms get worse, for example, someone may feel better cared for and more comfortable in a hospice or hospital. [10]Evans WG, Cutson TM, Steinhauser KE, et al. Is there no place like home? Caregivers recall reasons for and experience upon transfer from home hospice to inpatient facilities. J Palliat Med. 2006; 9: 100-10 [3]National Audit Office. End of Life Care. The Stationery Office, November 2008

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT CARE HOME

What is it?

Some people receive end of life care in a care home. Someone may have been living in a care home and consider it their home before they are thought to be in the last year or months of life. Care homes are for people who find it difficult to look after themselves, where it is not possible to receive the care they need in their own home. Care homes provide personal care including help with washing, toileting and help to take medication. Staff are available day and night. People may have their own rooms, with a lounge and other communal areas.

Care homes vary in the type of environment. Some residential homes have a homely atmosphere and encourage people to bring in some of their own furniture and even their pets.

Nursing care homes offer skilled nursing care for people who are very frail or who have a disability or a serious long term condition and need frequent medical attention from a doctor or nurse. Other homes offer specialist care for people with conditions such as dementia.

It is important to note that not all residential homes will keep people at the end of life, whereas all nursing homes will. 

End of life care in a care home may involve the local hospital's palliative care team, the local hospice team, the GP, community nurses and district nurses. Palliative care is the type of care provided to someone who has a life limiting or terminal illness. It concentrates on helping them live as well as possible and making them as comfortable as possible.

Care homes can be run by voluntary organisations, private individuals, private companies or local councils.

Who can have it?

Whether someone chooses to be cared for at the end of life in a care home depends on what services they offer. You can find this out from your local social services department, GP or district nurse, palliative care team or your hospital doctor or nurse.

If someone is already resident in a care home, they can choose to remain there for all their end of life care.

The NHS advises that when considering a care home for end of life care, someone should ask if it is accredited by the NHS end of life Gold Standards Framework, which means that the home has staff who are especially trained in end of life care and good links with professionals such as GPs.

Care homes charge a fee for their services. How someone pays depends on their personal situation. The local council may be able to pay all or part of the fees if someone’s capital and savings are below a certain limit. Someone who is relying on local authority funding, will not be able to be cared for in a home that costs more than the authority is prepared to pay, unless they or their family can pay the difference.

What will my care be like?

Little research has been carried out into the quality of end of life care provided by care homes. Much will depend on individual homes. One report in 2008 said that few care home staff had sufficient training in providing end of life care. [12]National Audit Office. End of Life Care. Stationary Office, November 2008 Some homes have very high staff turnover. In recent years there have been moves to improve end of life care in care homes.

One national survey involved nearly 50,000 bereaved relatives of people receiving care in different settings, including care homes, in the last three months of life. The survey asked people about the overall quality of the professional care their relatives had received. It found that the overall quality of care given to people who were in care homes was rated as outstanding, excellent or good by 83 in 100 relatives. [5]Department of Health. First national VOICES survey of bereaved people: key findings report. July 2012

Below are some other findings from the survey, from the relatives of those who had been cared for in care homes.

Relief of pain

Bereaved relatives were asked how well pain was relieved in those who had been cared for in hospital (and who were in pain). Forty five in 100 surveyed said complete pain relief was provided all the time. 29 in 100 said complete pain relief was provided some of the time.

Dignity and respect

Bereaved relatives were asked whether the person being cared for in a hospice was shown dignity and respect at all time. 61 people in 100 said that their relatives in care homes had been shown dignity and respect all of the time by staff.

Involvement in decisions about care

Eighty six in 100 people in the survey felt that the patient had been involved in decisions about their care as much as they had wanted.

Support in the last few days of life

Eighty nine in 100 relatives of people in care homes said that in the last two days of life, the respective personal and nursing care needs of the person being cared for had been met. Ninety six in 100 said they had had adequate privacy in the last stages.

Will relatives looking after me be supported?

Forty six in 100 family carers of people who had died in care homes felt they had as much support from staff as they wanted. Sixty four in 100 felt support was adequate at the time the person they were caring for died. Ninety six in 100 carers felt they were dealt with in a sensitive manner after the person they were caring for died. [5]Department of Health. First national VOICES survey of bereaved people: key findings report. July 2012

Will I have access to professional/medical care at all times?

There may not always be immediate access to medical or palliative care in a care home. It depends on the home’s arrangements with GPs, district nurses and local palliative care staff, and with out of hours medical and pharmacy services. For example, not all homes will be able to hold stocks of the drugs commonly used in palliative care such as those for pain control (but the necessary drugs will be prescribed for the individual person). Those in nursing care homes should have access to skilled nursing care.

Will I have immediate access to emergency care/life support in a crisis?

People in care homes needing emergency medical care, or who wish to have access to treatments which may sustain life, would be admitted to hospital but could return to the care home after treatment.

Will I be able to die in the care home/where I wish?

People living in care homes can choose to remain in the home, but may need to be admitted into hospital in the last few days or hours of life, if there is insufficient professional support available. One report found that one quarter of deaths among care home residents take place in hospital. [12]National Audit Office. End of Life Care. Stationary Office, November 2008

In a survey of bereaved relatives of people who had end of life care in care homes, 53 in 100 said patients had enough choice about where they wanted to die and 88 in 100 thought the patient had died in the right place.

Sometimes, people and their families change their minds about where they would like to be in the last days or weeks of life. If an illness gets worse, they may feel they will be better cared for and more comfortable in hospital. [16]Evans WG, Cutson TM, Steinhauser KE, et al. Is there no place like home? Caregivers recall reasons for and experience upon transfer from home hospice to inpatient facilities. J Palliat Med. 2006; 9: 100-10. [17]Gerrard R, Campbell J, Minton O, et al. Achieving the preferred place of care for hospitalized patients at the end of life. Palliat Med. 2011; 25: 333-6

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT HOSPICE

What is it?

A hospice is a medical and nursing environment which provides holistic care for people with life-limiting or terminal illness. Hospice care helps people to live as actively as they can to the end of their lives. Hospices aim to take care of people’s physical, spiritual, emotional and social needs. A hospice will also support carers, family members and friends, both during someone’s illness and during their bereavement.

Hospices are run by a team of doctors, nurses, social workers, therapists, counsellors, and trained volunteers. The services they provide vary but all offer medical and nursing care, including control of symptoms such as pain. A hospice may also offer:

  • physiotherapy
  • rehabilitation (helping someone build up their health and strength, for example through exercise)
  • spiritual and psychological help
  • information about financial and other practical issues
  • respite care (giving family carers a break from caring)
  • bereavement care
  • complementary therapies, such as massage

Hospices have beds for people who are being cared for in the hospice itself, but a hospice team can also provide hospice care to people at home, or in a care home.

Hospices aim to provide a homely atmosphere and encourage people to bring their own belongings during their stay. They also encourage visits from family and friends. Many inpatient hospices are run by charities.

Who can have it?

Hospices can help people with all types of illness including cancer, heart failure and lung disease. People can use hospice care at any stage in their illness, not just the end of life. Some people are admitted to hospices, perhaps at an early stage of illness, for a short period of intensive care, or they may be admitted during the final stage of illness. Some people spend time in a hospice during the day, while living at home.

Whether people can access hospice care or a hospice depends on what level of palliative care they need at the end of life. A GP or hospital doctor can refer someone for hospice care. Hospices are free for people using them.

For more information, contact the charity Help the Hospices at www.helpthehospices.org.uk

What will my care be like?

Some research has found that the type of care provided by hospice staff – whether it takes place in an in-patient hospice, hospital, care home or at home - offers better quality of care and more patient satisfaction in many aspects than standard care. Hospice care seems to be better in helping control symptoms, particularly pain. There are also reports of increased patient satisfaction with care. [7]Natalia Calanzani, Irene J Higginson, Barbara Gomes. Current and Future needs for hospice care: an evidence-based report. January 2013

One national survey involved nearly 50,000 bereaved relatives of people receiving care in different settings, including hospices, in the last three months of life. The survey asked people about the overall quality of the professional care their relatives had received. It found that the overall quality of care given to people who were in a hospice was rated as outstanding, excellent or good by 87 in 100 relatives. [6]Department of Health. First national VOICES survey of bereaved people: key findings report. July 2012

Below are some other findings from the survey, from the relatives of those who had been cared for in a hospice.

Relief of pain

Bereaved relatives were asked how well pain was relieved in those who had been cared for in a hospice (and who were in pain). Sixty two in 100 said that complete pain relief given to their relative at home was provided all the time. 25 in 100 said complete pain relief was provided some of the time.

Dignity and respect

Bereaved relatives were asked whether the person being cared for in a hospice was shown dignity and respect at all time. Eighty in 100 said that the person had been shown dignity and respect all of the time by hospice nurses. Eighty seven in 100 said the person had been shown dignity and respect all of the time by hospice doctors.

Involvement in decisions about care

Eighty nine in 100 relatives felt that the person being cared for had been involved in decisions about their care as much as they had wanted.

Support in the last few days of life

Ninety-six and 95 in 100 relatives said that in the last two days of life, the respective personal and nursing care needs of the person being cared for had been met. Ninety-five in 100 felt that their relative had had adequate privacy in the last stages.

Most hospices use a programme called the Liverpool Care Pathway (LCP), which aims to help staff provide high quality care in the last days and hours of life. [20]http://www.liv.ac.uk/mcpcil/liverpool-care-pathway/

Will I have access to professional/medical care at all times?

Someone being cared for in a hospice will have immediate access to nursing care at all times.

Will I have immediate access to emergency care/life support in a crisis?

Hospices usually focus on care which will control symptoms, provide emotional or spiritual support, and help people live as well as they can, rather than on active treatment of a disease. But anyone who needs care in an emergency or who wishes to have access to treatments which may sustain life will need to be transferred to hospital. They can return to the hospice once the emergency care is completed.

Will relatives looking after me be supported?

Hospice care includes support for the family and friends of those receiving end of life care.
Fifty six in 100 carers of people who had died in a hospice felt they had as much support from hospice staff as they wanted. Eighty five in 100 felt support was adequate at the time the person they were caring for died. Ninety nine in 100 carers felt they were dealt with in a sensitive manner after the person they were caring for died. [6]Department of Health. First national VOICES survey of bereaved people: key findings report. July 2012

Will I be able to die in the hospice if I wish?

Hospices aim to take care of people's palliative care needs for as long as they are needed. If someone does not need specialist treatment until they die they may be transferred to a care home or discharged back home.

In a large survey of bereaved relatives of those who had been cared for in a hospice, 70 in 100 felt the person had had enough choice about where they might die. And 93 in 100 thought they had died in the right place. [6]Department of Health. First national VOICES survey of bereaved people: key findings report. July 2012

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT HOSPITAL

What is it?

Over half of all people nearing the end of their lives are cared for in hospital. This is especially true for people with heart and lung conditions. [3]National Audit Office. End of Life Care. The Stationery Office, November 2008

Hospitals are a “medical” environment. Some try to create a more homely atmosphere and encourage people to bring in their belongings and photos to make their stay more personal, as a reminder of home. Access to family and friends will depend on each hospital’s policy but as someone approaches the end of life most allow unlimited visiting and may provide overnight facilities.

All hospitals have specialist palliative care teams who help hospital staff to care for patients nearing the end of their life. Palliative care concentrates on relieving distressing symptoms and providing whatever support the patient and their family needs, rather than on cure. It is also called holistic care since it deals with the whole person, rather than just with one aspect of their care.
In many cases people nearing the end of life need to go into hospital for short periods for treatment. For example, cancer patients may need chemotherapy and radiotherapy to help control their symptoms, but most of the time they will be encouraged to be at home.

Who can have it?

Anyone can choose to receive end of life care in hospital. The services and support available will depend on the area where you live.   

What will my care be like?

Research in the past has shown that hospitals do not always meet the needs of patients nearing the end of life or their families. [12]National Audit Office. End of Life Care. Stationary Office, November 2008 One analysis of NHS complaints made in England between 2004 and 2006 found that more than half the complaints concerned end of life care, with most relating to staff providing inadequate information to patients and their families before death. [25]Healthcare Commission 2007. Is anyone listening? A Report on Complaints Handling in the NHS. Commission for Healthcare Audit and Inspection, 2007

Problems include treatment being continued against patients’ wishes, failing to help people return home to die if they wish, lack of emotional engagement and lack of communication. [26]Gott M. How to improve end of life care in acute hospitals. Nursing Older People Sept 2009

Will I have access to professional/medical care at all times?

Someone being cared for in a hospital should have immediate access to professional care at all times. 

Will I have immediate access to emergency care/life support in a crisis?

An acute hospital can provide emergency care and life support, if it is appropriate, if someone’s symptoms suddenly get worse. For example, someone who is finding it difficult to breathe can be put on a ventilator. An example of other treatments which can extend life include a machine to help the kidneys work properly (dialysis).

Some people have found that hospitals give life extending treatment to people at the end of life when it is not wanted. If someone does want active treatment, a hospital will discuss with them how much is considered to be in their best interests.

Someone can make their wishes known in advance about whether they would want to refuse treatment by making an advance decision. This is a document in which someone sets out whether they want to refuse treatments in the future and if so, which ones. Will I be able to die where I wish?

Someone cannot stay in hospital indefinitely, and if their condition is stable they may be discharged back to a care home or their own home to continue having end of life care. Once someone who is admitted to hospital has received all possible treatments, the medical team can make arrangements to enable them to return home to die if this is what they wish to happen. One factor is whether the person has enough support at home from family members who are willing and able to support them.

This decision aid is to help adults who are considering the place where they, or a family member or companion, would like to be cared for as they approach the end of life. In this decision aid, end of life means the last months or year of a person's life.

People making this decision will be at a stage in their life where they wish to plan where they will be cared for. They may have a terminal or life limiting illness or they may simply have reached a stage in life where they wish to make plans about their care, in advance. The options about where to have this end of life care are:

  • At home
  • In a care home, which may be your home already
  • In a hospice
  • In a hospital

Making a decision about preferred place of care as the end of life approaches can be difficult but doing so in advance will make it more likely that people's wishes are met. The decision depends on people's own living arrangements and finances, their health, the friends, family and carers they have in their lives, their preferences for care at the end of life, and the services available in their local area.

This decision aid will help you think about what is important to you and your family when considering where to have end of life care. It may be helpful to start talking about these options with your family, carers, doctors and other health professionals.

The issues people think about when deciding where they would like to be cared for at the end of life include:

  • Is one place better for me than another if I am having treatment for a health problem?
  • Is one place better for me than another for access to emergency treatment?
  • Is one place better for me than another for treatment to control my symptoms?

The right choice for you may change over time. This decision aid aims to help you make the right choice for you now, but you may wish to change your mind as your life changes.

MORE INFORMATION ABOUT END OF LIFE: PLACE OF CARE

What is meant by end of life care?

End of life care is the term used to describe the support and care given to people within the last year of their life. It helps people with advanced, progressive or incurable illness, to live as well as possible until they die. End of life care also includes support for people’s partners, families and informal carers both before and after someone’s death. [4]Department of Health. End of Life Care Strategy. Providing high quality care for all adults at the end of life. July 2008

When end of life care begins depends on someone’s needs. It may last a few days or many months. End of life care begins when someone needs it and continues for as long as they need it.

End of life care includes palliative care. This is care which concentrates on relieving symptoms rather than on cure. It provides whatever support is needed to the patient and their family. The support may be physical, psychological, social, spiritual or practical. It is based on the individual care needs of the patient and their family. This kind of care is a form of holistic care because it deals with the whole person, rather than just with one aspect of their care. Palliative care was originally developed by hospices but this approach is also used in other settings.

People who are considering the place they would like to be cared for towards the end of life may have a progressive or life limiting illness, or they may have reached a stage in life where they want to think about how they wish to be cared for in the future. Someone in this position has the opportunity to discuss and plan how and where they wish to be cared for in advance. These discussions can take place with health professionals such as the GP or hospital doctor and they also include people’s families and informal carers. [4]Department of Health. End of Life Care Strategy. Providing high quality care for all adults at the end of life. July 2008 Some people may prefer not to involve anyone else when making these decisions.

Who gives end of life care?

Many people can be involved in end of life care, depending on someone’s needs. They include health professionals (doctors and nurses) who can assess someone’s health and advise about treatments; physiotherapists who can help maintain mobility and independence; occupational therapists who can make adaptations to a person's environment; counsellors who are trained in giving emotional support; social services staff who can advise on issues such as money and access to equipment; professional carers who can help with daily tasks such as washing, dressing and meals; and where needed, religious ministers.

Where can I have end of life care?

End of life care is offered in a variety of settings. These are

  • In someone’s own home (or in the home of a relative or friend). Some people prefer to remain at home for end of life care. The main carer here is usually a family member, with some support provided by health and social care professionals.
  • A care home. Care homes can be run by voluntary or private organisations or by local councils.
  • An inpatient hospice. This is a specialist unit which focuses on supporting people who are terminally ill and their families. Hospice care can also be provided in hospitals, people’s homes and care homes.
  • In hospital. All hospitals have specialist palliative care teams who help hospital staff provide care for people nearing the end of life.

It is entirely up to the individual whether they decide in advance their preferred places of care as they approach the end of life. No one has to do so.

Why is it useful to decide about end of life care in advance?

People who say in advance where they want to be cared for are more likely to achieve their wishes than those who do not. [2]Gomes B and Higginson IJ. Factors influencing death at home in terminally ill patients with cancer: systematic review. BMJ. 2006; 332: 515-21 This kind of planning will make it more likely that someone will spend their remaining months or years in the place of their choice.

If someone has a terminal or life limiting illness, it can be a good idea to consider in advance where they would like to be cared for in the final months or year of life. Planning ahead in this way is sometimes called advance care planning. [36]NHS Choices. Planning ahead for the end of life. www.nhs.uk/Planners/end-of-life care/Pages/planning-ahead

People usually carry out advance care planning because they have a condition that is expected to get worse. They may become too ill to be able to decide how and where they wish to be cared for, or to communicate their decisions in the future. However, anyone can make plans for their future care, whether they are approaching the end of life, or not.

It can make things easier for their family to know someone’s wishes in advance. It means relatives may not have to make difficult decisions on their own, later on.

How can I go about planning ahead?

Most people start by thinking about what they might want for end of life care and talking about it with their family and health professionals.

Someone can write down their preferences for end of life care, in a care plan, or an advance care plan. They can also write an advance statement about their preferences for future care including where they would like it to take place. The statement can be a future guide to anyone who might have to make decisions about someone’s care, in their best interests, if they lose the capacity to make decisions or to communicate them.

An advance statement is not legally binding but anyone making decisions about a person's care must take it into account. That person can decide where it is kept and who sees it or knows where it is.
It may help to read the booklet Planning for your future care: a guide [37]http://www.endoflifecare.nhs.uk/assets/downloads/EoLC_Planning_for_your_future_care_FINAL_010212.pdf produced by the National End of Life Care Programme.

In the UK there are some schemes which aim to help more people receive end of life care in their preferred place. For example, the Electronic Palliative Care Coordination Systems (EPaCCS) is an electronic means of recording people’s wishes about end of life care, so that they are known about by all relevant health professionals. It is worth asking the GP if the practice is using EPaCCS, or another end of life care register.

Are there any other things I should think about?

It is possible to decide whether a person might wish to refuse any types of treatment in the future. This is called an Advance Decision to Refuse Treatment (an ADRT, in the past also called a living will). For example, someone may decide not to have a treatment to keep them alive longer if they felt the treatment did not add to the quality of their life. Examples might include ventilation and cardio-pulmonary resuscitation (CPR), which can be used if someone cannot breathe by themselves or if their heart stops, or a machine to help the kidneys work properly (dialysis).

A person may wish to discuss this with a doctor or nurse who knows their medical history, before making up their mind whether they would wish to have these treatments in future. More information about advance decisions can be found at [29]http://www.nhs.uk/Planners/end-of-life-care/Pages/advance-decision-to-refuse-treatment.aspx.

If someone becomes unable to make decisions in the future, someone will need to make decisions for them. Who does this will depend on the situation. Generally, professionals will make decisions about someone’s health and social care, and their family or carers will decide on the more day-to-day matters.

It is possible to officially appoint someone trusted to make decisions on someone's behalf, in advance. This is called making a lasting power of attorney (LPA). This enables someone to give another person the right to make decisions about their care and welfare.

There are special rules about appointing a lasting power of attorney. The Office of the Public Guardian has guidance for people who want to make a lasting power of attorney [39]http://www.justice.gov.uk/downloads/forms/opg/lpa-guidance-health-and-welfare.pdf.
Someone approaching the end of life may also wish to make a will, which enables them to decide what happens to their money, property and possessions after their death. It will need to be formally witnessed and signed to make it legally valid. For more information see [40]www.gov.uk/make-will.

Can I get any financial or other help for end of life care?

People choosing to receive care at home, in a care home, or in a hospice can be assessed for NHS continuing healthcare. This is care given over an extended period of time to meet the physical or mental health needs of adults with a disability, injury or illness. It involves a package of care that is arranged and funded by the NHS and is free of charge to the person receiving the care. This is sometimes called fully funded NHS care. For more information, see [41]http://www.nhs.uk/Planners/end-of-life-care/Pages/nhs-continuing-healthcare.aspx.

Someone who is looking after a relative, partner or friend who is ill is entitled to a carer's assessment to see whether they qualify for local authority help, such as respite care.

There are other sources of support which may be available for people who are seriously ill and their carers. They include incapacity benefits, a carer's allowance and an attendance allowance. For more information, see [42]http://www.nhs.uk/CarersDirect/moneyandlegal/Pages/Moneyandlegalhome.aspx.
