MORE INFORMATION ABOUT ESTABLISHED KIDNEY FAILURE (KIDNEY DIALYSIS)

What do the kidneys do?

The kidneys are bean-shaped organs, each about the size of a fist. They are just below your ribcage, one on either side of your spine, at the back of the body. Every day the kidneys filter about 200 litres of fluid, removing 2 to 3 litres (more than 3 to 5 pints) of water and waste products from the blood. If the kidneys did not remove them, these wastes would build up in the blood and damage the body.

Blood is moved through the kidneys in blood vessels. Tiny filters within the kidneys act like a sieve. They keep proteins and cells in the blood where they are needed. Extra fluid and waste is passed to the bladder in urine. The bladder stores urine until you go to the toilet. The kidneys also produce a hormone called erythropoietin, which is needed to make red blood cells. Healthy kidneys help keep your blood pressure stable. And kidneys are important for absorbing the right amount of calcium and phosphate, which helps keep your bones strong.

What is kidney disease?

Chronic kidney disease means your kidneys are gradually losing their ability to filter waste products out of your blood.

You may also hear it called chronic renal failure, chronic kidney failure, or chronic renal insufficiency. Chronic means it’s a long-term condition, and renal means it involves your kidneys.

Doctors use the term kidney function as a way to describe how well your kidneys filter blood. Kidney function can be measured with a blood test. Young people with two healthy kidneys usually have around 90 percent to 100 percent of their kidney function. Your kidney function tends to get lower with age. This isn’t usually a problem, unless it keeps on getting lower.

People can lose some of their kidney function without it causing any problems or symptoms. But health problems begin when the kidneys can no longer do all their jobs well enough. When this happens varies from person to person. Some people might get symptoms when they still have quite a lot of kidney function, while others don’t get symptoms even though they have lost more kidney function.

What is established kidney failure?

Doctors divide chronic kidney disease into five stages, based on the kidney function shown by your blood tests. If your blood tests show kidney function of 15 percent or less, this is called chronic kidney disease stage 5, also known as established kidney failure. You may also hear it called end stage kidney failure.

If you have established kidney failure, you may get symptoms because of waste building up in your blood. But this doesn’t happen to everyone. You may not get symptoms until your kidney function is much lower than 15 percent.

What are the symptoms of established kidney failure?

If your kidneys have lost their ability to filter the blood well enough to keep you healthy, you may get some of these symptoms:[1]National Institutes of Health. The kidneys and how they work. March 2012. Available at http://kidney.niddk.nih.gov/kudiseases/pubs/yourkidneys/ (accessed on 19 April 2012).

  • Feeling tired or short of breath
  • Feeling generally ill, drowsy, or finding it difficult to concentrate
  • Feeling sick or vomiting
  • Needing to go to the toilet more or less often than usual to pass urine. You may need to get up at night to go to the toilet
  • Puffiness around your eyes or swelling in your limbs (called oedema)
  • Muscle cramps
  • Losing your appetite or losing weight without trying. You may find your sense of taste changes
  • Noticing your urine appearing foamy or much darker in colour
  • Rashes or itchy skin
  • You may lose interest in sex (and if you’re a man, you may be less able to get or maintain an erection)
  • Pain, including aching bones or joints.

Not everyone gets all these symptoms. You may not get them for many years, or at all.

Why has this happened to me?

Established kidney failure happens when chronic kidney disease gets worse. Certain risk factors make this more likely:[2]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Chronic kidney disease: Early identification and management of chronic kidney disease in adults in primary and secondary care. September 2008. Clinical guideline 73. Available at http://publications.nice.org.uk/chronic-kidney-disease-cg73/guidance (accessed on 19 April 2012).

  • Having diabetes
  • Having high blood pressure
  • Being older 
  • Your ethnic origin (people of African, African-Caribbean, or South Asian descent are more likely to have chronic kidney disease than other people in the UK)
  • Heart disease
  • Smoking
  • Being overweight
  • Having kidney disease in your family.

Your doctor will probably be able to tell you the cause of your kidney failure, but sometimes the cause isn't clear.

 

How do I find out I have established kidney failure?

Most people find out they have established kidney failure after having blood tests. The main blood test to diagnose established kidney failure is called a serum creatinine test.

Serum creatinine measures a chemical in the blood. It is used to calculate what is called the estimated glomular filtration rate (eGFR for short). The eGFR calculation is a calculation of how much blood your kidneys are able to filter in one minute. This number is about the same as your kidney function. So if you have 100 percent kidney function, your eGFR is also likely to be 100.

Your eGFR is used by doctors to put your kidney disease into ‘stages’.  There are five stages of kidney disease. If you have completely normal kidney function, you have an eGFR of 90 or over. If you have an eGFR between 15 and 90, you have some kidney damage (stages 1 to 4).

If your eGFR is less than 15, you have chronic kidney disease stage 5, which is established kidney failure.

How many people have established kidney failure?

Around 3.5 million people in the UK have chronic kidney disease stage 3 to 5.[3]Stevens PE, O’Donoghue DJ, de Lusignan S, et al. Chronic kidney disease management in the United Kingdom: NEOERICA project results. Kidney International. 2007; 72: 92-99.

Around 1 or 2 people in every 100 people with chronic kidney disease stage 3A or 3B will go on to get established renal failure.[4]Keith DS, Gregory AN, Gullion CM, et al. Longitudinal follow-up and outcomes among a population with chronic kidney disease in a large managed care organization. Archives of Internal Medicine. 2004; 164: 659-663. 

About 50,000 people in the UK have had kidney transplants, are having dialysis for established kidney failure, or are waiting for a kidney transplant.[5]Renal Association UK Renal Registry. The thirteenth annual report, December 2010. Available at http://www.renalreg.com/Reports/2010.html (accessed on 6 June 2012).

What will happen?

There is no cure that can make your kidneys work well again, but some treatments can improve your symptoms, and reduce your risk of complications.

What happens to you will depend on many things:

  • Your state of health, age, and any other illnesses you have
  • How quickly your kidney function is worsening
  • Which treatment you choose
  • Whether you are able to have a transplant from a living donor, or go on the waiting list for a deceased donor transplant.

Nothing can make your kidneys work well again, but some treatments can improve your symptoms, and reduce your risk of complications.

Complications from kidney disease include:[6]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence Chronic kidney disease: Early identification and management of chronic kidney disease in adults in primary and secondary care. Clinical Guideline 73. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/cg73 (accessed on 6 June 2012).

  • High blood pressure, which can increase your chances of a heart attack or stroke
  • Anaemia, which can make you very tired
  • Weaker bones, which means you are more likely to have a fracture.

Drugs can manage or prevent some of these complications. If you have been treated for chronic kidney disease, you may already be taking some of these medicines.

If you have established kidney failure, treatments can help take over the work of your kidneys and improve your symptoms.

  • Kidney dialysis takes on some of the work of your kidneys, by removing the extra fluid and waste products from your blood.
  • A kidney transplant takes over all of the work of your kidneys, by providing you with a new kidney.
  • Conservative care treats your symptoms but does not take over the work of your kidneys.

Some people with kidney disease have kidney function that stays stable, or gets lower only slowly. The speed at which your kidney function gets worse is one factor that may help you decide which treatment to choose.

But your kidney function is not the only thing that affects what happens to you. Your general state of health, age, and any other illnesses you have are also important.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT HOSPITAL OR CLINIC HAEMODIALYSIS

What is haemodialysis in clinic or hospital?

Kidney dialysis keeps you healthy by filtering your blood, taking on some of the work that your kidneys can no longer do for themselves. Dialysis is a treatment that removes the extra fluid and waste products from your blood. 

During haemodialysis, you are attached to a machine that takes unfiltered blood from your body, and removes the waste, before the blood is transferred back into your body. You usually need haemodialysis at least three times a week, for at least four hours each session. However, the amount of time varies from person to person.[7]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Chronic kidney disease quality standard. November 2011. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/guidance/qualitystandards/chronickidneydisease/ckdqualitystandard.jsp (accessed on 20 April 2012). 

Having haemodialysis in clinic or hospital means you will travel to your nearest dialysis unit to have your dialysis. Usually staff at the unit will handle the dialysis. Some hospitals have units where you can go to do your haemodialysis yourself, with help from the staff if you need it.

You can change your mind about where you have haemodialysis. If you start having haemodialysis in a clinic, you could switch to having it done at home later on.

You will need to have two needles inserted for haemodialysis, so that blood can be removed from your body and then put back in. After the needles are in place, tubing is attached and connected to the dialysis machine. Blood flows out of your body through one needle to the dialysis machine, and then back into your body through another.

The blood that is taken from your body is cleaned in a filter known as a dialyser. Dialysis uses a membrane as a filter. Dialysis fluid (also called dialysis solution, or dialysate) controls the balance of fluid, salts, and minerals in your blood. The membrane has tiny holes that the excess fluid and substances in the blood pass through to be taken away in the dialysis fluid.

You need to sit or lie down while you are attached to the machine for treatment. You can read, work, or even do sitting-down exercises.

The first step before starting haemodialysis is to create an easy point of access to your blood vessels, called a vascular access point. This is so blood can flow in and out of your body. It is done before you begin dialysis treatment.[8]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Chronic kidney disease quality standard. November 2011. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/guidance/qualitystandards/chronickidneydisease/ckdqualitystandard.jsp (accessed on 6 June 2012). This makes dialysis quicker and easier, and reduces the risk of problems.

There are three kinds of vascular access - an arteriovenous (AV) fistula, an AV graft, and a venous catheter. 

AV fistulas

A surgeon creates an AV fistula by connecting an artery directly to a vein, usually in your arm. For the surgery, you’ll usually be given a local anaesthetic to numb the area where the fistula is being created. Most people don’t need to stay in hospital for the operation.

Connecting the artery to the vein causes more blood to flow into the vein. As a result, the vein grows larger and stronger, making it easier for blood to pass in and out. 

This operation should be done several months before you start dialysis, because it takes time for the vein to grow. A fistula is less likely than other kinds of vascular access to form blood clots or become infected.  It should last for many years, and usually longer than any other kind of vascular access.[9]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Vascular access for haemodialysis. September 2010. Available at http://kidney.niddk.nih.gov/KUDiseases/pubs/vascularaccess/index.aspx (accessed on 6 June 2012).

AV grafts

If your veins don’t develop into an adequate fistula, you can get a tube - called a graft - instead. A graft connects an artery to a vein and is implanted under the skin in your arm. The graft becomes an artificial vein that can be used during haemodialysis. A graft doesn’t need to grow in the way a fistula does, so it can be used sooner, often within two or three weeks. Compared with properly formed fistulas, grafts tend to have more problems with clotting and infection and need replacement sooner.[9]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Vascular access for haemodialysis. September 2010. Available at http://kidney.niddk.nih.gov/KUDiseases/pubs/vascularaccess/index.aspx (accessed on 6 June 2012). However, a well-cared-for graft can last several years.

Catheters

If your kidney disease has worsened quickly, you may not have time to get a permanent vascular access before you start haemodialysis. A venous catheter can be used as a temporary access.

A catheter is a soft flexible plastic tube inserted into a vein in your neck, chest, or leg near the groin. It has two channels to allow a two-way flow of blood.

Catheters are not ideal for permanent access. They can clog, become infected, and cause narrowing of the veins in which they are placed. But if you need to start haemodialysis immediately, a catheter will work for several weeks or months while a permanent access develops. Some people are not able to have a permanent AV fistula or graft, and so need to have a catheter inserted permanently. Some people choose to have a permanent catheter to avoid having to use needles, or having an operation to insert a graft. 

Other treatments

If you need haemodialysis, you are likely to need other medical treatments as well. You may need to take:

  • Vitamins
  • Iron
  • Injections of drugs called erythropoietin to treat anaemia
  • Phosphate binders
  • Blood pressure medications
  • Insulin, if you have diabetes.
Who can choose haemodialysis in clinic or hospital?

Usually, people with established kidney failure can choose which type of dialysis they want.

There is no right or wrong way to have dialysis, and it is usually possible to change from one type of treatment to another. You can discuss this with your care team.

What are the effects of haemodialysis in clinic or hospital on symptoms?

Unless you are very sick for reasons other than your kidney disease, dialysis should help you feel better. Most people will notice a difference after a few weeks. 

Although it can take time to get used to, dialysis should not make your health worse. If dialysis is working well, you should feel well, look healthy, and be able to do the things you want to do.

Doctors use the term dialysis adequacy to describe the amount of dialysis you need to be well. You may have to make changes to your treatment to make sure the dialysis is adequate. If your dialysis is not adequate, you may feel tired and weak, feel sick, and not want to eat.

Anaemia, which can make you feel tired and weak, seems to be equally likely regardless of the type of dialysis you have.[22]Richardson D, Ford D, Gilg J, et al. Pain is a common symptom for people having dialysis. Among people having any type of dialysis, around 50 in 100 dialysis patients aged over 55 feel long-term physical pain. What are the effects of haemodialysis in clinic or hospital on what you can do?

You will need to fit your daily activities around your dialysis. This will vary according to the type of dialysis you have.

Work life

It may be possible for you to work while having dialysis. But it is likely that working during dialysis will mean adjustments for you, your employer, and your family.

Dialysis can make you feel extremely tired, and this may prevent you from working. Employers are required by law to make reasonable adjustments to allow you to work, and this can include giving you time off to attend appointments.

If you have haemodialysis you will need to make time for your dialysis sessions. It may be possible for you to work around your dialysis. If you have haemodialysis in hospital or a dialysis unit, they should do all they can to arrange your appointments so that your treatment can fit in with your work schedule. But dialysis appointments are often during the day and this may make it more difficult to go to work.

Social life and travel

If you have haemodialysis, you will usually have treatment in hospital at least three times a week. But you don't usually need hospital treatment between sessions. You have some time for days out, short holidays, and other activities. 

Exercise

Many people who are on dialysis feel out of breath and extremely tired. But some people find regular and moderate exercise helps, if they can manage it. Exercises such as walking, jogging, cycling, or swimming may be suitable for people on dialysis. A study of people on dialysis showed that people who exercised up to five times a week lived longer than other people on dialysis.[27]Stack AG, Molony DA, Rives T, et al. Association of physical activity with mortality in the US dialysis population. American Journal of Kidney Diseases. 2005; 45: 690-701.

What are the effects of haemodialysis in clinic or hospital on your quality of life?

If dialysis works well, it should help you to feel better. But dialysis takes up a lot of time and restricts how you live your life. It can put strain on your relationships with people close to you.

Having dialysis in a hospital or clinic might mean your quality of life is not as good as if have dialysis at home. One group of people having dialysis were asked about how happy they were with their quality of life. Those having home dialysis were happier. But that might have been because people who choose to have home haemodialysis may have a better quality of life to begin with.[28]Home haemodialysis an effective alternative to hospital or satellite unit haemodialysis. What are the unwanted side effects of haemodialysis in clinic or hospital?

Haemodyalisis can have unwanted side effects. These are some common ones:

  • Tiredness. Haemodyalisis can make you feel exhausted and short of breath immediately after treatment. However, this can also be caused by anaemia, which is a common problem with established kidney failure. So it’s not always easy to tell whether your fatigue is caused by dialysis or not.
  • Itchy skin and muscle cramps. The itchy skin is thought to be because of a build up of phosphate in your blood. The muscle cramps often happen in your lower legs as your muscles adjust to the fluid loss during dialysis.
  • Restless legs. People having haemodialysis sometimes get restless legs. This means that they have a strong urge to move their legs caused by an unpleasant feeling in these limbs. This feeling is worse when they are not active and at night.Kavanagh D, Siddiqui S, Geddes CC. Restless legs syndrome in patients on dialysis. American Journal of Kidney Diseases. 2004; 43: 763-71.
  • Low blood pressure. This can be caused by the sudden drop in fluid levels that happens during dialysis. Low blood pressure can make you feel sick and dizzy during or soon after dialysis.
  • Bone pain. People on any type of dialysis may not have the right amounts of minerals like calcium and phosphorus. This can cause pain in the bones and joints, weaker bones, and may make you more likely to have fractures and broken bones.[30]National Kidney and Urologic Diseases Information Clearinghouse. How will haemodialysis in clinic or hospital affect my life?

Time spent in hospital or having treatment

In one group of people aged 70 or over, those having any type of dialysis spent on average 25 days in hospital each year.[36]Carson RC, Juszczak M, Davenport A, et al. Is maximum conservative management an equivalent treatment option to dialysis for elderly patients with significant comorbid disease? Clinical Journal of the American Society of Nephrology. 2009; 4: 1611–1619.

If you have haemodialysis, you will probably need to be attached to a dialysis machine at least three times a week, for four or more hours each time. However, the amount of time varies from person to person. You can’t get up and move around while this is happening. You will also need blood tests and checks, to see whether your treatment is working well enough. These tests and checks may have to be done in a hospital or clinic.

Diet and fluid restrictions

All types of dialysis involve some restrictions to your diet and what you can drink. This can take some adjustment for you and any family members who help prepare your meals. You will be able to speak to a dietitian or other member of your health care team to discuss how best to make changes to your diet.

Most people having haemodialysis need to adjust their diet. The restrictions on your diet and how much fluid you drink can be quite strict.

People on haemodialysis may be encouraged to eat high-quality protein from meat, fish, poultry, and eggs.[37]National Kidney and Urologic Diseases Information Clearinghouse. Haemodialysis. December 2011. Available at http://kidney.niddk.nih.gov/KUDiseases/pubs/hemodialysis/index.aspx#diet (accessed 18 April 2012).

You may need to cut down on foods high in potassium, phosphate, and salt.

You will need to restrict how much fluid you drink. This is needed because your body will gradually make less and less urine once you are having haemodialysis. Excess fluid is taken out during dialysis sessions, but it can build up between sessions, causing puffiness. It can damage your tissues if this happens long-term. So you need to ensure you are not taking in more fluid than you’re getting rid of in urine.

What is home haemodialysis?

Kidney dialysis keeps you healthy by filtering your blood, taking on some of the work that your kidneys can no longer do for themselves. Dialysis is a treatment that removes the extra fluid and waste products from your blood. 

Haemodialysis

During haemodialysis, you are attached to a machine that takes unfiltered blood from your body, and removes the waste, before the blood is transferred back into your body. You will probably need haemodialysis at least three times a week, for at least four hours each session. However, the amount of time varies from person to person.[7]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Chronic kidney disease quality standard. November 2011. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/guidance/qualitystandards/chronickidneydisease/ckdqualitystandard.jsp (accessed on 20 April 2012).

You will need to have two needles inserted for haemodialysis, so that blood can be removed from your body and then put back in. After the needles are in place, tubing is attached and connected to the dialysis machine. Blood flows out of your body through one needle to the dialysis machine, and then back into your body through another.

The blood that is taken from your body is cleaned in a filter known as a dialyser. Dialysis uses a membrane as a filter. Dialysis fluid (also called dialysis solution, or dialysate) controls the balance of fluid, salts, and minerals in your blood. The membrane has tiny holes that the excess fluid and substances in the blood pass through to be taken away in the dialysis fluid.

You need to sit or lie down while you are attached to the machine for treatment.

Having haemodialysis at home means you have a kidney dialysis machine in your home and you manage the dialysis process yourself.[39]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Renal failure - home versus hospital haemodialysis: guidance. October 2002. Technical appraisal 48. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/TA48 (accessed on 20 April 2012).

You’ll be able to discuss your questions and concerns with your health care team. You won’t be expected to do everything yourself at first. If you’re having haemodialysis at home, but decide you’d prefer to have it done in a clinic, that can be arranged.

The first step before starting haemodialysis is to create an easy point of access to your blood vessels, called a vascular access point. This is so blood can flow in and out of your body. It is done before you begin dialysis treatment.[8]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Chronic kidney disease quality standard. November 2011. There are three kinds of vascular access - an arteriovenous (AV) fistula, an AV graft, and a venous catheter.

AV fistulas

A surgeon creates an AV fistula by connecting an artery directly to a vein, usually in your arm. For the surgery, you’ll usually be given a local anaesthetic to numb the area where the fistula is being created. Most people don’t need to stay in hospital for the operation.

Connecting the artery to the vein causes more blood to flow into the vein. As a result, the vein grows larger and stronger, making it easier for blood to pass in and out. 

This operation should be done several months before you start dialysis, because it takes time for the vein to grow. A fistula is less likely than other kinds of vascular access to form blood clots or become infected.  It should last for many years, and usually longer than any other kind of vascular access.[9]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Vascular access for haemodialysis. September 2010. Available at http://kidney.niddk.nih.gov/KUDiseases/pubs/vascularaccess/index.aspx (accessed on 6 June 2012).

AV grafts

If your veins don’t develop into an adequate fistula, you can get a tube - called a graft - instead. A graft connects an artery to a vein and is implanted under the skin in your arm. The graft becomes an artificial vein that can be used during haemodialysis. A graft doesn’t need to grow in the way a fistula does, so it can be used sooner, often within two or three weeks. Compared with properly formed fistulas, grafts tend to have more problems with clotting and infection and need replacement sooner.[9]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Vascular access for haemodialysis. September 2010. Available at http://kidney.niddk.nih.gov/KUDiseases/pubs/vascularaccess/index.aspx (accessed on 6 June 2012). However, a well-cared-for graft can last several years.

Catheters

If your kidney disease has worsened quickly, you may not have time to get a permanent vascular access before you start haemodialysis. A venous catheter can be used as a temporary access.

A catheter is a soft flexible plastic tube inserted into a vein in your neck, chest, or leg near the groin. It has two channels to allow a two-way flow of blood.

Catheters are not ideal for permanent access. They can clog, become infected, and cause narrowing of the veins in which they are placed. But if you need to start haemodialysis immediately, a catheter will work for several weeks or months while a permanent access develops. Some people are not able to have a permanent AV fistula or graft, and so need to have a catheter inserted permanently. Some people choose to have a permanent catheter to avoid having to use needles, or having an operation to insert a graft.

Other treatments

Whichever type of dialysis you choose, you are likely to need other medical treatments as well. You may need to take:

  • Vitamins
  • Iron
  • Injections of drugs called erythropoietin to treat anaemia
  • Phosphate binders
  • Blood pressure medications
  • Insulin, if you have diabetes.

  • Whether your vascular access works well

You need a clean and hygienic place for you to exchange dialysis fluid and set up dialysis machines if you have it at home. You also need space to store dialysis fluid and some equipment to dispose of if after dialysis.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT CONTINUOUS AMBULATORY PERITONEAL DIALYSIS (CAPD)

What is continuous ambulatory peritoneal dialysis?

Kidney dialysis keeps you healthy by filtering your blood, taking on some of the work that your kidneys can no longer do for themselves. Dialysis is a treatment that removes the extra fluid and waste products from your blood.

Peritoneal dialysis

In peritoneal dialysis, instead of using a machine to filter your blood, the internal lining of your abdomen acts as the artificial kidney. This means you don’t have to stay in one place while having peritoneal dialysis.

The internal walls of your abdomen are lined with a membrane called the peritoneum. This membrane is used for dialysis. In peritoneal dialysis, dialysis fluid (also called a dialysis solution, or dialysate) is flushed into your abdomen, through a catheter. Waste products from your blood are pulled through the membrane (the peritoneum) into the dialysis fluid, along with excess water. This is drained away, and replaced with clean dialysis fluid.

A cycle of peritoneal dialysis usually needs to be done several times a day, every day. The whole cycle of draining and filling usually takes less than 45 minutes. How long the dialysis fluid is left inside your abdomen varies. It depends on what kind of peritoneal dialysis you have and the kind of treatment you need. But it usually needs to stay in your abdomen for several hours.

Before starting peritoneal dialysis, you need to have a catheter inserted into your abdomen. This is so the dialysis fluid can flow in and out. It’s called a Tenckhoff catheter and you’ll need an operation to put it in. You might have a general anaesthetic to put you to sleep during the surgery. Or you might have a local anaesthetic to numb the area so you can’t feel the catheter being put in. You may have to stay in hospital for a day or two afterwards. The catheter is usually ready to be used after two or three weeks. It remains in place. Some people find this difficult to get used to.

Continuous ambulatory peritoneal dialysis (CAPD)

If you have this kind of peritoneal dialysis, you do your own dialysis, and can walk and move around with the dialysis solution in your abdomen. You flush 1 to 3 litres of dialysis solution into your abdomen four times a day, every day. You then drain out the dialysis solution after several hours before adding dialysis solution again. This cycle is called an exchange. You can be flexible about how you time the exchanges. Although most people need four exchanges a day, they don’t have to be exactly six hours apart, for example. You’ll need to attach the new bags and dispose of the old ones for each exchange. Your nurses will teach you how to manage this. You don't need a machine for CAPD. All you need is gravity to fill and empty your abdomen.

Other treatments

Whichever type of dialysis you choose, you are likely to need other medical treatments as well. You may need to take:

  • Vitamins
  • Iron
  • Injections of drugs called erythropoietin to treat anaemia
  • Phosphate binders
  • Blood pressure medications
  • Insulin, if you have diabetes.
Who can choose continuous ambulatory peritoneal dialysis?

Usually, people with established kidney failure can choose which type of dialysis they want.

There is no right or wrong way to have dialysis, and it is usually possible to change from one type of treatment to another. This often happens with peritoneal dialysis, as some people can't stay on this type of dialysis long term. You can discuss this with your care team.

Peritoneal dialysis is also not suitable for everyone. It may be less suitable for people with very little kidney function, or who have other major illnesses. People who have had operations on their abdomen in the past, people with certain bowel conditions such as diverticulitis, or people who are very overweight, may not be able to have peritoneal dialysis.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT AUTOMATED PERITONEAL DIALYSIS (APD)

What is automated peritoneal dialysis?

Kidney dialysis keeps you healthy by filtering your blood, taking on some of the work that your kidneys can no longer do for themselves. Dialysis is a treatment that removes the extra fluid and waste products from your blood.

Peritoneal dialysis

In peritoneal dialysis, instead of using a machine to filter your blood, the internal lining of your abdomen acts as the artificial kidney. This means you don’t have to stay in one place while having peritoneal dialysis.

The internal walls of your abdomen are lined with a membrane called the peritoneum. This membrane is used for dialysis. In peritoneal dialysis, dialysis fluid (also called a dialysis solution, or dialysate) is flushed into your abdomen, through a catheter. Waste products from your blood are pulled through the membrane (the peritoneum) into the dialysis fluid, along with excess water. This is drained away, and replaced with clean dialysis fluid.

A cycle of peritoneal dialysis usually needs to be done several times a day, every day. The whole cycle of draining and filling usually takes less than 45 minutes. How long the dialysis fluid is left inside your abdomen varies. It depends on what kind of peritoneal dialysis you have and the kind of treatment you need. But it usually needs to stay in your abdomen for several hours.

Before starting peritoneal dialysis, you need to have a catheter inserted into your abdomen. This is so the dialysis fluid can flow in and out. It’s called a Tenckhoff catheter and you’ll need an operation to put it in. You might have a general anaesthetic to put you to sleep during the surgery. Or you might have a local anaesthetic to numb the area so you can’t feel the catheter being put in. You may have to stay in hospital for a day or two afterwards. The catheter is usually ready to be used after two or three weeks. It remains in place. Some people find this difficult to get used to.

Automated peritoneal dialysis (APD)

This type of peritoneal dialysis uses a machine that performs three to five exchanges during the night, usually while you sleep. The machine automatically adds and then drains the dialysis fluid for you. You have the machine at home. Most people need to spend eight to 10 hours attached to the machine every night. In the morning, you begin one exchange that usually lasts the entire day. After spending the night on the machine, most people keep fluid inside their abdomen during the day, but you will not need to be connected to the dialysis machine during this time.

Some people have help from nurses or other health care professionals to have assisted automated peritoneal dialysis (aAPD) at home.

Other treatments

Whichever type of dialysis you choose, you are likely to need other medical treatments as well. You may need to take:

  • Vitamins
  • Iron
  • Injections of drugs called erythropoietin to treat anaemia
  • Phosphate binders
  • Blood pressure medications
  • Insulin, if you have diabetes.
Who can choose automated peritoneal dialysis?

Usually, people with established kidney failure can choose which type of dialysis they want.

There is no right or wrong way to have dialysis, and it is usually possible to change from one type of treatment to another. This often happens with peritoneal dialysis, as some people can't stay on this type of dialysis long term. You can discuss this with your care team.

Peritoneal dialysis is also not suitable for everyone. It may be less suitable for people with very little kidney function, or who have other major illnesses. People who have had operations on their abdomen in the past, people with certain bowel conditions such as diverticulitis, or people who are very overweight, may not be able to have peritoneal dialysis.

You need a clean and hygienic place for you to exchange dialysis fluid and set up dialysis machines if you have it at home. You also need space to store dialysis fluid and some equipment to dispose of if after dialysis.
