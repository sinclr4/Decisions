STEP 5: MY DECISION

Our Decision Support telephone service is here to assist and guide you through the Shared Decision Making process.

The Decision Support team is made of professional Health Coaches who are clinicians, commonly nurses, who have undertaken additional training to become a Health Coach.

The Health Coaches will assist you by:

  • Providing you with information
  • Helping you to understand your condition
  • Recognise what is important to you regarding the outcome of treatment
  • Identifying potential solutions
  • Encourage you in discussing options with your family
  • Transferring skills which will assist you in using the information and resources available to you
  • Support you in building confidence in discussing your choice with your doctor
  • You may find that this can be achieved in one telephone call with a Health Coach however; if further calls are required to support you in reaching your decision the Health Coach will schedule these with you.

Important Things To Note

  • A Health Coach does not know which treatment option is best for you.
  • The Health Coach does not have information about your medical history.
  • You will still need to talk over your decision and reasons for your decision with your doctor or nurse.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT CONSERVATIVE CARE

What is conservative care?

Conservative care is a package of treatments that treats your symptoms. It doesn’t filter extra fluid and waste products out of your blood. The aim of conservative care is to make you comfortable and relieve your symptoms, not to extend your life. 

Conservative care is sometimes called non-dialysis care, non-dialytic care, supportive care, or conservative management. If you have conservative care, you don’t have kidney dialysis or a kidney transplant. Instead, you have treatments to manage your symptoms. These are treatments you are probably already taking. Even if you decide to have dialysis or a transplant, you may continue to take some of these treatments as well.

Which treatments you take depends on what symptoms you have. They can include:[1]Jassal SV, Kelman EE, Watson D. Non-dialysis care: an important component of care for elderly individuals with advanced stages of chronic kidney disease. Nephron Clinical Practice. 2011; 119 (supplement 1): S5-S9.

  • Drugs to help with anaemia. Anaemia can make you tired and weak because your blood doesn’t have enough red blood cells to carry oxygen around the body well enough. You may have an injection of drugs called erythropoietin-stimulating agents (EPO), which help your body to make red blood cells, and have a drip containing iron.
  • Pain relief medicine.
  • Drugs to relieve itching.
  • Drugs called diuretics to reduce puffiness and swelling from excess fluid.
  • Drugs to help with feeling sick.

You may also take medicines to prevent and manage other problems, such as heart disease, bone disease, and high levels of phosphate in your blood.

You will be looked after by a team of health care professionals. The people involved may vary from one hospital to another. The team may include kidney nurses, district nurses, your GP, a dietitian, and a renal physician (kidney doctor). Older people may be looked after by a specialist in elderly care.

If you are relatively well, you may have appointments for treatment in hospital or a clinic. If you are very unwell and you have difficulty getting around, you may be able to have treatment in your home, or in a nursing home or hospice.

Who can choose conservative care?

Anyone can choose conservative care, if they decide that they don’t want to have dialysis or a kidney transplant.

Ideally, you would start thinking about whether you want conservative care or dialysis several months before you start on either treatment. You can start on conservative care at any time. Dialysis takes more preparation time, so it helps to have thought about it in advance.

Many people who choose conservative care are elderly and have other serious illnesses beside kidney disease.[2]Sarbjit V, Jassal EE, Watson KD. Non-Dialysis Care: An important component of care for elderly individuals with advanced stages of chronic kidney disease. Nephron Clinical Practice. 2011; 119 (supplement 1): S5-S9. For example, some people have cancer, heart failure, or dementia, as well as kidney disease. They or their carers may decide that the benefit of having dialysis is outweighed by the side effects and the amount of time they need to spend on dialysis.

Some people choose conservative care because they are frail and near the end of their lives. They may feel that the preparation work for dialysis is likely to take too long or be too disruptive to be worthwhile.

If you decide to try conservative care first, you can change your mind later. For example, if your circumstances change or you start to get more symptoms, you can talk to your doctor about starting dialysis. However, it can take months to prepare for dialysis, so it’s a good idea to be clear about your choices at the start. People who delay the start of dialysis treatment sometimes need to start dialysis as an emergency. This can lead to complications and less effective treatment.[3]Thilly N, Boini S, Soudant M, et al. Outcomes of patients with delayed dialysis initiation: Results from the AVENIR study. American Journal of Nephrology. 2011; 33: 76-83. [4]Wong CFM. Factors affecting survival in advanced chronic kidney disease patients who choose not to receive dialysis. Renal Failure. 2007; 29: 653-659.

What are the effects of conservative care on your length of life?

Conservative care does not prolong your life.

Your length of life after starting on conservative care depends on many things. The main ones are how healthy you are, which other illnesses you have, how old you are, how much kidney function you still have, and how quickly this is getting worse. 

All the figures we give are based on averages of groups of people in studies. They can’t predict how long you will live personally. 

  • In a group of people over the age of 70, people having conservative care lived on average 14 months (just over one year).[5]Carson RC, Juszczak M, Davenport A, et al. Is maximum conservative management an equivalent treatment option to dialysis for elderly patients with significant comorbid disease? Clinical Journal of the American Society of Nephrology. 2009; 4: 1611- 1619. Pubmed: 19808244
  • In a group of people of different ages, people having conservative care lived on average 21 months (just under two years).[6]Chandna SM, Silva-Gane M, Marshall C, et al. Survival of elderly patients with stage 5 CKD: comparison of conservative management and renal replacement therapy. Nephrology, Dialysis, Transplantation. 2011; 26: 1608-1614.
  • In another group of people, 47 in 100 people having conservative care were alive two years after the start of treatment.[7]Murtagh FEM, Marsh JE, Donohoe P, et al. Dialysis or not? A comparative survival study of patients over 75 years with chronic kidney disease stage 5. Nephrology, Dialysis, Transplantation. 2007; 22: 1955–1962.

It is likely that many people didn’t die of their kidney disease, but of their other illnesses. One study found that only half of people having conservative care for established kidney failure actually died as a result of kidney failure. The other half died of an infection, heart disease or stroke, or cancer.[8]Personal communication, Dr Andrew Mooney, March 2012.

What are the effects of conservative care on symptoms?

Conservative care aims to treat your symptoms, to make you more comfortable. This doesn’t mean you will have no symptoms at all. 

In one group of older people with established renal failure who chose to have conservative care:[7]Murtagh FEM, Marsh JE, Donohoe P, et al. Dialysis or not? A comparative survival study of patients over 75 years with chronic kidney disease stage 5. Nephrology, Dialysis, Transplantation. 2007; 22: 1955–1962.

  • 75 in every 100 people felt weak
  • 58 in every 100 people had a poor appetite
  • 56 in every 100 people had pain
  • 56 in every 100 people had itching
  • 49 in every 100 people were short of breath.

However, some of these symptoms may have been caused by illnesses other than renal failure.

Anaemia - a common cause of weakness - can be effectively treated by the drugs used in conservative care.  In one group, everyone having conservative care had blood that was no longer anaemic within a couple of months of seeing a specialist nurse.[4]Wong CFM. Factors affecting survival in advanced chronic kidney disease patients who choose not to receive dialysis. Renal Failure. 2007; 29: 653-659.

What are the effects of conservative care on what you can do?

How much you can do when you have conservative care is down to how well you feel, whether you are able to walk easily, and whether you get very tired. 

You’re not usually restricted by the effects of the treatments you have with conservative care, such as needing to spend time in hospital having treatment, needing to take equipment with you, or resting after treatments.

Work life

If you are well enough to work, there’s no reason why you can’t do so.

Social life and travel

If you are well enough to have a social life and to travel, there’s no reason why you can’t do so. That includes travel overseas.

In one group of older people with established renal failure, 75 in every 100 people had some difficulty getting around. More than half of these people said their difficulty in getting around was moderate or severe.[7]Murtagh FEM, Marsh JE, Donohoe P, et al. Dialysis or not? A comparative survival study of patients over 75 years with chronic kidney disease stage 5. Nephrology, Dialysis, Transplantation. 2007; 22: 1955–1962. This might affect your ability to go shopping, take holidays, or visit friends and family.

Exercise

If you are well enough to exercise, there’s no reason why you can’t do so. 

What are the effects of conservative care on your quality of life?

We don’t know how conservative care affects how much you enjoy life. But people who wait before starting dialysis say they have a similar quality of life to those who are already having dialysis. This applies to their mental health, physical symptoms, and ability to do things.[3]Thilly N, Boini S, Soudant M, et al. Outcomes of patients with delayed dialysis initiation: Results from the AVENIR study. American Journal of Nephrology. 2011; 33: 76-83.

What are the unwanted side effects of conservative care?

You don’t get side effects from choosing conservative care. This because having conservative care doesn't usually involve taking new treatments. However, you may get side effects from the treatments you're already taking to manage your symptoms.

How will the treatment affect my life?

Time in hospital and having treatment

The time you spend having treatment will depend on the treatments you have as part of conservative care.  Time in hospital is likely to depend on how well you are, and whether you have other serious illnesses.

In one group of people aged 70 or over with established renal failure, people choosing conservative care spent on average 16 days in hospital each year.[13]Carson RC, Juszczak M, Davenport A, Burns A: Is maximum conservative management an equivalent treatment option to dialysis for elderly patients with significant comorbid disease? Clin J Am Soc Nephrol 2009; 4: 1611–1619

But in another group, 60 in 100 people having conservative care didn’t need to stay in hospital at all. Of those who did, more than half of admissions to hospital were for conditions other than their kidney disease.[4]Wong CFM. Factors affecting survival in advanced chronic kidney disease patients who choose not to receive dialysis. Renal Failure. 2007; 29: 653-659. The average length of stay for people who were admitted to hospital for their kidney disease was 10 days.

Restrictions on diet and fluid

When you start conservative care, your dietitian may suggest a diet that controls salt and potassium, to protect the amount of renal function you have left. But as time goes on, especially if you have a poor appetite and are losing weight, you may be able to eat whatever you want.[2]Sarbjit V, Jassal EE, Watson KD. Non-Dialysis Care: An important component of care for elderly individuals with advanced stages of chronic kidney disease. Nephron Clinical Practice. 2011; 119 (supplement 1): S5-S9.

Whether you need to restrict the amount of fluid you drink will depend on your symptoms. If excess fluid is causing swelling or puffiness, you may need to cut down on salt and fluid as much as is comfortable.

End of life

As you approach the end of your life, you may like to consider choices you have concerning death, such as where you’d prefer to be cared for when you die. People who choose conservative care are more likely to die at home or in a hospice or nursing home, rather than in hospital.[8]Personal communication, Dr Andrew Mooney, March 2012. If this is important to you, you can discuss your wishes with your doctors and nurses.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT KIDNEY DIALYSIS

What is kidney dialysis?

Kidney dialysis aims to keeps you healthy by filtering your blood, taking on some of the work that your kidneys can no longer do for themselves. Dialysis is a treatment that removes the extra fluid and waste products from your blood. 

There are two main types of dialysis treatment - haemodialysis and peritoneal dialysis.

Haemodialysis

During haemodialysis, a machine takes unfiltered blood from your body and removes the waste, before the blood is transferred back into your body. You usually need haemodialysis at least three times a week, for at least four hours each session. However, the amount of time varies from person to person.[17]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Chronic kidney disease quality standard. November 2011. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/guidance/qualitystandards/chronickidneydisease/ckdqualitystandard.jsp (accessed on 20 April 2012).

Haemodialysis can either be done in hospital, in a dialysis centre, or in your own home.[18]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Renal failure - home versus hospital haemodialysis: guidance. October 2002. Technical appraisal 48. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/TA48 (accessed on 20 April 2012). You need to sit or lie down while you are attached to the machine for treatment. 

To allow blood to flow in and out of your body, you need an easy point of access to your blood vessels, called a vascular access point. You may have an operation to create an access point from your own blood vessels (called an AV fistula) or surgery to implant a piece of tube under your skin (called an AV graft).[17]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Chronic kidney disease quality standard. November 2011. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/guidance/qualitystandards/chronickidneydisease/ckdqualitystandard.jsp (accessed on 20 April 2012).

These are both permanent access points, which make dialysis quicker and easier, and reduce the risk of problems. However, if your kidney disease worsens quickly, you may not have time to get a permanent access point before you start haemodialysis. In this case, a catheter can be used for a temporary access while a permanent access is created. A catheter is a soft flexible plastic tube inserted into a vein. Some people have a catheter inserted permanently.

Peritoneal dialysis

In peritoneal dialysis, instead of using a machine to filter your blood, the internal lining of your abdomen acts as the artificial kidney. This means you don’t have to stay in one place while having peritoneal dialysis.

The internal walls of your abdomen are lined with a membrane called the peritoneum. This membrane is used for dialysis. In peritoneal dialysis, dialysis fluid (also called a dialysis solution, or dialysate) is flushed into your abdomen, through a catheter. Waste products from your blood are pulled through the membrane (the peritoneum) into the dialysis fluid, along with excess water. This is drained away, and replaced with clean dialysis fluid.

A cycle of peritoneal dialysis usually needs to be done several times a day, every day. The whole cycle of draining and filling usually takes less than 45 minutes. How long the dialysis fluid is left inside your abdomen varies. But it usually needs to stay in your abdomen for several hours.

Before starting peritoneal dialysis, you need to have a catheter inserted into your abdomen. This is so the dialysis fluid can flow in and out. It’s called a Tenckhoff catheter and you’ll need an operation to put it in. The catheter remains in place. Some people find this difficult to get used to.

There are two main ways to have peritoneal dialysis.

  • Continuous ambulatory peritoneal dialysis (CAPD): You flush 1 to 3 litres of dialysis solution into your abdomen four times a day, every day. You drain out the dialysis solution after several hours before adding dialysis solution again. This cycle is called an exchange. You can walk and move around with the dialysis solution in your abdomen. 
  • Automated peritoneal dialysis (APD): This type of dialysis uses a machine that performs three to five exchanges during the night, usually while you sleep. You have the machine at home. Most people need to spend eight to 10 hours attached to the machine every night. In the morning, you begin one exchange that usually lasts the entire day. 

Other treatments

Whichever type of dialysis you choose, you are likely to need other medical treatments as well. You may need to take:

  • Vitamins
  • Iron
  • Injections of drugs called erythropoietin to treat anaemia
  • Phosphate binders
  • Blood pressure medications
  • Insulin, if you have diabetes.
Who can choose dialysis?

Most people with kidney failure can have dialysis. Usually, people with established kidney failure can choose which type of dialysis they want.

There is no right or wrong way to have dialysis, and it is usually possible to change from one type of treatment to another. You can discuss this with your care team. 

What are the effects of dialysis on your length of life?

It’s common for people to live for many years while having dialysis treatment. Your length of life after starting on dialysis depends on many things. The main ones are your age, how healthy you are, how much kidney function you have left, and how quickly it's getting worse.[20]National Institute of Health and Clinical Excellence. Chronic kidney disease: Early identification and management of chronic kidney disease in adults in primary and secondary care. September 2008. Clinical guideline 73. They can’t predict how long you will live personally.

Is maximum conservative management an equivalent treatment option to dialysis for elderly patients with significant comorbid disease? Clinical Journal of the American Society of Nephrology. 2009; 4: 1611-1619.
  • In a group of people of different ages, people having dialysis lived on average 67 months.[23]van de Luijtgaarden M, Noordzij M, Stel VS, et al. Effects of comorbid and demographic factors on dialysis modality choice and related patient survival in Europe. Nephrology Dialysis Transplantation. 2011; 26: 2940-2947.
  • In one group of patients aged over 75, 76 in 100 people who had any type of dialysis were alive two years after starting treatment.[7]Murtagh FEM, Marsh JE, Donohoe P, et al. Dialysis or not? In the UK, between 95 in 100 and 99 in 100 people aged 18 to 54 are alive a year after starting dialysis.[25]Castledinea C, Steenkampa R, Feesta T, et al. We don’t know whether you live longer if you choose haemodialysis or peritoneal dialysis.

Starting dialysis early (when your eGFR is 10 or above) does not seem to make any difference to how long you live.[27]Cooper BA, Branley P, Bulfone L, et al. A randomized, controlled trial of early versus late initiation of dialysis. New England Journal of Medicine. 2010; 363: 609-619.

People who are on dialysis are living for longer now than in the past because dialysis techniques are improving all the time. In the past, people on dialysis may only have lived a few years. But now some people have been on dialysis for more than 30 years.[7]Murtagh FEM, Marsh JE, Donohoe P, et al. Dialysis or not? What are the effects of dialysis on symptoms?

Unless you are very sick for reasons other than your kidney disease, dialysis should help you feel better. Most people will notice a difference after a few weeks. 

Although it can take time to get used to, dialysis should not make your health worse. If dialysis is working well, you should feel well, look healthy, and be able to do the things you want to do. 

Doctors use the term dialysis adequacy to describe the amount of dialysis you need to be well. You may have to make changes to your treatment to make sure the dialysis is adequate. 

If your dialysis is not adequate, you may feel tired and weak, feel sick, and not want to eat.

Anaemia, which can make you feel tired and weak, seems to be equally likely with haemodialysis or peritoneal dialysis.[29]Richardson D, Ford D, Gilg J, et al. UK Renal Registry Twelfth annual report: Chapter 9: Anaemia variables in UK adult dialysis patients in 2008: national and centre-specific analyses. Nephron. 2010; 115 (supplement 1): S153-S86. [30]North American Pediatric Renal Trials and Collaborative Studies. 2008 Annual Report online. Available from www.emmes.com/study/ped/annlrept/Annual%20Report%20-2008.pdf (accessed on 6 June 2012).

Pain is a common symptom for people having dialysis. Around 50 in 100 dialysis patients aged over 55 feel long-term physical pain. More than 80 in 100 people rated their pain as moderate to severe.[31]Davison SN. Chronic kidney disease: psychosocial impact of chronic pain. Geriatrics. 2007; 62: 17-23.

Depression is also common among people who have dialysis. Between 5 in every 100 and 50 in every 100 people having dialysis have depression.[31]Davison SN. Chronic kidney disease: psychosocial impact of chronic pain. Geriatrics. 2007; 62: 17-23.

Another sign of kidney disease is high blood pressure. In one group of people, 47 in every 100 people having haemodialysis had a healthy blood pressure level and 26 in every 100 people who have peritoneal dialysis had a healthy blood pressure level.[33]Harper J, Nicholas J, Webb L, et al. UK Renal Registry 12th Annual Report (December 2009): Chapter 11: Blood pressure profile of prevalent in the UK in 2008: national and centre-specific analyses. Nephron Clinical Practice. 2010; 115 (supplement 1): S239-S260.

What are the effects of dialysis on what you can do?

You will need to fit your daily activities around your dialysis. This will vary according to the type of dialysis you have.

Work life

It may be possible for you to work while having dialysis. But it is likely that working during dialysis will mean adjustments for you, your employer, and your family.

Dialysis can make you feel extremely tired, and this may prevent you from working. Employers are required by law to make reasonable adjustments to allow you to work, and this can include giving you time off to attend appointments or providing a suitable room in which to do an exchange.

Social life and travel

You can travel while having dialysis. The length of your travels will depend on your type of dialysis.

If you have haemodialysis, you will usually have treatment at least three times a week. But you don't usually need treatment between sessions, enabling you to schedule outings. You should have time for days out, short holidays, and other activities.

If you have continuous ambulatory peritoneal dialysis, you may be able to travel for longer, including travelling overseas within the EU. Your health care team may be able to arrange for boxes of dialysis fluid to be delivered to your holiday destination, but some advance notice (one to three months) will be required. You can speak to your health care team to find out if this is possible.

If you have automated peritoneal dialysis, you need to be attached to a dialysis machine at night. This makes it harder to travel.

Exercise

Many people who are on dialysis feel out of breath and extremely tired. But some people find regular and moderate exercise helps, if they can manage it. Exercises such as walking, jogging, cycling, or swimming may be suitable for people on dialysis. A study of people on dialysis showed that people who exercised up to five times a week lived longer than other people on dialysis. [34]Stack AG, Molony DA, Rives T, et al. Association of physical activity with mortality in the US dialysis population. American Journal of Kidney Diseases. 2005; 45: 690-701.

What are the effects of dialysis on your quality of life?

If dialysis works well, it should help you to feel well. But dialysis takes up a lot of time and restricts how you live your life. It can put strain on your relationships with people close to you.

Having dialysis at home might improve your quality of life compared with having dialysis in hospital. One group of people having dialysis were asked about how happy they were with their quality of life. Those having home dialysis were happier. But that might have been because people who choose to have home haemodialysis may have a better quality of life to begin with.[35]Evidence-Based Healthcare and Public Health. Home haemodialysis an effective alternative to hospital or satellite unit haemodialysis. Evidence-Based Healthcare and Public Health. 2005; 9: 123-124.

What are the unwanted side effects of dialysis?

Both haemodialysis and peritoneal dialysis can have unwanted side effects. These are some common side effects:

  • Tiredness. Both haemodialysis and peritoneal dialysis can make you feel exhausted and short of breath immediately after treatment. However, this can also be caused by anaemia, which is a common problem with established kidney failure. So it’s not always easy to tell whether your fatigue is caused by dialysis or not
  • Itchy skin and muscle cramps. These are usually caused by haemodialysis. The itchy skin is thought to be because of a build up of phosphate in your blood. The muscle cramps often happen in your lower legs as your muscles adjust to the fluid loss during dialysis.
  • Restless legs. People having either haemodialysis or peritoneal dialysis sometimes get restless legs. This means that they have a strong urge to move their legs caused by an unpleasant feeling in these limbs. This feeling is worse when they are not active and at night.[36]Kavanagh D, Siddiqui S, Geddes CC. Restless legs syndrome in patients on dialysis. American Journal of Kidney Diseases. 2004; 43: 763-71.
  • Low blood pressure. This is another common side effect of haemodialysis. It can be caused by the sudden drop in fluid levels that happens during dialysis. Low blood pressure can make you feel sick and dizzy during and soon after dialysis.
  • Weight gain. The dialysis fluid used during peritoneal dialysis contains some sugars that are absorbed into the body and can make you gain weight, particularly if you aren’t able to make changes to your diet or exercise regularly. One study found that around 7 in 100  people who have peritoneal dialysis put on 10 kg (1.5 stone) over two years.[37]NHS Choices. Dialysis. August 2011. Available at http://www.nhs.uk/Conditions/Dialysis/Pages/Introduction.aspx (accessed on 13 June 2012).
  • Hernia. A hernia occurs when an internal part of your body, such as an organ, pushes through a weakness in the muscle or surrounding tissue wall. People having peritoneal dialysis are at risk of developing a hernia because holding fluid inside the peritoneal cavity for many hours places strain on the muscles of the abdomen. Surgery is usually needed to repair a hernia. A hernia can make some movements difficult and painful, such as bending, coughing, lifting heavy objects, or having sex.
  • Bone pain. People on any type of dialysis may not have the right amounts of minerals like calcium and phosphorus. This can cause pain in the bones and joints, weaker bones, and may make you more likely to have fractures and broken bones.[38]National Kidney and Urologic Diseases Information Clearinghouse. Chronic kidney disease - mineral and bone disorder. Available at http://kidney.niddk.nih.gov/kudiseases/pubs/CKD_Mineral_Bone (accessed on 30 May 2012).

Problems with vascular access points

If you have haemodialysis, all three types of vascular access points can have problems that need further treatment or surgery. The most common problems are infections and blood clots. The access point may also close or become too narrow.[39]National Institutes of Health. Vascular access for hemodialysis. February 2008. Available at http://kidney.niddk.nih.gov/KUDiseases/pubs/vascularaccess/index.aspx (accessed on 11 April 2012). Venous catheters are most likely to develop infection and clotting problems that need treatment.[40]National Institutes of Health. Vascular access for hemodialysis. February 2008. Available at http://kidney.niddk.nih.gov/KUDiseases/pubs/vascularaccess/index.aspx (accessed on 11 April 2012).

Infections

Some people who have peritoneal dialysis get an infection of the peritoneum, called peritonitis. The most common symptoms of peritonitis are abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, a raised temperature, fever, and chills.[41]NHS Choices. Side effects of dialysis. August 2011. Available at http://www.nhs.uk/Conditions/Dialysis/Pages/Side-effects.aspx (accessed on 13 April 2011) Around 40 people in 100 will get peritonitis if they have peritoneal dialysis for 12 months. Around 50 people in 100 will get peritonitis if they have peritoneal dialysis for two years.[42]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Peritoneal dialysis: Peritoneal dialysis in the treatment of stage 5 chronic kidney disease. July 2011. Clinical guidance 125. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/cg125 (accessed on 6 June 2012).

People who have peritoneal dialysis can also get exit site infections. This is an infection of the area where the catheter comes out through the skin. This causes redness, tenderness, and sometimes pus to collect around the exit site. Occasionally, an exit site infection spreads down the catheter. This is called a tunnel infection. People who have a tunnel infection need an operation to remove the catheter.[43]UK National Kidney Federation. What problems can happen on PD? Available at http://www.kidney.org.uk/Medical-Info/pd/pdproblems.html (accessed on 30 May 2012.

People who have haemodialysis can also get infections. One of the most common infections is caused by bacteria called Staphylococcus aureus. In one study, 90 people in every 100 who had haemodialysis got at least one Staphylococcus aureus infection at some stage during dialysis.[42]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Peritoneal dialysis: Peritoneal dialysis in the treatment of stage 5 chronic kidney disease. July 2011. Clinical guidance 125. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/cg125 (accessed on 6 June 2012).

These infections can be treated with antibiotics. But if the bacteria get into the blood it can cause a serious condition called sepsis, which can cause death. This is the second most common cause of death, after heart disease, in people having haemodialysis.[42]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Peritoneal dialysis: Peritoneal dialysis in the treatment of stage 5 chronic kidney disease. July 2011. Clinical guidance 125. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/cg125 (accessed on 6 June 2012).

How will dialysis affect my life?

Time spent in hospital or having treatment

In one group of people aged 70 or over, those having any type of dialysis spent on average 25 days in hospital each year.[46]Carson RC, Juszczak M, Davenport A, et al. Is maximum conservative management an equivalent treatment option to dialysis for elderly patients with significant comorbid disease? Clinical Journal of the American Society of Nephrology. 2009; 4: 1611–1619.

If you have peritoneal dialysis, you will need to spend one or two days in hospital after an operation to put in a catheter. Most of your dialysis will happen at home (or wherever you are), but you may need time in hospital if you have infections or complications.

If you have continuous ambulatory peritoneal dialysis, you need to carry out the fluid exchange procedure four times a day, every day. It takes about 45 minutes. Apart from that, you can walk around and get on with things. If you have automated peritoneal dialysis, dialysis takes place over eight to 10 hours, overnight, every night.

If you have haemodialysis, you will probably need to be attached to a dialysis machine at least three times a week, for at least four hours a session, although times vary from person to person. This may be at your home, or in a dialysis centre or hospital. You can’t get up and move around while this is happening. You will also need blood tests and checks, to see whether your treatment is working well enough. The amount of time and the frequency of treatments varies for different people. 

Diet and fluid restrictions

All types of dialysis involve some restrictions to your diet and what you can drink. This can take some adjustment for you and any family members who help prepare your meals. You will be able to speak to a dietitian or other member of your health care team to discuss how best to make changes to your diet. 

You may need to restrict the amount of salt, potassium, and phosphate in your diet. You may also need to restrict the amount of fluid you drink. For haemodialysis, the restrictions on diet and fluids are likely to be stricter.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT KIDNEY TRANSPLANT

What is a kidney transplant?

A kidney transplant involves getting a healthy kidney from a donor to take over the work of your damaged kidneys. The donor can either be someone who is living (often a family member or a friend) or someone who has just died (called a deceased donor).

A transplant can allow you to live your life much as you did before your kidneys stopped working. But it’s not suitable for everyone.

The transplant process usually begins when you find out that your kidneys are failing and you are considering your treatment options. If you’d like to be considered for a transplant, you’ll be evaluated to see whether you are healthy enough for this operation and if there’s a good chance that the transplant will succeed.[47]National Kidney Foundation. Can everyone on dialysis have a kidney transplant? February 2011. Available at http://www.kidney.org.uk/Medical-Info/transplant/txhave.html (accessed on 13 April 2012).

If you are a candidate for a transplant, the next step is finding a suitable kidney. You’ll have tests to check your blood group as well as your tissue type, which involves looking at genetic markers called HLA factors (HLA is short for human leucocyte antigen). Kidneys that are a closer match tend to last longer.

Most people are put on a waiting list to find a suitable kidney from a person who has just died. But more and more people (over 1 in 3) now have a transplant with a kidney from a living donor, which provide the best chance for long-term transplant success.[48]Statistics and Clinical Audit, NHS Blood and Transplant. Transplant activity in the UK. Activity Report 2010/11. Available at http://www.organdonation.nhs.uk/ukt/statistics/transplant_activity_report/current_activity_reports/ukt/activity_report_2010_11.pdf (accessed on 7 June 2012). [49]British Transplant Society. UK guidelines for living donor kidney transplant. May 2011. Available at http://www.bts.org.uk/transplantation/standards-and-guidelines (accessed on 31 May 2012).

The operation

Whether you receive a kidney from a living or deceased donor, the transplant operation is essentially the same. 

You’ll have a general anaesthetic, so you will be asleep during the operation. The surgeon will make a cut in one side of your lower abdomen and insert the new kidney. This cut is usually around 15 to 20 centimetres long. The kidney’s main blood vessels will be attached to those in your groin, and its ureter will be connected to your bladder (the ureter is the tube that drains urine from your kidney). Your damaged kidneys will probably be left in place.[50]U.S. Department of Health and Human Services. National Kidney and Urologic Diseases Information Clearinghouse (NKUDIC). Treatment methods for kidney failure: transplantation. September 2010. Available at http://kidney.niddk.nih.gov/KUDiseases/pubs/transplant/index.aspx (accessed on 13 April 2012).

Before or immediately after surgery, you will begin taking immunosuppressive (anti-rejection) medicines. These drugs suppress your immune system, making it less likely to attack the new kidney, causing your body to reject it. Your new kidney may start making urine straight away, or it may take days or weeks to start working. You’ll be closely monitored, and may stay at hospital for one to two weeks. 

Who is suitable for a kidney transplant?

Not everyone with kidney failure is suitable for a kidney transplant. To be suitable for a transplant, you need to be healthy enough for the surgery, and there needs to be a good chance of the transplant succeeding. If you have heart disease, cancer, or another serious health problem, your medical team will weigh up whether the risks of surgery may be too great. Around half of people with kidney failure are suitable for a kidney transplant.[47]National Kidney Foundation. Can everyone on dialysis have a kidney transplant? February 2011. Available at http://www.kidney.org.uk/Medical-Info/transplant/txhave.html (accessed on 13 April 2012).

There is no upper age limit for a kidney transplant. Your health and fitness for the surgery are the most important things.[52]UK Renal Association. Clinical practice guidelines. Module 4: assessment for renal transplantation. 2008. Available at http://www.renal.org (accessed on 7 June 2012).][NHS Blood and Transplant. Frequently asked questions about kidney donation. http://www.organdonation.nhs.uk/ukt/about_transplants/frequently_asked_questions_about_transplants/f_a_qs_about_kidney_transplantation.asp (accessed on 7 June 2012). Someone who is 70 and in good health may be a better candidate for a transplant than someone who is 40 and has several health issues.

Being suitable for a kidney transplant doesn’t necessarily mean you will get a transplant. For example, many older adults die before they are matched with a kidney.[53]NHS Blood and Transplant. Organ and tissue donation - your questions answered. Deceased kidney donation. Available at: http://www.organdonation.nhs.uk/ukt/about_transplants/organ_allocation/kidney_%28renal%29/kidney_%28renal%29.asp (accessed on 31 May 2012).

In the UK, the average wait time for a kidney from a deceased donor is about three years, although some people receive a kidney much sooner. Your waiting time will depend on several factors. These include how rare your blood group is and your age.

Children and young adults are given a priority for well-matched kidneys. That’s because their potential lifespan is much longer.[53]NHS Blood and Transplant. Organ and tissue donation - your questions answered. Deceased kidney donation. Available at: http://www.organdonation.nhs.uk/ukt/about_transplants/organ_allocation/kidney_%28renal%29/kidney_%28renal%29.asp (accessed on 31 May 2012).

What are the effects of a kidney transplant on your length of life?

Having a kidney transplant can help you live longer than not having a transplant.

All the figures we give are based on averages of groups of people. They can’t predict how long you will live personally.

Figures from the UK show that:[55]Statistics and Clinical Audit, NHS Blood and Transplant. Transplant activity in the UK. Activity Report 2010/11. Available at http://www.organdonation.nhs.uk/ukt/statistics/transplant_activity_report/current_activity_reports/ukt/activity_report_2010_11.pdf (accessed on 6 June 2012).

  • For kidneys from living donors, 96 in 100 people were alive five years after having a transplant, and 90 in 100 people were alive 10 years after.
  • For kidneys from deceased donors, about 89 in 100 people were alive five years after having a transplant, and between 66 in 100 and 74 in 100 people were alive 10 years after.

If you are older, a transplant might not help you live longer. In one group of people over age 65, those who had a transplant didn’t live longer, on average, than those waiting for a transplant.[56]Ansell D, O’Neill JO, Johnson R, et al. What are the effects of a kidney transplant on symptoms?

Once you have a successful kidney transplant, you again have a functioning kidney, so your symptoms of kidney failure - such as tiredness and weakness, itching, and pain - should get much better or go away completely. It may take you a few weeks to fully appreciate the impact of your transplanted kidney on your symptoms.

A transplant improves your symptoms because it takes over all of the jobs of your damaged kidneys. As well as filtering wastes and removing excess fluid from your body, a transplanted kidney produces hormones and controls the production of red blood cells.[58]Kidney Research UK. Kidney transplantation. Available at http://www.kidneyresearchuk.org/health/factsheets/ckd-and-issues/kidney-transplantation.php (accessed on 13 April 2012).

What are the effects of a kidney transplant on what you can do?

You should be able to return to your normal activities once you have recovered from your kidney transplant.

You may be more prone to getting infections after a transplant, because of the drugs you’re taking to suppress your immune system. So you might want to take extra care not to expose yourself to these illnesses, particularly within the first few months of your transplant. 

Work life

You should be able to return to your work when you are recovered from your operation.

Social life and travel

Provided you are in good health, you should be able to travel abroad after a kidney transplant. Doctors often recommend not travelling abroad within the first year of your transplant. If you are travelling to a country where travel vaccinations are recommended, you’ll need to check with your doctor before getting these. Some vaccines are not safe for people who are taking drugs that suppress their immune system.[59]National Kidney Federation. Holiday/travel vaccinations. February 2011. Available at http://www.kidney.org.uk/Medical-Info/drugs/vacc2.html (accessed on 13 April 2012). And when you’re abroad, you'll need to take common sense steps to avoid infection, such as drinking bottled water in areas where the tap water may not be safe to drink. 

Exercise

You can get back to your exercise routine after you’ve recovered from your transplant surgery. You should avoid activities that may put your kidney at risk of injury (such as playing rugby and other contact sports). That’s because a transplanted kidney sits in your lower abdomen, where it is less protected than your original kidneys (which are nestled below your ribcage).

What are the effects of a kidney transplant on your quality of life?

Some research shows that people who’ve had a transplant rate their quality of life more highly than those who haven't had a transplant.[60]Landreneau K, Lee K, Landreneau MD, et al. Quality of life in patients undergoing hemodialysis and renal transplantation - a meta-analytic review. Nephrology Nursing Journal: Journal of the American Nephrology Nurses' Association. 2010; 37: 37-44. This can mean that they have a greater feeling of wellbeing and are able to do more of the things that they want to do. People who’ve had a transplant also rate their ‘physical functioning’ higher. This can mean they get around better, perform better on physical tasks, can work longer, and spend less time feeling tired.

What are the unwanted side effects of a kidney transplant?

A kidney transplant is a big operation and there are risks related to the surgery, the transplanted kidney, and the medicines you need to take long term to stop your body from rejecting the kidney.

Problems from the surgery and transplanted kidney

A kidney transplant carries a risk of bleeding, infection, and reactions to the general anaesthetic, which can be serious.

After your surgery, you may get blood or urine leaking around your new kidney. If urine is leaking or blocked, you may need another operation. If a blood vessel leaks, you may need a blood transfusion and surgery to correct it. A blocked blood vessel is more serious. If the new kidney does not get enough blood, it may need to be removed. 

A blood vessel may also become narrowed (this is called arterial stenosis). This can happen months or even years after your transplant, causing a rapid and potentially dangerous rise in blood pressure. You will probably need surgery to widen the blood vessel. 

There is a risk of dying from a kidney transplant, either from the surgery or from problems that arise soon after. About 1 to 2 in 100 people die during or soon after a kidney transplant.[61]British Transplantation Society. Guidelines for antibody incompatible transplantation. January 2011. Available at http://www.bts.org.uk/transplantation/standards-and-guidelines (accessed 13 April 2012).

There is also a chance that the transplant will fail and the kidney will need to be removed. Around 5 in 100 transplants fail within the first year.[61]British Transplantation Society. Guidelines for antibody incompatible transplantation. January 2011. Available at http://www.bts.org.uk/transplantation/standards-and-guidelines (accessed 13 April 2012).

If your transplant fails, you can go on dialysis and also be considered for another kidney transplant. Or you might decide you want conservative care.

Within a year of having a kidney transplant, between 15 in 100 and 35 in 100 people will have an episode where their body tries to reject their kidney.[63]Webster AC, Pankhurst T, Rinaldi F, et al. Polyclonal and monoclonal antibodies for treating acute rejection episodes in kidney transplant recipients. In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. This is usually treatable.

Most kidneys continue working for several years. About 70 in 100 kidneys from deceased donors and 79 in 100 kidneys from living donors are still working 10 or more years after the transplant.[Statistics and Clinical Audit, NHS Blood and Transplant. Transplant activity in the UK. Activity Report 2010/11. Available at http://www.organdonation.nhs.uk/ukt/statistics/transplant_activity_report/current_activity_reports/ukt/activity_report_2010_11.pdf (accessed on 7 June 2012).]  For many older people, their transplanted kidney will last for the rest of their life.

There’s a chance you may get an infection or cancer from a donated kidney. All kidneys and their donors are carefully screened to help prevent these problems, but no kidney is without risk. 

Problems from immunosuppressive medicines

For a kidney transplant to last, you must take medicines long-term to prevent your body from rejecting the kidney. These are called immunosuppressive drugs. They ‘suppress’ your immune system so it won’t recognise the kidney as foreign and attack it.

There are many different types of immunosuppressive drugs, and most people take a combination of these. These drugs can cause a variety of side effects, including stomach irritation, hair growth or thinning, shaking of the hands (tremors), swollen gums, changes in appearance (for example, your face may become fuller), weight gain or loss, and spots (acne).[64]British National Formulary. Drugs affecting the immune response. Section 8.2. British Medical Association and Royal Pharmaceutical Society of Great Britain. Also available at http://bnf.org (accessed on 7 June 2012). Many of these problems may go away after you’ve been taking the medicines for a while. Some of the drugs may also raise your risk of diabetes, high blood pressure, high cholesterol, and weak bones (osteoporosis).

The most serious side effects of these medicines are an increased risk of infections and cancer.

  • Infections: By suppressing your immune system, these drugs weaken your body’s resistance to infections. This means you’re more likely to become ill when you come in contact with viruses and other germs. And when you do get an infection, it may last longer than usual. Many people get infections of the urinary tract. If you get these frequently, this can damage your kidney. Your doctor may prescribe a regular low-dose antibiotic to prevent this. You are also more likely to become ill from infections that don’t usually cause problems in people with fully functioning immune systems. These include cytomegalovirus (CMV) and BK virus. People who’ve had a kidney transplant are usually advised to get vaccinated against the flu, pneumonia, and meningitis.
  • Cancer: Immunosuppressive drugs increase your risk of developing certain cancers. Up to 20 in 100 people will develop cancer within 10 years of having a kidney transplant.[65]Lutz J, Heemann U. Tumours after kidney transplantation. Current Opinion in Urology. 2003; 13: 105-109. Skin cancer is the most common type, followed by cancer of the immune system (lymphoma). To minimise your risk of skin cancer, it’s important not to spend too much time in the sun (for example, sun-bathing) and to wear sunscreen or protective clothing. Most people who develop skin cancer can be successfully treated with surgery to remove the cancer.[66]Winkelhorst JT, Brokelman WJ, Tiggeler RG, Wobbes T. Incidence and clinical course of de novo malignancies in renal allograft recipients. European Journal of Surgical Oncology. 2001; 27: 409-413.
How will the treatment affect my life?

Time in hospital and taking treatment

You will probably stay in hospital one to two weeks after your transplant, until your new kidney is working and stable. Some transplant units will send you home after you have recovered from your surgery, even if your kidney isn’t working. But you will still be closely monitored through frequent appointments.[67]Kidney Research UK. Kidney transplantation. Available at http://www.kidneyresearchuk.org/health/factsheets/ckd-and-issues/kidney-transplantation.php (accessed 13 April 2012).

You will continue to have up to three regular appointments a week after your transplant. This will then drop to one appointment a week, and continue to become less frequent. Several years after your transplant, you will probably have appointments every three to six months.[67]Kidney Research UK. Kidney transplantation. Available at http://www.kidneyresearchuk.org/health/factsheets/ckd-and-issues/kidney-transplantation.php (accessed 13 April 2012).

You will need to take drugs to suppress your immune system for as long as your new kidney is working. For many people, this is the rest of their life. You will also have regular appointments to check on your kidney and your health.

Diet and fluid restrictions

There may not be any diet and fluid restrictions after a kidney transplant. However, some people do need to modify what they eat, because of other health problems (such as high blood pressure and high cholesterol) or their immunosuppressive drugs.

  • My kidney disease is getting worse. Should I try to have a kidney transplant before I get symptoms?
  • I can have dialysis. Which type of dialysis fits best into my life?
  • I can have a kidney transplant. What kind of kidney transplant would I be happy with?
  • If I have conservative care, what choices do I have to make about my kidney disease and lifestyle?

MORE INFORMATION ABOUT ESTABLISHED KIDNEY FAILURE

What do the kidneys do?

The kidneys are bean-shaped organs, each about the size of a fist. They are just below your ribcage, one on either side of your spine, at the back of the body. Every day the kidneys filter about 200 litres of fluid, removing 2 to 3 litres (more than 3 to 5 pints) of water and waste products from the blood. If the kidneys did not remove them, these wastes would build up in the blood and damage the body.

Blood is moved through the kidneys in blood vessels. Tiny filters within the kidneys act like a sieve. They keep proteins and cells in the blood where they are needed. Extra fluid and waste is passed to the bladder in urine. The bladder stores urine until you go to the toilet. The kidneys also produce a hormone called erythropoietin, which is needed to make red blood cells. Healthy kidneys help keep your blood pressure stable. And kidneys are important for absorbing the right amount of calcium and phosphate, which helps keep your bones strong.

What is kidney disease?

Chronic kidney disease means your kidneys are gradually losing their ability to filter waste products out of your blood.

You may also hear it called chronic renal failure, chronic kidney failure, or chronic renal insufficiency. Chronic means it’s a long-term condition, and renal means it involves your kidneys.

Doctors use the term kidney function as a way to describe how well your kidneys filter blood. Kidney function can be measured with a blood test. Young people with two healthy kidneys usually have around 90 percent to 100 percent of their kidney function. Your kidney function tends to get lower with age. This isn’t usually a problem, unless it keeps on getting lower.

People can lose some of their kidney function without it causing any problems or symptoms. But health problems begin when the kidneys can no longer do all their jobs well enough. When this happens varies from person to person. Some people might get symptoms when they still have quite a lot of kidney function, while others don’t get symptoms even though they have lost more kidney function.

What is established kidney failure?

Doctors divide chronic kidney disease into five stages, based on the kidney function shown by your blood tests. If your blood tests show kidney function of 15 percent or less, this is called chronic kidney disease stage 5, also known as established kidney failure. You may also hear it called end stage kidney failure.

If you have established kidney failure, you may get symptoms because of waste building up in your blood. But this doesn’t happen to everyone. You may not get symptoms until your kidney function is much lower than 15 percent.

 

 

What are the symptoms of established kidney failure?

If your kidneys have lost their ability to filter the blood well enough to keep you healthy, you may get some of these symptoms:[69]National Institutes of Health. The kidneys and how they work. March 2012. Available at http://kidney.niddk.nih.gov/kudiseases/pubs/yourkidneys/ (accessed on 19 April 2012).

  • Feeling tired or short of breath
  • Feeling generally ill, drowsy, or finding it difficult to concentrate
  • Feeling sick or vomiting
  • Needing to go to the toilet more or less often than usual to pass urine. You may need to get up at night to go to the toilet
  • Puffiness around your eyes or swelling in your limbs (called oedema)
  • Muscle cramps
  • Losing your appetite or losing weight without trying. You may find your sense of taste changes
  • Noticing your urine appearing foamy or much darker in colour
  • Rashes or itchy skin
  • You may lose interest in sex (and if you’re a man, you may be less able to get or maintain an erection)
  • Pain, including aching bones or joints.

Not everyone gets all these symptoms. You may not get them for many years, or at all.

Why has this happened to me?

Available at http://publications.nice.org.uk/chronic-kidney-disease-cg73/guidance (accessed on 19 April 2012).

  • Having diabetes
  • Having high blood pressure
  • Being older 
  • Your ethnic origin (people of African, African-Caribbean, or South Asian descent are more likely to have chronic kidney disease than other people in the UK)
  • Heart disease
  • Smoking
  • Being overweight
  • Having kidney disease in your family.

Your doctor will probably be able to tell you the cause of your kidney failure, but sometimes the cause isn't clear.

 

How do I find out I have established kidney failure?

Most people find out they have established kidney failure after having blood tests. The main blood test to diagnose established kidney failure is called a serum creatinine test.

Serum creatinine measures a chemical in the blood. It is used to calculate what is called the estimated glomular filtration rate (eGFR for short). The eGFR calculation is a calculation of how much blood your kidneys are able to filter in one minute. This number is about the same as your kidney function. So if you have 100 percent kidney function, your eGFR is also likely to be 100.

Your eGFR is used by doctors to put your kidney disease into ‘stages’.  There are five stages of kidney disease. If you have completely normal kidney function, you have an eGFR of 90 or over. If you have an eGFR between 15 and 90, you have some kidney damage (stages 1 to 4).

If your eGFR is less than 15, you have chronic kidney disease stage 5, which is established kidney failure.

What will happen?

There is no cure that can make your kidneys work well again, but some treatments can improve your symptoms, and reduce your risk of complications.

What happens to you will depend on many things:

  • Your state of health, age, and any other illnesses you have
  • How quickly your kidney function is worsening
  • Which treatment you choose
  • Whether you are able to have a transplant from a living donor, or go on the waiting list for a deceased donor transplant.

Nothing can make your kidneys work well again, but some treatments can improve your symptoms, and reduce your risk of complications. There is more information about kidney transplant, dialysis, and other treatments in the next section, 'compare options'.

Complications from kidney disease include:[74]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence Chronic kidney disease: Early identification and management of chronic kidney disease in adults in primary and secondary care. Clinical Guideline 73. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/cg73 (accessed on 6 June 2012).

  • High blood pressure, which can increase your chances of a heart attack or stroke
  • Anaemia, which can make you very tired
  • Weaker bones, which means you are more likely to have a fracture.

Drugs can manage or prevent some of these complications. If you have been treated for chronic kidney disease, you may already be taking some of these medicines.

If you have established kidney failure, treatments can help take over the work of your kidneys and improve your symptoms.

  • Kidney dialysis takes on some of the work of your kidneys, by removing the extra fluid and waste products from your blood.
  • A kidney transplant takes over all of the work of your kidneys, by providing you with a new kidney.
  • Conservative care treats your symptoms but does not take over the work of your kidneys.

Some people with kidney disease have kidney function that stays stable, or gets lower only slowly. The speed at which your kidney function gets worse is one factor that may help you decide which treatment to choose.

But your kidney function is not the only thing that affects what happens to you. Your general state of health, age, and any other illnesses you have are also important.
