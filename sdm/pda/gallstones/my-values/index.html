Our Decision Support telephone service is here to assist and guide you through the Shared Decision Making process.

The Decision Support team is made of professional Health Coaches who are clinicians, commonly nurses, who have undertaken additional training to become a Health Coach.

The Health Coaches will assist you by:

  • Providing you with information
  • Helping you to understand your condition
  • Recognise what is important to you regarding the outcome of treatment
  • Identifying potential solutions
  • Encourage you in discussing options with your family
  • Transferring skills which will assist you in using the information and resources available to you
  • Support you in building confidence in discussing your choice with your doctor
  • You may find that this can be achieved in one telephone call with a Health Coach however; if further calls are required to support you in reaching your decision the Health Coach will schedule these with you.

Important Things To Note

  • A Health Coach does not know which treatment option is best for you.
  • The Health Coach does not have information about your medical history.
  • You will still need to talk over your decision and reasons for your decision with your doctor or nurse.

MORE INFORMATION ABOUT GALLSTONES

What is the gallbladder?

The gallbladder is small, pouch-like organ that lies under the liver, near the upper part of the intestine. It stores a watery liquid made in the liver which is dark green to yellowish-brown in colour, called bile. Bile contains a number of substances including cholesterol, calcium and a yellowish substance called bilirubin which comes from old, used up red blood cells that are processed in the liver.

The liver makes up to a litre of bile each day, and most of it goes down tubes from the liver into the intestine where it helps to digest fat [1]US National Library of Medicine. Gallstones. http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmedhealth/PMH0049865. The tubes start small (bile ducts) and join to form a larger tube (the common bile duct) that goes into the intestine.

Between meals, bile is stored in the gallbladder where it becomes thicker and more concentrated. When more bile is needed for digestion, it leaves the gallbladder through a tube called the cystic duct, which joins the common bile duct going to the intestine [2]NHS Choices. Gallstones. http://www.nhs.uk/conditions/Gallstones/Pages/Introduction.aspx [1]US National Library of Medicine. Gallstones. http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmedhealth/PMH0049865.

What are gallstones?

Gallstones are small stones that form in the gallbladder [4]Gallstones. NHS Choices http://www.nhs.uk/conditions/Gallstones/Pages/Introduction.aspx [5]US National Library of Medicine. Gallstones. http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmedhealth/PMH0049865.

They start as crystals and may grow to the size of gravel and eventually pebbles. There may be one, several, or even dozens of stones [6]British Society of Gastroenterology. Gallstones. http://www.bsg.org.uk/patients/general/gallstones.html#What are gallstones?.

Most gallstones are made from the cholesterol in bile, though some are made from the calcium and bilirubin. As these are coloured, they are called pigment gallstones.

Gallstones may [6]British Society of Gastroenterology. Gallstones. http://www.bsg.org.uk/patients/general/gallstones.html#What are gallstones?:

  • Stay in the gallbladder
  • Come out of the gallbladder and go down the common bile duct into the intestine and get passed with faeces or
  • Get stuck coming out of the gallbladder or in the cystic duct or common bile duct
What are the main symptoms of gallstones and what will happen to me?

Some people don’t get any symptoms from their gallstones. These are called asymptomatic gallstones. They may only be found when a person is having tests for something else, such as an ultrasound scan on their abdomen. Some people get symptoms from the start.

One of the most common symptoms of gallstones is sudden intense pain in the centre of the abdomen above the belly button, or just under the ribs on the right side. This is called biliary colic and it happens when the muscle of the gallbladder is contracting to squeeze bile out, but the route is blocked by a gallstone stuck in one of the ducts between the gallbladder and the intestine. [8]NHS Choices. Gallstones. http://www.nhs.uk/Conditions/Gallstones/Pages/Symptoms.aspx; British Society of Gastroenterology. Gallstones. http://www.bsg.org.uk/patients/general/gallstones.html. The pain is often sudden and usually starts after eating a fatty meal. It may last for a few minutes or over an hour [9]NHS Choices. Gallstones. http://www.nhs.uk/Conditions/Gallstones/Pages/Symptoms.aspx. It may be continuous or come in waves [10]British Society of Gastroenterology. Gallstones. http://www.bsg.org.uk/patients/general/gallstones.html. It tends to be worse than indigestion and it doesn’t go away if a person goes to the toilet, passes wind or is sick. Fifty six people in 100 with gallstones get biliary colic [11]Sanders, G., Kingsnorth, A. N. Gallstones. BMJ 2007; 335: 295-299.

Other symptoms of gallstones include [9]NHS Choices. Gallstones. http://www.nhs.uk/Conditions/Gallstones/Pages/Symptoms.aspx:

  • Feeling sick
  • Being sick

In 36 people in 100, the gallbladder becomes inflamed [11]Sanders, G., Kingsnorth, A. N. Gallstones. BMJ 2007; 335: 295-299. This is called acute cholecystitis, and it usually happens if a gallstone gets trapped in the duct or opening of the gallbladder [9]NHS Choices. Gallstones. http://www.nhs.uk/Conditions/Gallstones/Pages/Symptoms.aspx. Ninety five out of 100 people with acute cholecystitis have gallstones or a build up of bile sludge at the neck of the gallbladder and 5 out of 100 do not have gallstones. [15]Clinical Knowledge Summaries. Cholecystitis. http://www.cks.nhs.uk/cholecystitis_acute/background_information/definition#-449782

If acute cholecystitis is not treated it can lead to:[15]Clinical Knowledge Summaries. Cholecystitis. http://www.cks.nhs.uk/cholecystitis_acute/background_information/definition#-449782

  • Tears in the gallbladder
  • Severe infection in nearby tissues (also called peritonitis)
  • Abscess on or near the gallbladder
  • A hole (fistula) between the gallbladder and part of the intestine (duodenum)

Acute cholecystitis can be serious and very occasionally fatal, and older people and those with other physical illnesses, such as diabetes, are more likely to have significant problems than people who are otherwise healthy. [15]Clinical Knowledge Summaries. Cholecystitis. http://www.cks.nhs.uk/cholecystitis_acute/background_information/definition#-449782

The symptoms of gallstones can be similar to those of:

  • Hiatus hernia: the upper part of the stomach pushes back into the chest area through a tear in the diaphragm (the sheet of muscle that separates the chest from the abdomen)
  • Peptic ulcer: a painful, inflamed area in the wall of the stomach or the first part of the intestine
  • Irritable bowel syndrome: pain and bloating in the abdomen, together with a change in bowel habit

Some people may have one of these health problems as well as gallstones and it may be difficult to decide which illness is causing their symptoms.

Many people live with asymptomatic gallstones for many years without treatment and without problems.

Gallstones can also lead to: [18]Clinical Knowledge Summaries. Acute pancreatitis. http://www.cks.nhs.uk/pancreatitis_acute/background_information/causes/gallstones#-482117

  • Inflammation of the pancreas (acute pancreatitis): if gallstones get into the common bile duct, and pass into the intestine through the tiny opening (ampulla) it shares with the pancreatic duct, they can cause back-pressure in the pancreas, which may make the pancreas produce chemicals which damage the pancreas. Acute pancreatitis is a serious and potentially life-threatening condition, and is very painful. About 50 in 100 cases of acute pancreatitis are caused by gallstones, and up to about 10 to 15 in 100 people who get acute pancreatitis may die [19]Clinical Knowledge Summaries. Acute pancreatitis. http://www.cks.nhs.uk/pancreatitis_acute/background_information/causes/gallstones#-473415
  • Jaundice: if a gallstone blocks the common bile duct leading from the liver and gallbladder to the intestine, the body can’t get rid of bilirubin in the bile, so it builds up in the skin and whites of the eyes, making them yellow, as well as causing itching. Some bilirubin may get into the urine and turn it darker in colour [10]British Society of Gastroenterology. Gallstones. http://www.bsg.org.uk/patients/general/gallstones.html.
  • Gallbladder cancer: this is a rare cancer which occurs in about 3 in 1000 people who have gallstones [11]Sanders, G., Kingsnorth, A. N. Gallstones. BMJ 2007; 335: 295-299.
Why do I have gallstones?

Gallstones happen when there is an imbalance of the chemicals in bile, so that one or more of them form crystalline lumps [11]Sanders, G., Kingsnorth, A. N. Gallstones. BMJ 2007; 335: 295-299. No one knows quite why this happens or why people get them.

Gallstones are more common in [23]Sanders, G., Kingsnorth, A. N. Gallstones. BMJ 2007; 335: 295-299; US National Library of Medicine. Gallstones http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmedhealth/PMH0049865/:

  • Women
  • Pregnancy

And in people who:

  • Are over 40 years of age
  • Are very overweight (obese)
  • Have close relatives who have had gallstones
  • Have lost a lot of weight very quickly (eg: after obesity surgery)
  • Take liquid food through a tube
  • Eat a high fat diet
  • Have had surgery to remove part of their small intestine
  • Have type 2 diabetes as well as being overweight and having high blood pressure and/or high cholesterol
  • Have liver disease
  • Use the contraceptive Pill or hormone replacement therapy (HRT)
How do I find out if I have gallstones?

This happens in three stages [11]Sanders, G., Kingsnorth, A. N. Gallstones. BMJ 2007; 335: 295-299:

  • A family doctor (GP) will ask about symptoms and other factors that may increase the likelihood of gallstones (see Why Do I Have Gallstones?). The GP will also examine a person’s abdomen to see if it is sore near the gallbladder, and to check for other possible reasons for the symptoms, such as liver or intestinal problems. If gallstones seem likely, the GP will make an appointment for the person to have other tests and to see a specialist
  • They will have blood tests, including tests to check that their liver is working properly and an ultrasound scan to see if there are gallstones in the gallbladder or bile ducts. If there is a gallstone in the bile duct a different procedure called endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography (ERCP) may be suggested. An ultrasound scan is a painless test in which harmless sound waves are bounced off the abdomen in order to get pictures of internal organs and structures
  • At an outpatient appointment at the hospital, a person with suspected gallstones will see a specialist in intestinal diseases who will discuss their symptoms and examine them. At the hospital, people sometimes have other types of scans to check the gallbladder and nearby structures.
How many people have gallstones?

About 10 to 15 in 100 people have gallstones at some time in their life [11]Sanders, G., Kingsnorth, A. N. Gallstones. BMJ 2007; 335: 295-299.

About 70 in 100 people with gallstones start with no symptoms [26]Festi D, Reggiani ML, Attili AF et al. Natural history of gallstone disease: Expectant management or active treatment? Results from a population-based cohort study. J Gastroenterol Hepatol. 2010 Apr;25(4):719-24.

Each year, about 1 to 4 people in 100 with symptom-free gallstones start to get symptoms [27]Halldestam I, Enell EL, Kullman E, Borch K. Development of symptoms and complications in individuals with asymptomatic gallstones. Br J Surg 2004;91:734-8.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT SURGICAL MANAGEMENT (LAPAROSCOPIC CHOLECYSTECTOMY)

What is it?

Gallstone surgery means removing the gallbladder with the stones inside it. In the past, surgeons tried removing the stones and leaving the gallbladder behind, but new stones appeared and the problem came back.

Not having a gallbladder isn’t harmful as bile stored in the gallbladder is not essential for digestion.

The gallbladder can be removed under a general anaesthetic by: [28]NHS Choices. Gallstones. http://www.nhs.uk/Conditions/Laparoscopiccholecystectomy/Pages/Whathappenspage.aspx

  • Laparoscopic cholecystectomy is the most commonly used way of removing the gallbladder. The surgeon makes three or four small cuts (incisions) in the abdomen, each about 1cm or less. One incision is in the bellybutton and the others are on the right side of the abdomen. Carbon dioxide gas is put in through a tube in one of the incisions to inflate the abdomen so that the surgeon can see the internal organs. Through the incision in the belly button goes a long thin telescope with a tiny light and video camera on the end. This is called a laparoscope. The surgeon looks at a monitor while removing the gallbladder using tiny surgical instruments inserted into the other incisions.
  • Open surgery is sometimes used to remove the gallbladder, should it become necessary to do so during the operation eg: because they can’t see the gallbladder and surrounding tissues clearly enough or if a person has other illnesses that make it safer to do open surgery than laparoscopic surgery. Open surgery means making a single large incision (about 10-15cm) in the abdomen to remove the gallbladder with standard size surgical instruments.

There are some variations in the way surgeons do laparoscopic cholecystectomy, including:

  • Day-case or overnight: most people can have laparoscopic cholecystectomy in the day surgery unit of their hospital, so they go home the same day as their operation, or they can spend the night after their surgery in hospital. If problems arise during surgery, people sometimes need to stay one or more days in hospital whether or not they had planned to have their gallbladder out as day surgery.
  • Single incision laparoscopic cholecystectomy: the gallbladder is removed through a single small incision in the bellybutton which is used to insert all of the equipment that is needed [29]NICE. Single-incision laparoscopic cholecystectomy. N2182, 2010. This is a technique in development and is only done by specially trained surgeons [29]NICE. Single-incision laparoscopic cholecystectomy. N2182, 2010
  • 2D or 3D: in an effort to reduce the number of operations that start as keyhole surgery but switch to open surgery some surgeons are using 3D instead of 2D video cameras on their laparoscopes to look inside the body and see the gallbladder and other structures [31]Gurusamy, K. S., Sahay, S., and Davidson, B. R. Three dimensional versus two dimensional imaging for laparoscopic cholecystectomy. Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews 2011; issue 1. 3D video enables surgeons to get a sense of the depth as well as the height and width of the place where they are operating [32]Gurusamy, K. S., Sahay, S., and Davidson, B. R. Three dimensional versus two dimensional imaging for laparoscopic cholecystectomy. Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews 2011; issue 1.
Who can have it?

Anyone with symptoms of gallstones can have their gallbladder removed, and most people can have keyhole surgery (laparoscopic cholecystectomy).

Open surgery may be recommended instead of keyhole surgery for people who:

  • Have had several abdominal operations in the past. This is because their internal tissues may be scarred or stuck together (adhesions) from the previous surgery, and make it difficult to get the small instruments needed for keyhole surgery into position

Some people may not be able to have surgery at all, if they:

  • Are elderly and/or have other major illnesses, such as heart or breathing problems
  • Are very overweight.
How effective is it?

Removing the gallbladder is most likely to get rid of gallstones permanently, as without a gallbladder no new stones can be made.

In a group of over 5000 people who had keyhole surgery, open surgery with a large incision (over 5cm) or open surgery with a smaller incision (under 8cm), there was no difference in the effectiveness of the three techniques [33]Keus F, Gooszen H. G, Van Laarhoven C. J. H. M. Open, small-incision, or laparoscopic cholecystectomy for patients with symptomatic cholecystolithiasis. An overview of Cochrane Hepato-Biliary Group reviews. Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews 2010; 2010: CD008318New, published in Issue 1, 2010.

Day-case keyhole surgery is as effective as keyhole surgery when a person stays in hospital overnight, and most people who expect to go home the same day as their surgery are able to do so [34]Gurusamy, K. S., Junnarkar, S., Farouk, M., Davidson, B. R. Day-case versus overnight stay in laparoscopic cholecystectomy. Cochrane Database Syst Rev. 2008;(3).

In a study of people having keyhole surgery, 19 in 100 people scheduled for day-case surgery needed to stay longer in hospital than expected as did 20 in 100 of those expecting to go home the day after their operation [34]Gurusamy, K. S., Junnarkar, S., Farouk, M., Davidson, B. R. Day-case versus overnight stay in laparoscopic cholecystectomy. Cochrane Database Syst Rev. 2008;(3). The main reasons for needing to stay longer in hospital than planned were pain, nausea and vomiting, need to switch to open surgery and clerical errors.

Having keyhole surgery to remove the gallbladder through a single incision is as effective as when three or four incisions are made, though the operation is likely to take longer [36]Pisanu, A., Reccia, I., Porceddu, G., Uccheddu, A. Meta-analysis of prospective randomized studies comparing single-incision laparoscopic cholecystectomy (SILC) and conventional multiport laparoscopic cholecystectomy (CMLC). Journal of Gastrointestinal Surgery 2012; 16: 1790-1801.

In a group of people having their gallbladder removed, standard keyhole surgery took an average 46 minutes, compared to 63 minutes for single incision keyhole surgery. Most hospitals do not offer this procedure.

There isn’t much information about the use of 3D rather than 2D video during keyhole surgery to remove the gallbladder [32]Gurusamy, K. S., Sahay, S., and Davidson, B. R. Three dimensional versus two dimensional imaging for laparoscopic cholecystectomy. Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews 2011; issue 1. In a small group of people who had their gallbladder out by keyhole surgery, there were no deaths and no complications after the operation regardless of whether their surgeon used 2D or 3D video. Surgeons didn’t need to switch to an open operation in either group.

Effects on symptoms and how you feel

If symptoms are definitely due to gallstones, these should disappear after surgery. However, if they don’t go away, this may have been because symptoms were not due to gallstones [33]Keus F, Gooszen H. G, Van Laarhoven C. J. H. M. Open, small-incision, or laparoscopic cholecystectomy for patients with symptomatic cholecystolithiasis. An overview of Cochrane Hepato-Biliary Group reviews. Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews 2010; 2010: CD008318New, published in Issue 1, 2010.

There isn’t much information about how often symptoms come back, so we can’t be sure how likely this is. 

In a group of people who had their gallbladder removed, 91 out of 100 of those who had abdominal pain that was very typical of gallstones reported few or no symptoms three and 12 months after surgery [39]Halldestam, I., Kullman, E., Borch, K. et al. Defined indications for elective cholecystectomy for gallstone disease British Journal of Surgery 2008; 95: 620-626. This compared with 77 out of 100 of those whose pain was not very typical of gallstones or was present in more than one place.

We don’t have any information on the effects of surgery to remove the gallbladder on people with gallstones that aren’t causing them any symptoms.

After keyhole surgery to remove their gallbladder people feel better more quickly than those who have open surgery, but after a while the effects on quality of life are similar. The only major long-term advantage of keyhole surgery over open surgery is that people are more satisfied with the way their scar looks [40]Carraro, A., Mazloum, D. E., Bihl, F., Health-related quality of life outcomes after cholecystectomy. World Journal of Gastroenterology 2011; 17: 4945-4951.

People who have single incision keyhole surgery may be more satisfied with the result than those who have multiple incision keyhole surgery [41]Pisanu, A., Reccia, I., Porceddu, G., Uccheddu, A.. Meta-analysis of prospective randomized studies comparing single-incision laparoscopic cholecystectomy (SILC) and conventional multiport laparoscopic cholecystectomy (CMLC). Journal of Gastrointestinal Surgery 2012; 16: 1790-1801.

In a group of people who had their gallbladder removed, those who had single incision keyhole surgery scored their satisfaction 8.2 on a scale of 0-10, compared to 7.2 with multiple incision keyhole surgery. Most hospitals do not offer single-incision surgery. [41]Pisanu, A., Reccia, I., Porceddu, G., Uccheddu, A.. Meta-analysis of prospective randomized studies comparing single-incision laparoscopic cholecystectomy (SILC) and conventional multiport laparoscopic cholecystectomy (CMLC). Journal of Gastrointestinal Surgery 2012; 16: 1790-1801.

Will it cause any unwanted effects?

All surgery – keyhole or open – carries a small likelihood of complications, such as bleeding, blood clots and wound infection. Other complications of surgery to remove the gallbladder include:

  • Damage to the common bile duct. This is rare but potentially serious and might need a second operation
  • Leakage of bile. This might require a drain to be inserted and possibly also a small plastic tube to be placed in the bile duct in a telescope procedure called an ERCP.

In a group of people who had their gallbladder removed by keyhole surgery: [33]Keus F, Gooszen H. G, Van Laarhoven C. J. H. M. Open, small-incision, or laparoscopic cholecystectomy for patients with symptomatic cholecystolithiasis. An overview of Cochrane Hepato-Biliary Group reviews. Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews 2010; 2010: CD008318New, published in Issue 1, 2010:

  • No one died
  • 9 in 1000 had complications during surgery
  • 24 in 1000 had minor complications after surgery
  • 26 in 1000 had serious complications after surgery
  • 2 in 1000 had injuries to their bile duct

Amongst those who had open surgery: [33]Keus F, Gooszen H. G, Van Laarhoven C. J. H. M. Open, small-incision, or laparoscopic cholecystectomy for patients with symptomatic cholecystolithiasis. An overview of Cochrane Hepato-Biliary Group reviews. Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews 2010; 2010: CD008318New, published in Issue 1, 2010

  • 2 in 1000 died
  • 1 in 1000 had complications during surgery
  • 37 in 1000 had minor complications after surgery
  • 77 in 1000 had serious complications after surgery
  • 2 in 1000 had injuries to their bile duct

Having day-case keyhole surgery does not increase the likelihood of unwanted effects. In a group of people who had their gallbladder removed, there was no significant difference between day surgery and surgery followed by a night in hospital for [34]Gurusamy, K. S., Junnarkar, S., Farouk, M., Davidson, B. R. Day-case versus overnight stay in laparoscopic cholecystectomy. Cochrane Database Syst Rev. 2008;(3):

  • Need for readmission (2 in 100 for day-case and for overnight surgery)
  • Pain – though there was a trend towards less need for major painkillers with day-case surgery
  • Nausea and vomiting on the day of surgery or the day afterwards
  • Quality of life
  • How satisfied people were with their surgery
  • Time taken to get back to normal activities or to work.

Amongst women with gallstones who are pregnant, surgery is avoided unless it is necessary. However if surgery is needed it can be performed safely in the middle months of pregnancy. Having surgery to remove the gallbladder isn’t any more dangerous for the fetus than not having surgery (conservative care). In a group of women who had gallbladder surgery, 6 in 100 gave birth prematurely and in 1 in 100 the fetus died [46]Date, R. S., Kaushal, M., Ramesh, A.. A review of the management of gallstone disease and its complications in pregnancy. American Journal of Surgery 2008; 196: 599-608. Amongst women who had didn’t have surgery for their gallstones, about 4 in 100 gave birth prematurely and in 2 in 100 the fetus died.

The gall bladder and the gallstones are usually removed (via the incision at either the bellybutton or just below the breast bone) in a special bag to protect the wound. Despite this the wound can still sometimes get infected if the bile and stones are infected.

It is unusual to have a tube (drain) put into the wound after surgery to drain off excess fluid as it may increase the likelihood of infection [47]Gurusamy, K. S., Samraj, K., Mullerat, P., Davidson, B. R., Routine abdominal drainage for uncomplicated laparoscopic cholecystectomy. Update of Cochrane Database Syst Rev. 2007;(3):CD006004; PMID: 17636819. A drain may be used if there is concern about leakage of bile or fluid collecting. In a group of people who had keyhole surgery to remove their gallbladder, 3 in 100 people with a drain had an infection, compared with none of those who didn’t have a drain. About 33 in 100 of those didn’t have a drain were able to go home on the day of their operation, compared to none of those who had a drain.

How long it takes to get better

After surgery, symptoms that were due to gallstones go away immediately. The wound may be painful for a few days.

People who have keyhole surgery may get back to normal activities, including work, more quickly than those who have open surgery.

In a group of people, those who had keyhole surgery spent three days less in hospital and needed 23 fewer days off work than those who had open surgery [33]Keus F, Gooszen H. G, Van Laarhoven C. J. H. M. Open, small-incision, or laparoscopic cholecystectomy for patients with symptomatic cholecystolithiasis. An overview of Cochrane Hepato-Biliary Group reviews. Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews 2010; 2010: CD008318New, published in Issue 1, 2010.

In another group of people, those having keyhole surgery had about one day less in hospital than those having small incision open surgery, and needed a similar amount of time to get back to normal activities or to work [33]Keus F, Gooszen H. G, Van Laarhoven C. J. H. M. Open, small-incision, or laparoscopic cholecystectomy for patients with symptomatic cholecystolithiasis. An overview of Cochrane Hepato-Biliary Group reviews. Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews 2010; 2010: CD008318New, published in Issue 1, 2010.

How likely are symptoms or gallstones to come back?

Removing the gallbladder is most likely to get rid of gallstones permanently. Without a gallbladder it is most unlikely that new stones can be made. If however at the time of surgery there were gallstones within the bile duct that were not detected, they may cause problems at a later date.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT CONSERVATIVE CARE (PAINKILLERS, DIETARY CHANGES)

What is it?

Conservative care for gallstones means taking medicines and/or making lifestyle changes instead of having an operation. Medicines for gallstones are: [50]Sanders, G., Kingsnorth, A. N. Gallstones. BMJ 2007; 335: 295-299; Portincasa P, Ciaula AD, Bonfrate L, Wang DQ. Therapy of gallstone disease: What it was, what it is, what it will be. World J Gastrointest Pharmacol Ther. 2012 Apr 6;3(2):7-20

  • Painkillers to relieve the pain that people with gallstones get in their abdomen. Examples are non steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) such as ibuprofen and diclofenac and stronger painkillers such as morphine and pethidine
  • Antispasmodics, for example hyoscine, that relax the muscles of the gallbladder and intestine and reduce painful spasms that occur when the gallbladder is blocked by gallstones
  • Bile acids, such as ursodeoxycholic acid (Urso), which aim to dissolve small bile stones and prevent them from forming again. Taking Urso reduces the amount of cholesterol that the liver makes and changes the mix of bile acids in bile so that it is less likely to form gallstones. They are not suitable for certain types of stones such as larger ones and there is little evidence for their effectiveness.

Lifestyle changes to treat gallstones are:

  • Avoiding fatty foods which contain a lot of cholesterol, such as pies, sausages, fatty meat, butter and margarine, cakes and biscuits
  • Losing weight if you are overweight by eating a healthy diet and taking more exercise. Rapid weight loss on a very low calorie diet can upset the balance of chemicals in the bile and increase the likelihood of getting gallstones. Losing weight slowly by eating less sweet, fatty food, and more vegetables and fruit, and taking regular exercise is more healthy.
Who can have it?

Anyone with gallstones can try taking medicines or making lifestyle changes to treat their symptoms. People with gallstones who have no symptoms may try changing their diet and losing weight to try to stop their gallstones from getting worse, and causing symptoms.

People who plan to have surgery to remove their gallbladder and are overweight may want to lose some weight before their operation as this can reduce their likelihood of getting complications during and after their surgery.

How effective is it?

Conservative care may avoid the need for surgery in some people with gallstones, especially those with mild or no symptoms.

In a group of people followed-up for an average of nine years [26]Festi D, Reggiani ML, Attili AF et al. Natural history of gallstone disease: Expectant management or active treatment? Results from a population-based cohort study. J Gastroenterol Hepatol. 2010 Apr;25(4):719-24, amongst those who had no symptoms at the start:

  • 78 in 100 stayed symptom-free
  • 11 in 100 developed mild symptoms
  • 11 in 100 developed severe symptoms.

In people who started with mild symptoms, these: 

  • Disappeared in 59 out of 100
  • Became severe in 25 out of 100
  • Stayed the same in 17 out of 100

In people who started with severe symptoms, these:

  • Disappeared in 52 in 100
  • Became mild in 17 in 100
  • Stayed the same in 31 in 100.

Of the total number, 33 in 100 had their gallbladder removed:

  • 41 in 100 of those who had no symptoms
  • 17 in 100 of those with mild symptoms
  • 41 in 100 of those with severe symptoms.

In a group of people with gallstones and symptoms who didn’t have surgery straight away, 51 in 100 had had an operation to remove their gallbladder 14 years later, and the rest hadn’t. Those who had surgery typically had it in the first two years after their symptoms started, and almost no one had surgery five years or more after their first symptoms. During the 14 years, 4 in 100 people progressed to having an inflamed gallbladder (acute cholecystitis), stones in their common bile duct or acute pancreatitis. [52]Schmidt, M., Sondenaa, K., Vetrhus, M et al. A randomized controlled study of uncomplicated gallstone disease with a 14-year follow-up showed that operation was the preferred treatment. Digestive Surgery 2011; 28: 270-276.

Delaying surgery in people with biliary colic may result in more complications from gallstones than having surgery straight away [53]Gurusamy, K. S., Samraj, K., Fusai, G., Davidson, B. R. Early versus delayed laparoscopic cholecystectomy for biliary colic. Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews 2008; CD007196. In a group of people who waited an average four months for surgery, 23 in 100 had complications of their gallstones, and 13 in 100 had repeated biliary colic that needed hospital treatment [53]Gurusamy, K. S., Samraj, K., Fusai, G., Davidson, B. R. Early versus delayed laparoscopic cholecystectomy for biliary colic. Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews 2008; CD007196. Their operations and recovery times were longer than for people who didn’t wait for surgery to remove their gallbladder.

In a group of people who took Urso to try to dissolve their gallstones, treatment was more effective in those with small stones (less than 5mm) than those with larger stones [55]Jazrawi RP, Pigozzi MG, Galatola G et al. TC. Optimum bile acid treatment for rapid gall stone dissolution. Gut 1992; 33: 381-386.

Effects on symptoms and how you feel

Taking painkillers may relieve pain caused by gallstones and may be more effective than antispasmodic medicines [56]Colli AC. Meta-analysis: Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs in biliary colic. Alimentary Pharmacology and Therapeutics 2012; 35: 1370-1378 [57]Kumar A, Deed JS, Bhasin B et al. Comparison of the effect of diclofenac with hyoscine-N-butylbromide in the symptomatic treatment of acute biliary colic. ANZ J Surg. 2004 Jul;74(7):573-6.

In a group of people with pain due to gallstones (biliary colic), those who took a non steroidal anti-inflammatory drug (NSAID) were nearly four times more likely to get rid of their pain than those who took a dummy pill (placebo) that wasn’t a painkiller [58]Colli AC. Meta-analysis: Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs in biliary colic. Alimentary Pharmacology and Therapeutics 2012; 35: 1370-1378. They also had half the number of gallstone complications compared with people who didn’t take NSAIDs. NSAIDs were more effective in treating pain than drugs that reduce muscle spasm, and they were just as effective as stronger painkillers, such as morphine.

In a group of people with biliary colic, an injection of an NSAID called diclofenac was more effective in relieving pain than an injection of a drug called hyoscine that reduces muscle spasms [57]Kumar A, Deed JS, Bhasin B et al. Comparison of the effect of diclofenac with hyoscine-N-butylbromide in the symptomatic treatment of acute biliary colic. ANZ J Surg. 2004 Jul;74(7):573-6.

Of those who had diclofenac 92 out of 100 said their pain was completely better four hours after their injection, compared to 70 out of 100 who had hyoscine. Amongst the diclofenac users, 17 in 100 went on to get gallstones stuck in their bile duct or gallbladder (acute cholecystitis) compared to 53 out of 100 who had hyoscine.

Will it cause any unwanted effects?

All medicines cause some unwanted effects.

The most common unwanted effects of diclofenac affect 1 to 10 in 100 users. They are [60]Electronic Medicines Compendium. Diclofenac sodium. http://www.medicines.org.uk/emc/medicine/26835/pil

  • Nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea, wind, indigestion, heartburn, loss of appetite, stomach pain
  • A rise in levels of liver enzymes in the blood
  • Headaches and dizziness
  • Spinning sensation
  • Skin rash

The most common unwanted effects of hyoscine affect fewer than 1 in 100 people. They are [61]Electronic Medicines Compendium. Buscopan tablets. http://www.medicines.org.uk/EMC/medicine/3694/PIL/Buscopan+Tablets/ :

  • Allergic reactions such as skin reactions e.g. nettle rash, itching
  • Dry mouth
  • Small blisters on hands and feet
  • Increased heart rate

The most common unwanted effects of Urso affect less than 10 in 100 people [62]Electronic Medicines Compendium. Destolit 150mg. http://www.medicines.org.uk/EMC/medicine/15695/XPIL/DESTOLIT+150+mg+tablets/ They are:

  • Diarrhoea
  • Pasty stools

 

How long it takes to get better

It is difficult to predict how long people who have conservative care for their gallstones will take to get better. If gallstones are small, they may pass into the intestine and out of the body, and switching to a low fat diet and eating healthily may prevent them from recurring.

People who use painkillers or antispasmodic drugs to treat symptoms may feel better in a few days or weeks, or it may take longer. These drugs do not make the gallstones go away.

How long people need to take Urso treatment to try to dissolve gallstones varies. The length of a course of treatment will depend how much and how quickly the gallstones shrink. It may last about six months, and may continue for up to two years [62]Electronic Medicines Compendium. Destolit 150mg. http://www.medicines.org.uk/EMC/medicine/15695/XPIL/DESTOLIT+150+mg+tablets/

How likely are symptoms or gallstones to come back?

Amongst people who use conservative care for gallstones, about 70 in 100 people with symptoms get them again within two years [64]US National Library of Medicine. Gallstones http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmedhealth/PMH0049865/

After taking gallstone-dissolving medicines, gallstones may recur in 13 in 100 people within a year, and in 61 in 100 after 11 years [65]Villanova N, Bazzoli F, Taroni F et al. Gallstone recurrence after successful oral bile acid treatment. A 12-year follow-up study and evaluation of long-term postdissolution treatment. Gastroenterology 1989; 97: 726-731.
