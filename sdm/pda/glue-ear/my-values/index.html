STEP 5: MY DECISION

Our Decision Support telephone service is here to assist and guide you through the Shared Decision Making process.

The Decision Support team is made of professional Health Coaches who are clinicians, commonly nurses, who have undertaken additional training to become a Health Coach.

The Health Coaches will assist you by:

  • Providing you with information
  • Helping you to understand your condition
  • Recognise what is important to you regarding the outcome of treatment
  • Identifying potential solutions
  • Encourage you in discussing options with your family
  • Transferring skills which will assist you in using the information and resources available to you
  • Support you in building confidence in discussing your choice with your doctor
  • You may find that this can be achieved in one telephone call with a Health Coach however; if further calls are required to support you in reaching your decision the Health Coach will schedule these with you.

Important Things To Note

  • A Health Coach does not know which treatment option is best for you.
  • The Health Coach does not have information about your medical history.
  • You will still need to talk over your decision and reasons for your decision with your doctor or nurse.

MORE INFORMATION ABOUT GLUE EAR

How does the ear work?

The ear takes in vibrations from the outside world and turns them into electrical signals. The brain translates these electrical signals into sounds.

The ear is made up of three parts:

  • Outer ear. This is the bit of the ear we can see. It captures the vibrations from the outside world and directs them towards the middle ear.
  • Middle ear. This is made up of the eardrum and three tiny bones (ossicles) connected in a row. It is usually filled with air. Vibrations from the outside world make the eardrum vibrate. The three tiny bones carry the vibrations from the eardrum to the inner ear.
  • Inner ear. This has a snail-shaped structure called the cochlea which translates the vibration of the fluid within the cochlea into electrical signals. These electrical signals pass to the brain through a nerve (the auditory nerve) where they are translated into sounds.
What is glue ear?

The middle ear becomes filled with a glue-like fluid. The eardrum can’t vibrate properly because of the pressure of the fluid. This will cause hearing problems.

A thin tube called the eustachian tube runs from the ear to the back of the nose. It brings air to the middle ear and also drains fluid away from the middle ear.

Glue ear mainly happens to children, because the eustachian tubes are not fully developed, and therefore don’t work well at letting air into the middle ear to drain the fluid away. Research tells us that there is a genetic component that makes people more susceptible to contracting glue ear.[1]Kvestad E, Kvaerner KJ, Røysamb E, et al. Otitis media: genetic factors and sex differences. Twin Research. 2004; 7: 239-244. [2]Rovers M, Haggard M, Gannon M, et al. Heritability of symptom domains in otitis media: a longitudinal study of 1,373 twin pairs. American Journal of Epidemiology. 2002; 155: 958-964. [3]Casselbrant ML, Mandel EM. Genetic susceptibility to otitis media. Current Opinion in Allergy and Clinical Immunology. 2005; 5: 1-4.

What are the symptoms of glue ear?

Having poor hearing is the most common symptom of glue ear. This may mean you: 

  • Do not hear sounds
  • Have difficulty understanding what people are saying
  • Have difficulty following conversations
  • Have difficulty saying some words
  • Talk louder or quieter than normal
  • Feel withdrawn and do not want to play with other children
  • Feel tired and irritable a lot
  • Feel clumsy and have a poor sense of balance.

Glue ear can affect one or both of your ears. If glue ear affects both your ears, your hearing may be worse than if it affects just one of your ears. [4]NICE. Surgical management of otitis media with effusion in children. February 2008. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/nicemedia/pdf/CG60NICEguideline.pdf (accessed on 01 February 2013).

How do I find out I have glue ear?

Most children find out they have glue ear after seeing their doctor with hearing problems. Glue ear can be difficult for parents or carers to spot because sometimes there are no obvious symptoms or signs.

If you do have earache, high temperature, and vomiting you may have acute otitis media. Acute otitis media is caused by an infection. It is not glue ear. Glue ear is not caused by an infection. However, children who keep getting acute otitis media may be more likely to get glue ear.

Your doctor will check if you have glue ear by looking inside your ears with a device called a pneumatic otoscope. They will also use a small bulb to blow a puff of air into your ear. In a healthy ear, the puff of air will make the eardrum move. If you have glue ear, the eardrum won’t move much because fluid will be pushing up against it from behind.

Other things your doctor will check for are changes to the colour of your eardrum and bubbles behind the eardrum.

Your doctor may ask you to see a specialist for further tests if they think you have glue ear, and if you have had symptoms for more than three months. The specialist usually performs two tests to check if you have glue ear.

  • Audiometry. The test used will depend on your age. In young children, visual response audiometry or play audiometry is used. In this test you hear sounds through speakers. In older children, pure tone audiometry is used. This test uses a device called an audiometer. This device plays sounds at different volumes and frequencies to check how much you can and can’t hear. You will wear headphones for the test. 
  • Tympanometry. This test uses a machine called a tympanometer. Attached to the machine is an earpiece which is placed in the outer ear. The earpiece changes the air pressure in the ear and plays a sound. The machine then measures how well the eardrum is working.
What effect does glue ear have on hearing?

Everyone with glue ear will have some hearing loss. The average hearing loss is about 24 decibels (dB). Decibels are a measure of how loud sounds are. Here are some examples:

  • The sound of someone breathing is about 10 dB.
  • A whisper is about 15 to 20 dB.
  • The voice of a teacher at the back of the classroom is 40 dB.
  • Normal conversation is about 50 to 60 dB.
  • A vacuum cleaner is about 80 dB.
  • A jet taking off (if you’re standing nearby) is about 120 dB.

A loss of 24 dB of hearing is similar to a person with normal hearing putting a finger in each ear. Hearing loss with glue ear can be as much as 45 dB if the fluid trapped in the middle ear is very thick.

A loss of 24 dB of hearing is less likely to cause speech, learning, or behaviour problems. But it is important that your child's school is aware of the hearing loss so that they can make appropriate adjustments to ensure your child isn't disadvantaged within the classroom. A hearing loss of 45 dB may affect speech, learning, and behaviour.

The hearing loss caused by glue ear usually goes away once fluid in the middle ear is cleared up.

Why has this happened to me or my child?

We don’t know why children get glue ear. We know that children are more likely to get it if:

  • They are aged six years or younger
  • They are a boy
  • They attend nursery or preschool
  • They have many brothers and sisters
  • They have brothers or sisters with glue ear
  • They regularly get coughs and colds
  • It’s winter
  • They are bottle fed or use a dummy
  • Somebody in the family home smokes
  • They have a genetic syndrome such as Down’s syndrome
  • They have craniofacial abnormalities such as cleft palate.
How many people have glue ear?

Around 4 in 5 children have had glue ear at least once before the age of four.[5]Browning GG, Rovers MM, Williamson I, et al. Grommets (ventilation tubes) for hearing loss associated with otitis media with effusion in children (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. [6]Zielhuis GA, Rach GH, Van den Broek P. The occurrence of otitis media with effusion in Dutch pre-school children. Clinical Otolaryngology. 1990; 15: 147-153. Most children grow out of glue ear after the age of about six years old.[7]Casselbrant ML, Brostoff LM, Cantekin EI, et al. Otitis media with effusion in preschool children. Laryngoscope. 1985; 95: 428-36.

 

What will happen if I have glue ear?

Glue ear usually gets better on its own with no treatment. About 50 in 100 cases of glue ear clear up within three months.[8]Zielhuis GA, Rach GH, Broek PV. Screening for otitis media with effusion in pre-school children. Lancet. 1989; 1: 311-314. About 75 in 100 cases of glue ear clear up within six months.[8]Zielhuis GA, Rach GH, Broek PV. Screening for otitis media with effusion in pre-school children. Lancet. 1989; 1: 311-314. About 95 in 100 cases clear up within one year.[8]Zielhuis GA, Rach GH, Broek PV. Screening for otitis media with effusion in pre-school children. Lancet. 1989; 1: 311-314.

Glue ear normally goes away completely as children get older. Some children get glue ear that doesn’t go away, others will keep getting glue ear. We don’t know why this happens to some children.

The main problem with glue ear is its effect on hearing. Hearing problems can last for a long time in some children with glue ear. This may cause difficulties with school, learning, and speech and language development.

Treatments for glue ear

The main treatments used for glue ear are active observation, hearing aids, and surgery. We talk more about them in the next part of the decision aid.

Other treatments sometimes used for glue ear that you might hear about are:

  • Antibiotics. These are drugs that kill bacteria. Children with glue ear are prone to frequent ear infections. But research tells us that there is not a substantial improvement in hearing as a result of the use of antibiotics for glue ear. In some special circumstances, however, antibiotics may be used.[11]van Zon A, van der Heijden GJ, van Dongen TM, et al. Antibiotics for otitis media with effusion in children (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.
  • Nasal balloons. This involves blowing up a balloon through each nostril three times a day. Blowing into balloons may help open up your eustachian tubes, which could cut your chances of getting glue ear. Doctors are testing nasal balloons on children aged 4 to 11 years to see how well they work.
  • Oral steroids. These are tablets that can reduce inflammation. They are not often used because taking them for long periods of time can cause serious side effects for children, such as stopping or slowing their growth. But the National Institute of Health Research (NIHR) is funding a large study to test how well these drugs work in children with glue ear when used short-term.[12]National Institute for Health Research Health Technology Assessment Programme. Oral STeroids for Resolution of otitis media with effusion (OME) In CHildren: the OSTRICH study. Available at http://www.hta.ac.uk/project/2896.asp (accessed 19 December 2012).

Pneumococcal conjugate vaccines and influenza vaccines protect young children against pneumococcal and influenza infections, which are a common cause of middle ear infections. But they don’t prevent glue ear.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT ACTIVE OBSERVATION

What is active observation?

Active observation is where your doctor or audiologist (a person who checks hearing) might check your glue ear about every three months to see if it’s improving, staying the same, or getting worse. Your doctor or audiologist will monitor your condition to check whether the glue ear is having any negative impact on your speech, language, educational, or social development. If there are no problems with your glue ear, you won’t need to see your doctor or audiologist so regularly. Instead, they will ask you to come back to see them if any problems arise.

You might need to see a speech and language therapist to check if your glue ear is affecting your learning or speech development.

 

 

Who can have active observation?

Anyone with glue ear can choose to have active observation. It may be that you do not need treatment or that you would like to wait before starting treatment.

What are the main effects of active observation on glue ear?

If you have active observation, your glue ear may get better, stay the same, or get worse. Usually it clears up on its own within a few months, although 50 in 100 children that get better have glue ear recurrence within three months.[13]Zeilhuis GA, Rach GH, Broek PV. Screening for otitis media with effusion in pre-school children. Lancet 1989; 1: 311-314.

  • About 50 in 100 cases of glue ear clear up within three months.[13]Zeilhuis GA, Rach GH, Broek PV. Screening for otitis media with effusion in pre-school children. Lancet 1989; 1: 311-314.
  • About 75 in 100 cases of glue ear clear up within six months.[13]Zeilhuis GA, Rach GH, Broek PV. Screening for otitis media with effusion in pre-school children. Lancet 1989; 1: 311-314.
  • About 95 in 100 cases clear up within one year.[13]Zeilhuis GA, Rach GH, Broek PV. Screening for otitis media with effusion in pre-school children. Lancet 1989; 1: 311-314.

The hearing loss caused by glue ear usually goes away once glue ear has cleared up.

What are the main effects of active observation on what you can do and how you feel?

Children with glue ear who have active observation do just as well with language development as those who have grommet surgery.[17]Rovers MM, Straatman H, Ingels K, et al. The effect of ventilation tubes on language development in infants with otitis media with effusion: A randomized trial. Pediatrics. 2000; 106: E42.

Children with glue ear who have active observation for around six to nine months before grommet surgery do just as well at school and with learning and development as those who have grommet surgery without any delay.[18]Paradise JL, Feldman HM, Campbell TF. Tympanostomy tubes and developmental outcomes at 9 to 11 years of age. New England Journal of Medicine. 2007; 356: 248-261.

We don't know the effect of active observation on children who already have speech, language, learning or developmental problems. There has not been any research looking at this.

You can go swimming when you're having active observation for glue ear. You can go under water and dive in a swimming pool but can’t go scuba diving.

You can travel by plane if you're having active observation for glue ear. You may feel pain in your ear when you fly because of the change in air pressure, especially during take-off and landing.

What are the main side effects or complications of active observation?

There are no side effects or complications with active observation. Your doctor may keep checking your condition. Long-term, untreated glue ear can cause permanent loss of hearing in a small number of children.[19]Lim DJ. Recent advances in otitis media. The Annals of Otology, Rhinology & Laryngology. 2002; 199: 1-124.

How might active observation affect my life?

Your child may need to see your doctor or audiologist (a person who checks hearing) about every three months so that they can check their condition. f there are no problems with your glue ear, you won’t need to see your doctor or audiologist so regularly. Instead, they will ask you to come back to see them if any problems arise.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT HEARING AIDS

What are hearing aids?

Hearing aids are small electronic devices that fit in or around your ear. They make sounds louder and clearer so that you can hear them better.

If you choose hearing aids, you will need check ups to be sure the hearing aid fits the ear properly and is working well to improve hearing. You are likely to have several checks with an audiologist (a person who checks hearing) and you may also have one or more checks with your doctor. The number of checks needed may depend on age and how much hearing has been lost.

There are different styles of hearing aids and your audiologist will help you choose the most appropriate for you.

Who can have hearing aids?

Anyone with glue ear can choose to have hearing aids if their hearing is affected and the hearing loss causes them problems.

What are the main effects of hearing aids on glue ear?

Hearing aids may help you to hear sounds louder and clearer while you are wearing them. They won’t treat your glue ear or cure your hearing problems.

About 98 in 100 parents of children with glue ear say their child can hear better with hearing aids.[20]Flanagan PM, Knight LC, Thomas A, et al. Hearing aids and glue ear. Clinical Otolaryngology and Allied Sciences. 1996; 21, 297-300. This information is from a small study of 48 children. A lot of the research looking at how well hearing aids improve the hearing in children with glue ear has been in small groups of children. This may mean the research is not relevant to all children with glue ear. Further research with larger numbers of children is needed to understand potential outcomes better.

What are the main effects of hearing aids on what you can do and how you feel?

Hearing aids may help improve your speech and concentration. About 3 in 10 parents of children with glue ear say hearing aids improve their child's speech.[21]Jardine AH, Griffiths MV, Midgley E. The acceptance of hearing aids for children with otitis media with effusion. Journal of Laryngology & Otology. 1999; 113: 314-317. About 6 in 10 parents of children with glue ear say hearing aids improve their child's concentration.[21]Jardine AH, Griffiths MV, Midgley E. The acceptance of hearing aids for children with otitis media with effusion. Journal of Laryngology & Otology. 1999; 113: 314-317. This information is from a small study of 39 children so it may not be relevant to all children with glue ear. Further research with larger numbers of children is needed to understand potential outcomes better.

If you can hear better with hearing aids, it may make it easier for you to do things like understand what people are saying, follow conversations, hear the TV, and listen to music. You may be able to do more and feel less frustrated and annoyed if you can hear things better.

You can go swimming if you have hearing aids. Diving or swimming below the surface of the water will increase the pressure on your eardrum and this may give you pain in your ear. If your hearing aids are not waterproof, you will need to take them off before swimming.

You can travel by plane if you have hearing aids. You may feel pain in your ear when you fly because of the change in air pressure, especially during take-off and landing.

What are the main side effects of hearing aids?

Wearing hearing aids can take a while to get used to. Not everyone feels comfortable wearing them.

Some children who use hearing aids say they are uncomfortable. Some say they can hear background noise, whistling noise and feedback, and the hearing aid makes noises too loud. But hearing aids shouldn’t cause these issues if they are well fitted by a good audiologist. Some older children find them embarrassing to wear, although nowadays hearing aids are very small and don't look obvious.

If a hearing aid is turned up too loud, it can cause something called overamplification. A hearing aid that is too loud might seem to make it easier to hear sounds but it can cause more damage to hearing over time. Your doctor or audiologist will be able to tell you more about how loud your hearing aid should be.

Children can lose hearing aids, or sometimes accidentally swallow the battery.

How might hearing aids affect my life?

You will need to remember to put on your hearing aid each day, and remember to take it off before going to bed. If the hearing aid is not waterproof, you will need to take them off before having a shower or a bath. You will need to change the battery in your hearing aid when it runs out.

As a parent, carer, or teacher, you will learn how to use the hearing aids correctly and care for them. A specialist teacher from the Local Authority's Sensory Support Service can help you achieve this.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT SURGERY

What is surgery for glue ear?

The main type of surgery for glue ear is called grommet surgery. This surgery involves putting a tiny plastic tube (grommet) into your eardrum. This helps to drain glue away from the middle ear and let air in, which means the eardrum can vibrate properly and you can hear better.

Most grommets stay in the eardrum for about seven months.[23]Kalcioglu MT, Cokkeser Y, Kizilay A, et al. Follow-up of 366 ears after tympanostomy tube insertion: why is it draining? Otolaryngology and Head and Neck Surgery. 2003; 128: 560-564. Some grommets are designed to stay in the ear for longer (two to three years). Most grommets fall out naturally. It doesn't hurt when they fall out and the hole in the eardrum usually heals by itself.

You may also have an operation to take out your adenoids. This operation is called an adenoidectomy. Adenoids are small lumps of tissue at the back of the nose. They help trap germs as part of the body's immune system. If your adenoids become infected and swollen, the infection may spread to the middle ear and can block your eustachian tubes and prevent glue draining away from their middle ear. Having your adenoids taken out may help prevent your eustachian tubes from getting blocked. This may help your glue ear.

Grommet surgery is carried out by a surgeon in a hospital under general anaesthetic, which means you will be asleep throughout the operation. If you have your adenoids taken out, it may be done at the same time as having grommet surgery, and under the same general anaesthetic. You won't have any visible cuts or scarring if you have these operations.

Who can have surgery?

Grommet surgery is usually for children with glue ear who:[24]NICE. Surgical management of otitis media with effusion in children. February 2008. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/nicemedia/pdf/CG60NICEguideline.pdf (accessed on 29 October 2012).

  • Have it in both ears
  • Have had it for more than three months
  • Have problems hearing. For example, a normal conversation cannot be heard.

Your doctor might advise that you have your adenoids taken out if you have glue ear and often get coughs and colds as well.

What are the main effects of surgery on glue ear?

Having grommet surgery may improve your hearing. Some children with glue ear have big improvements in their hearing after grommet surgery and others have a small improvement in their hearing.

Most children with glue ear who have grommet surgery have an improvement in their hearing of around 12 dB three months after surgery. An improvement of 12 decibels (dB) is like being able to hear breathing and some whispers if you were unable to hear these sounds before. Six to nine months after surgery, this improvement in hearing decreases to about 4 dB. [5]Browning GG, Rovers MM, Williamson I, et al. Grommets (ventilation tubes) for hearing loss associated with otitis media with effusion in children (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

After one year, there is no difference in hearing between children with glue ear who have grommet surgery and those who don't.[5]Browning GG, Rovers MM, Williamson I, et al. Grommets (ventilation tubes) for hearing loss associated with otitis media with effusion in children (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. This could be because the hearing of those who don't have an operation gets better over time and eventually improves to the same level as children who have had an operation. Or it might be because sometimes grommets fall out after surgery and these children develop glue ear and hearing loss again.

What are the main effects of surgery on what you can do and how you feel?

On average, children with glue ear who have grommet surgery soon after they've been diagnosed with glue ear do as well (but no better) at school and with learning and development as those who delay having grommet surgery.[18]Paradise JL, Feldman HM, Campbell TF. Tympanostomy tubes and developmental outcomes at 9 to 11 years of age. New England Journal of Medicine. 2007; 356: 248-261.

We don't know the effect of surgery on children who already have speech, language, learning, or developmental problems. There has not been any research looking at this.

Having your adenoids removed may help prevent you from getting regular ear, nose, and throat infections.

You can go swimming two weeks after having grommet surgery, but you shouldn't dive or swim below the surface of the water. Getting dirty water into your middle ear may give you an infection.

You can travel by plane after having grommet surgery. Flying may be less painful for your ears compared with if you don't have surgery, especially during take-off and landing.

What are the main side effects and complications of surgery?

Having general anaesthetic during surgery may make you feel a bit wobbly and dizzy when you wake up after surgery. You may feel sick.

Grommet surgery

You might have mild pain in your ears after grommet surgery. Other side effects or complications include:

  • Ear infection. About 7 in 100 children get this after surgery.[28]MRC Multicentre Otitis Media Study Group. Adjuvant adenoidectomy in persistent bilateral otitis media with effusion: hearing and revision surgery outcomes through 2 years in the TARGET randomised trial. Clinical Otolaryngology. 2012; 37; 107-116. Ear infections can cause discharge (oozing) from the ear. Ear infections can be treated with antibiotic ear drops. If ear drops don't work and the infections keep coming back, the infected grommet may need to be taken out.
  • Hole in the eardrum that doesn't heal. Between 1 in 100 and 6 in 100 children get this after surgery.[29]Hellstrom S, Groth A, Jorgensen F, et al. Ventilation tube treatment: A systematic review of the literature. Otolaryngology-Head and Neck Surgery 2011; 145: 383-395. Perforated eardrums usually heal themselves. You may need an operation to close the hole if it doesn't heal.
  • Scarring of the eardrum. About 38 in 100 children get this after surgery.[5]Browning GG, Rovers MM, Williamson I, et al. Grommets (ventilation tubes) for hearing loss associated with otitis media with effusion in children (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. This can last for many years but rarely causes hearing problems.
  • The grommet falling out sooner than it’s meant to (before six to nine months). You might need an operation to put the grommet back in.
  • The grommet not falling out. If your grommets are still in your ears after three years, you may need to have an operation to remove them.
  • The grommet falling into the middle ear. This almost never happens. If it does, you will need an operation to remove the grommet.

Removal of adenoids (adenoidectomy)

Your throat may be sore after having your adenoids removed. This will usually last a day or so and is rare. You may also get bleeding after having your adenoids taken out. About 6 in 1,000 children who have their adenoids taken out have bleeding after the operation.[28]MRC Multicentre Otitis Media Study Group. Adjuvant adenoidectomy in persistent bilateral otitis media with effusion: hearing and revision surgery outcomes through 2 years in the TARGET randomised trial. Clinical Otolaryngology. 2012; 37; 107-116. If the bleeding does not stop on its own, you may need another operation to stop the bleeding.

Other possible side effects or complications include infections, bad breath, blocked nose, change in voice, earache, damage to teeth, and stiff jaw.

How might surgery affect my life?

You will need to go to hospital to have surgery for glue ear. You will be able to go home the same day as having grommet surgery and adenoid surgery.

About 34 in 100 children who have grommet surgery need to have the operation done again within two years of the first operation.[32]MRC Multi-Centre Otitis Media Study Group. Surgery for persistent otitis media with effusion: generalizability of results from the UK trial (TARGET). Clinical Otolaryngology. 2001; 26: 417-424. This will mean spending more time in a hospital. About 13 in 100 children who have their adenoids removed at the same time as having grommet surgery need to have a second operation.[32]MRC Multi-Centre Otitis Media Study Group. Surgery for persistent otitis media with effusion: generalizability of results from the UK trial (TARGET). Clinical Otolaryngology. 2001; 26: 417-424.

If you have a big improvement in your hearing immediately after surgery, everyday sounds might seem loud and make you feel uncomfortable. This usually goes away after a day or two as you get used to the new level of sound.
