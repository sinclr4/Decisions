Our Decision Support telephone service is here to assist and guide you through the Shared Decision Making process.

The Decision Support team is made of professional Health Coaches who are clinicians, commonly nurses, who have undertaken additional training to become a Health Coach.

The Health Coaches will assist you by:

  • Providing you with information
  • Helping you to understand your condition
  • Recognise what is important to you regarding the outcome of treatment
  • Identifying potential solutions
  • Encourage you in discussing options with your family
  • Transferring skills which will assist you in using the information and resources available to you
  • Support you in building confidence in discussing your choice with your doctor
  • You may find that this can be achieved in one telephone call with a Health Coach however; if further calls are required to support you in reaching your decision the Health Coach will schedule these with you.

Important Things To Note

  • A Health Coach does not know which treatment option is best for you.
  • The Health Coach does not have information about your medical history.
  • You will still need to talk over your decision and reasons for your decision with your doctor or nurse.

MORE INFORMATION ABOUT HIGH BLOOD PRESSURE

What is blood pressure?

Blood pressure is a measure of how strongly your blood pushes against your blood vessels (arteries) as it moves around your body, and how your blood vessels absorb the force of your blood pushing against them.

To understand why you have high blood pressure and how treatments work, it helps to know a bit about how blood flows through your body and what controls the pressure.

  • Your blood is pumped around the body by your heart.
  • Blood leaves the heart through your arteries. This blood carries oxygen and food to all the cells in your body.
  • You need a certain amount of pressure to keep blood moving through your arteries.
  • When your heart beats, it pushes blood out and around your body. This causes the pressure to rise. Your blood pressure is lowest when the heart relaxes and fills with blood.

There are two parts to your blood pressure reading:

  • The first (upper) number is your systolic pressure. It measures the pressure of the blood when your heart pushes blood out.
  • The second (lower) number is your diastolic pressure. This is the pressure measured when your heart relaxes and fills up with blood.
  • Blood pressure is measured as millimetres of mercury (mmHg). So, a blood pressure of 145/95 mmHg means your systolic blood pressure is 145 and your diastolic blood pressure is 95. If you have high blood pressure, you may have a high systolic blood pressure, a high diastolic blood pressure, or both.
What is high blood pressure?

It’s normal for blood pressure to vary from one person to another. It’s also normal for your blood pressure to rise and fall throughout the day and the night. There’s no cut-off point that means you will definitely have high blood pressure that causes you health problems.

If your blood pressure goes up and stays up it's called high blood pressure. High blood pressure is also called 'hypertension'.

Doctors and nurses say you have high blood pressure if, over a period of time, either your upper (systolic) blood pressure has been 140 mmHg or higher, or your lower (diastolic) blood pressure has been 90 mmHg or higher.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Hypertension: clinical management of primary hypertension in adults. August 2011. Available at: http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG127 (accessed on 18 December 2012).

No one knows exactly what goes on in your body to cause high blood pressure. In around 10 in 100 people with high blood presure there is a cause, such as kidney disease.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Hypertension: clinical management of primary hypertension in adults. August 2011. Available at: http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG127 (accessed on 18 December 2012). There are many systems in the body that control your blood pressure. If there are changes in any of them, this can cause changes in your blood pressure.

What are the symptoms of high blood pressure?

If you have high blood pressure you probably won't feel ill or have any symptoms.[3]Marshall IJ, Wolfe CDA, McKevitt C. Lay perspectives on hypertension and drug adherence: systematic review of qualitative research. BMJ. 2012; 344: e3953. Most people have no symptoms and only find out they have high blood pressure when it causes them health problems. Over time, high blood pressure can damage your blood vessels and increase your chances of having a heart attack or a stroke. It can also lead to kidney disease and heart failure. If your high blood pressure causes you other health problems, you may get symptoms.

Very rarely, untreated blood pressure can rise suddenly without any warning and get very high. It is very serious and can lead to death. People with blood pressure this high need treatment straight away.

The symptoms of this type of severe high blood pressure include: a bad headache, feeling confused, nausea, restlessness or feeling dizzy. If you have these symptoms, you should see a doctor urgently. Your GP may also suggest you see a specialist.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Hypertension: clinical management of primary hypertension in adults. August 2011. Available at: http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG127 (accessed on 18 December 2012).

What has caused my high blood pressure?

You probably won't be able to tell why you have high blood pressure. Around 90 in 100 people with high blood pressure never know the cause.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Hypertension: clinical management of primary hypertension in adults. August 2011. Available at: http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG127 (accessed on 18 December 2012).

There are certain things that increase the risk of high blood pressure. These are called risk factors. Your high blood pressure may be caused by a combination of risk factors. Some of the most common risk factors for high blood pressure are:

Getting older
Our systolic (and, to a lesser extent, our diastolic) blood pressure tends to go up as we get older. For example, just under 10 in 100 men aged between 16 and 24 have high blood pressure, compared with 60 in 100 men aged between 65 and 74.[6]British Heart Foundation. Coronary heart disease statistics 2008. July 2008. Available at: http://www.bhf.org.uk (accessed on 18 December 2012). The same is true for women.

Being pregnant
Up to 10 in 100 pregnant women get high blood pressure (called gestational high blood pressure).[7]Williams B, Poulter NR, Brown MJ, et al. The BHS Guidelines Working Party guidelines for management of hypertension: report of the fourth working party of the British Hypertension Society, 2004-BHS IV. Journal of Human Hypertension. 2004; 18: 139-185. This can go away on its own without treatment. It can sometimes be linked to a condition called pre-eclampsia. Because pre-eclampsia can cause women to go into labour too early, or lead to babies weighing too little when they are born, your doctor will keep a close check on your blood pressure.

Your ethnic group
In the UK, people of black African or Caribbean origin are more likely to have high blood pressure than other British people.[7]Williams B, Poulter NR, Brown MJ, et al. The BHS Guidelines Working Party guidelines for management of hypertension: report of the fourth working party of the British Hypertension Society, 2004-BHS IV. Journal of Human Hypertension. 2004; 18: 139-185. As many as half of all black Afro-Caribbean people aged over 40 have high blood pressure. People in this group also have a higher risk of stroke and kidney failure than other British people. People of South Asian (Indian, Pakistani, or Bangladeshi) origin in the UK are also more likely to have high blood pressure and are more prone to stroke and heart attacks than other British people.[7]Williams B, Poulter NR, Brown MJ, et al. The BHS Guidelines Working Party guidelines for management of hypertension: report of the fourth working party of the British Hypertension Society, 2004-BHS IV. Journal of Human Hypertension. 2004; 18: 139-185. We're not sure why there are these differences among ethnic groups.

Being overweight
If you are overweight you are more likely to have high blood pressure than someone who is of normal weight.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Hypertension: clinical management of primary hypertension in adults. August 2011. Available at: http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG127 (accessed on 18 December 2012). You may be more likely to have high blood pressure if you carry this extra weight mainly on your abdomen or waist rather than on your hips and thighs.[11]National Institutes of Health; National Heart, Lung, and Blood Institute. Your guide to lowering blood pressure. Available at: http://www.nhlbi.nih.gov/health/public/heart/hbp/hbp_low/hbp_low.pdf (accessed on 18 December 2012). We don't know why this is the case.

Not exercising enough
If you don't get regular exercise, your chances of getting high blood pressure are much higher than for someone who is active and fit.[12]Chobanian AV, Bakris GL, Black HR, et al. The seventh report of the Joint National Committee on prevention, detection, evaluation, and treatment of high blood pressure. Available at: http://hyper.ahajournals.org/content/42/6/1206.full.pdf+html (accessed on 18 December 2012).

What you eat and drink
Your diet, and how much alcohol you drink, can affect your blood pressure. If you have more than two alcoholic drinks a day, you are up to twice as likely to get high blood pressure as someone who doesn't drink.[13]Fuchs FD, Chambless LE, Whelton PK, et al. Alcohol consumption and the incidence of hypertension. Hypertension. 2001; 37: 1242-1250. People who add salt to their food or who eat salty food are more likely to have high blood pressure than people who have less salt in their diet.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Hypertension: clinical management of primary hypertension in adults. August 2011. Available at: http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG127 (accessed on 18 December 2012). In the UK people are advised to eat less than 6 grams (or one teaspoon) of table salt a day.

Smoking
You're more likely to have high blood pressure if you smoke.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Hypertension: clinical management of primary hypertension in adults. August 2011. Available at: http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG127 (accessed on 18 December 2012). Smoking can temporarily raise your blood pressure. If your blood pressure is raised for a long period of time, this can lead to high blood pressure. Stopping smoking reduces your risk of heart and blood vessel diseases, such as a heart attack and a stroke, but doesn't affect your blood pressure.[16]National Institutes of Health; National Heart, Lung and Blood Institute. Does smoking tobacco cause high blood pressure? Available at: http://www.nhlbi.nih.gov/hbp/prevent/factors/smoke.htm (accessed on 18 December 2012).

Stress
There is some evidence that being in a very stressful situation can push up your blood pressure for a short time. We don't know whether feeling stressed has a lasting effect on your blood pressure because there hasn't been enough research on this to be sure.[17]Beevers G, Lip GY, O'Brien E (eds). ABC of hypertension. 4th edition. BMJ Books, London, UK; 2001. If you cope with stress by smoking, drinking alcohol, or eating more, this can raise your blood pressure.

Other health problems
You are more likely to have high blood pressure if you have certain other health problems. You are at higher risk of having high blood pressure if your have high cholesterol, some heart diseases (such as left ventricular hypertrophy), kidney disease, or diabetes.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Hypertension: clinical management of primary hypertension in adults. August 2011. Available at: http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG127 (accessed on 18 December 2012). 

How do I find out I have high blood pressure?

You may not get symptoms if you have high blood pressure. The only way to find out if you have high blood pressure is to have it measured. It’s most likely to be a nurse who checks your blood pressure as part of your general health check at your GP surgery.

The blood pressure test
Blood pressure is measured with a device called a sphygmomanometer. Blood pressure is measured as millimetres of mercury (mmHg).

To measure your blood pressure, a nurse wraps a cuff around your upper arm, above your elbow. Some can be placed on or around your wrist. Your nurse will pump air into the cuff to tighten it and then slowly release the air in the cuff. Newer machines show your blood pressure on a digital reading. Older machines show your blood pressure on a gauge. Your nurse may read a gauge while they use a stethoscope to listen for the sound of the blood rushing back through.

Your nurse should check your blood pressure in both arms, and may check it more than once.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Hypertension: clinical management of primary hypertension in adults. August 2011. Available at: http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG127 (accessed on 18 December 2012).
If your blood pressure is above 140/90 mmHg the first time, you'll need more checks to see if it is usually high, or if this was just a one-off high reading.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Hypertension: clinical management of primary hypertension in adults. August 2011. Available at: http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG127 (accessed on 18 December 2012).

Your blood pressure can go up and down, depending on the time of day and what you're doing. So to confirm if you have high blood pressure all the time, you’ll need to have your blood pressure measured at home, for 24 hours.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Hypertension: clinical management of primary hypertension in adults. August 2011. Available at: http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG127 (accessed on 18 December 2012).

This is called ambulatory blood pressure monitoring (ABPM for short). You wear a small, light device that takes measurements of your blood pressure (at least two every hour) while you move around and do normal things. You wear the monitor for a day and then return it to your GP surgery. Your ambulatory blood pressure reading is the average of all the readings taken during the day.

About 25 in 100 people who have an initial high blood pressure reading in the GP surgery will have a normal ambulatory blood pressure.[22]Hodgkinson J, Mant J, Martin U. Relative effectiveness of clinic and home blood pressure monitoring compared with ambulatory blood pressure monitoring in diagnosis of hypertension: systematic review. BMJ. 2011; 342: d3621. That means they don't really have high blood pressure, and don't need treatment. They should have their blood pressure checked again in five years.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Hypertension: clinical management of primary hypertension in adults. August 2011. Available at: http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG127 (accessed on 18 December 2012).

Not everyone can have ambulatory blood pressure readings done over 24 hours. If your doctor or nurse doesn't use ambulatory blood pressure monitoring, he or she should still take several readings before deciding that you have high blood pressure. A single reading is not enough. Your doctor or nurse may ask you to use a machine to take blood pressure readings at home, over four to seven days. This is called home blood pressure monitoring (HBPM for short), and is a different type of machine to an ambulatory blood pressure monitor. You’ll still need to go back to your GP surgery with the readings, to confirm if you have high blood pressure.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Hypertension: clinical management of primary hypertension in adults. August 2011. Available at: http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG127 (accessed on 18 December 2012).

 

How many people have high blood pressure?

High blood pressure is very common and affects an equal number of men and women. At least 25 in 100 people in the UK have high blood pressure.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Hypertension: clinical management of primary hypertension in adults. August 2011. Available at: http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG127 (accessed on 18 December 2012).

Our blood pressure tends to go up as we get older. For example, just under 10 in 100 men aged between 16 and 24 have high blood pressure, compared with 60 in 100 men aged between 65 and 74.[6]British Heart Foundation. Coronary heart disease statistics 2008. July 2008. Available at: http://www.bhf.org.uk (accessed on 18 December 2012).

What will happen?

It's hard to say what will happen to you if you have high blood pressure. It depends on things like how high your blood pressure is, how long it’s been high, and whether you have other health problems such as high cholesterol. Your chances of staying healthy are better if your blood pressure is kept low and under good control (less than 140/90 mmHg).[27]National Institutes of Health; National Heart, Lung and Blood Institute; National High Blood Pressure Education Program. The sixth report of the Joint National Committee on prevention, detection, evaluation, and treatment of high blood pressure. Archives of Internal Medicine. 1997; 157: 2413-2446.

Without treatment, it's likely that high blood pressure will continue to rise. This may make it harder to lower your blood pressure if you decide to have treatment later on. It may also damage your blood vessels and your organs, such as your kidneys.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Hypertension: clinical management of primary hypertension in adults. August 2011. Available at: http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG127 (accessed on 18 December 2012).

There are some general things we can say about high blood pressure and the health problems it can lead to. The main health problems related to high blood pressure are heart attacks, strokes, heart failure, and kidney failure.

Heart attacks and strokes

  • High blood pressure can damage the walls of your blood vessels. The damage to the wall makes it easier for fatty material to stick to it. This can cause blood clots to form.[17]Beevers G, Lip GY, O'Brien E (eds). ABC of hypertension. 4th edition. BMJ Books, London, UK; 2001.
  • If a blood clot blocks the flow of blood to your heart it can lead to a heart attack. If a blood clot blocks the flow of blood to your brain it can lead to a stroke.
  • The higher your blood pressure and the longer it stays high, the greater your risk of having a heart attack or a stroke.
  • Not everybody with high blood pressure will get heart disease or have a stroke. There are many things that affect your chances of getting heart disease other than blood pressure. Your personal risk will depend on whether you have other risk factors, such as whether you smoke.
  • The people who are at the greatest risk of having a heart attack or a stroke are those who already have signs of heart disease.[30]World Health Organization and International Society of Hypertension. Guidelines for the management of hypertension. Journal of Hypertension. 1999; 17: 151-185.
  • If you have angina (a type of chest pain that you get especially when you exert yourself) or if you've already had a heart attack, your chances of having a heart attack or stroke are higher if your blood pressure is high. If this is the case, your doctor will pay extra attention to your blood pressure.

Heart failure
If your blood pressure is high, your heart has to work harder to pump blood through your blood vessels. Over time, the extra work damages the heart. Eventually the damage can cause heart failure.

The main symptom of heart failure is being short of breath, even after doing something that shouldn't make you feel so tired (for example, walking up a few stairs). You may also get swollen ankles and feel very tired.

Kidney failure
If the blood vessels in your kidneys become narrow and damaged because of high blood pressure, your kidneys may not work properly. Over time they may stop working completely.[31]Mulrow CD. Evidence based hypertension. BMJ Books, London, UK; 2001.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT NO TREATMENT

What is no treatment for high blood pressure?

No treatment for high blood pressure means you decide not to make any lifestyle changes or take any medicines to lower your blood pressure. Instead, you and your doctor may regularly measure your blood pressure to check it has not risen to a dangerously high level.

Who can have no treatment for high blood pressure?

Anyone can decide not to have treatment for high blood pressure. Because of the risk that your blood pressure will rise over time to a level that could raise your risk of health problems such as a stroke or a heart attack, your GP will probably advise that you have some form of treatment. It is your decision whether or not to have treatment.

What are the main effects of no treatment on high blood pressure?

It’s difficult to say what will happen to your blood pressure if you decide to not have treatment. Whether and how quickly your blood pressure rises depends on things like how high your blood pressure is, how long it’s been high, and whether you have other health problems such as high cholesterol. Your chances of staying healthy are better if your blood pressure is kept low and under good control (less than 140/90 mmHg).[27]National Institutes of Health; National Heart, Lung and Blood Institute; National High Blood Pressure Education Program. The sixth report of the Joint National Committee on prevention, detection, evaluation, and treatment of high blood pressure. Archives of Internal Medicine. 1997; 157: 2413-2446.

Without any treatment, it’s likely that your blood pressure will continue to rise. This may make it harder to lower your blood pressure if you decide to have treatment later on. It could also damage your blood vessels and your organs, such as your kidneys.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Hypertension: clinical management of primary hypertension in adults. August 2011. Available at: http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG127 (accessed on 18 December 2012).

High blood pressure that is not treated is more likely to damage your blood vessels than high blood pressure that is treated. This can lead to heart problems such as a heart attack or heart failure.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Hypertension: clinical management of primary hypertension in adults. August 2011. Available at: http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG127 (accessed on 18 December 2012).

On average if we took everyone in England, figures show that for every 2 mmHg rise in systolic blood pressure, the risk of dying from heart disease increases by 7% and the risk of dying from a stroke increases by 10%.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Hypertension: clinical management of primary hypertension in adults. August 2011. Available at: http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG127 (accessed on 18 December 2012).

Very rarely, blood pressure can rise suddenly without any warning and get very high. This happens to around 1 in 100 people who have high blood pressure.[36]Ramsay L, Williams B, Johnston G, et al. Guidelines for management of hypertension: report of the third working party of the British Hypertension Society. Journal of Human Hypertension. 1999; 13: 569-592. It is very serious and can lead to death.

 

What are the main effects of no treatment for high blood pressure on what you can do and how you feel?

We don’t know for sure how deciding not to have treatment for high blood pressure affects what you can do and how you feel. Not many studies have looked into this.

We do know that without any treatment, high blood pressure is likely to continue to rise. This can cause symptoms such as a bad headache, feeling confused, nausea, dizziness, trouble sleeping, or feeling very sleepy. These symptoms may affect what you can do and how you feel.

What are the main side effects of no treatment for high blood pressure?

This option is not a treatment for so you won’t have any side effects. Your blood pressure may continue to rise and this can lead to serious health problems or death.

How might no treatment for high blood pressure affect my life?

It’s difficult to predict whether deciding not to have treatment for high blood pressure will affect your life.

Making the decision not to have treatment for high blood pressure probably won’t affect your day to day life much. You may have to make time to see your GP regularly to check your blood pressure.

Without treatment, your blood pressure is likely to continue to rise, and this may cause symptoms and health problems that affect your life, your ability to work, and the decisions you can make.

Blood pressure that continues to rise over time raises the risk of health problems such as a stroke or a heart attack. Having a heart attack or a stroke can affect your quality of life.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT LIFESTYLE CHANGES

What are lifestyle changes for high blood pressure?

Lifestyle changes to reduce blood pressure are a range of things that you can do to help bring your blood pressure down.

Lifestyle changes can include things like taking regular exercise, stopping smoking if you are a smoker, and complementary therapies to help you relax, like meditation and yoga. Your nurse or GP can help you find appropriate lifestyle changes that can help reduce your blood pressure.

This Patient Decision Aid will focus on the effects of lifestyle changes that we’re fairly sure can help bring down blood pressure:

  • Changing your diet
  • Getting regular physical exercise
  • Cutting down the amount of salt in your diet
  • Losing weight if you are overweight
  • Cutting down the amount of alcohol you drink.

Changing your diet

The DASH (which stands for Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension) diet has high amounts of fruits and vegetables and low amounts of fatty foods. On this diet you eat:

  • Less saturated fat (found in butter, meat, cheese, and cream)
  • Less cholesterol (found in saturated fats, eggs, and dairy products)
  • Lots of fruit (five servings a day)
  • Lots of vegetables (five servings a day)
  • Small amounts of dairy products
  • Wholegrain products (such as brown rice and wholemeal bread)
  • Fish, poultry, and nuts.

On the DASH diet you avoid red meat, sweets, and sugary drinks. There are no special foods and no hard-to-follow recipes.

Exercise

Being physically active means getting a moderate amount of activity regularly. Types of exercise that could help include walking briskly, swimming with moderate effort, or cycling at a moderate speed. Doctors recommend that you exercise at a moderate intensity (enough to feel your heart beating faster and be out of breath) for 30 minutes, five times a week.

Salt reduction

Reducing the amount of salt in your diet means changing the types of food you eat, and perhaps using 'low salt' instead of table salt. A low-salt diet means eating less than 6 grams (or one teaspoon) of table salt a day.[7]Williams B, Poulter NR, Brown MJ, et al. The BHS Guidelines Working Party guidelines for management of hypertension: report of the fourth working party of the British Hypertension Society, 2004-BHS IV. Journal of Human Hypertension. 2004; 18: 139-185. This is the same as 2.4 grams of sodium. On average, adults in the UK eat 7 to 10 grams of salt a day.

Losing weight

This means, if you are overweight, losing enough weight so that you are at the recommended weight for a person the same height as you. To lose weight, you have to eat fewer calories than you burn.

You can do this by following a weight loss programme that includes a diet and exercise. You may also have behaviour therapy to help you change the way you eat and exercise. Your weight loss programme may also include drugs. You should aim to lose 0.5 to 1 kilogram (1 to 2 pounds) each week.

Cutting down on alcohol

Drinking less alcohol means cutting back to the recommended limit of no more than three units of alcohol a day for men, and no more than two units a day for women.

Different beers and wines contain different amounts of alcohol. As a general guide: a small glass (125 millilitres) of wine, half a pint of ordinary strength lager, or a single shot (25 millilitres) of a spirit (such as whisky or gin) all contain around one unit of alcohol.

﻿Who can choose to make lifestyle changes for high blood pressure?

Doctors recommend that everyone who has high blood pressure should consider if there are lifestyle changes that they could make to help lower their blood pressure.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Hypertension: clinical management of primary hypertension in adults. August 2011. Available at: http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG127 (accessed on 18 December 2012).

What are the main effects of lifestyle changes on high blood pressure?

Making the right and appropriate lifestyle changes for you can reduce your blood pressure, and can delay, reduce, or remove the need for drug treatments.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Hypertension: clinical management of primary hypertension in adults. August 2011. Available at: http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG127 (accessed on 18 December 2012).

Here’s what we know about the different ways making lifestyle changes can help if you have high blood pressure.

Physical exercise

  • In one group of people, regular physical exercise lowered their systolic blood pressure by an average of 3.1 mmHg and their diastolic blood pressure by 1.8 mmHg.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Hypertension: clinical management of primary hypertension in adults. August 2011. Available at: http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG127 (accessed on 18 December 2012). Around 31 people in 100 with high blood pressure who got regular exercise were likely to reduce their systolic blood pressure by 10 mmHg.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Hypertension: clinical management of primary hypertension in adults. August 2011. Available at: http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG127 (accessed on 18 December 2012).
  • People with high blood pressure who get any amount of physical activity are between 16% and 67% less likely to die of heart problems, and between 15% and 57% less likely to die early from any cause, than people with high blood pressure who don’t exercise at all.[42]Rossi A, Dikareva A, Bacon SL. The impact of physical activity on mortality in patients with high blood pressure: a review. Journal of Hypertension. 2012; 30: 1277-1288.

Reducing your salt intake

  • In one group of people who reduced the amount of salt in their diet to less than 6 grams (around one teaspoon) a day, their systolic blood pressure fell by 3.4 mmHg and their diastolic blood pressure by 2.2 mmHg. Around 23 people in 100 with high blood pressure who reduced the amount of salt in their diet were likely to reduce their systolic blood pressure by 10 mmHg.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Hypertension: clinical management of primary hypertension in adults. August 2011. Available at: http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG127 (accessed on 18 December 2012).
  • In one group of people, reducing the amount of salt in their diet didn’t make a difference to how likely people were to die early of heart problems related to high blood pressure. This group included a mix of people with normal and high blood pressure, so we can’t be sure what the effects of reducing salt intake are on how likely people with high blood pressure are to die of health problems.[44]Taylor RS, Ashton KE, Moxham T, et al. Reduced dietary salt for the prevention of cardiovascular disease: A meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials (Cochrane Review). American Journal of Hypertension. 2011; 24: 843-853.

Losing weight

  • In one group of people who were overweight, following low-calorie diets reduced their systolic blood pressure by 6 mmHg and their diastolic blood pressure by 4.8 mmHg. Around 40 in 100 people who followed these kinds of diets were likely to reduce their systolic blood pressure by 10 mmHg.
  • In one group of people who followed a diet designed to help them lose weight, losing an average of 4 kg (around 9 lbs) helped them reduce their systolic and diastolic blood pressure.[45]Siebenhofer A, Jeitler K, Berghold A, et al. Long-term effects of weight-reducing diets in hypertensive patients (Cochrane review). Wiley, Chichester, UK.

Following a healthy diet

  • Combining a healthy diet with an exercise programme can lower blood pressure more than following either a healthy diet or an exercise programme alone. In one group of people, exercising for between 30 and 60 minutes five times a week reduced their blood pressure by around 6.7 mmHg. People who exercised and followed the DASH diet reduced their blood pressure by an average of 12.1 mmHg.[46]Edwards KM, Wilson KL, Sadja J, et al. Effects on blood pressure and autonomic nervous system function of a 12-week exercise or exercise plus DASH-diet intervention in individuals with elevated blood pressure. Acta Physiologica. 2011; 203: 343-350.

Cutting down on alcohol

  • Drinking more than 21 units a week for men and more than 14 units a week for women increases blood pressure. People who drink more than this amount get more health problems than people who drink less.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Hypertension: clinical management of primary hypertension in adults. August 2011. Available at: http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG127 (accessed on 18 December 2012). Around 30 people in 100 who received help from a healthcare professional on how to reduce their alcohol consumption were likely to reduce their systolic blood pressure by 10 mmHg.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Hypertension: clinical management of primary hypertension in adults. August 2011. Available at: http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG127 (accessed on 18 December 2012).


What are the main effects of lifestyle changes on what you can do and how you feel?

We don’t know if making lifestyle changes to lower your blood pressure has an effect on what you can do in your normal everyday life. There aren’t many studies that have looked into this.

Making lifestyle changes can make you feel better, and some people may find this helps them to do more.

Making some kinds of lifestyle changes to lower your blood pressure can improve your quality of life and how you feel. In one group of people with high blood pressure between 120 and 159 mmHg systolic and 80 and 95 mmHg diastolic, those who exercised for at least three hours a week, reduced their sodium intake to 2.3 grams a day or less, and followed the DASH diet, had a better quality of life and felt better than people who didn't make these changes.[49]Young DR, Coughlin J, Jerome GJ, et al. Effects of the PREMIER interventions on health-related quality of life. Annals of Behavioral Medicine. 2010; 40: 302-312.

What are the main side effects of lifestyle changes?

Making lifestyle changes to lower your blood pressure may cause some unwanted effects, but these are unlikely to last long and probably won’t need any treatment.

Physical activity can cause injuries like joint sprains and muscle strains. Most people don't need to see their GP before they start to exercise at a moderate level. But you should check with your GP first if you've had a heart attack or other serious health problems in the past.

There is no evidence that eating a low-fat diet that includes lots of fruit and vegetables (the DASH diet) is harmful. Some people may get diarrhoea or feel bloated.

Most people won't get any problems from reducing their alcohol intake. If you're a very heavy drinker and you stop drinking suddenly, you can get withdrawal symptoms. If you have these symptoms you may need help from a doctor or nurse to deal with them.

Reducing the amount of salt you eat probably won't hurt you, as most people eat too much. If you reduce the amount of salt in your diet to a very low amount (less than 1 gram a day), you may get muscle cramps, diarrhoea, and vomiting.[50]Matyas E, Jeitler K, Horvath K, et al. Benefit assessment of salt reduction in patients with hypertension: systematic overview. Journal of Hypertension. 2011; 29: 821-828. Restricting the amount of salt in your diet can be dangerous if you have heart failure.[44]Taylor RS, Ashton KE, Moxham T, et al. Reduced dietary salt for the prevention of cardiovascular disease: A meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials (Cochrane Review). American Journal of Hypertension. 2011; 24: 843-853. Switching to a salt substitute that is high in potassium can cause problems if you are also taking certain drug treatments for high blood pressure.

There is no evidence that losing weight will hurt you.

How might lifestyle changes affect my life?

Making lifestyle changes to lower your blood pressure may take time and commitment. It’s likely you will have to make some changes to your daily life. This may include things like making time for exercise, buying and preparing different types of food, or switching to low salt alternatives, and possibly encouraging your family or partner to do the same. This may be difficult at first. It should become easier in time.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT DRUG TREATMENTS

What are drug treatments for high blood pressure?

Drug treatments for high blood pressure are medicines that you take to help lower your blood pressure. There are lots of different blood pressure medicines available, but this Patient Decision Aid will look at six types of drugs:

  • ACE inhibitors. ACE stands for angiotensin converting enzyme. It's a chemical that's produced naturally by the body. ACE inhibitors can stop your blood vessels narrowing. This allows blood to pass more easily along your blood vessels and lowers your blood pressure. Some common ACE inhibitors are enalapril, ramipril, perindopril and lisinopril.
  • Angiotensin receptor blockers (ARBs). Angiotensin II receptor blockers can stop your blood vessels narrowing. This allows blood to pass more easily along your blood vessels and lowers your blood pressure. Some common angiotensin II receptor blockers are candesartan, losartan, valsartan and irbesartan.
  • Calcium channel blockers (CCBs). Calcium channel blockers can help keep your blood vessels relaxed and open. This makes it easier for your blood to flow, which lowers your blood pressure. Some common calcium channel blockers are amlodipine, diltiazem, felodipine and nifedipine.
  • Diuretics. These drugs help your body get rid of extra water. They are sometimes called water tablets because they work by flushing excess salt and water from your body in your urine. This lowers your blood pressure. A group of diuretics called thiazides are usually used to treat high blood pressure. Some of the most common ones are bendroflumethiazide, chlortalidone, and indapamide. Another type of diuretic is called spironolactone. This is usually used if you have tried other drug treatments and still have high blood pressure.
  • Beta-blockers. Beta-blockers work by slowing down your heartbeat. They also reduce the force that blood is pumped at each heartbeat. Some common beta-blockers are bisoprolol, atenolol, and metoprolol.
  • Alpha-blockers. These drugs can help keep your blood vessels relaxed and open, making it easier for blood to flow through them. This lowers your blood pressure. Some alpha-blockers are doxazosin and prazosin.

You may need to take more than one medicine to bring your blood pressure down. Most people need at least two drugs. Which kinds of drug work best depends on your age and your ethnic background. This is because some drugs work better in younger people (aged under 55) and others work better in people older than this. Some drugs work better in black people (of African or Caribbean origin) and others work better in people from other ethnic backgrounds.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Hypertension: clinical management of primary hypertension in adults. August 2011. Available at: http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG127 (accessed on 18 December 2012).

  • If you are aged 55 or older, or of African or Caribbean origin (regardless of your age), you usually try a calcium channel blocker first.

What are the main side effects of drug treatments?

Taking drug treatments for high blood pressure can cause some unwanted effects. The treatments are less likely to have serious side effects if you start taking the right drug for you at the most appropriate strength, and you have regular checks with your GP or nurse to discuss any side effects you are having. Higher doses of some medicines can make it more likely you will have side effects, so your GP will start you on a low dose and see how your blood pressure responds.

Between 72 and 144 people in 1,000 who take drug treatments for high blood pressure get side effects that stop them from carrying on taking their treatments.[72]Diao D, Wright JM, Cundiff DK, et al. Pharmacotherapy for mild hypertension (Cochrane review). Wiley, Chichester, UK.
