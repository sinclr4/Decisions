MORE INFORMATION ABOUT HIGH CHOLESTEROL

What is cholesterol?

Cholesterol is a type of fat. The body needs cholesterol to help build cells, and to make some hormones, vitamin D, and a substance called bile that helps digestion. There is cholesterol in every cell in the body.[1]Heart UK. Cholesterol. Available at http://heartuk.org.uk/cholesterol-health/cholesterol (accessed on 20 February 2013).

Most cholesterol is made by the liver from the fats you eat. You can also get some cholesterol directly from foods, such as egg yolks, dairy products, liver, kidney, and shellfish. To move around the body, cholesterol is carried in the blood attached to proteins. This combination is called a lipoprotein because it's made up of a lipid (the name for fat when it's in the blood) and a protein. There are two main types of lipoprotein, and they have different roles.

  • Low density lipoprotein (LDL): This type carries most of the cholesterol around the body to where it is needed. ‘Low density’ means that it is lower in protein and higher in fat. Although LDL cholesterol performs an important role, it is often called ‘bad cholesterol’. This is because, if someone has too much LDL cholesterol, they can get a build up of cholesterol in blood vessels called arteries. This can make it difficult for blood to flow through them.
  • High density lipoprotein (HDL): This type of lipoprotein is higher in protein and lower in fat than LDL. Instead of transporting cholesterol around the body, it sucks up extra cholesterol and returns it to the liver. HDL cholesterol is often called ‘good cholesterol’ because it helps prevent cholesterol from building up in the arteries.

When doctors talk about cholesterol, they may also talk about the following:

  • Total cholesterol. This is the total amount of cholesterol in the blood, including both good (HDL) and bad (LDL) cholesterol.
  • Total cholesterol to HDL ratio. This is the balance between your overall cholesterol and your good (HDL) cholesterol. The lower the result is, the better.
  • Non-HDL cholesterol. This is your total cholesterol minus your good (HDL) cholesterol.
  • Triglycerides. These are another type of fat in the blood. High levels of triglycerides can be harmful. Many people who have too much bad (LDL) cholesterol in their blood also have high levels of triglycerides.
What is high cholesterol?

The name 'high cholesterol' is a bit misleading. Your cholesterol levels can actually be relatively low but still be higher than is healthy for you, based on your overall risk of heart and circulation problems. You may also hear doctors say you have an 'abnormal cholesterol profile'.

If you’ve been diagnosed with high cholesterol, it can mean one, or a combination, of the following:

  • The overall level of cholesterol in your blood is too high 
  • You have too much bad (LDL) cholesterol in your blood
  • You have too little good (HDL) cholesterol in your blood.

Your doctor will look at other cholesterol measurements as well, including the overall level of cholesterol in relation to your good (HDL) cholesterol (called the total cholesterol to HDL ratio).

Having high cholesterol levels can lead to heart and circulation problems, as fatty deposits called plaques can build up along the inside of the arteries. These plaques can clog up the arteries, making them narrower. This condition is called atherosclerosis, and it can lead to heart attacks and strokes.

There are no clearly defined levels of cholesterol that mean you will definitely have heart and circulation problems, or will definitely avoid them. This is because your chance of heart and circulation problems depends on other things as well, including whether you smoke and have other health problems (for example, high blood pressure). Your doctor will use a calculator – such as the Qrisk calculator (http://www.qrisk.org) – to work out your risk. This will show whether you might benefit from treatment to lower your cholesterol.

High cholesterol is a long-term (chronic) condition. Treatments can help bring your cholesterol to a healthy level. You will probably continue with these treatments for the rest of your life.

What are the symptoms of high cholesterol?

Most people with high cholesterol don’t get any symptoms, as it is not something you can usually see or feel. Sometimes people get yellow bulges or lumps under their skin where cholesterol has collected. The bulges are called xanthomas.[2]Bhatnagar D, Soran H, Durrington PN. Hypercholesterolaemia and its management. BMJ. 2008; 337: 993. They may appear anywhere on the body, but often form around the eyelids, knuckles, ankles, elbows, and knees.[3]Ginsberg HN, Goldberg IJ. Disorders of lipoprotein metabolism. In: Braunwald E, Fauci AS, Kasper DL, et al (editors). Harrison's principles of internal medicine. 15th edition. volume 2. McGraw-Hill, New York, NY; 2001. Xanthomas on or around the eyelids are called xanthelasmas.

Why has this happened to me?

There are several things that increase your chances of having high cholesterol. It may be caused by a combination of these things.

  • What you eat: Eating a lot of saturated fats and trans fats is likely to increase the amount of bad (LDL) cholesterol in your blood.[2]Bhatnagar D, Soran H, Durrington PN. Hypercholesterolaemia and its management. BMJ. 2008; 337: 993. [5]Cliff WJ, Schoeff GI (editors) .Coronaries and cholesterol. Chapman and Hall, London, UK; 1989. [6]Ball M, Mann J. Lipids and heart disease: a guide for the primary care team. 2nd edition. Oxford University Press, Oxford, UK; 1994. Saturated fat is found mostly in foods from animals, such as beef, lamb, pork, butter, cream, and cheese. Trans fats are found in food such as hard margarine, full-fat margarine, fast food, and baked goods (doughnuts, pastries, biscuits). 
  • Your genes: Some people seem to be able to eat lots of fatty food without getting high levels of cholesterol in their blood. But other people have to be very careful about what they eat. This difference may be caused by differences in their genes (the basic material in all cells that controls how cells grow and behave). Differences in genes may affect how much cholesterol your body makes and how it handles the fat in the food you eat.
  • Lack of exercise: People who don't take regular exercise tend to have lower levels of good (HDL) cholesterol. Exercising more can raise HDL cholesterol.
  • Being overweight: Gaining weight can increase your cholesterol, particularly if you gain much of your weight around your middle.[2]Bhatnagar D, Soran H, Durrington PN. Hypercholesterolaemia and its management. BMJ. 2008; 337: 993. [8]Hubert HB, Feinleb M, McNamara PM, et al. Obesity as an independent risk factor for cardiovascular disease: A 26-year follow-up of participants in the Framingham Heart Study. Circulation. 1983; 67: 968-977. [9]Alberti KG, Eckel RH, Grundy SM, et al. Harmonizing the metabolic syndrome: a joint interim statement of the International Diabetes Federation Task Force on Epidemiology and Prevention; National Heart, Lung, and Blood Institute; American Heart Association; World Heart Federation; International Atherosclerosis Society; and International Association for the Study of Obesity. Circulation. 2009; 120: 1640-1645.
  • Getting older: Cholesterol tends to rise as we get older. For women, cholesterol is particularly likely to rise around the menopause.
  • Drinking alcohol to excess: Drinking a lot of alcohol on a regular basis can increase your cholesterol.[10]British Cardiac Society; British Hypertension Society; Diabetes UK; HEART UK; Primary Care Cardiovascular Society; Stroke Association. JBS 2: Joint British Societies' guidelines on prevention of cardiovascular disease in clinical practice. Heart. 2005; 91 (supplement 5): S1-S52. A typical glass of wine is two units of alcohol.[11]Heart UK. For good cholesterol levels. March 2012. Available at http://heartuk.org.uk/files/uploads/huk_fs_d02_forgoodcholesterol.pdf (accessed on 2 January 2013).
  • Smoking: People who smoke have lower levels of good (HDL) cholesterol. Stopping smoking can improve these levels.[Gepner AD, Piper ME, Johnson HM, et al.   • Having an inherited condition: Familial hypercholesterolaemia (FH for short) is an inherited condition that leads to very high cholesterol levels. If one of your parents has familial hypercholesterolaemia, you have a 50 in 100 chance of also having the condition.[12]British Heart Foundation. You are more likely to have high cholesterol if you have certain illnesses. These include:[10]British Cardiac Society; British Hypertension Society; Diabetes UK; HEART UK; Primary Care Cardiovascular Society; Stroke Association.   • Diabetes
  • Kidney disease
  • Liver disease
  • An underactive thyroid gland (hypothyroidism).

Treating these conditions can help lower your cholesterol.

How do I find out I have high cholesterol?

The only way to find out whether you have high cholesterol is to have a blood test.

When doctors take a blood sample to check cholesterol, they don't just measure the total amount of cholesterol in your blood. They usually also measure the different types of cholesterol in your blood. This is called a lipid profile. 

The lipid profile gives you five readings, or numbers. The levels of lipids in your blood are usually measured in millimoles per litre of blood, or mmol/L for short. You might also see lipid levels described in milligrams per decilitre of blood, or mg/dL for short. (This may sound complicated, but the final numbers are much simpler.)

These numbers show your levels of:

  • Total cholesterol. This is the total amount of cholesterol in your blood, including both good and bad cholesterol.
  • LDL cholesterol. This is the 'bad' cholesterol that can clog up your arteries.
  • HDL cholesterol. This is the 'good' cholesterol that picks up extra cholesterol, preventing it from building up in your arteries.
  • Total cholesterol to HDL ratio. This is the balance between your overall cholesterol and your good (HDL) cholesterol.
  • Triglycerides. These are another type of fat in the blood. High levels of triglycerides can be harmful. Many people who have too much 'bad' cholesterol in their blood also have high levels of triglycerides.

Blood lipids can vary from day to day, and from test to test, so your doctor will do at least two blood tests before recommending treatment for high cholesterol. As a rough guide, doctors often say a person’s cholesterol is high if:[10]British Cardiac Society; British Hypertension Society; Diabetes UK; HEART UK; Primary Care Cardiovascular Society; Stroke Association. JBS 2: Joint British Societies' guidelines on prevention of cardiovascular disease in clinical practice. Heart. 2005; 91 (supplement 5): S1-S52.

  • Their total cholesterol is 5.0 mmol/L or above
  • Their LDL cholesterol is 3.0 mmol/L or above
  • Their total cholesterol to HDL ratio is 6 or above.

However, these numbers alone can't say whether your cholesterol is likely to cause you health problems. People with the same cholesterol levels can have very different chances of having heart and circulation problems.

For example, someone with a LDL cholesterol level of 3.0 mmol/L who smokes and has high blood pressure is likely to be offered treatment for high cholesterol. On the other hand, someone with the same cholesterol level might not need treatment if they don’t smoke and their blood pressure is normal.

Your doctor will look at several factors to work out your overall chance of heart and circulation problems. These may include:

  • Your age
  • Your ethnicity - for example, south Asian people may have a greater chance of heart and circulation problems[15]Chaturvedi N. Ethnic Differences in Cardiovascular Disease. Heart. 2003; 89: 681-686.
  • Whether you smoke
  • Whether your mother, father, or a sibling had a heart attack or angina (a type of chest pain) before the age of 60
  • Whether you have certain conditions, such as long-term (chronic) kidney disease,  rheumatoid arthritis, or lupus
  • Whether you are being treated for high blood pressure
  • Whether you are overweight.

Your doctor will enter this information into a calculator to estimate your chance of having a heart attack or stroke over the next 10 years. One example is the Qrisk calculator, which was created specifically for people in the UK. You can work out your own risk on the Qrisk site (http://www.qrisk.org).

If you have a 20 in 100 risk of having a heart attack or stroke over the next 10 years, you are considered at high risk. Your doctor will recommend you have treatment to bring down your cholesterol.[16]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Lipid modification. May 2008. Clinical guideline 67. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/cg67 (accessed on 7 January 2013).

How many people have high cholesterol?

High cholesterol affects a similar number of men and women. In England in 2008, 58 in every 100 men and 61 in every 100 women had high cholesterol.[17]British Heart Foundation. Coronary heart disease statistic 2012. Available at http://www.bhf.org.uk/research/statistics/heart-statistics-publications.aspx (accessed on 3 January 2013).

This gives only part of the picture, though, as the chance of having high cholesterol varies with age.[17]British Heart Foundation. Coronary heart disease statistic 2012. Available at http://www.bhf.org.uk/research/statistics/heart-statistics-publications.aspx (accessed on 3 January 2013).

  • For both men and women, those aged 16 to 24 have the lowest risk, with 25 in every 100 men and 36 in every 100 women having high cholesterol in 2008.
  • For men, those aged 45 to 54 have the highest risk, with 76 in every 100 having high cholesterol in 2008.
  • For women, those aged 55 to 64 have the highest risk, with 83 in every 100 having high cholesterol in 2008. 

 

What will happen?

We can’t say for certain what will happen if you have high cholesterol. This depends on several things, including:

  • How high your cholesterol is
  • How long it has been high, and
  • Whether you have other health problems that increase your chance of heart and circulation problems.

We do know that you have a better chance of staying healthy if your cholesterol is kept low.[10]British Cardiac Society; British Hypertension Society; Diabetes UK; HEART UK; Primary Care Cardiovascular Society; Stroke Association. JBS 2: Joint British Societies' guidelines on prevention of cardiovascular disease in clinical practice. Heart. 2005; 91 (supplement 5): S1-S52. This is because people who have high cholesterol are more likely to get atherosclerosis, which is also known as hardening of the arteries.

If you have atherosclerosis, fatty deposits called plaques build up inside your arteries. This can make them narrower and restrict blood flow, which can cause problems throughout the body. The most serious of these problems are heart attacks and strokes.

  • If you have narrowed arteries in your heart, this can cause chest pain and pressure (angina). If a blockage is severe, it can cause a heart attack.
  • If arteries leading to your brain are narrowed, you may get temporary numbness or weakness in your arms or legs, difficulty speaking, and other signs of a transient ischaemic attack (TIA), which is also called a mini-stroke. If a TIA is left untreated or a blockage is more severe, this can cause a stroke. 
  • If you have narrowed arteries in your arms and legs, you can get aching pain, tingling, or numbness in your limbs. This is called peripheral arterial disease.
  • If you have narrowed arteries leading to your kidneys, this can cause high blood pressure and kidney failure.
  • If you have narrowed arteries in your genitals, this can lead to difficulties with sex. Less blood flow to a woman’s vagina can make sex less enjoyable. Less blood flow to a man’s penis can cause erection problems.

Plaques can also suddenly break open (rupture). This forms a blood clot which can rapidly block an artery, causing sudden symptoms and potentially resulting in a heart attack or stroke. This can happen even if someone has no symptoms of narrowed arteries and otherwise seems healthy. The longer someone has cholesterol that is too high, the more plaques they are likely to develop. And the more plaques someone has, the higher the chance that one will rupture.

Having treatment to lower your cholesterol can reduce your chance of these problems. For example:

  • For every 10 percent (one tenth) drop in total cholesterol, your chance of heart problems drops by 25 percent (one quarter) after five years.[10]British Cardiac Society; British Hypertension Society; Diabetes UK; HEART UK; Primary Care Cardiovascular Society; Stroke Association. JBS 2: Joint British Societies' guidelines on prevention of cardiovascular disease in clinical practice. Heart. 2005; 91 (supplement 5): S1-S52. [21]Law MR, Wald NJ, Thompson SG. By how much and how quickly does reduction in serum cholesterol concentration lower risk of ischaemic heart disease? BMJ. 1994; 308: 367-372.
  • For every 1.0 mmol/L decrease in total cholesterol, your chance of these problems drops by 30 percent (about one third).[22]Gould AL, Davies GM, Alemao E, et al. Cholesterol reduction yields clinical benefits: meta-analysis including recent trials. Clinical Therapeutics. 2007; 29: 778-794.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT NO TREATMENT

What is no treatment for high cholesterol?

Having no treatment for high cholesterol means not doing anything at this stage to lower your cholesterol. You may decide to have treatment later on, as your health or your life changes.

It could also mean making sure you're getting the best treatment for other conditions that can affect your heart and circulation, such as high blood pressure or diabetes.

Who can have no treatment for high cholesterol?

Anyone can choose not to have treatment for high cholesterol. Because of the risk that your cholesterol will remain high and lead to heart and circulation problems, your GP will probably advise that you have some form of treatment. The choice is yours. You can change your mind and decide to have treatment later on.

Some people choose not to have to treatment because:

  • They're not worried about their cholesterol
  • They're not willing to take medicines or make lifestyle changes to lower their cholesterol
  • They are frail or in poor health from another condition which is likely to end their life before their cholesterol level starts to cause problems.

Sometimes people have another condition – such as high blood pressure or diabetes – for which they're already having treatment. They may decide with their doctor that the most important thing is to treat that problem as well as possible, before treating their cholesterol. Most people with diabetes or high blood pressure who also have high cholesterol will be advised to have treatment for their cholesterol as well. 

What are the main effects of no treatment on high cholesterol?

We can’t say for certain what will happen to your cholesterol if you decide not to have treatment, but it will probably remain high. The longer it is high, the more likely you are to develop atherosclerosis. This is when fatty deposits called plaques build up along the inside of your arteries. These plaques can clog your arteries or rupture, which can lead to heart attacks, strokes, and other heart and circulation problems. The chance of these problems increases with age.

If you're already having treatment for another condition that can cause heart and circulation problems – such as high blood pressure or diabetes – you and your doctor may decide that you don't need extra treatment to lower your cholesterol at this point. You may want to talk to your doctor about how to get the best treatment possible for any other conditions you may have.

What are the main effects of no treatment for high cholesterol on what you can do and how you feel?

High cholesterol doesn’t usually cause symptoms. Whether you lower cholesterol through treatment or not, you probably won’t notice any difference in how you feel or what you can do day to day.

What are the main side effects of no treatment for high cholesterol?

If you decide not to have treatment for high cholesterol, then you won’t have any side effects from treatment. Your cholesterol will probably remain high, increasing your chance of heart and circulation problems.

How might no treatment for high cholesterol affect my life?

Deciding not have to treatment for high cholesterol probably won’t affect your day-to-day life. Your cholesterol will probably remain high. This raises your chance of having a heart attack or stroke, or other serious health problems that can affect what you can do and how long you live.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT LIFESTYLE CHANGES

What are lifestyle changes for high cholesterol?

Lifestyle changes to reduce cholesterol are things that people can do to help bring down their cholesterol and lower their chance of heart and circulation problems.

Lifestyle changes can include:

  • Changing your diet
  • Taking regular exercise
  • Stopping smoking (if you smoke).

Changing your diet

The amount of fat you eat can affect your cholesterol levels. This doesn't mean that all fat is bad. You just need to be careful about the types of fat you eat.

The less-healthy kinds of fat are called saturated fats and trans fats. Saturated fats are found in meat and dairy products. Trans fats are found in some fast foods, biscuits, and baked goods. In general, less-healthy types of fat are solid at room temperature. Healthier types tend to be liquids, like olive oil and vegetable oil. These healthier types of fat are called polyunsaturated or monounsaturated fats.

A healthy, low-fat diet involves eating at least five portions of fruit and vegetables a day, lean cuts of meat and poultry, and fat-free or low-fat dairy products. Doctors also recommend eating at least two portions of fish a week, including at least one portion of oily fish (such as salmon, herring, sardines, or mackerel). It's best to avoid sugary soft drinks, sweets, and baked goods, and to drink alcohol only in moderation. Drinking high amounts of alcohol on a regular basis can increase  cholesterol.[10]British Cardiac Society; British Hypertension Society; Diabetes UK; HEART UK; Primary Care Cardiovascular Society; Stroke Association. JBS 2: Joint British Societies' guidelines on prevention of cardiovascular disease in clinical practice. Heart. 2005; 91 (supplement 5): S1-S52.

It's also important to choose healthy ways of cooking food. For example, frying food in butter or ghee increases the amount of less-healthy fat in your diet. Steaming, poaching, baking, or microwaving food lowers these fat levels. Instead of flavouring food with sauces that are buttery, creamy, or cheesy, you might consider using spices, herbs, or lemon juice.[16]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Lipid modification. May 2008. Clinical guideline 67. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/cg67 (accessed on 7 January 2013).

To learn more, visit http://www.nhs.uk/Livewell/healthy-eating/Pages/Healthyeating.aspx

Taking regular exercise

This means getting a moderate amount of exercise on most days. Types of exercise include walking briskly, swimming, walking stairs, or cycling. Some household tasks also count as exercise, including vacuuming, and using a power mower.[16]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Lipid modification. May 2008. Clinical guideline 67. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/cg67 (accessed on 7 January 2013).

Doctors recommend that most people exercise at a moderate intensity for 30 minutes, at least five days a week.[16]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Lipid modification. May 2008. Clinical guideline 67. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/cg67 (accessed on 7 January 2013). 'Moderate intensity' means exercising hard enough to raise your heart rate and breathing, and break a sweat. As a guide, you should be able to talk during moderate-intensity exercise, but not sing. 

It's not necessary to do the 30 minutes all at one time. It can be broken into bouts of physical activity of at least 10 minutes each.

People who have not exercised for some time can get advice about what exercise will suit them best from their GP, a nurse, or an exercise specialist. Some people may be reluctant to exercise due to health problems. Their doctor can provide advice on what types of exercise might be best for them, how much they should do, and for how long. 

You can learn more at http://www.nhs.uk/LiveWell/Fitness/Pages/Fitnesshome.aspx

Stopping smoking

Stopping smoking means giving up cigarettes and other forms of smoking tobacco completely. There are lots of things that can help you stop smoking.

  • Getting professional help from your doctor, a nurse, or a trained counsellor
  • Joining a self-help group with other people trying to stop smoking
  • Using nicotine replacement products like patches, nasal sprays, or gum. These products help you crave cigarettes less by giving your body some nicotine. Nicotine is the chemical in cigarettes that makes them addictive. 
  • Asking your doctor about anti-smoking drugs, such as bupropion (brand name Zyban) and varenicline (Champix).

If you want to stop smoking, your GP can provide support and advice, treatments, and a referral to an intensive support service, such as the NHS Stop Smoking Services. The NHS also offers a free smoking helpline. The number is 0800 022 4 332.

Who can choose to make lifestyle changes for high cholesterol?

Most people can choose to make at least some lifestyle changes to lower their cholesterol. Some people will choose to make several.

Treating high cholesterol doesn't have to mean choosing one treatment or another. You can do things like change your diet and exercise more, at the same time as taking medicines or food supplements to lower cholesterol. Doing more than one of these things together may lower cholesterol more than doing just one of them.

Not everyone can make all the lifestyle changes suggested here. For example, people with rheumatoid arthritis may not be able to do some kinds of exercise because of painful, stiff joints. If you have difficulty exercising, you can talk to your doctor about what you might be able to do.

What are the main effects of lifestyle changes on high cholesterol?

Making lifestyle changes can lower your cholesterol and reduce your risk of heart and circulation problems. If you decide to also take medicines, making lifestyle changes may mean you can take a lower dose.

Here’s what we know about the ways different lifestyle changes can help if you have high cholesterol.

Changing your diet

  • Eating less saturated fat can lower your cholesterol. Your doctor will discuss what cholesterol levels are best for you. What are the main effects of lifestyle changes on what you can do and how you feel?

We don’t know if lowering your cholesterol through lifestyle changes will have an effect on what you can do in your everyday life. High cholesterol doesn’t usually cause symptoms, so lowering it probably won’t change how you feel or what you can do.

Stopping smoking can improve how you feel. For example, you may not get short of breath as easily, and your sense of taste and smell may improve.[35]Dresler C, Leon M. What are the main side effects of lifestyle changes?

Making lifestyle changes to lower cholesterol may cause some unwanted effects. It’s unlikely these will last long, and you will probably not need any treatment.

Physical activity can cause injuries if you don't exercise sensibly. You should check with your GP, a nurse or an exercise specialist before you start exercising if you have any concerns or if you have not  exercised for a long time.

There's no evidence that eating a diet low in saturated and trans fats is harmful. Some people may get an upset stomach or feel bloated when trying new foods.

Stopping smoking may cause side effects, such as headaches, irritability, and insomnia (not being able to sleep at night). These side effects usually go away after a short period of time. Some people gain weight after stopping smoking. The average amount that people put on is 3.6 kilograms to 5.9 kilograms (8.0 pounds to 13.0 pounds).[36]Ockene JK, Emmons KM, Mermelstein RJ, et al. Relapse and maintenance issues for smoking cessation. Health Psychology. 2000; 19 (supplement 1): S17-S31. This is not always permanent. Some people lose the weight they gain over time. Weight gain may be reduced while people take nicotine replacement therapy.

How might lifestyle changes affect my life?

Making lifestyle changes to lower cholesterol can take time and commitment. It’s likely you will have to make some changes to your daily life. These may include:

  • Making time for exercise
  • Buying and preparing different types of food, and possibly encouraging your family or partner to do the same
  • Changing your routine so that you aren’t tempted to smoke.

These changes may be difficult at first.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT MEDICINES

What are drug treatments for high cholesterol?

Drug treatments are medicines people take to help lower their cholesterol. There are several different medicines available. This decision aid looks at three main types:

  • Statins. Statins are the main drug treatment for high cholesterol. They work mainly by lowering levels of 'bad' (LDL) cholesterol. Commonly prescribed statins (and their brand names) are atorvastatin (Lipitor), fluvastatin (Lescol), pravastatin (Lipostat), rosuvastatin (Crestor), and simvastatin (Zocor). 
  • Fibrates. These drugs help mainly by raising the level of 'good' (HDL) cholesterol and lowering the level of triglycerides. Triglycerides are another type of harmful fat in the blood. Some examples of fibrates (with brand names) are fenofibrate (Lipantil, Supralip) and gemfibrozil (Lopid).
  • Absorption inhibitors. These drugs lower 'bad' (LDL) cholesterol levels in the blood by stopping the absorption of cholesterol in the gut. The only absorption inhibitor available at the moment is called ezetimibe (brand name Ezetrol).

Doctors usually prescribe a statin first, as these drugs work well for most people. If your cholesterol is still too high while taking a statin, your doctor may suggest that you increase your dose or recommend taking an absorption inhibitor (ezetimibe) as well.[16]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Lipid modification. May 2008. Clinical guideline 67. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/cg67 (accessed on 7 January 2013). [38]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Ezetimibe for the treatment of primary (heterozygous-familial and non-familial) hypercholesterolaemia. Technology appraisal 123. November 2007. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/ta132 (accessed 8 January 2013).

For a cholesterol medicine to be effective, you need to take it every day. If a medicine works well for you and doesn't cause side effects, your doctor will probably recommend you take it long term, possibly for the rest of your life.

Who can choose drug treatments for high cholesterol?

Doctors offer drug treatments for high cholesterol to people who are over the age of 40 and have a high chance of developing heart or circulation problems over the next 10 years.[16]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Your doctor will estimate your risk by looking at your cholesterol levels and other factors that can affect your chances of having a heart attack, stroke, and related problems. These include whether you smoke, are overweight, or have high blood pressure or other health problems that can raise your risk. People who have at least a 20 in 100 risk of developing heart or circulation problems in the next 10 years are considered at high risk and will be offered drug treatment.[16]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT FOOD SUPPLEMENTS

What are food supplements for high cholesterol?

Food supplements are nutrients people add to their diet. This decision aid looks at two types:

  • Omega 3 fish oil
  • Plant stanols and sterols.

Omega 3 fish oil

This is a type of polyunsaturated fat that may help protect against heart and circulation problems. There are two ways to get more omega 3 fish oil in your diet.

  • You can eat more oily fish, such as herring, mackerel, sardines, salmon, pilchards, and trout. Most people are advised to eat no more than four portions a week. Women who are pregnant, breastfeeding, or may become pregnant are advised to eat no more than two portions.[68]NHS choices. Fish and shellfish. Available at http://www.nhs.uk/livewell/goodfood/pages/fish-shellfish.aspx (accessed on 10 January 2013).
  • You can take fish oils in capsules. You don't need a prescription to get these. Your doctor can advise you on how much to take.

Plant stanols and sterols

These are natural substances found in plants that are similar to human cholesterol. They help lower cholesterol by stopping it from being absorbed in the gut.

Natural sources of sterols and stanols include wheat germ oil, soybean oil, corn oil, sesame seeds, nuts, and some fruits such as oranges and figs. The amount in these foods is too small to have much effect on cholesterol. So treatment with stanols and sterols involves eating foods enriched with higher amounts of these substances. These may include enriched margarines, yoghurts, fruit juices, and cereal bars. If you’re taking stanols and sterols as supplements, you will probably need about 2 to 3 grams of these substances a day.

Most people can choose these treatments, but it’s a good idea to speak with your GP before adding supplements to your diet. Your doctor can confirm how much of the nutrient is best for you and whether there might be any restrictions. For example:

We can't say for sure that any of the treatments for high cholesterol will improve your quality of life. If lowering your cholesterol reduces your chances of serious health problems, you may feel this improves your quality of life. Lifestyle changes can have health benefits beyond lowering cholesterol, which may improve your quality of life.
