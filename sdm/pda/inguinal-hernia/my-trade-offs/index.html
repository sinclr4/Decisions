STEP 5: MY DECISION

Our Decision Support telephone service is here to assist and guide you through the Shared Decision Making process.

The Decision Support team is made of professional Health Coaches who are clinicians, commonly nurses, who have undertaken additional training to become a Health Coach.

The Health Coaches will assist you by:

  • Providing you with information
  • Helping you to understand your condition
  • Recognise what is important to you regarding the outcome of treatment
  • Identifying potential solutions
  • Encourage you in discussing options with your family
  • Transferring skills which will assist you in using the information and resources available to you
  • Support you in building confidence in discussing your choice with your doctor
  • You may find that this can be achieved in one telephone call with a Health Coach however; if further calls are required to support you in reaching your decision the Health Coach will schedule these with you.

Important Things To Note

  • A Health Coach does not know which treatment option is best for you.
  • The Health Coach does not have information about your medical history.
  • You will still need to talk over your decision and reasons for your decision with your doctor or nurse.

MORE INFORMATION ABOUT INGUINAL HERNIA

What is a hernia?

A hernia is a weakness, or hole, in the muscles of the abdomen that allows part of the contents of the abdomen to bulge through, forming a swelling, or lump, under the skin.

Normally these muscles are strong enough to hold the contents of the abdomen – including fat, intestines and organs - in place. But if there is a weak point, a hernia can form.

There are several different types of hernias, of which inguinal hernia is the most common.

What is an inguinal hernia?

An inguinal hernia is a lump, or swelling, that appears under the skin of the groin (inguinal region) where the top of the leg meets the torso.

Inguinal hernias can appear on one or both sides of the groin.

Men sometimes notice an inguinal hernia as a swelling or enlargement of the scrotum – the skin that covers the testicles. In very rare cases there may be no visible lump or swelling.

Women can also get inguinal hernias. Inguinal hernias are less common and often smaller in women.

When there is a lump, it is usually most obvious when people stand up, strain on the toilet, or cough, or lift something heavy. It often disappears when people lie down.

What are the symptoms of inguinal hernia?

Inguinal hernias don’t always cause symptoms. Up to one third of people with inguinal hernias have no symptoms at all. [1]Chung L, Norrie J, O’Dwyer PJ. Long-term follow-up of patients with a painless inguinal hernia from a randomized clinical trial. Br J Surg 2011 Apr;98(4):596-9 

Some people have pain or discomfort at the hernia site that may get worse during or after activity but is rarely severe. [2]Hair A, Paterson C, Wright D, et al. What effect does the duration of an inguinal hernia have on patient symptoms? J Am Coll Surg 2001 Aug;193(2):125-9 [3]Jenkins JT, O’Dwyer PJ. Inguinal hernias. BMJ 2008;336:269-72 [4]Page B, Paterson C, Young D, et al. Pain from primary inguinal hernia and the effect of repair on pain. Br J Surg 2002 Oct;89(10):1315-8
These symptoms can interfere with people’s ability to work and enjoy leisure activities like sport.
The chance of having symptoms increases with the length of time the hernia has been present.

Almost 90 out of 100 people with inguinal hernia have some pain after 10 years. [2]Hair A, Paterson C, Wright D, et al. What effect does the duration of an inguinal hernia have on patient symptoms? J Am Coll Surg 2001 Aug;193(2):125-9

For women the main symptom may be pain in the pelvic area that appears or gets worse in particular situations.

These include: [6]Metzger DA. Hernias in women: uncommon or unrecognized? Laparoscopy Today, January 01, 2004

  • bowel movements
  • full bladder
  • intercourse
  • menstruation.

More extreme symptoms can be a sign of a serious complication called strangulation that needs emergency treatment. [7]National Digestive Diseases Information Clearinghouse (NDDIC), Inguinal Hernia

These include:

  • tenderness or redness around the lump
  • sudden pain that gets worse over a short period of time.
Why has this happened to me?

Some people are born with a weakness in the abdominal wall that eventually leads to an inguinal hernia.

In other people the problem develops later in life when things happen to weaken the abdominal muscles.

The chance of getting an inguinal hernia is increased by: [8]Carbonell JE, Sanchez JL, Peris RT, et al. Risk factors associated with inguinal hernias: a case control study. Eur J Surg 1993 Sep;159(9):481-6 [9]Flich J, Alfonso JL, Delgado E, et al. Inguinal hernia and certain risk factors. Eur J Epidemiol 1992 Mar;8(2):277-82

  • prolonged physical effort, such as lifting heavy objects repeatedly over long periods of time
  • chronic cough
  • smoking [10]Cannon DJ, Read RC. Metastatic emphysema: a mechanism for acquiring inguinal herniation. Ann Surg 1981 September; 194(3):270-278
  • a family history of hernia [11]Simons MP, Aufenaker T, Bay-Nielsen M, et al. European Hernia Society Guidelines on the treatment of inguinal hernia in adult patients. Hernia. 2009 August; 13(4):343-403
  • previous operations. [11]Simons MP, Aufenaker T, Bay-Nielsen M, et al. European Hernia Society Guidelines on the treatment of inguinal hernia in adult patients. Hernia. 2009 August; 13(4):343-403

There is no evidence that occasional heavy lifting increases the chance of developing an inguinal hernia.

Sportsmen may be particularly prone to hernias if their activities involve cutting, pivoting, kicking and sharp turns, as in football or ice hockey. These are known as ‘sports hernias’. [13]Caudill P, Nyland J, Smith C, et al. Sports hernias: a systematic literature review. Br J Sports Med 2008 Dec;42(12):954-64 Sports hernias are different from inguinal hernias and should probably be treated by experts with a special interest in diagnosing and treating them.

How do I find out I have an inguinal hernia?

Most inguinal hernias can be diagnosed by careful physical examination. The person may be asked to stand and cough so the doctor can feel the lump as it moves into the groin or scrotum. The doctor then checks to see whether it can be pushed back into its proper position in the abdomen. [7]National Digestive Diseases Information Clearinghouse (NDDIC), Inguinal Hernia

Sometimes doctors need to rule out other possible causes of groin pain or discomfort, particularly if there is no obvious lump. In these cases the surgeon may ask for medical imaging (ultrasound or MRI) to try to help with the diagnosis and help to exclude other causes of groin pain.

How many people have inguinal hernia?

Inguinal hernias are the commonest type of hernia, accounting for three quarters (75%) of all abdominal wall hernias. [3]Jenkins JT, O’Dwyer PJ. Inguinal hernias. BMJ 2008;336:269-72

They are much more common in men than in women. Twenty seven in every 100 men will get an inguinal hernia at some time in their lives compared with 3 in every 100 women. [16]Kingsnorth A, LeBlanc K. Hernias: inguinal and incisional. Lancet 2003;362:1561-71

About 30 in every 100 people who get a hernia on one side of their groin will go on to get a second hernia on the other side. [17]Understanding NICE guidance. Laparoscopic surgery for inguinal hernia repair, September 2004

Repair of inguinal hernia is one of the commonest operations. About 70,000 repairs are carried out each year in England alone.

What will happen?

Inguinal hernias don’t get better on their own and most get bigger and more uncomfortable with time. This can affect people’s ability to work and take part in leisure activities, including sport. [2]Hair A, Paterson C, Wright D, et al. What effect does the duration of an inguinal hernia have on patient symptoms? J Am Coll Surg 2001 Aug;193(2):125-9

Having an inguinal hernia is not usually a serious condition but a small minority of people go on to develop potentially dangerous complications that may need emergency surgery. [7]National Digestive Diseases Information Clearinghouse (NDDIC), Inguinal Hernia [20]Patient.co.uk. Inguinal hernia

  • The hernia can become irreducible, which means the lump gets stuck in the groin or scrotum and can’t be pushed back into the abdomen. This is sometimes called an incarcerated hernia.
  • An irreducible hernia can become strangulated, when the blood supply to the part of the intestine in the hernia is cut off.
  • An irreducible hernia can lead to bowel obstruction if the contents of the bowel can no longer flow freely.

The chance of an inguinal hernia becoming irreducible rises from 6.5 per cent after one year to 30 per cent after 10 years. [2]Hair A, Paterson C, Wright D, et al. What effect does the duration of an inguinal hernia have on patient symptoms? J Am Coll Surg 2001 Aug;193(2):125-9

Strangulation and bowel obstruction are rare. In people with few or no symptoms of inguinal hernia, they affect between two and eight people per thousand each year. [22]Fitzgibbons RJ, Giobbie-Hurder A, Gibbs JO, et al. Watchful waiting vs repair of inguinal hernia in minimally syjmptomatic men: a randomized clinical trial. JAMA 2006;295:285-292 [23]O’Dwyer PJ, Norrie J, Alani A, et al. Observation or operation for patients with an asymptomatic inguinal hernia: a randomised clinical trial. Ann Surg 2006;244:167-173

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT CONSERVATIVE TREATMENT

What is it?

Conservative treatment means living with the hernia, making lifestyle changes and watching for changes to the hernia.

People who choose conservative treatment can decide to have surgery at a later stage if the hernia gets bigger or the symptoms get worse.

People having conservative treatment for their inguinal hernias usually have regular medical appointments to check on their hernias.

They may be advised to make lifestyle changes to reduce the chance that the hernia will get worse. These can include:

  • giving up smoking
  • avoiding heavy lifting.

Special belts called trusses that are designed to apply pressure on the hernia lump and stop it popping out are sometimes recommended for people who don’t want surgical repair or are not fit enough to have it. They may also be useful for younger men who need to carry on with heavy work while waiting for surgery. [24]Kingsnorth A, LeBlanc K. Hernias: inguinal and incisional. Lancet 2003 Nov 8;362(9395):1561-71 But they are effective in only 31 in 100 people who use them, and 64 in 100 find them uncomfortable to wear. [25]Law NW, Trapnell JE. Does a truss benefit a patient with inguinal hernia? BMJ 1992;304:1092

Who can have it?

Anyone with an uncomplicated inguinal hernia can choose to have conservative treatment. And everyone who chooses surgical repair will have conservative treatment while waiting for the operation. 

Doctors often recommend surgery, even for uncomplicated hernias, because they think it is the best way to avoid complications in future. [23]O’Dwyer PJ, Norrie J, Alani A, et al. Observation or operation for patients with an asymptomatic inguinal hernia: a randomised clinical trial. Ann Surg 2006;244:167-173 But this is still a matter of choice.

The people most suited to conservative treatment are [23]O’Dwyer PJ, Norrie J, Alani A, et al. Observation or operation for patients with an asymptomatic inguinal hernia: a randomised clinical trial. Ann Surg 2006;244:167-173:

  • those with few or no symptoms from their hernias
  • those who are not fit for surgery because of advanced age or other health problems.

People with severe pain from their hernias or signs of incarceration need emergency surgery because they could be at risk of bowel obstruction or strangulation.

 

How effective is it?

Conservative treatment is not a cure for inguinal hernia. The only way to cure an inguinal hernia is by surgical repair. What we don’t know is whether some people with inguinal hernia can manage indefinitely with conservative care without needing surgery. There hasn’t been enough research to prove this either way. [3]Jenkins JT, O’Dwyer PJ. Inguinal hernias. BMJ 2008;336:269-72

We do know that a lot of people who have conservative treatment at first decide to have surgery later. As many as 72 people in 100 on conservative treatment eventually switch to surgery. [29]Mizrahi H, Parker MC. Management of asymptomatic inguinal hernia: a systematic review of the evidence. Arch Surg 2012 Mar;147(3):277-81

The people most likely to switch to surgery are [30]Sarosi GA, Wei Y, Gibbs JO, et al. A clinician’s guide to patient selection for watchful waiting management of inguinal hernia. Ann Surg 2011 Mar;253(3):605-10:

  • people who have pain with strenuous activity
  • people with chronic constipation
  • married people
  • people in good health.
What are the effects on symptoms?

About a third of all people with inguinal hernia have few or no symptoms when they are first diagnosed. Some people may never get any worse without surgery but most people do over time. [1]Chung L, Norrie J, O’Dwyer PJ. Long-term follow-up of patients with a painless inguinal hernia from a randomized clinical trial. Br J Surg 2011 Apr;98(4):596-9 [30]Sarosi GA, Wei Y, Gibbs JO, et al. A clinician’s guide to patient selection for watchful waiting management of inguinal hernia. Ann Surg 2011 Mar;253(3):605-10

After 10 years as many as 90 people in 100 have pain from their hernias. [2]Hair A, Paterson C, Wright D, et al. What effect does the duration of an inguinal hernia have on patient symptoms? J Am Coll Surg 2001 Aug;193(2):125-9

What are the potential health problems?

The main possible complication of choosing conservative management instead of surgical repair is that the hernia will get worse and lead to health problems. It could just get bigger and more painful or uncomfortable; or it could become stuck or incarcerated in the groin or scrotum.

Incarcerated hernias need emergency surgery because they can lead to strangulation or bowel obstruction, which can be life-threatening. [7]National Digestive Diseases Information Clearinghouse (NDDIC), Inguinal Hernia [20]Patient.co.uk. Inguinal hernia

The chance of incarceration increases with the length of time people have had an inguinal hernia. Just over 6 people in a 100 get incarcerated hernias after one year compared with 30 in 100 after 10 years. [2]Hair A, Paterson C, Wright D, et al. What effect does the duration of an inguinal hernia have on patient symptoms? J Am Coll Surg 2001 Aug;193(2):125-9

Strangulation and bowel obstruction are much less common. In people with few or no symptoms of inguinal hernia, they affect between 2 and 8 people per 1000 each year. [22]Fitzgibbons RJ, Giobbie-Hurder A, Gibbs JO, et al. Watchful waiting vs repair of inguinal hernia in minimally syjmptomatic men: a randomized clinical trial. JAMA 2006;295:285-292 [23]O’Dwyer PJ, Norrie J, Alani A, et al. Observation or operation for patients with an asymptomatic inguinal hernia: a randomised clinical trial. Ann Surg 2006;244:167-173

In people having conservative treatment for inguinal hernia, 27 in 10,000 will have a strangulation after two years. This goes up to 55 in 10,000 after four years. [29]Mizrahi H, Parker MC. Management of asymptomatic inguinal hernia: a systematic review of the evidence. Arch Surg 2012 Mar;147(3):277-81

Because these serious complications are so rare, people with few or no symptoms can safely stay with conservative treatment until their symptoms get worse. [40]Fitzgibbons RJ, Giobbie-Hurder A, Gibbs JO, et al. Watchful waiting vs repair of inguinal hernia in minimally symptomatic men: a randomized clinical trial. JAMA 2006;295:285-292

Effects on what you can do and how you feel

What people can do and how they feel while having conservative management for their inguinal hernia depends on:

  • how big the hernia is
  • how much pain or discomfort it causes
  • how much it interferes with work and leisure activities.

After 10 years on conservative treatment, symptoms are severe enough to affect leisure activities in 30 out of 100 people and lead to time off work in 13 out of 100. [2]Hair A, Paterson C, Wright D, et al. What effect does the duration of an inguinal hernia have on patient symptoms? J Am Coll Surg 2001 Aug;193(2):125-9

People having conservative treatment while waiting for surgical repair of inguinal hernia say they have a worse quality of life than people of similar age and health status. Quality of life is lowest for people with the largest hernias. [42]Mathur S, Bartlett AS, Gilkison W, et al. Quality of life assessments in patients with inguinal hernia. ANZ J Surg 2006 Jun;76(6):491-3

Manual workers having conservative treatment say they have lower quality of life than those in sedentary jobs. This may be because their jobs are more physically demanding.
Nearly a quarter of people having conservative treatment while waiting for surgical repair report sexual difficulties, which usually clear up after surgery. [43]Zieren J, Menenakos C, Paul M, et al. Sexual function before and after mesh repair of inguinal hernia. Int J Urol 2005 Jan;12(1):35-8

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT SURGICAL REPAIR

What is it?

There are two main ways to repair an inguinal hernia [7]National Digestive Diseases Information Clearinghouse (NDDIC), Inguinal Hernia [45]Patient.co.uk. Inguinal hernias:

  • Open surgery, where a single cut is made in the groin area. When the hernia is found, the hole can be either stitched closed or covered and closed with a thin sheet of material called a mesh. Mesh operations are now the most common. With time the mesh becomes part of the muscle layer, resulting in a very strong permanent repair.
  • ‘Keyhole’ surgery (laparoscopy), where a few small incisions are made in the abdomen. A small telescopic camera called a laparoscope is then inserted to give the surgeon a close-up view of the hernia and surrounding tissues. Special instruments are then used to repair the hernia, normally using a mesh. 

There are two different types of laparoscopy, which give similar results [46]Liebl RJ, Jager C, Kraft B, et al. Laparoscopic hernia repair – TAPP or/and TEP? Langenbecks Arch Surg 2005 Apr;390(2):77-82:

  • transabdominal preperitoneal (TAPP), where the operation takes place inside the space that encloses the abdominal organs
  • totally extra-peritoneal (TEP), where the operation takes place in the space between the muscles and the lining of the abdomen.

Open repair of inguinal hernia can be carried out under local or general anaesthetic. Some research has suggested that there is a quicker recovery after laparoscopic surgery, however the accuracy of these studies is questionable and we don't really know if there is a difference between the open and laparoscopic procedures. People can often choose which they prefer.  [47]O’Dwyer PJ, Serpell MG, Millar K. Local or general anaesthesia for open hernia repair: a randomized trial. Ann Surg 2003 April;237(4):574-579

Laparoscopy is a more complicated procedure that takes longer than open surgery and is usually carried out under general anaesthetic. [48]NICE Technology appraisal 83. Laparoscopic surgery for inguinal hernia repair. September 2004

In the UK more than 90 out of 100 hernia repairs are carried out using open mesh surgery. [49]Kingsnorth A. The case for open repair. Ann R Coll Surg Engl 2005;87 

Who can have it?

Anyone with a diagnosed inguinal hernia can ask for surgical repair. Whether they can have it depends on their surgeons. Doctors prefer to operate on people who are generally fit and healthy to reduce the chance of complications.

Whether they have open surgery or laparoscopy, TAPP or TEP, general or local anaesthetic will depend on several things, including [48]NICE Technology appraisal 83. Laparoscopic surgery for inguinal hernia repair. September 2004:

  • their own preference
  • whether they have a first-time or recurrent hernia
  • whether the hernia is on one or both sides of the groin
  • whether they are suitable for general anaesthetic.

Women are likely to be advised to have laparoscopic repair to cover the inguinal and femoral areas at the same time. This is because of their higher chance of getting femoral hernias. [Simons MP, Aufenacker T, Bay-Nielsen M, et al. European Hernia Society guidelines on the treatment of inguinal hernia in adult patient. Hernia 2009 August; 13(4):343-403]

How effective is it?

Surgical repair is a permanent solution for most people with inguinal hernias. But there is a small risk that the hernia will come back. Between 13 and 25 people in every 1000 having a hernia repair will get a recurrent hernia. [48]NICE Technology appraisal 83. Laparoscopic surgery for inguinal hernia repair. September 2004

The chance of getting another recurrence after a second hernia repair is much higher, affecting nearly 9 people in 100. [52]Bisgaard T, Bay-Nielsen M, Kehlet H. Re-recurrence after operation for recurrent inguinal hernia. A nationwide 8-year follow-up study on the role of type of repair. Ann Surg 2008 Apr;247(4):707-11

Women have a higher chance of getting recurrent hernias than men. [Koch A, Edwards A, Haapaniemi S, et al. Prospectice evaluation of 6895 groin hernia repairs in women. Br J Surg 2005 Dec;92(12):1553-8] Forty out of 100 women having operations for recurrence are found to have femoral hernias that may have been overlooked at the time of the first operation. [Simons MP, Aufenacker T, Bay-Nielsen M, et al. European Hernia Society guidelines on the treatment of inguinal hernia in adult patient. Hernia 2009 August; 13(4):343-403]

People having open repair with a mesh are between 50 and 75 per cent less likely to get recurrent hernias than those having open repairs without a mesh. [53]Scott NW, McCormack K, Graham P, et al. Open mesh versus non-mesh for repair of femoral and inguinal hernia. Chochrane Database Syst Rev 2002;(4):CD002197

There is no difference in recurrence rates between open mesh repair and laparoscopy. But people having laparascopic repair with a mesh are between 30 and 50 per cent less likely to have a recurrence than those having laparoscopic repair without a mesh. [54]McCormack K, Scott NW, Go PM, et al. Laparoscopic techniques versus open techniques for inguinal hernia repair. Cochrane Database Syst Rev 2003;(1):CD001785

We can’t say how TAPP laparoscopy compares with the TEP technique in terms of recurrence. There hasn’t been enough research. [46]Liebl RJ, Jager C, Kraft B, et al. Laparoscopic hernia repair – TAPP or/and TEP? Langenbecks Arch Surg 2005 Apr;390(2):77-82

Recurrent hernias don’t always cause problems. Between 30 and 50 people in every 100 with recurrent hernias either don’t know they have one or don’t want to have another repair. [3]Jenkins JT, O’Dwyer PJ. Inguinal hernias. BMJ 2008;336:269-72

What are the effects on symptoms?

About 15 people in 100 continue to suffer pain or discomfort as long as 2-3 years after the operation. [57]Franneby U, Sandblom G, Nordin P. Risk factors for long-term pain after hernia surgery. Ann Surg 2006 August;244(2):212-19 For 3 out of 100 people the pain is severe enough to interfere with daily activities.

The cause of the pain is not well understood. But the people most likely to have it are:

  • younger people (below age 60)
  • those who had a lot of pain before the operation
  • those who had complications of surgery

The use of mesh during surgery reduces the chance of long term pain. And people having laparoscopic repair may be less likely to have long term pain than those having open repair. [58]EU Hernia trialists collaboration. Repair of groin hernia with synthetic mesh: meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials. Ann Surg 2002 Mar;235(3):322-32 [54]McCormack K, Scott NW, Go PM, et al. Laparoscopic techniques versus open techniques for inguinal hernia repair. Cochrane Database Syst Rev 2003;(1):CD001785

People with chronic pain after hernia repair can be treated in special pain clinics, or may need further surgery if it is severe and does not respond to medical treatment. [3]Jenkins JT, O’Dwyer PJ. Inguinal hernias. BMJ 2008;336:269-72

What are the potential health problems?

Most of the complications of surgical repair are not dangerous.

Just over 22 people in 100 get complications after surgery. The main ones are:

  • a haematoma (blood-filled swelling) of the wound or scrotum
  • difficulty passing urine immediately after the operation
  • a wound infection
  • numbness around the wound
  • swelling of the testicle on the side of the operation

All of these get better. In rare cases the testicle on the side of the operation may become smaller after some time. This can happen in about five people in 1,000 after a first-time hernia repair. The chance is much higher after repair of recurrent hernias, affecting about 5 people in 100. [61]Fitzgibbons Jr RJ. Can we be sure that polypropylene mesh causes infertility? Ann Surg 2005;241:559-61

Effects on what you can do and how you feel

People with inguinal hernia are often told it takes between three and four weeks to recover after surgical repair. But they can often make do with a shorter period of convalescence. [62]Bay-Nielsen M, Thomsen H, Andersen FH. Convalescence after inguinal herniorrhaphy. Br J Surg 2004 Mar;91(3):362-7

It is common to feel sore for the first few days after the operation and to have some swelling or bruising around the wound area. The soreness or discomfort may last a week or so, sometimes a little longer. [63]Royal College of Surgeons leaflet: Helping you to make a speedy recovery after surgery for groin hernia repair

Most people are able to return to work by about two weeks after the operation and to leisure activities after another one or two weeks. Local discomfort is the main reason why some people take longer to get back to normal. [62]Bay-Nielsen M, Thomsen H, Andersen FH. Convalescence after inguinal herniorrhaphy. Br J Surg 2004 Mar;91(3):362-7 People doing heavy manual work or work that involves a lot of physical exertion may be advised to take longer off work. Most people are able to drive again within one week. [65]Vironen J, Nieminen J, Eklund A. Randomized clinical trial of Lichtenstein patch or Prolene Hernia System for inguinal hernia repair. Br J Surg 2006 Jan;93(1):33-9

There are different views about the effects of surgical repair on sexual function. At present there is no firm evidence to confirm any effect, good or bad.

There are also different views on the impact of surgical repair on fertility. The latest research suggests there is no adverse effect. [66]Hallén M, Westerdahl J, Nordin P, et al. Mesh hernia repair and male infertility: a retrospective register study. Surgery 2012 Jan;151(1):94-8

There is evidence that people who have a laparoscopic hernia repair may have less severe post-op discomfort than after open repair, and are able to return to work and normal activities sooner. [54]McCormack K, Scott NW, Go PM, et al. Laparoscopic techniques versus open techniques for inguinal hernia repair. Cochrane Database Syst Rev 2003;(1):CD001785 [68]Abbas AE, Abd Ellatif ME, Noaman N. Patient-perspective quality of life after laparascopic and open hernia repair: a controlled randomized trial. Surg Endosc 2012 Sep;26(9):2465-70 [48]NICE Technology appraisal 83. Laparoscopic surgery for inguinal hernia repair. September 2004
