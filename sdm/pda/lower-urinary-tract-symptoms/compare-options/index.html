Our Decision Support telephone service is here to assist and guide you through the Shared Decision Making process.

The Decision Support team is made of professional Health Coaches who are clinicians, commonly nurses, who have undertaken additional training to become a Health Coach.

The Health Coaches will assist you by:

  • Providing you with information
  • Helping you to understand your condition
  • Recognise what is important to you regarding the outcome of treatment
  • Identifying potential solutions
  • Encourage you in discussing options with your family
  • Transferring skills which will assist you in using the information and resources available to you
  • Support you in building confidence in discussing your choice with your doctor
  • You may find that this can be achieved in one telephone call with a Health Coach however; if further calls are required to support you in reaching your decision the Health Coach will schedule these with you.

Important Things To Note

  • A Health Coach does not know which treatment option is best for you.
  • The Health Coach does not have information about your medical history.
  • You will still need to talk over your decision and reasons for your decision with your doctor or nurse.

MORE INFORMATION ABOUT LOWER URINARY TRACT SYMPTOMS

What is the urinary tract?

The part of your body that makes and gets rid of urine is called your urinary tract. It includes your kidneys, ureters, bladder, and urethra. Your kidneys filter water and waste products out of your blood to make urine.

Urine drains from your kidneys down tubes called ureters. Most people have two ureters, one for each kidney. Both ureters empty into your bladder. Urine is stored in your bladder until you go to the toilet. When you urinate, urine passes out of your body from the bladder through a tube called the urethra. Together, the bladder and the urethra make up the lower urinary tract. 

Men have a gland called the prostate gland at the base of the bladder. (Glands make substances that are used somewhere else in the body.) Only men have a prostate, which is about the size of a walnut. The front of your prostate is wrapped around your urethra. The prostate helps make the milky fluid that comes out of your penis when you ejaculate. The fluid from your prostate helps keep your sperm healthy and helps them swim.

The back of your prostate presses against your back passage (rectum). This is why your doctor examines your rectum if there's a problem with your prostate. He or she can feel the gland through the wall of your rectum.

The two bundles of nerves that control your erections run on either side of your prostate.

Your prostate is also linked to your testicles. These are the two organs that make sperm and the male hormone testosterone. They lie in a pouch of skin on either side of your penis. Your sperm are carried by tubes that run from your testicles and through your prostate into your urethra. Your prostate adds its fluid to the sperm when they reach your urethra.[1]Barry MJ, Fowler FJ Jr, O'Leary MP, et al. The American Urological Association symptom index for benign prostatic hyperplasia. Journal of Urology. 1992; 148: 1549-1557.

What are lower urinary tract symptoms in men?

There are two main types of lower urinary tract symptoms. Doctors call them voiding symptoms and storage symptoms. Voiding symptoms happen while you are urinating. Storage symptoms affect how well your bladder stores urine.

Voiding symptoms include:

  • having to wait before you start urinating
  • stopping and starting when urinating
  • having to push or strain to urinate
  • having a weak flow or weak stream of urine
  • dribbling at the end of passing urine
  • feeling as if your bladder is never quite empty.

Storage symptoms include:

  • having to urinate more often than you used to
  • finding it difficult to wait before urinating
  • having to get up at night to urinate
  • being suddenly desperate to urinate.

Not everyone gets all of these symptoms. Not everyone who has symptoms finds them troublesome.

Your doctor may use a questionnaire to measure your symptoms. One of the most common questionnaires is called the International Prostate Symptom Score (IPSS) questionnaire.

This questionnaire has seven questions that ask you to rate how severe your symptoms are.[1]Barry MJ, Fowler FJ Jr, O'Leary MP, et al. The American Urological Association symptom index for benign prostatic hyperplasia. Journal of Urology. 1992; 148: 1549-1557. Your answers are used to calculate your symptom score. There is also an eighth question about how your symptoms affect your life.

The questionnaire is used to work out whether your symptoms are mild, moderate, or severe. This may affect the treatments you are offered.

What causes male lower urinary tract symptoms?

Lower urinary tract symptoms in men can be caused by an enlarged prostate gland, or by an overactive bladder, or both.

It can be hard to tell which one is causing your symptoms, because the symptoms can be similar. Some men with an enlarged prostate have no symptoms.

Enlarged prostate

As you get older, your prostate may get bigger. You may hear it called benign prostatic hyperplasia, or BPH. This condition is not cancer. An enlarged prostate can cause problems by pressing on the urethra and restricting the flow of urine.

Having an enlarged prostate is more common among older men than younger men. Up to 30 in 100 men aged over 65 get lower urinary tract symptoms that they consider troublesome.[3]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. The management of lower urinary tract symptoms in men. May 2010. Available at http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG97 (accessed on 9 August 2012). An enlarged prostate is often slow to develop. For between 30 and 40 men out of 100 with an enlarged prostate, the symptoms don’t get any worse.

Overactive bladder

Lower urinary tract symptoms in men can be caused by an overactive bladder. When your bladder is working normally, you start to feel the need to urinate when it’s about half full. You don’t need to use the toilet straight away, and you can hold on for a while. When you have an overactive bladder, the bladder will contract when it contains only a small amount of urine, so that your bladder feels fuller than it is.

Overactive bladder may be caused by changes in your prostate.[4]McConnell J. Epidemiology, etiology, pathophysiology, and diagnosis of benign prostatic hyperplasia. In: Campbell MF, Walsh PC, Retik AB (editors). Campbell's Urology. 8th edition. WB Saunders, Philadelphia, PA; 2002. For example, the wall of your bladder may become thick and irritable because the prostate has grown and is pressing against it. This can make the bladder overactive.

Why has this happened to me?

These things can increase your chances of getting lower urinary tract symptoms:

  • Your age. Your chances of getting an enlarged prostate increase as you get older.[5]Medina JJ, Parra RO, Moore RG. Benign prostatic hyperplasia (the aging prostate). Medical Clinics of North America. 1999; 83: 1213-1229.
  • Your family history. Enlarged prostate glands run in some families. If your father or brother had an enlarged prostate, you are at greater risk of getting it.
  • Your race. Black men are more likely to have surgery for an enlarged prostate compared with white men. Asian men are less likely than white men to have surgery.[6]Sideny S, Quesenberry CP, Sadler MC, et al. Incidence of surgically treated benign prostatic hypertrophy and of prostate cancer among black and white multiphasic examinees in a prepaid health care plan. American Journal of Epidemiology. 1991; 134: 825-829. [7]Platz EA, Kawachi I, Rimm EB, et al. Race, ethnicity and benign prostatic hyperplasia in the health professionals follow-up study. Journal of Urology. 2000; 163: 490. [8]Lagiou P, Wuu J, Trichopoulou A, et al. Diet and benign prostatic hyperplasia: a study in Greece. Urology. 1999; 54: 284-290.
  • Other medical conditions. If you have diabetes or have had heart failure you might be more likely to have lower urinary tract symptoms. 
  • Medicines. Taking diuretics, phenothiazines, antidepressants and beta-agonists may cause lower urinary tract symptoms, or make them worse.
  • Caffeine and alcohol. These may cause lower urinary tract symptoms or make them worse.
  • If you’ve had surgery or an injury anywhere in your urinary tract or genitals, you may be more likely to have lower urinary tract symptoms.
How do I know I have lower urinary tract symptoms?

Usually, you notice lower urinary tract symptoms yourself. You are likely to have some tests, to see how severe your symptoms are, and what might be causing them. If you are deciding about treatment for lower urinary tract symptoms, you will probably have had some of the tests listed below. There is no one test that can show whether your symptoms are caused by an enlarged prostate, or by an overactive bladder.

Your doctor might start by ruling out cancer. With a digital rectal examination, he or she can feel your prostate for abnormalities, which might be a sign of prostate cancer. A blood test called a Prostate Specific Antigen test can help tell if you have an increased risk of prostate cancer.

Your doctor will ask about your health, past illnesses, operations, or treatments you have had (your medical history), and about any medicines you are taking. Medicines can affect the way your bladder works.

Several tests measure how quickly and how strongly you are urinating, and whether there is any urine left in your bladder when you finish urinating.

Your doctor might test your blood or urine to check for infection, bladder cancer, diabetes, or kidney problems. Scans such as ultrasound can give your doctor a picture of your prostate and the surrounding area.

Your doctor may ask you to complete a urinary frequency volume chart. This means that you keep a record of how often you urinate, and at what time of day or night. You measure how much urine you produce each time you urinate. This is the most accurate way of recording how much and when you urinate.

 

How many men have lower urinary tract symptoms?

Lower urinary tract symptoms happen more often to older men, partly because the prostate can get bigger as you get older. It is unusual for men under 40 to have an enlarged prostate.

  • Up to 30 in 100 men aged over 50 will develop troublesome urinary problems.
  • By the age of 55, about 25 in 100 men will have noticed their stream of urine isn't as strong as it used to be. This goes up to 50 in 100 men by age 75.
What will happen?

Your chances of getting symptoms increase as you get older. Symptoms will not always get worse as you age.[9]McConnell J. Epidemiology, etiology, pathophysiology, and diagnosis of benign prostatic hyperplasia. In: Campbell MF, Walsh PC, Retik AB (eds). Campbell's Urology. 8th edition. WB Saunders, Philadelphia, PA; 2002. Some men find that their symptoms change slowly, and others find that they don't change at all.[10]Scher HI. Hyperplastic and malignant disease of the prostate. In: Braunwald E, Hauser SL, Fauci AS, et al. Harrison's principles of internal medicine. 15th edition. McGraw Hill, New York, NY; 2001.

Some men find that their symptoms get better on their own without any treatment. About 10 in 100 men develop severe symptoms caused by their enlarged prostate.

An enlarged prostate gland is not cancer, and having one does not increase your risk of getting prostate cancer.

How your condition develops can depend on how severe your symptoms are to start with. In one study of men who decided to have no treatment straight away, those with mild symptoms were less likely to get severe symptoms or go on to have surgery during the next four years. Of those men with worse symptoms, after four years:[11]Barry MJ, Fowler FJ, Bin L, et al. The natural history of patients with benign prostatic hyperplasia as diagnosed by North American urologists. Journal of Urology. 1997; 157: 10-15.

  • About 50 out of 100 still had the same level of symptoms
  • About 25 in 100 had surgery
  • About 13 in 100 developed severe symptoms
  • About 13 in 100 improved and had mild symptoms.

Acute urinary retention

This is when you find you are suddenly unable to urinate at all. It is more likely in older men. About 10 in 100 men in their 70s with an enlarged prostate will have an episode of acute urinary retention.[12]Jacobsen S, Jacobson D, Girman C, et al. Natural history of prostatism: risk factors for acute urinary retention. Journal of Urology. 1997; 158: 481-487. 

Acute urinary retention is painful. It's an emergency. You may need to go to hospital. A tube called a catheter will be put into your bladder through your penis to drain off the urine.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT CONSERVATIVE MEASURES

What is it?

Conservative measures for lower urinary tract symptoms include things you can try yourself, without using drugs or surgery.

For some men, it can mean doing nothing for the time being. Symptoms don't always get worse or become severe or troublesome. Some men choose to wait and keep an eye on their symptoms before deciding if they need any more treatment.

The main conservative measures for lower urinary tract symptoms include:

  • Pelvic muscle floor training: exercising the muscles around your bladder to strengthen them. A doctor or nurse can show you how to do this and the other physical techniques in this list.
  • Bladder training: gradually increasing the time between visits to the toilet so that your bladder stretches and can hold more urine. This should help you gain more control over your bladder.
  • Post-void milking: massaging the skin behind the scrotum to squeeze out urine that is left in the urethra after urination.
  • Lifestyle changes: reducing fluids (perhaps before bedtime), reducing alcohol and caffeine.
  • Containment products: these include absorbent pads, bed pads, and external collection devices.
  • Watchful waiting: keeping an eye on your symptoms before deciding to try any other treatment.
Who can have it?

Anyone with lower urinary tract symptoms can choose conservative management rather than drug treatment or surgery. You may choose to try these measures before deciding whether to have further treatment. 

Conservative measures are not appropriate for acute retention. Acute retention is when you suddenly can’t urinate at all, even though you need to. It is an emergency and is treated in hospital.

What are the effects of conservative measures on lower urinary tract symptoms?

Doctors think that conservative measures may help with symptoms, although there isn’t enough evidence to be sure.

Most conservative measures help with problems holding onto urine (such as needing to urinate frequently or urgently) rather than problems when you are urinating. 

Pelvic floor muscle training (PFMT) and bladder training may, over time, help you need to urinate less often. PFMT may also help with problems when you are urinating, by strengthening the muscles around your bladder. Doctors recommend giving PFMT at least three months to work. Bladder training can take between three weeks and three months to show an improvement.

A technique called post void milking might help with “dribbling” after you urinate. It involves massaging the skin behind the scrotum to make sure the urethra is empty once you’ve finished urinating. If this works for you, it should show an improvement right away.

Sensibly reducing how much fluid you drink may help you need to urinate less. Cutting down on things that may irritate your bladder, such as caffeine, alcohol, carbonated (fizzy) drinks, and artificial sweeteners, might help you need to urinate less.

Pads and collecting devices mean you don’t need to worry about incontinence or being near a toilet so much. These will work as soon as you start using them. Some men use these aids in the short term while deciding what other treatments to have.

What are the main effects of conservative measures on what you can do?

You might feel more in control of your symptoms.

Absorbent pads and collecting devices might help you get out and about for longer without worrying about being near a toilet. Having a period where you can stop worrying about your symptoms might help you think more clearly about what to do next.

Bladder training or PFMT might help you enjoy normal activities without worrying as much about being near a toilet.

You may feel more comfortable about being at work, travelling, or having days or evenings out. You may feel more confident about your sex life.

Drinking less in the evenings, or cutting down on alcohol, caffeine, and fizzy drinks might help you get more uninterrupted sleep and make you feel less tired when you wake.

What are the main side effects of conservative measures?

Most conservative measures don’t have side effects.

Bladder training might be uncomfortable at first, as you get used to “holding it in” a little longer every time.

Cutting out caffeine suddenly may cause caffeine withdrawal symptoms, such as headaches, sleepiness, and irritability.

Cutting out alcohol suddenly may cause withdrawal symptoms if you are a heavy drinker.

How might conservative measures affect my life?

If you are using pads or collecting devices, you will need to be able to empty or dispose of them. 

Lifestyle changes such as cutting down on alcohol or caffeine might mean you don’t want to socialise as much, or in the same places. Giving up things you enjoy, such as coffee, a drink in the pub, or a “nightcap”, may affect your quality of life.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT MEDICAL TREATMENT

What is it?

Medical treatment for your lower urinary tract symptoms means taking drugs to help control your symptoms. 

Your doctor may advise you to take more than one drug for your symptoms, because different drugs treat different symptoms. There are three main types of drugs to treat lower urinary tract symptoms:

Alpha blockers

These are used for moderate and severe problems with urinating, for men where it's likely symptoms are caused by an enlarged prostate. They make the muscles around the prostate and the bladder relax. This makes it easier to urinate. They can also be used, along with catheterisation, to treat acute urinary retention (when you suddenly can’t pass urine at all). There are several different alpha blockers: alfuzosin, doxazosin, tamsulosin, and terazosin.

5-alpha-reductase inhibitors

These drugs are used to shrink the size of the prostate gland or to stop it getting larger. The drugs of this type used to treat an enlarged prostate are dutasteride and finasteride.

Antimuscarinics

You might also hear these drugs called anticholinergics. They are used to treat an overactive bladder. These drugs relax the bladder so it takes longer before you feel like your bladder is full. Several antimuscarinic drugs are used including tolterodine and oxybutynin.

Some men try over-the-counter treatments, such as saw palmetto (Serenoa repens). This is a plant extract made from the American dwarf palm tree. This plant has been used for hundreds of years by Native Americans, both as a food and to treat sexual and prostate problems, but we are not sure how it works.

Who can have it?

You need a prescription from your doctor for most medicines that treat lower urinary tract symptoms.

Your doctor needs to be sure that the drugs are suitable for the type of symptoms you have. If your symptoms suggest you have an overactive bladder, you may need antimuscarinic drugs. If your symptoms suggest you have an enlarged prostate that is pressing on your urethra, you may need alpha blockers or 5-alpha-reductase inhibitors. 

You can buy an alpha blocker called tamsulosin over the counter. The pharmacist will ask you questions to check whether the drug is right for you. You will need to see a doctor if you want to take this treatment for more than a few weeks.

You can buy saw palmetto over the counter.

What are the main effects of medical treatment (drugs) on lower urinary tract symptoms?

Alpha blockers

Alpha blockers may help with problems urinating. They are not a cure. They work by relaxing the muscles in the bladder and prostate, helping urine to flow more freely. They are usually taken by men with moderate or severe symptoms. They take about a week to start working.

In one group of men who took an alpha blocker, symptoms improved by about 2.5 points on a scale of 0 to 35 points. A change of 5 points is described as moderate improvement and a change of 3 points as slight improvement.[13]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. The management of lower urinary tract symptoms in men. May 2010. Available at http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG97 (accessed on 9 August 2012)

Alpha blockers may increase the amount of urine you are able to pass when you go to the toilet. Some men don't notice much of a difference.[13]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. The management of lower urinary tract symptoms in men. May 2010. Available at http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG97 (accessed on 9 August 2012)

5-alpha-reductase inhibitors (5-ARIs)

5-ARIs may help with problems while urinating. They are not a cure. These drugs work by shrinking your prostate, which may relieve pressure on your urethra. They are usually taken by men with mild or moderate symptoms. They take several months to start working. Finasteride may take six months to show an improvement. Dutasteride may take three months. They may help reduce symptoms for about three years. After that time they don’t work as well.

In groups of men who took 5-ARIs, symptom scores improved by about 0.8 points after one year and 1.8 points after two years, on a scale of 0 to 35 points. 

5-ARIs can increase the amount of urine you pass when you go to the toilet. Some men don't notice much of a difference.[13]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. The management of lower urinary tract symptoms in men. May 2010. Available at http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG97 (accessed on 9 August 2012)

5-ARIs may reduce the risk of urinary retention (when you can’t urinate completely or at all). In a group of more than 11,000 men, between 1 in 100 and 2 in 100 men who took 5-ARIs had urinary retention problems, compared with between 3 in 100 and 4 in 100 men who didn’t take 5-ARIs.[13]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. The management of lower urinary tract symptoms in men. May 2010. Available at http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG97 (accessed on 9 August 2012)

Antimuscarinics

Antimuscarinics (sometimes called anticholinergics) may help with symptoms of an overactive bladder. They work by making the bladder contract less often.

In a group of men who took an antimuscarinic, episodes of urgency incontinence (when you suddenly need to urinate and can’t wait) were reduced by about half after three months.[13]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. The management of lower urinary tract symptoms in men. May 2010. Available at http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG97 (accessed on 9 August 2012)

Saw palmetto

Saw palmetto may increase the amount of urine you can pass. Some men don't notice much of a difference.[13]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. The management of lower urinary tract symptoms in men. May 2010. Available at http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG97 (accessed on 9 August 2012)

Combined treatment

Your doctor may suggest that you take more than one drug for your symptoms. Taking an alpha blocker together with a 5-ARI improves symptom scores and the amount of urine you can pass more than taking either drug alone.

Taking an antimuscarinic together with an alpha blocker improves symptom scores more than taking an alpha blocker alone.[13]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. The management of lower urinary tract symptoms in men. May 2010. Available at http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG97 (accessed on 9 August 2012)

What are the main effects of medical treatment on what you can do?

If medical treatments work for you, you might feel more in control of your symptoms. 

Alpha blockers and 5-ARIs help you pass more urine when you go to the toilet. You may need to urinate less often. You may find you worry less about being near to a toilet when you go out. Men who took alpha blockers said that their quality of life was better than without the drugs.[13]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. The management of lower urinary tract symptoms in men. May 2010. Available at http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG97 (accessed on 9 August 2012)

If you feel more in control of your symptoms, you may feel more comfortable about being at work, travelling, or having days or evenings out. You may feel more confident about your sex life.

Antimuscarinics may reduce episodes of urgency incontinence (when you suddenly need to use the toilet and can’t wait). This may make you feel more comfortable about going out to work and socialising.[13]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. The management of lower urinary tract symptoms in men. May 2010. Available at http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG97 (accessed on 9 August 2012)

Needing to urinate less at night may improve your sleep and make you feel less tired when you wake.

What are the main side effects of medical treatment?

Many of the problems below affect men whether or not they take drugs for lower urinary tract symptoms. They are more common for men who do take these drugs. 

Alpha blockers may cause:[13]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. The management of lower urinary tract symptoms in men. May 2010. Available at http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG97 (accessed on 9 August 2012)

  • Dizziness affects about 8 in 100 men who take alpha blockers.
  • Tiredness affects between 5 in 100 and 6 in 100 men.
  • Dizzy spells or head rushes when you stand affect between 2 in 100 and 3 in 100 men.
  • Erection problems or impotence happen to about 3 in 100 men.
  • Ejaculation problems happen to about 2 in 100 men.
  • A blocked or runny nose can affect about 6 in 100 men.

5-alpha reductase inhibitors (5-ARIs) may cause:[13]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. The management of lower urinary tract symptoms in men. May 2010. Available at http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG97 (accessed on 9 August 2012)

  • Reduced sex drive affects about 5 in 100 men.
  • Ejaculation problems affect between 2 in 100 and 3 in 100 men.
  • Erection problems affect about 7 in 100 men.
  • Gynaecomastia (swelling of the breast tissue) affects between 1 in 100 and 2 in 100 men.

Antimuscarinics (anticholinergics) may cause dry mouth in about 7 in 100 men.[13]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. The management of lower urinary tract symptoms in men. May 2010. Available at http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG97 (accessed on 9 August 2012) Other side effects may include constipation, stomach pain and weakened flow of urine.

We don’t know whether saw palmetto causes side effects.

How might medical treatment affect my life?

You need to keep taking your medication regularly if it is to help. Some people find it hard to stick to a routine of taking their medicine. You may need to have follow-up appointments with your doctor to check whether the drugs and dosage are right for you.

Taking drugs for lower urinary tract symptoms may affect your sex life. This could put a strain on your relationship, or make you feel depressed.[13]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. The management of lower urinary tract symptoms in men. May 2010. Available at http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG97 (accessed on 9 August 2012)

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT SURGERY

What is it?

There are several types of surgery to treat an enlarged prostate.

Transurethral resection of the prostate (TURP) involves cutting away part of the prostate gland to stop it pressing on the urethra. A surgeon puts a thin tube into the opening of the penis and up the urethra to the prostate. The tube has a light and a small camera on the end. It also has a tiny loop attached to it. The loop is heated with an electric current and used to cut away the part of the prostate that is blocking the urethra. The pieces of prostate are flushed down the tube with water. A thin, flexible tube called a catheter is put into the urethra to drain away urine after the operation.

This operation can also be done using a slightly different instrument with two electrodes. This is called bipolar transurethral resection of the prostate. The results are the same as for TURP.

Transurethral holmium laser enucleation of the prostate (HoLEP) is a type of surgery that uses a laser, which produces intense heat in a small area. A small laser is put into the opening in your penis and up your urethra. The laser cuts away the overgrown parts of the prostate that are blocking the urethra by peeling them away from the prostate's outer lining. This is is a bit like peeling an orange from the inside.

Lasers are also used to destroy part of the prostate in other, similar operations. These are not used routinely on the NHS because there is not enough evidence about how well they work.

Cystoplasty is a major operation used to treat an overactive bladder in men with severe problems holding urine. This operation is used to make the bladder bigger using a part of your bowel, so that you can hold more urine. It is only used if other treatments have not worked.

Who can have it?

TURP or HoLEP surgery is usually offered to men with lower urinary tract symptoms only if they have had tests showing that their prostate is enlarged, and that it is blocking the urethra. 

To have any kind of surgery you have to be fit for surgery. Things that may affect your fitness for surgery include:

  • Your age and frailty
  • Other health problems you may have
  • Whether you are fit enough to have an anaesthetic.
What are the main effects of surgery on lower urinary tract symptoms?

Transurethral resection of the prostate (TURP) can help with difficulty urinating. Between 70 in 100 and 75 in 100 men who have TURP benefit from surgery.

In a group of men who had TURP, three years after surgery the amount of urine passed in millilitres per second [ml/s] had increased by about 5 ml/s. Any improvement of 2 ml/s or more is likely to be noticeable.[13]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. The management of lower urinary tract symptoms in men. May 2010. Available at http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG97 (accessed on 9 August 2012)

Holmium laser enucleation of the prostate (HoLEP) can help with difficulty urinating. In a group of about 100 men who had HoLEP, the amount of urine passed in millilitres per second [ml/s] increased by about 2.7 ml/s more, compared with men who had TURP, after three months.[13]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. The management of lower urinary tract symptoms in men. May 2010. Available at http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG97 (accessed on 9 August 2012)

Surgery may improve night-time bed-wetting in some men.

What are the main effects of surgery on what you can do?

You might feel more in control of your symptoms. You may have a stronger stream of urine and be able to urinate more easily. Being able to pass more urine each time you use the toilet may mean that you need to urinate less often. You may find you don’t have to worry so much about being near to a toilet all the time.

You may feel more comfortable about being at work, travelling, or having days or evenings out. You may feel more confident about your sex life. About 6 in 100 men who have TURP and 7 in 100 men who have HoLEP say they are better able to get an erection after surgery.[13]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. The management of lower urinary tract symptoms in men. May 2010. Available at http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG97 (accessed on 9 August 2012)

Needing to urinate less at night may improve your sleep and make you feel less tired when you wake.

What are the main side effects of surgery?

The main side effects of surgery for the voiding symptoms of lower urinary tract symptoms include:[13]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. The management of lower urinary tract symptoms in men. May 2010. Available at http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG97 (accessed on 9 August 2012)

  • Bleeding. Bleeding can be heavy for a few days after the operation. Bleeding is likely to be worse if you have TURP, compared with HoLEP.
  • Needing a blood transfusion during the operation. About 3 in 1000 men having HoLEP need a blood transfusion. About 33 in 1000 men having TURP need a blood transfusion.
  • Catheterisation. Men need a catheter to help them urinate for 1-2 days after surgery. About 3 in 100 men having TURP will need to have re-catheterisation. This means they will need another catheter fitted for a while after the operation. This is not permanent.
  • Infection. About 8 in 100 men having TURP get an infection after surgery. About 3 in 100 men having HoLEP get an infection.
  • Re-operation. Between 7 in 100 and 8 in 100 men who have TURP will need another operation. Between 5 in 100 and 6 in 100 men who have HoLEP will need another operation.
  • Ejaculation problems. About 65 in 100 men who have TURP will have a dry climax (retrograde ejaculation). Between 40 in 100 and 43 in 100 men who have HoLEP will have ejaculation problems. Retrograde ejaculation may not affect how much you enjoy sex.[30]Frieben RW,Lin HC, Hinh PP, et al. The impact of minimally invasive surgeries for the treatment of symptomatic benign prostatic hyperplasia on male sexual function: a systematic review. Asian Journal of Andrology. 2010; 12: 500-508.
  • Erection problems. Between 7 in 100 and 8 in 100 men said they had more difficulty getting an erection after either kind of surgery.

 

How might surgery affect my life?

Problems with your sex life caused by the operation may have an effect on your relationships. You may feel depressed.

It takes several weeks after surgery before you can do all the things you may have done before, such as driving, having sex, or strenuous physical activity.

You may need to keep taking drugs for your symptoms after surgery. Some people find it hard to stick to a routine of taking their medications.

You may need follow-up appointments with your doctor or surgeon after the operation.

Between 25 in 100 and 30 in 100 men get no benefit from surgery.[13]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. The management of lower urinary tract symptoms in men. May 2010. Available at http://guidance.nice.org.uk/CG97 (accessed on 9 August 2012)

TIME YOU SPEND IN HOSPITAL OR ON TREATMENT

Conservative measures

You are unlikely to spend much time in hospital. You can see a nurse or doctor to learn bladder training and pelvic floor muscle training, but you don't need to stay in hospital. You may need follow-up appointments every few months.

more information

Medical treatment

You need to keep taking drugs for your symptoms for them to keep working. Improvements with drugs may last for several years. If the drugs stop helping, your doctor may advise you to stop taking them.

more information

Surgery

TURP takes about an hour. You may have to stay in hospital for up to three nights. After you get home it may take a few weeks before you can resume all your normal activities. We don’t know how long saw palmetto takes to have any effect on symptoms. 

more information

Surgery

We don’t know how quickly surgery can improve your symptoms but if surgery works for you, you should see improvements within a few days. There has not been enough research looking at this question.
