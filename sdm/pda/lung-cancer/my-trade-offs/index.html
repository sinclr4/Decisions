The Decision Support team is made of professional Health Coaches who are clinicians, commonly nurses, who have undertaken additional training to become a Health Coach.

The Health Coaches will assist you by:

  • Providing you with information
  • Helping you to understand your condition

MORE INFORMATION ABOUT LUNG CANCER

What is lung cancer?

When your body's cells are healthy, they divide, grow old, then die and are replaced with new cells in the natural way. When you get cancer, your cells grow and multiply too quickly. In lung cancer, abnormal cells group together to form a lump called a tumour. This gets bigger over time. The speed at which it grows varies from one person to another.

Nearly all types of lung cancer can spread to other parts of your body and form more tumours. These are called secondary tumours, secondaries, or metastases.

The most common kind of lung cancer is non-small-cell lung cancer (NSCLC). About 90 in 100 cases of lung cancer are this type. It can start in different parts of the lung. There are three different types of non-small-cell cancer.

  • Adenocarcinoma: this kind of cancer usually starts in the airways at the outer areas of the lungs. It is the most common type of non-small-cell lung cancer.
  • Squamous cell carcinoma: this is a cancer that often affects the larger airways. 
  • Large cell carcinoma: this type of cancer is less common and is made up of larger cells than other forms of lung cancer.[1]Cancer Research UK. Types of lung cancer. March 2011. Available at: http://cancerhelp.cancerresearchuk.org/type/lung-cancer/about/types-of-lung-cancer#non-small (accessed on 12 September 2012).
What do the lungs do?

You have two lungs inside your rib cage, one on each side of your heart. They are like two spongy, elastic bags that fill up with air as you breathe in, and empty as you breathe out. The right lung is divided into three sections, and the left lung into two sections. These sections are called lobes.

Keeping your lungs healthy is important. They take oxygen from the air you breathe, and your blood carries this oxygen around your body. When you breathe out, your lungs get rid of carbon dioxide, which is a waste product made by your body.

What are the different stages of lung cancer?

Doctors describe the stage a cancer has reached by looking at three important factors. It is called the TNM system: T is for Tumour, N is for lymph Nodes (the first place a cancer usually spreads to), and M is for Metastasis (when the cancer spreads to other parts of your body outside of the lung). Each factor is given a number. Generally, lower numbers mean your cancer is less advanced.[2]Cancer Research UK. Lung cancer stages. March 2011. Available at: http://cancerhelp.cancerresearchuk.org/type/lung-cancer/treatment/lung-cancer-staging (accessed on 12 September 2012).

This decision aid is for people with localised lung cancer.

Doctors use these TNM factors to group cancers into numbered stages.

  • Stages 1 and 2: the cancer is limited to one area of the lung. It may have reached the closest lymph nodes. This is called localised cancer.
  • Stage 3: the cancer is larger and may have grown into the surrounding tissues. There may be cancer cells in the lymph nodes further away from the lung (in the middle of the chest or base of the neck). This is called locally advanced cancer.
  • Stage 4: the cancer has spread to another part of the body. This is called secondary or metastatic cancer.
What are the symptoms of lung cancer?

Most people with early stage lung cancer do not have any symptoms. The tumour is detected by a chest x-ray or scan carried out for another purpose.

In people who do have symptoms, the most common are coughing and breathing problems. Although your symptoms may start off seeming like a cold or flu, if you have lung cancer they won't get better. They won't be cured by remedies you can buy at a pharmacy or get from your GP. Other symptoms include coughing up blood, pain, and losing weight without trying.

While most lung cancers are caused by tobacco smoking, people who've never smoked can also get lung cancer. So it's important to get symptoms checked out, even if you're a life-long non-smoker.

Why has this happened to me?

The biggest cause of lung cancer is smoking. Smoking causes between 8 and 9 out of 10 deaths from lung cancer. Men who smoke are 22 times more likely to die from lung cancer than those who don't smoke. Women who smoke are 12 times more likely to die from lung cancer than those who don't smoke. It can take many years for the cancer to develop. Sometimes people who have never smoked also get lung cancer. Passive smoking, when you do not smoke but people near you do, is now known to increase the risk of lung cancer. 

Some materials used in industry can give you a greater chance of getting lung cancer. These include asbestos and other chemicals that can cause lung cancer, such as arsenic and chromium. 

Other possible factors that can increase the risk of lung cancer include air pollution, having another lung disease, and exposure to radon.

Radon is an invisible gas that has no taste or smell, and is found naturally in soil and rocks. Buildings on land that contains a lot of radon tend to include high levels. In the UK, these areas are Devon, Cornwall, Somerset, Northamptonshire, and Derbyshire.

How do I find out if I have lung cancer?

You may find out by chance when having a test for another purpose. Or you might find out after going to the doctor with symptoms such as a cough that won't go away.

Doctors will ask you questions and do some tests to diagnose lung cancer. Your doctor may:

  • Ask you about your symptoms
  • Listen to your breathing through a stethoscope
  • Refer you to your local hospital for a chest x-ray or CT scan
  • Refer you to a hospital specialist.[3]Cancer Research UK. Lung cancer tests. March 2011. Available at: http://cancerhelp.cancerresearchuk.org/type/lung-cancer/diagnosis/lung-cancer-tests (accessed on 12 September 2012).

A chest x-ray takes pictures of your lungs to see if there are any signs of lung cancer or another illness. Your doctor will refer you for an urgent x-ray if you have symptoms, such as coughing up blood, that could possibly indicate lung cancer. Your doctor may also refer you for an x-ray if you have other symptoms, such as a simple cough, especially if they have gone on for more than three weeks. The results of the chest x-ray will help the doctor decide whether you need to see a specialist.[4]NHS Choices. X-ray. Available at: http://www.nhs.uk/conditions/X-ray/Pages/Introduction.aspx (accessed on 12 September 2012). If the x-ray or your symptoms show signs of lung cancer, you should be offered an urgent appointment with a hospital specialist. A small number of people with lung cancer have a normal chest x-ray so your doctor may refer you for further tests anyway.

Your specialist may arrange for some more tests. The most common are:

  • CT scan: a special type of x-ray using a computer to get a more detailed picture of your lungs.
  • PET-CT scan: uses injections of tiny amounts of radioactive chemicals to show what's happening in your body. Cancers show up as very active because they contain lots of cells dividing.
  • Bronchoscopy: a flexible tube with a tiny camera on the end (bronchoscope) is passed down through your nose or mouth into your lungs. This lets your doctor examine and remove small samples of cells (biopsies) from inside your lungs.
  • Needle biopsy of the lung: samples taken of cells that are then looked at in a laboratory to find out whether you have cancer, and if so what type.
How many people have lung cancer?

In the UK, lung cancer is the second most common cancer in men (after prostate cancer) and the third most common in women (after breast cancer and bowel cancer). Lung cancer is also the leading cause of death from cancer in the UK. 

Around 41,400 new cases of lung cancer are diagnosed each year in the UK and more than 35,000 people die from it. More British people die from lung cancer each year than from breast and bowel cancers combined.

Lung cancer is becoming less common in men, since a peak in the early 1980s. And figures in women are levelling off, though they aren't going down yet. This is probably because fewer people are smoking.

However, a study of people born since 1950 shows trends like this can change quite quickly. Researchers found that among people in their 20s and 30s, there were small increases in the number dying from lung cancer. They suggest this may be due to the known rise in smoking among teenagers.[5]Macmillan Cancer Support. The rich picture on lung cancer. Available at http://be.macmillan.org.uk/Downloads/Therichpictureonpeoplewithlungcancer.pdf (accessed on 12 September 2012).

What will happen?

The type of lung cancer you have, and how far it had spread when it was found, will affect what happens to you. The kind of treatment you have and the way you decide to live with your cancer will also make a difference.

Without treatment, lung cancer is likely to spread.

Many people with lung cancer are diagnosed only when the disease is advanced and it has already spread either to tissues nearby or to more distant parts of the body. If you have been diagnosed when your cancer is still within the lung, your chances of living longer are better.[6]Cancer Research UK. Statistics and outlook for lung cancer. March 2011. Available at: http://cancerhelp.cancerresearchuk.org/type/lung-cancer/treatment/statistics-and-outlook-for-lung-cancer (accessed on 12 September 2012).

The outlook for people with lung cancer is better than it used to be. For people with non-small-cell lung cancer that has not spread outside the lung, more than half are likely to be alive five years after being diagnosed. Even people with more advanced lung cancer may live for at least a year after being diagnosed. A number of people live for a normal life-span after being diagnosed with lung cancer.

Treatment options

Active monitoring: you do not have immediate treatment. Instead, you are regularly checked to monitor the growth of the tumour and in case you get symptoms or any symptoms you already have get worse. This approach may be used if the treatments available are risky for you, or if the diagnosis of lung cancer is not certain. If your symptoms do get worse, your doctor may recommend treatment.

Radical radiotherapy: your doctor may suggest that you have an intensive course of radiotherapy, to try to destroy the cancer cells. This is called radical radiotherapy. It is sometimes offered to people who have illnesses such as heart or other lung disease, because the risk of having surgery is higher for them. One other alternative is radiofrequency ablation (the use of heat to destroy cancerous tissue) but this is not often used. It is not a type of radiotherapy.

Surgery: if you have a small tumour that hasn't spread, you'll probably be offered surgery to remove it. This is likely to give you the best chance of living longer.

Surgery with or without chemotherapy: doctors may offer you treatment with anti-cancer drugs (adjuvant chemotherapy) after an operation if you have lung cancer at certain early stages, and depending on the size of the tumour. Depending on your stage of cancer, the combination of surgery followed with chemotherapy may help you live longer.

  • What type of lung cancer do I have?
  • How far has it spread?
  • Do I need more investigations?

  • What will happen if I decide not to have treatment?

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT WATCHFUL WAITING

What is active monitoring?

If you decide to have active monitoring, you do not have immediate treatment. Instead, you are regularly checked by the medical team in case you develop symptoms or your symptoms get worse. 

You don't have treatment until you decide you want it. If your symptoms get worse or you get new symptoms, your doctor might recommend that you start a suitable treatment. You should let your medical team know straight away if your condition gets worse.

Who can have active monitoring?

Anyone can choose active monitoring, as an alternative to immediate active treatment. For some people, immediate active treatment might be too risky or your doctor might not be sure about your diagnosis, and they may suggest active monitoring instead. If you think active monitoring might be right for you, you can discuss it with your doctor. 

How effective is active monitoring in treating lung cancer?

Active monitoring does not itself involve treatment, so there are no effects on the cancer. Your cancer is likely to continue to grow. Active monitoring does not help you live longer. Your symptoms may continue to get worse. 

What are the main effects of active monitoring on what you can do and how you feel?

Active monitoring has no effect on what you can do. It may have an effect on how you feel.

If you are able to carry on with the level of symptoms you have and you do not want treatment at the moment, you may feel this is the best situation for now.

If you think that perhaps you really ought to be having treatment, active monitoring could make you more anxious or depressed. Supportive care is given with active monitoring, often by nurses or other members of the health care team. This can reduce symptoms such as anxiety, depression, and breathlessness.

How might active monitoring affect my life?

Active monitoring in itself will have no effect on your life. It allows you to carry on for the moment without having to undergo treatment in hospital. If knowing that you have cancer that is not being treated worries you, then active monitoring may cause extra anxiety.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT RADIOTHERAPY

What is radical radiotherapy?

Radiotherapy (radiation treatment) uses high-energy rays to damage cancer cells and stop them from growing and dividing. Radiotherapy affects cancer cells only in the treated area. We are looking in this section at radical radiotherapy, which is intended to cure lung cancer.

Some people have chemotherapy before, or at the same time as, radiotherapy. [7]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. The diagnosis and treatment of lung cancer (update). April 2011. Available at: http://guidance.nice.org.uk/cg121 (accessed on 30 November 2012). Your doctor may suggest you have chemotherapy with radiotherapy if you are fit enough, and depending on the stage of the cancer and the size of the tumour.

Standard radiation therapy usually involves visiting the hospital or clinic on a daily (outpatient) basis for about four to six weeks.

Another type of radiotherapy is known as CHART (continuous hyperfractionated accelerated radiotherapy). CHART uses smaller doses of radiation than standard radiotherapy, but treatment is given three times a day over about two weeks. This means that the total dose is the same or slightly less than what you get with standard radiotherapy.

Some people have more intensive radiotherapy, using high-dose radiation, targeted very precisely to the part of the body affected by the cancer. This is called stereotactic radiotherapy. It is only suitable for small tumours in certain locations. You may hear this type of radiotherapy called SBRT, which stands for stereotactic body radiotherapy, or SABR, which stands for stereotactic ablative radiotherapy.

Radiotherapy doesn’t hurt, and it doesn’t make you radioactive.

Who can have radical radiotherapy?

Radiotherapy is often used for people with lung cancer where surgery is thought to be too risky, or for people who do not want surgery. 

How effective is radical radiotherapy in treating lung cancer?

The main effects of radical radiotherapy are to get rid of the cancer and help you to live longer.

In one group of people with stage 1 or 2 cancer (localised cancer) who weren't able to have surgery or chose not to have surgery, 37 in 100 people who had CHART (continuous hyperfractionated accelerated radiotherapy) lived longer than two years after treatment. [8]Rowell NP, Williams C. Radical radiotherapy for stage I/II non-small cell lung cancer in patients not sufficiently fit for or declining surgery (medically inoperable) (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

Stereotactic radiotherapy, also known as SBRT, delivers small doses precisely to the tumour and is suitable for small tumours. In one group of people with stage 1 or 2 cancers, about 65 in 100 people who had this treatment lived longer than two years.[9]Brock J, Ashley S, Bedford J, et al. Review of hypofractionated small volume radiotherapy for early-stage non-small cell lung cancer. Clinical Oncology. 2008; 20: 666-676.

What are the main effects of radical radiotherapy on what you can do and how you feel?

There have not been many studies looking at the specific effects of radical radiotherapy on what you can do and how you feel.[8]Rowell NP, Williams C. Radical radiotherapy for stage I/II non-small cell lung cancer in patients not sufficiently fit for or declining surgery (medically inoperable) (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. However, you might need to travel to hospital every day for your treatment. Some people stay in hospital, or nearby, if they are having more intensive radiotherapy treatment.

What are the main side effects of radical radiotherapy?

You are likely to feel tired and run down while having radiotherapy treatment. Tiredness may last for a few weeks after the end of treatment.

It is quite common to get a cough during or after radiotherapy for lung cancer.

You may lose hair on your chest during radiotherapy. Sometimes hair loss after radiotherapy is permanent. You will not lose any hair from your head.

Radiotherapy does not hurt, but some people get chest pain within 24 hours of having the treatment. It will go away by itself.

Radiotherapy can also cause itchy skin in the area being treated.

Some side effects from radiotherapy begin months after your treatment has finished.  It is possible that your breathing may get worse. This happens to between 1 in 100 and 30 in 100 people.[9]Brock J, Ashley S, Bedford J, et al. Review of hypofractionated small volume radiotherapy for early-stage non-small cell lung cancer. Clinical Oncology. 2008; 20: 666-676.

Another long-term side effect is narrowing of the gullet (oesophagus), which can make swallowing painful or difficult.[9]Brock J, Ashley S, Bedford J, et al. Review of hypofractionated small volume radiotherapy for early-stage non-small cell lung cancer. Clinical Oncology. 2008; 20: 666-676. This soreness usually gets better by itself, a few weeks after the treatment has finished. Eating softer food usually helps. If needed, the soreness can be relieved by a small procedure to stretch the narrowing and allow food to pass through easily again.

Radiotherapy can cause swelling in your lungs (pneumonitis). This can affect around 7 in 100 people.[9]Brock J, Ashley S, Bedford J, et al. Review of hypofractionated small volume radiotherapy for early-stage non-small cell lung cancer. Clinical Oncology. 2008; 20: 666-676.

There is a very rare chance of spinal cord damage. 

What can be done to reduce side effects of treatment and continuing cancer symptoms?

Radiotherapy can cause swelling in your lungs (pneumonitis). This might make you feel breathless. Your doctor can prescribe you medicines, such as antibiotics or corticosteroids, to reduce the swelling and to help you feel less breathless.

The cancer itself can also make you feel breathless. If the tumour has caused a blockage in the airways, this can be relieved either by having radiotherapy or by inserting a tube called a stent to open the airway. The breathlessness might be caused by a build-up of fluid around the lungs (called pleural effusion). This fluid can be removed (drained).

Programmes involving explanation and breathing exercises, led by a nurse or therapist, can help reduce anxiety and improve breathing ability. They can also help manage or reduce other symptoms such as pain.[14]Rueda JR, Solà I, Pascual A, et al. Non-invasive interventions for improving well-being and quality of life in patients with lung cancer. In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. [15]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. The diagnosis and treatment of lung cancer (update). April 2011. Available at: http://guidance.nice.org.uk/cg121 (accessed on 11 September 2012).

Exercise programmes can stop the decline in physical strength that is common among people with advanced lung cancer.[16]Granger CL, McDonald CF, Berney S, et al. Exercise intervention to improve exercise capacity and health related quality of life for patients with non-small cell lung cancer: a systematic review. Lung Cancer. 2011; 72: 139-153.

How might radical radiotherapy affect my life?

The aim of the treatment is to cure the cancer.

If your lung cancer treatment leaves you with side effects, you may feel anxious and worried. You may need additional support to help you deal with the side effects of treatment.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT SURGERY + CHEMOTHERAPY

What is surgery with or without chemotherapy?

If you have surgery for lung cancer, the surgeon aims to remove all the cancer from your lung. The type of operation you have depends on how far your cancer has spread and how fit you are.

There are three main types of operation that can be used to remove a tumour from the lungs.

  • A small part of the lung is removed. This is a wedge resection or segmentectomy. This type of operation isn't done very often.
  • One lobe of the lung is removed. This is called a lobectomy. This is the most common type of operation. When two lobes are removed it's called a bilobectomy.
  • The whole lung is removed. This is called a pneumonectomy. This is done if your doctor thinks that some cancer cells have spread to another part of the lung.
  • Some types of lung cancer operations are done using a type of keyhole surgery called video-assisted thorascopic surgery. You might hear this be called VATS.

Some people have chemotherapy after surgery, if their cancer is at a stage where chemotherapy may help. In a few cases, your doctor might suggest chemotherapy before surgery to try to shrink the tumour. Not everyone with localised lung cancer needs to have chemotherapy. Your doctor will discuss whether it might be helpful for you.

Chemotherapy drugs are used to kill cancer cells. They are usually given as injections into your vein or as a drip (also called an IV or an intravenous infusion). Some come as tablets. You usually have to go to hospital to have injections and drips, as an outpatient.

Who can have surgery with or without chemotherapy?

Anyone can have surgery if their cancer is at an early stage, they have no other major health problems, and they are physically strong enough.

Some patients cannot have surgery because removing the cancerous part of the lung wouldn't leave them with enough remaining lung to breathe well enough. You might hear this called 'lung capacity'. Other reasons why you might not be fit enough to have surgery include heart problems, or having health too poor to withstand a major operation.

Not everyone with localised lung cancer needs to have chemotherapy. It depends if their tumour is at the right stage for chemotherapy to be helpful, and whether they want to have chemotherapy. Your doctor will discuss whether it might be helpful for you. 

How effective is surgery with or without chemotherapy in treating lung cancer?

The main effect of having surgery for lung cancer is to remove the cancer and improve your chances of living longer. 

Having chemotherapy as well as surgery may help some people live longer than just having surgery. This is called adjuvant chemotherapy.[15]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. The diagnosis and treatment of lung cancer (update). April 2011. Available at: http://guidance.nice.org.uk/cg121 (accessed on 11 September 2012). [18]Brown J, Thorpe H, Napp V, et al. Assessment of quality of life in the supportive care setting of the big lung trial in non-small-cell lung cancer. Journal of Clinical Oncology. 2005; 23: 7417-7427. About 5 in 100 more people live for at least another five years if they have chemotherapy as well as surgery than if they have surgery without chemotherapy.[19]Pignon JP, Tribodet H, Scagliotti GV, et al. Lung adjuvant cisplatin evaluation: a pooled analysis by the LACE Collaborative Group. Journal of Clinical Oncology. 2008; 26: 3552-3559. You can discuss with your doctor whether having chemotherapy after surgery is likely to help you live longer. 

The chances of the cancer coming back or spreading are also reduced slightly after surgery followed by chemotherapy.

What are the main effects of this treatment on what you can do and how you feel?

This treatment is intended to cure your cancer and help you to live longer. It's not intended to relieve symptoms, such as pain and breathlessness. Many people with early lung cancer don't have symptoms. If you do have symptoms, this treatment is likely to help them. This does not happen for everyone. Surgery removes part of your lung, which means that some people are more breathless after surgery for cancer. 

What are the main side effects of surgery with or without chemotherapy?

Surgery side effects

Most people feel breathless and have pain after surgery for lung cancer. It is a major operation and involves opening up the rib cage.

Your health care team can prescribe medicines called painkillers to help control pain after surgery. These can be taken as tablets or given through a vein in your arm (as a drip). Some people will continue to feel pain for a few years after surgery.

Breathlessness can be caused by fluid around the lung, in an area called the pleural space. Breathlessness affects about 25 in 100 people after surgery. Between 5 and 7 in 100 people get pneumonia after surgery. For 8 in 100 people, these problems mean they will need another operation.[20]Van Schil P, Van Meerbeeck J, Kramer G, et al. Morbidity and mortality in the surgery arm of EORTC 08941 trial. European Respiratory Journal. 2005; 26: 192-197.

The operation can also cause heart problems. The heart may beat too fast, too slowly, or erratically. This happens to between 13 and 27 in 100 people. Heart problems like this may be long-lasting. They can be treated by drugs.[20]Van Schil P, Van Meerbeeck J, Kramer G, et al. Morbidity and mortality in the surgery arm of EORTC 08941 trial. European Respiratory Journal. 2005; 26: 192-197.

Another problem after surgery is infection of the wound site. This can be serious and can cause death, but this is very rare.

Other side effects include difficulty in swallowing and feeling sick, which are usually short term. 

Chemotherapy side effects

Different types of chemotherapy can cause different side effects. Your specialist can tell you more about the specific side effects that might be caused by each type of chemotherapy.

Feeling sick is a very common side effect of chemotherapy. Your doctor can prescribe medicines called anti-emetics to help with this. Another side effect of chemotherapy can be anaemia (reduction of red blood cells and white blood cells). This can make you weak, tired, and prone to infection. Your doctor will monitor you carefully for signs of infection.

Other side effects include hair loss, constipation, and diarrhoea. You might feel tingling in your fingers and toes. Chemotherapy may also make you feel very tired.

What can be done to reduce side effects of treatment and continuing cancer symptoms?

Breathlessness is a common result of lung cancer and of a number of its treatments. It can sometimes be reduced by further specialised radiotherapy treatment, or removal of fluid in and around the lungs.

Programmes involving explanation and breathing exercises, led by a nurse or therapist, can help reduce anxiety and improve breathing ability. They can also help manage or reduce other symptoms such as pain.[15]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. The diagnosis and treatment of lung cancer (update). April 2011. Available at: http://guidance.nice.org.uk/cg121 (accessed on 11 September 2012). [14]Rueda JR, Solà I, Pascual A, et al. Non-invasive interventions for improving well-being and quality of life in patients with lung cancer. In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

Exercise programmes can stop the decline in physical strength that is common among people with advanced lung cancer.[16]Granger CL, McDonald CF, Berney S, et al. Exercise intervention to improve exercise capacity and health related quality of life for patients with non-small cell lung cancer: a systematic review. Lung Cancer. 2011; 72: 139-153.

How might surgery with or without chemotherapy affect my life?

The aim of the treatment is to cure the cancer.

If your lung cancer treatment leaves you with side effects, you may feel anxious and worried. You may need additional support to help you deal with the side effects of treatment.
