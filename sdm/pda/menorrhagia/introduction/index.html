STEP 5: MY DECISION

Our Decision Support telephone service is here to assist and guide you through the Shared Decision Making process.

The Decision Support team is made of professional Health Coaches who are clinicians, commonly nurses, who have undertaken additional training to become a Health Coach.

The Health Coaches will assist you by:

  • Providing you with information
  • Helping you to understand your condition
  • Recognise what is important to you regarding the outcome of treatment
  • Identifying potential solutions
  • Encourage you in discussing options with your family
  • Transferring skills which will assist you in using the information and resources available to you
  • Support you in building confidence in discussing your choice with your doctor
  • You may find that this can be achieved in one telephone call with a Health Coach however; if further calls are required to support you in reaching your decision the Health Coach will schedule these with you.

Important Things To Note

  • A Health Coach does not know which treatment option is best for you.
  • The Health Coach does not have information about your medical history.
  • You will still need to talk over your decision and reasons for your decision with your doctor or nurse.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT MONITORING

What is monitoring?

Monitoring involves havng no immediate treatment. Women can have regular checks to see if their symptoms change, but they can choose not to have any treatment unless they get new symptoms or their symptoms get worse. Women can monitor symptoms themselves (self-monitoring) or with their healthcare team.

Who can have monitoring?

Anyone can choose to have monitoring. Some women do not want to have any treatment or would like to wait for a while before starting treatment.

What are the main effects of monitoring on heavy menstrual bleeding?

It's difficult to predict what will happen with monitoring. Some women find their symptoms get better without having treatment. [1]Shapley M, Blagojevic M, Jordan KP, et al. The spontaneous resolution of heavy menstrual bleeding in the perimenopausal years. BJOG: An International Journal of Obstetrics & Gynaecology. 2012; 119: 545-553.

In one group of women with heavy menstrual bleeding aged from 40 to 54 years who chose not to have treatment:[1]Shapley M, Blagojevic M, Jordan KP, et al. The spontaneous resolution of heavy menstrual bleeding in the perimenopausal years. BJOG: An International Journal of Obstetrics & Gynaecology. 2012; 119: 545-553.

  • 19 in 100 women had no symptoms after six months
  • 12 in 100 women had no symptoms after one year
  • 10 in 100 women had no symptoms after two years.

Fewer women went without any symptoms of heavy menstrual bleeding the longer they went without treatment.[1]Shapley M, Blagojevic M, Jordan KP, et al. The spontaneous resolution of heavy menstrual bleeding in the perimenopausal years. BJOG: An International Journal of Obstetrics & Gynaecology. 2012; 119: 545-553.

In one group of women aged 40 to 44, 4 in 100 women said their bleeding became lighter within two years, without treatment. In a group of women aged 50 to 54, 25 in 100 women said their bleeding became lighter within two years, without treatment.[1]Shapley M, Blagojevic M, Jordan KP, et al. The spontaneous resolution of heavy menstrual bleeding in the perimenopausal years. BJOG: An International Journal of Obstetrics & Gynaecology. 2012; 119: 545-553.

What are the main effects of monitoring on what you can do and how you feel?

We don't know if monitoring will affect what women can do and how they feel. If symptoms don't improve, some women are unable to do normal everyday things. Some women find heavy menstrual bleeding can be upsetting and stressful.

What are the main side effects of monitoring?

Monitoring is unlikely to cause unwanted effects. If symptoms don't improve or get worse, this can affect a woman's daily life and what they can do.

How might monitoring affect my life?

How much monitoring will affect a woman's life will depend on how much the symptoms bother them. Monitoring might not affect daily life much. Women can have regular GP appointments to check on the symptoms.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT HORMONE TREATMENT

What is hormone treatment?

Hormone treatments include tablets (the pill), injections, or a small device fitted inside the womb (the hormonal coil). These treatments reduce menstrual bleeding.

The hormonal coil

The hormonal coil is a small plastic device that is fitted inside the womb where it slowly releases a hormone called progestogen. This stops the lining of the womb from getting thicker. The hormonal coil also stops pregnancy while it is fitted. It is also known as the levonorgestrel-releasing intrauterine system (LNG-IUS for short), or Mirena.

Tablets

Taking hormone tablets can reduce the build up of blood in the lining of the womb. If a woman chooses to take tablets, they can have either a combined tablet containing the hormones oestrogen and progesterone, or a tablet containing the hormone progesterone on its own. These medicines are often used to prevent pregnancy, but they can also help with heavy and painful periods. The combined tablet is also called the combined oral contraceptive pill (COC). The combined pill is usually taken for 21 out of the 28 days of the menstrual cycle. If progesterone alone is taken, it is during days 5 to 26 of the menstrual cycle.

Injections

Progesterone can be taken as an injection for heavy menstrual bleeding. The injection is called Depo-Provera. Injections need to be administered every 12 weeks. Having progesterone this way will reduce the chances of getting pregnant.

GnRH analogues

There are injections of another type of hormone treatment, called gonadotropin-releasing hormone analogues, or GnRH analogues, to help heavy periods. GnRH analogues can also be taken as a nasal spray. These medicines are usually used to thin the lining of the womb before having surgery to remove it.

Who can have hormone treatment?

Anyone who is not planning on getting pregnant while having treatment, and who has heavy menstrual bleeding that is not caused by large fibroids or unusual growths in the womb, can have hormone treatment for heavy menstrual bleeding.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012).

Doctors recommend that teenagers and women who are older than 40 take progesterone in the form of tablets.[6]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Long-acting reversible contraception. Octoebr 2005. Clinical guideline 30. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG30 (accessed on 5 December 2012).

Treatment with gonadotropin-releasing hormone analogues will not be available unless other treatments have been tried and they haven't helped, or if an operation on the womb is not an option. Doctors usually use this treatment to thin the lining of the womb before having surgery to remove it. This treatment can't be taken for longer than six months at a time.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012).

What are the main effects of hormone treatment on heavy menstrual bleeding?

Different types of hormone treatment have different effects on menstrual bleeding. This is what we know:[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012).

  • The hormonal coil can reduce the amount of blood lost during a period by between 71% and 96%. It may take up to six months before having a noticeable improvement.
  • The combined pill can reduce the amount of blood lost by around 43%.
  • Progesterone tablets can decrease the amount of blood lost by around 83%.
  • We don't know if having progesterone injections can help heavy menstrual bleeding.
  • Having injections of GnRH analogues can shrink fibroids and reduce the amount of blood lost during a period. Most studies have looked at having injections of GnRH analogues before having surgery. Not many studies have looked at whether having injections of GnRH analogues on their own helps heavy menstrual bleeding.
What are the main effects of hormonal treatments on what you can do and how you feel?

We don't know if having hormone treatments will affect what a woman can do and how she feels. Hormone treatments can help by reducing the amount of blood lost during a period.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012). Some women find this means they are able to do more everyday things.

What are the main side effects of hormonal treatment?

Having hormone treatment for heavy menstrual bleeding can cause unwanted effects.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012).

The hormonal coil
Having a hormonal coil fitted can cause irregular bleeding that may last for six months or more, breast tenderness, acne, or headaches. These side effects happen to around one in every 100 women who has a hormonal coil fitted. It's possible to have a perforated womb while having a hormonal coil fitted. This doesn't happen as often. This affects around one in every 10,000 women.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012).

Combined pill
Taking the pill can cause mood changes, headaches, nausea, swelling caused by a build-up of fluid in the body (called oedema) or breast tenderness. These side effects happen to around one in every 100 women who takes the pill. Sometimes it's possible to have deep vein thrombosis, a stroke, or a heart attack. This doesn't happen as often. These side effects affect around one in every 10,000 women.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012).

Progesterone tablets
Taking progesterone tables can cause weight gain, bloating, breast tenderness, headaches, or acne. These side effects happen to around one in 100 women who take progesterone tablets. It's also possible to have depression. This doesn't happen as often. This affects around one in every 10,000 women.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012).

Progesterone injections
Having progesterone injections can cause weight gain, irregular bleeding, missed periods, bloating, oedema, or breast tenderness. These side effects happen to around one in 100 women who have progesterone injections. It's also possible to have weaker bones after stopping progesterone injections. These doesn't happen as often. This affects around one in every 1,000 women.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012).

GnRH analogues
Having injections of GnRH analogues can cause weight hot flushes, increased sweating and dryness in the vagina. These side effects happen to around one in 100 women who have injections of GnRH analogues. It's also possible to have weaker bones after having injections for longer than six months. These doesn't happen as often. This affects around one in every 1,000 women.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012).

How might hormonal treatment affect my life?

Hormone treatments have variable contraceptive effects. This means having hormone treatment may affect the chances of getting pregnant.

If hormone tablets are chosen, it is important to take the medicines on the correct days to have the best chance of improving the symptoms.

If progesterone injections, GnRH analogues, or the hormonal coil is chosen, they need to be administered by a GP or at hospital. The progesterone injections needs to be administered once every 12 weeks. GnRH treatment can last up to six months. Hormonal coils need to be replaced every five years.

Having medical treatment may affect some aspects of a woman's sex life. In one group of women with heavy menstrual bleeding, having a hormonal coil fitted didn't make any difference to how happy they were with their sex life or how likely they were to have sexual problems. But after five years of treatment, their partners were less satisfied with their sex lives.[16]Halmesmaki K, Hurskainen R, Teperi J, et al. The effect of hysterectomy or levonorgestrel-releasing intrauterine system on sexual functioning among women with menorrhagia: a 5-year randomised controlled trial. BJOG: An International Journal of Obstetrics & Gynaecology. 2007; 114: 563-568.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT NON-HORMONE TREATMENT

What are non-hormone treatments?

Non-hormone treatments are tablets that are taken orally to help heavy menstrual bleeding. They don't contain hormones. Non-hormone treatments won't affect a woman's chances of getting pregnant.

  • Tranexamic acid, which works by helping the blood in the womb to clot.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012). Three or four tablets are taken for the few days after the period has started.
  • Mefenamic acid, which is a type of painkiller called a non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drug (NSAID). This can help with painful periods as well as heavy menstrual bleeding.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012). They are taken just before, or from the start of the period until the bleeding has stopped to lighten the blood flow.

A doctor might suggest taking either tranexamic acid or mefenamic acid, or taking both treatments together.

Who can have non-hormone treatment?

Anyone with heavy menstrual bleeding, that is not caused by large fibroids or unusual growths in the womb, can have non-hormone treatment for heavy menstrual bleeding.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012). If painful periods accompany heavy menstrual bleeding, NSAIDs can be used first.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012). A doctor might suggest taking either tranexamic acid or mefenamic acid, or taking both treatments together.

Tranexamic acid and mefenamic acid are available on prescription from a GP. Tranexamic acid is also available to buy over the counter at a pharmacy.

What are the main effects of non-hormone treatment on heavy menstrual bleeding?

Non-hormone treatments can lighten periods.

  • Tranexamic tablets can reduce the amount of blood lost during a period by 29 to 58%.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012). Around 60 in 100 women who take tranexamic acid have lighter periods.[22]Working Party for Guidelines for the Management of Heavy Menstrual Bleeding. An evidence-based guideline for the management of heavy menstrual bleeding. New Zealand Medical Journal. 1999; 112: 174-177.
  • Mefenamic acid can reduce the amount of blood lost during a period by 20% to 49%.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012). Around 70 in 100 women who take mefenamic acid have lighter periods.[22]Working Party for Guidelines for the Management of Heavy Menstrual Bleeding. An evidence-based guideline for the management of heavy menstrual bleeding. New Zealand Medical Journal. 1999; 112: 174-177.

In one group of women who had heavy menstrual bleeding, women who had non-hormone treatment for five years had less pain and less bleeding than before they had treatment.[25]Cooper KG, Jack SA, Parkin DE, et al. Five-year follow up of women randomised to medical management or transcervical resection of the endometrium for heavy menstrual loss: clinical and quality of life outcomes. BJOG: An International Journal of Obstetrics & Gynaecology. 2001; 108: 1222-1228.

What are the main effects of non-hormone treatment on what you can do and how you feel?

Non-hormone treatment can affect what you can do and how you feel.

Non-hormone treatments can help by reducing the amount of blood lost during a period. Some women may find this means they are able to do more everyday things.

In one group of women who had heavy menstrual bleeding, women who had non-hormone treatment for five years felt better physically and emotionally, had more energy and felt less tired, and were less bothered by pain, than before they started treatment.[25]Cooper KG, Jack SA, Parkin DE, et al. Five-year follow up of women randomised to medical management or transcervical resection of the endometrium for heavy menstrual loss: clinical and quality of life outcomes. BJOG: An International Journal of Obstetrics & Gynaecology. 2001; 108: 1222-1228.

In one group of women who had heavy menstrual bleeding, women who had tranexamic acid had a better social life and sex life than women who took a dummy pill (a placebo).[27]Preston JT, Cameron IT, Adams EJ, et al. Comparative study of tranexamic acid and norethisterone in the treatment of ovulatory menorrhagia. British Journal of Obstetrics & Gynaecology. 1995; 102: 401-406.

What are the main side effects of non-hormone treatment?

Having non-hormone treatment can cause unwanted effects.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012).

Tranexamic acid
Taking tranexamic acid can cause indigestion or headaches. This affects around one in 1,000 women.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012).

Mefenamic acid
Taking mefenamic acid can cause indigestion or diarrhoea. This affects around one in 1,000 women. It's possible to get stomach ulcers, bleeding, or swelling in the stomach or abdomen, or problems for women with asthma. This doesn't happen as often. This affects around one in 10,000 women.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012).

How might non-hormone treatment affect my life?

Having non-hormone treatment is not likely to affect a woman's life much.

Choosing to take non-hormone tablets means it's important to remember to take the medicines on the correct days to have the best chance of improving symptoms.

Some women find it bothersome to take several tablets a day.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT SURGERY OR PROCEDURE OTHER THAN HYSTERECTOMY

What is surgery or a procedure other than hysterectomy?

Surgery treatment for heavy menstrual bleeding involves having an operation to remove the womb lining (the endometrium), or to block the blood supply to the womb.

An operation to remove the lining of the womb is called endometrial ablation, or endometrial resection. This operation can be done in a number of ways. During the operation a surgeon may insert a hysteroscope (which looks a bit like a very small telescope) into the vagina and through the cervix so they can see inside the womb. They then use tiny instruments to remove the lining of the womb. They may use:

  • A heated wire that burns away the lining of the womb. This is called transcervical endometrial resection.
  • A heated ball that burns away the lining of the womb. This is called rollerball ablation.
  • A laser beam that cuts away the lining of the womb. This is called laser ablation.
  • A special device that carries an electric current that burns away the lining of the womb (an electrode). This is called vaporising electrode ablation.

The doctor may suggest taking drugs that thin the womb lining before having one of these operations.

There are other ways of doing this operation allowing surgeons to remove the lining of the womb without using a hysteroscope. Instead they may use one of the following methods:

  • They may insert a sheath containing a special device that carries an electric current that burns away the lining of the womb (an electrode) which is placed through the cervix. This sheath is pulled back to allow the electrode to expand and fill the uterus. A small electric current is then passed through the electrode into the uterus to remove the lining of the womb, after which the electrode is removed. This is called impedance-controlled bipolar radiofrequency ablation.
  • They may put a balloon into the womb through your cervix and then fill it with fluid. The fluid is heated up and left in place for about eight minutes to kill the womb lining. This is called thermal uterine balloon therapy.
  • They may insert a small probe through the cervix and into the womb. The probe is moved over the lining of the womb to remove it. This is called microwave ablation.
  • They may insert a heated salt solution into the uterus to remove the lining of the womb. This is called free fluid thermal endometrial ablation.

The operations that don't use a hysteroscope do not require a general anaesthetic during surgery. Local anaesthetics are usually used instead. This means being awake during the operation, but the area will be numbed.[31]Lethaby A, Hickey M, Garry R. Endometrial resection/ablation techniques for heavy menstrual bleeding (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. Available at: http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/14651858.CD001501.pub3/full (accessed on 15 November 2012).

If heavy periods are caused by growths in the womb called fibroids, an operation called uterine artery embolisation can be chosen.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012). During this operation a surgeon inserts a small tube into the groin so that small plastic beads can be passed into the blood vessels that supply blood to the fibroids. This reduces the blood supply to the womb and causes the fibroid to shrink. This should help make periods lighter.

The doctor may suggest drugs that thin the womb lining before having one of these operations.

Who can have surgery or a procedure other than hysterectomy?

Anyone who has heavy menstrual bleeding that affects their quality of life, who does not want to get pregnant after having treatment, can have surgery.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012). Having endometrial ablation to remove the lining of your womb may prevent some women from being able to get pregnant in future.

Having heavy menstrual bleeding that affects quality of life, and is likely to be caused by large fibroids (bigger than three centimetres), can be helped with a different type of operation, called uterine artery embolisation.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012). This type of operation might affect a woman's ability to get pregnant in the future. Doctors don't know for certain.[35]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Uterine artery embolisation for fibroids. November 2010. Interventional procedure guidance 367. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/ipg367 (accessed on 30 January 2013).

What are the main effects of this treatment on heavy menstrual bleeding?

Around 90 in every 100 women who have their womb lining removed using endometrial ablation have lighter periods or no periods afterwards.[36]Lethaby A, Shepperd S, Cooke I, et al. Endometrial resection and ablation versus hysterectomy for heavy menstrual bleeding (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. [22]Working Party for Guidelines for the Management of Heavy Menstrual Bleeding. An evidence-based guideline for the management of heavy menstrual bleeding. New Zealand Medical Journal. 1999; 112: 174-177.

Women with heavy menstrual bleeding who have newer types of operation are just as happy with the results as those who have older ones.[38]Lethaby A, Hickey M, Garry R. Endometrial destruction techniques for heavy menstrual bleeding (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

All the different types of operation to remove the lining of the womb seem to work equally well.[39]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Impedance-controlled bipolar radiofrequency ablation for menorrhagia. December 2004. Interventional procedure guidance 104. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/ipg104 (accessed on 7 November 2012).

In one group of women who had thermal balloon ablation, women were less bothered by pain two years after treatment than before they had their operation.[40]Sesti F, Ruggeri V, Pietropolli A, et al. Thermal balloon ablation versus laparoscopic supracervical hysterectomy for the surgical treatment of heavy menstrual bleeding: a randomized study. Journal of Obstetrics & Gynaecology Research 2011; 37: 1650-1657.

What are the main effects of this treatment on what you can do and how you feel?

Most women with heavy menstrual bleeding who have surgery to remove their womb lining feel happy with the results.

In one group of women who had thermal balloon ablation, women had a better quality of life, a better social life, and felt better two years after treatment than before they had their operation.[40]Sesti F, Ruggeri V, Pietropolli A, et al. Thermal balloon ablation versus laparoscopic supracervical hysterectomy for the surgical treatment of heavy menstrual bleeding: a randomized study. Journal of Obstetrics & Gynaecology Research 2011; 37: 1650-1657.

In one group of women who had endometrial ablation, most were satisfied with the results, whatever type of operation they had. Between 87 and 89 in 100 women were satisfied with surgery to remove their womb lining.[42]Middleton LJ, Champaneria R, Daniels JP, et al. Hysterectomy, endometrial destruction, and levonorgestrel releasing intrauterine system (Mirena) for heavy menstrual bleeding: systematic review and meta-analysis of data from individual patients. BMJ. 2010; 341: 3929.

 

What are the main side effects of this treatment?

Having surgery for heavy menstrual bleeding can cause unwanted effects.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012).

Between 10 in 100 and 40 in 100 women who have their womb lining removed will need another operation because their symptoms have come back.[22]Working Party for Guidelines for the Management of Heavy Menstrual Bleeding. An evidence-based guideline for the management of heavy menstrual bleeding. New Zealand Medical Journal. 1999; 112: 174-177.

Having endometrial ablation can cause a vaginal discharge, period pain, and cramps. These side effects affect around one in every 100 women who has this type of operation. It's possible to get an infection after the operation. This affects around one in 1,000 women. Endometrial ablation can cause damage to the womb (a perforated womb). This happens less often. This affects around one in 10,000 women.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012).

How might this treatment affect my life?

Having surgery will mean taking some time off work or time away from normal daily life to have the operation and to recover.

Most women should be able to go home from hospital on the same day as having an operation to remove the womb lining. Most women are able to return to normal activities within two to three weeks.[22]Working Party for Guidelines for the Management of Heavy Menstrual Bleeding. An evidence-based guideline for the management of heavy menstrual bleeding. New Zealand Medical Journal. 1999; 112: 174-177.

Having an operation to remove the lining of the womb may affect the chances of a woman becoming pregnant after the treatment. This effect may be permanent.

Having endometrial ablation leaves women with only a small chance of becoming pregnant after the operation, but it is still possible. If a woman does become pregnant there can be complications. This may not be a suitable treatment for women considering become pregnant in the future. Therefore some form of contraception should be used to prevent the possibility of pregnancy. [5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012).

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT HYSTERECTOMY

What is a hysterectomy?

This is an operation to remove the womb. Sometimes, the cervix, the ovaries, and the fallopian tubes are removed during the same operation. Women can discuss this with their surgeon before the operation.

If a woman chooses to have a hysterectomy, there are several types of operation a surgeon can do:

  • Total hysterectomy. This is where the womb and the neck of the womb (the cervix) are removed.
  • Sub-total hysterectomy. This is where the womb is removed but the cervix is left in place.

If a woman chooses to have a hysterectomy, there are several different ways a surgeon can do the operation:

  • Open surgery. This is where the womb is removed through a cut in the lower part of the abdomen.
  • Keyhole surgery. This is where the womb is removed through several small cuts in the abdomen.
  • Vaginal hysterectomy. This is where the womb is removed through a cut in the top of the vagina.

Women can discuss with their surgeon which operation is best.

Who can have a hysterectomy?

Any woman with heavy menstrual bleeding can choose to have a hysterectomy.

Having a hysterectomy to remove the womb means that periods will stop completely and pregnancy will not be possible after treatment. Hysterectomy is usually only offered after trying other treatments that haven't helped and heavy menstrual bleeding still affects daily life, and if the woman doesn't want to have any periods or have a baby in future.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012).

What are the main effects of a hysterectomy on heavy menstrual bleeding?

Having a hysterectomy can help with heavy menstrual bleeding. Having a hysterectomy means the womb will be removed. Periods will stop completely so there will be no menstrual bleeding any more.[49]Marjoribanks J, Lethaby A, Farquhar C. Surgery versus medical therapy for heavy menstrual bleeding (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

In one group of women who had a keyhole hysterectomy for heavy menstrual bleeding, 18 in 100 still had some light bleeding (spotting) six months after their operation. After two years no women reported any spotting or any menstrual bleeding.[40]Sesti F, Ruggeri V, Pietropolli A, et al. Thermal balloon ablation versus laparoscopic supracervical hysterectomy for the surgical treatment of heavy menstrual bleeding: a randomized study. Journal of Obstetrics & Gynaecology Research 2011; 37: 1650-1657. What are the main side effects of a hysterectomy?

Having a hysterectomy can cause unwanted effects. Some women are more likely to have side effects if they have heavy menstrual bleeding caused by fibroids and have a hysterectomy.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012).

Having a hysterectomy can cause the ovaries to stop working properly.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012). This can cause symptoms such as dryness in the vagina and hot flushes. Some women choose to have treatments that can help these symptoms.

Pain after the operation can be helped with painkillers. Having a hysterectomy can cause an infection. This can affect around one woman in 100.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012). If this happens, antibiotics can help clear the infection.

Some women have bleeding, or problems that affect the urinary tract or bowel. This doesn't happen as often. This can cause problems like needing to go to the toilet more often, or problems controlling passing water.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012).

Some women may have a blood clot after having a hysterectomy. This doesn't happen as often. This can affect around one in 10,000 women.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012).

There is a risk of dying during the operation. This doesn't happen often. This can affect around one woman in 100,000.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012).

Women are less likely to die during the operation or get problems after the operation if they have a hysterectomy through the vagina (a vaginal hysterectomy) than having the operation through a cut in the abdomen (an abdominal hysterectomy).[63]Maresh MJ, Metcalfe MA, McPherson K, et al. The VALUE national hysterectomy study: description of the patients and their surgery. British Journal of Obstetrics and Gynaecology. 2002; 109: 302-312. In one group of women who had an operation to remove their womb, women who had keyhole surgery (a laparoscopic hysterectomy) had the most problems during or after their operation.[63]Maresh MJ, Metcalfe MA, McPherson K, et al. The VALUE national hysterectomy study: description of the patients and their surgery. British Journal of Obstetrics and Gynaecology. 2002; 109: 302-312.

How might having a hysterectomy affect my life?

Hysterectomy operations take place in hospital. Some women have to stay in hospital for a few days and usually go home within a week. In one group of women who had a hysterectomy, most were able to go home within five days.[53]Bhattacharya S, Middleton LJ, Tsourapas A, Hysterectomy, endometrial ablation and mirena for heavy menstrual bleeding: A systematic review of clinical effectiveness and cost-effectiveness analysis. Health Technology Assessment. 2011; 15: 1-252.

Having a hysterectomy means having an operation to remove the womb. This means periods will stop completely and pregnancy is no longer possible after treatment.

Some women find it can take around eight weeks after they have a hysterectomy to get better.[66]Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists. The management of menorrhagia in secondary care. RCOG national evidence-based guideline 5. RCOG Press, London, UK; 1999. During this time, resting as much as possible is advised, and not lifting anything heavy, such as bags of shopping, to allow the body to heal. Some women need someone to help them during this time. Having an open hysterectomy often leaves one or more permanent scars on the lower abdomen. These may fade after some time.

Having a hysterectomy can help with sexual problems. In one group of women who had a hysterectomy, they found they were no more likely to have sexual problems after their operation and some women found their sex lives improved after their operation.[67]Halmesmaki K, Hurskainen R, Teperi J, et al. The effect of hysterectomy or levonorgestrel-releasing intrauterine system on sexual functioning among women with menorrhagia: a 5-year randomised controlled trial . BJOG: An International Journal of Obstetrics & Gynaecology. 2007; 114: 563-568. Doctors usually recommend waiting around four to six weeks after having a hysterectomy before having sex.

Animation about Heavy Menstrual Bleeding (Menorrhagia)

This decision aid is to help you decide what treatment you choose for heavy menstrual bleeding.

The main options for treatment are:

  • Monitoring. This means checking your symptoms to see if they change. You can monitor your symptoms yourself (self-monitoring) or with your healthcare team. You can choose to have other treatments later if you decide you want them.
  • Hormone treatments. These are tablets, injections, or a small device fitted inside your womb (the hormonal coil). These are treatments to reduce your bleeding.
  • Non-hormone treatments. These are medicines that don't contain hormones that reduce your bleeding.
  • Surgery or procedure other than hysterectomy. This is an operation to remove the lining of your womb, or reduce the blood supply to your womb. This makes the blood flow lighter.
  • Hysterectomy. This is an operation to remove your womb. Your ovaries and fallopian tubes may be removed as well.

Your choices depend on many things, including your age, how much your symptoms bother you, what's causing your heavy periods, and whether you want to have children.

The decisions women with heavy menstrual bleeding are making include:

  • My symptoms are getting worse. Should I consider taking medicines or should I try active monitoring first?
  • My symptoms are severe. Should I consider having an operation or should I try medicines first?
  • I want to have some kind of surgery to treat my symptoms. Should I choose to have surgery to remove my womb, or procedures to treat or remove the lining of my womb?

The right choice for you may change over time. This decision aid aims to help you make the right choice for you now, but you may wish to change to a different option in future.

MORE INFORMATION ABOUT HEAVY MENSTRUAL BLEEDING (MENORRHAGIA)

What happens during menstrual bleeding?

Periods start at puberty, usually during the early teens. Periods finish when women reach the menopause, usually in their 50s. The cycle of events that happens in the body that causes a woman to have a period is called a menstrual cycle. Women's menstrual cycles are controlled by their hormones.

Here's what happens every month:

  • Women's hormones make the lining of the womb (the endometrium) thicker and softer, so it's ready for pregnancy.
  • If a woman doesn't become pregnant, the hormone levels start to drop.
  • This drop in hormones causes the womb lining to swell.
  • The extra blood and tissue that made the womb lining thicker comes away. This is called a period.
  • Most girls and women lose about seven to eight teaspoonfuls of blood during a normal period (30 to 40 millilitres).[68]Apgar BS, Kaufman AH, George-Nwogu U, et al. Treatment of menorrhagia. American Family Physician. 2007; 75: 1813-1819.
  • There is more blood in the first few days of a period, and less in the last day or two.

The menstrual cycle usually lasts about 28 days, but it can be shorter or longer.

What is heavy menstrual bleeding?

Heavy menstrual bleeding means that some women bleed more heavily than others during their menstrual periods. The exact amount of bleeding is not definite. Different women have different ideas of what heavy bleeding is. If periods are too heavy for a woman manage, and it affects their quality of life, that counts as heavy menstrual bleeding.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012). 

What are the symptoms of heavy menstrual bleeding?

Some women find it hard to judge whether their periods are heavier than normal. These are some of the things that may suggest their periods are heavy:

  • An unusually high number of tampons or pads are used
  • There is flooding (heavy bleeding) through to clothes or bedding
  • Tampons and pads need to be used together
  • Blood gets onto bedding or clothes despite wearing tampons and pads.

Some women are mainly bothered by the heaviness of the bleeding. Other women have painful periods as well.

Why has this happened to me?

Heavy bleeding is not necessarily a sign that something is wrong. For 40 to 60 in every 100 women with heavy menstrual bleeding, there is no specific cause.[70]Hickey M, Higham J, Fraser IS. Progestogens versus oestrogens and progestogens for irregular uterine bleeding associated with anovulation (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. Heavy menstrual bleeding is more common as women get older.[71]Janssen CA, Scholten PC, Heintz AP. Menorrhagia: a search for epidemiological risk markers. Maturitas. 1997; 28: 19-25.

It's common for women who have heavy menstrual bleeding to have other changes in their womb. But this doesn't mean the changes have definitely caused heavy menstrual bleeding.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012).

For example, heavy menstrual bleeding can be a sign of benign growths in the womb, called fibroids or polyps. Fibroids and polyps are found in around 40 in every 100 women with heavy menstrual bleeding.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012).

Rarely, heavy bleeding may be caused by a blood clotting disorder or can be a symptom of other health problems.[74]NHS Choices. Causes of heavy periods (menorrhagia). Available at http://www.nhs.uk/Conditions/Periods-heavy/Pages/Causes.aspx (accessed 26 November 2012). Certain medicines, like anticoagulant treatment (medicines that stop the blood from clotting) and chemotherapy (a cancer treatment), can also cause heavy menstrual bleeding.[74]NHS Choices. Causes of heavy periods (menorrhagia). Available at http://www.nhs.uk/Conditions/Periods-heavy/Pages/Causes.aspx (accessed 26 November 2012). Having a non-hormonal coil fitted can also increase bleeding in the 6 to 12 months after it is fitted.[76]Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists. The initial management of menorrhagia. Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists, London, UK; 1998.

How do I find out if I have heavy menstrual bleeding?

When menstrual bleeding is too heavy for a woman to manage, they should see their GP.

The GP will ask about symptoms and the menstrual cycle. He or she will also ask if there is any bleeding between cycles or after sexual intercourse and will ask about any medications, including contraceptives, that are being used.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012).

The GP may need to do some physical tests, which could include a pelvic exam. A pelvic exam involves the GP examining the vulva (the external sexual organs) for any external cause of bleeding. He or she will feel the womb from inside the vagina and by pressing on the abdomen. They may use an instrument called a speculum to hold the walls of the vagina open so that they can check the neck of the womb (the cervix) for infections and unusual growths.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012).

Some women may need to have a blood test.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012).

The GP may recommend an ultrasound scan of the womb to help show if there is a specific cause of the heavy bleeding. During the scan a small probe is either inserted into the vagina or placed on the skin of the lower abdomen and pelvic area. The ultrasound probe is attached to a machine that sends out high-frequency sound waves, which reflect off the womb to create a picture.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012).

Depending on the results of these tests, the GP may suggest more specialist tests.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012).

  • The tests may include a hysteroscopy.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012). This means a doctor will use an instrument called a hysteroscope, which is attached to a small camera, to look inside the womb. This test takes place in hospital, but staying overnight is not usual. It takes around 30 minutes. A surgeon will use a speculum to hold the walls of the vagina open, and then insert a hysteroscope into the vagina, through the cervix, and into the womb. Some women have a local anaesthetic, meaning they will be awake but the pelvic area will be numbed. Some women may have a general anaesthetic, meaning they will be asleep throughout the procedure.
  • Some women have a tiny sample of tissue removed from the womb to look for any changes to the cells under a microscope.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012). This is called a biopsy. The doctor will discuss this with the patient first if they think a biopsy is required.
  • Another way specialist doctors can examine the womb is by doing a test called saline infusion sonography. This test usually comes after other tests have been done.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012). A thin tube (a catheter) is put inside the womb through the vagina. A salt water solution is then passed through the catheter to fill the womb. This helps improve the images the doctors get during an ultrasound scan.
  • The doctor may also suggest an MRI scan. This test usually comes after other tests have been done.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012). An MRI uses magnetic fields and radio waves to produce detailed images of the inside of the body. During the scan, the woman lies on a flat bed which is moved into a scanning tube which scans the womb. The scan takes around half an hour and is painless.
How many women have heavy menstrual bleeding?

It's difficult to say how common heavy periods are because many women don't see their doctor about them. About 33 women in 100 describe their periods as heavy.[86]Stabinsky SA, Einstein M, Breen JL. Each year, 5 in 100 women between 30 and 49 go to their doctor with heavy periods.[87]Vessey MP, Villard-Mackintosh L, McPherson K, et al. The epidemiology of hysterectomy: findings in a large cohort study. British Journal of Obstetrics and Gynaecology. 1992; 99: 402-407.

About 20 in 100 women in the UK have their womb removed before they are 60. Heavy periods are the reason for at least 50 in 100 of these operations.[88]Coulter A, Kelland J, Long A. The management of menorrhagia. Effective Health Care Bulletin. 1995; 9: 1-14.

What will happen?

Heavy menstrual bleeding is not always a sign of anything serious. But it can disrupt some women's lives and make them feel miserable. Some women find it gets in the way of their normal activities, social life, and work.[5]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Heavy menstrual bleeding. January 2007. Clinical guideline 44. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG44 (accessed on 6 November 2012).

  • Some women need to take time off work or miss social events when they have their period.
  • They may not feel confident about playing sport or going swimming.
  • They may prefer to stay at home if their periods are very heavy.

Losing a lot of blood can make some women feel tired and weak because their body doesn't have enough red blood cells. Doctors call this condition anaemia. Some women may need treatment, such as iron tablets, to help them make more red cells. This happens to about 66 in 100 women with heavy menstrual bleeding.[90]Duckitt K, Collins S . Menorrhagia. January 2012. Clinical Evidence. (Based on June 2011 search.) Available at http://clinicalevidence.bmj.com/x/systematic-review/0805/overview.html (accessed on 6 November 2012).[91]Lethaby A, Farquhar C. Treatments for heavy menstrual bleeding. BMJ. 2003; 327: 1243-1244.

Some women with heavy menstrual bleeding find their symptoms improve without them having treatment.

Heavy menstrual bleeding may last for only a short time. For example, periods may be heavy at puberty or in the year or so before the menopause.
