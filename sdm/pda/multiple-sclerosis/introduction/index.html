Our Decision Support telephone service is here to assist and guide you through the Shared Decision Making process.

The Decision Support team is made of professional Health Coaches who are clinicians, commonly nurses, who have undertaken additional training to become a Health Coach.

The Health Coaches will assist you by:

  • Providing you with information
  • Helping you to understand your condition
  • Recognise what is important to you regarding the outcome of treatment
  • Identifying potential solutions
  • Encourage you in discussing options with your family
  • Transferring skills which will assist you in using the information and resources available to you
  • Support you in building confidence in discussing your choice with your doctor
  • You may find that this can be achieved in one telephone call with a Health Coach however; if further calls are required to support you in reaching your decision the Health Coach will schedule these with you.

Important Things To Note

  • A Health Coach does not know which treatment option is best for you.
  • The Health Coach does not have information about your medical history.
  • You will still need to talk over your decision and reasons for your decision with your doctor or nurse.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT ACTIVE MONITORING AND SUPPORT

What is active monitoring and support?

If you choose to have active monitoring and support, you may have regular appointments to monitor your condition and any symptoms you are having. These appointments may be face to face, but they can also be done over the phone with an MS specialist nurse.

You may also be offered non-drug treatments such as:

  • physiotherapy
  • occupational therapy
  • psychological support, and
  • self-care support.

You may also be offered drug treatment to treat your symptoms, which are different from the disease modifying drugs covered in this decision aid.  

How often you see an MS professional depends on your overall condition, how you are managing with any symptoms you are having, and how often you have non-drug treatments.

If you are stable without symptoms, or without symptoms that bother you, you may need to have your symptoms checked every six to 12 months.[1]Best Practice. Multiple sclerosis. July 2012. Available at: http://bestpractice.bmj.com/best-practice/monograph/140.html (accessed 28 September 2012).

Who can have active monitoring and support?

Anyone can choose to have active monitoring of symptoms if they would prefer not to have other treatments. 

You may be advised not to take some disease-modifying drugs if you are pregnant, planning to become pregnant, or breastfeeding. Although there is no evidence that the drugs can harm your baby, it's usually recommended that you don't take them while pregnant or breastfeeding until there is more research to say that it is safe. If you are trying for a baby, or pregnant, you may be advised to use active monitoring instead of drug treatment until after your baby is born and if you are breastfeeding.

It is not usually recommended that people who have severe depression or liver disease take disease modifying drug treatment.[2]British National Formulary. Drugs affecting the immune response. Section 8.2. British Medical Association and Royal Pharmaceutical Society of Great Britain. Also available at http://bnf.org (accessed on 28 September 2012). Your doctor will be able to tell you more about this.

What are the main effects of active monitoring and support on relapsing remitting MS?

Active monitoring will not have a direct effect on the number of relapses you have or on how your illness progresses. But careful monitoring can help you spot the signs that you may need to change your treatment. If your symptoms begin to get worse, your doctor may suggest starting other treatments. You don't need to start on other treatments unless you want to.

What are the main effects of active monitoring and support on what you can do and how you feel?

Active monitoring will not have a direct effect on what you can do. Treatments such as physiotherapy, occupational therapy, psychological support, and self-care support may help you to stay active, and feel emotionally supported.

What are the main side effects of active monitoring and support?

You are not likely to have 'side effects' from active monitoring. But by choosing not to take disease modifying drugs you will be more likely to have a potentially disabling relapse.

How might active monitoring and support affect my life?

You may have regular appointments to check your overall condition and talk about your symptoms. How much time this takes, and how often it happens, will depend on how stable your symptoms are. 

If you also have physiotherapy, occupational therapy, and psychological therapy to help you manage your condition, you will need to find time to attend these appointments.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT INTERFERON BETA

What is interferon beta?

Interferons are naturally occurring proteins that your body makes to fight infections and to reduce inflammation. They can also be made in a laboratory and used as medicines.

By reducing inflammation, interferon beta slows down nerve damage, so you're less likely to get symptoms of MS.[3]Dhib-Jalbut S. Mechanisms of action of interferons and glatiramer acetate in multiple sclerosis. Neurology. 2002; 58 (supplement): S3-S9.

 There are two types of interferon beta:

  • interferon beta-1a (brand names Avonex, Rebif)
  • interferon beta-1b (brand names Betaferon, Extavia)

These drugs are all similar, but you use them in slightly different ways.

  • Avonex is injected into your muscle once a week.
  • Betaferon and Extavia are injected under your skin every other day.
  • Rebif is injected under your skin three times a week.
Who can have interferon beta?

Interferon beta doesn't help everyone. People with relapsing-remitting MS are considered for treatment with interferon beta on the NHS if:[4]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Management of multiple sclerosis in primary and secondary care. November 2003. Clinical guideline 8. Available at http://publications.nice.org.uk/multiple-sclerosis-cg8 (accessed on 28 September 2012).

  • They can walk 100 metres or more without help
  • They have had at least two relapses in the past two years
  • They are aged 18 years or over.

You may be advised not to take interferon beta if you are pregnant, planning to become pregnant, or breastfeeding. Although there is no evidence that the drug can harm your baby, the manufacturer recommends that you don't take it while pregnant or breastfeeding, until there is more research to say that it is safe. You can discuss this decision with your doctor.

It is not usually recommended that people who suffer from severe liver disease take interferon beta.[5]British National Formulary. Drugs affecting the immune response. Section 8.2. British Medical Association and Royal Pharmaceutical Society of Great Britain. Also available at http://bnf.org (accessed on 28 September 2012). Your doctor will be able to tell you more about this.

People's immune systems may start to react against treatment with interferons. Their bodies may then produce another type of protein (antibody) against the interferons in our bloodstream. There is evidence that these antibodies reduce how well interferon beta is able to prevent relapses. These are called neutralising antibodies.

These antibodies develop in about 30 in 100 taking Betaferon or Extavia, 25 in 100 of people taking Rebif, and 5 in 100 people taking Avonex.[6]Multiple Sclerosis Trust. Neutralising antibodies. June 2012. Available at: http://www.mstrust.org.uk/atoz/neutralising.jsp (accessed on 28 September 2012). It is recommended that people taking interferon beta should have a blood test 12 months after starting treatment, and again two years after starting treatment to check for neutralising antibodies. If neutralising antibodies are found in your blood, interferon beta will no longer work well enough, and your doctor will help you to choose another treatment.

What are the main effects of interferon beta on relapsing-remitting multiple sclerosis?

The main effects of interferon beta are to reduce the number of relapses you have and to slow the rate at which your level of disability becomes worse.

In a group of people with MS who were followed up for two years, injections of interferon beta reduced the number of relapses they had by an average of about one third.[7]Rice GP, Incorvaia B, Munari L, et al. Interferon in relapsing-remitting multiple sclerosis (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.  

Interferon beta may help to slow the rate at which disability becomes worse for people who have MS. In one group of people with MS, about 20 in 100 people taking interferon had got worse two years later, compared with about 29 in 100 who didn't take interferon.[7]Rice GP, Incorvaia B, Munari L, et al. Interferon in relapsing-remitting multiple sclerosis (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. 

What are the main effects of interferon beta on what you can do and how you feel?

Taking interferon beta may stop you having a relapse, and may help to slow the rate at which your disability gets worse. This may help you feel less anxious about your disease.[7]Rice GP, Incorvaia B, Munari L, et al. Interferon in relapsing-remitting multiple sclerosis (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

What are the main side effects of interferon beta ?

There are side effects associated with interferon treatment.

About 48 in every 100 people taking interferon beta get flu-like symptoms that last for up to a day after the injection. Most people stop having these symptoms after a few months of treatment.

About 28 in 100 people get fever, 50 in 100 people get a headache, and 62 in 100 people get a skin reaction like redness or itching at the place where they have the injection.[7]Rice GP, Incorvaia B, Munari L, et al. Interferon in relapsing-remitting multiple sclerosis (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. Many people find that they have fewer problems the more they get used to using the auto-injector. Varying the injection site and adjusting the auto-injector can help. Steroid creams and E45 cream can  help with rashes.

Interferon beta may also cause nausea and vomiting in some people.[7]Rice GP, Incorvaia B, Munari L, et al. Interferon in relapsing-remitting multiple sclerosis (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

Most of these side effects usually only last a short time, and they usually happen less often over time.

An MS professional will discuss with you in more detail side effects and how to manage them.

How might interferon beta affect my life?

All types of interferon beta need to be injected. 

Most people want to know how the various types of interferon beta might fit around their lifestyle. They want to know about how they will manage injections in different situations such as:

  • At work
  • On holiday
  • In social situations, or
  • Doing leisure activities.

An MS specialist nurse can advise you how the various types of interferon beta might affect these things. They will also show you how to inject yourself with different injection devices. This will include a demonstration of how to use an automatic injection device (auto injector).

Some people find that auto injectors make injecting themselves easier - particularly in hard-to-reach places. With most types of auto injector, it also means that you don't need to see the needle going in, which can be a help if you are not keen on needles.

All types of interferon beta that are injected under the skin (Rebif, Betaferon, and Extavia) use a shorter, thinner needle than Avonex, which is injected into the muscle.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT GLATIRAMER ACETATE

What is glatiramer acetate?

Glatiramer acetate is a mix of substances called amino acids. It stops cells from the immune system attacking the myelin coating around your nerves.[3]Dhib-Jalbut S. Mechanisms of action of interferons and glatiramer acetate in multiple sclerosis. Neurology. 2002; 58 (supplement): S3-S9. This prevents some of the inflammation that causes the symptoms of MS as it helps your nerves to function normally. You have injections of glatiramer acetate under your skin once a day.

Who can have glatiramer acetate?

Glatiramer acetate doesn't help everyone. People with relapsing-remitting MS should be able to have treatment on the NHS if:[14]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Management of multiple sclerosis in primary and secondary care. November 2003. Clinical Guideline 8. Available at http://publications.nice.org.uk/multiple-sclerosis-cg8 (accessed on 28 September 2012).

  • They can walk 100 metres or more without help
  • They have had at least two relapses in the past two years
  • They are aged 18 years or over.

You may be advised not be able to take glatiramer acetate if you are pregnant, planning to become pregnant, or breastfeeding. Although there is no evidence that the drug can harm your baby, the manufacturer recommends that you don't take it while pregnant or breastfeeding, until there is more research to say that it is safe. You can discuss this decision with your doctor.

If you have ever had heart disease, including problems such as angina, an irregular heartbeat (arrhythmia), or any type of heart problems, you may not be able to take this medicine. [5]British National Formulary. Drugs affecting the immune response. Section 8.2. British Medical Association and Royal Pharmaceutical Society of Great Britain. Also available at http://bnf.org (accessed on 28 September 2012). Glatiramer acetate can cause the heart to beat faster than normal straight after the injection. This may not be safe for some people with heart disease.

What are the main effects of glatiramer acetate on relapsing-remitting MS?

The main effect of glatiramer acetate is to reduce the number of relapses you have. 

In a group of people with MS who were followed up for two years, injections of glatiramer acetate reduced the number of relapses they had by an average of about one third.[16]La Mantia L, Munari LM, Lovati R. Glatiramer acetate for multiple sclerosis (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. 

In a group of people with MIS who were followed up for two years, glatiramer acetate did not reduce the chance of worsening disability.[16]La Mantia L, Munari LM, Lovati R. Glatiramer acetate for multiple sclerosis (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

 


What are the main effects of glatiramer acetate on what you can do and how you feel?

Taking glatiramer acetate reduces the chances that you will have a relapse within two years of starting treatment. As your symptoms stabilise you may feel more in control of your body and be more active. This may help you feel less anxious about your disease.

What are the main side effects of glatiramer acetate?

About 15 in 100 people get an allergic reaction after at least one of their glatiramer acetate injections.[18]Johnson KP, Brooks BR, Cohen JA, et al. Copolymer 1 reduces relapse rate and improves disability in relapsing-remitting multiple sclerosis: results of a phase III multicenter, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial. Neurology. 2001; 57 (supplement 5): S16-S24. When this happens their skin turns red, their chest feels tight, and they feel anxious. The reaction can last up to 30 minutes.

Some people get swelling, itching, and pain at the point where the drug is injected.[19]La Mantia L, Munari LM, Lovati R. Glatiramer acetate for multiple sclerosis (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. Many people find that they have fewer problems the more they get used to using the auto-injector. Varying the injection site and adjusting the auto-injector can help. Steroid creams and E45 cream can  help with rashes.

People having glatiramer acetate injections can get a condition called lipoatrophy.[20]Edgar CM, Brunet DG, Fenton P, et al. Lipoatrophy in patients with multiple sclerosis on glatiramer acetate. Canadian Journal of Neurological Sciences. 2004; 31: 58-63. If you have lipoatrophy, the fat beneath the skin is lost in small areas. This makes small 'craters' or indentations in the skin, which do not usually get better with time. We do not know why lipoatrophy happens. It seems to be more common in women than in men. This condition can be minimised by inspecting injection sites regularly and avoiding further injections in places that have been affected.

Glatiramer acetate can cause the heart to beat faster than normal straight after the injection, so it may not be suitable for people who have heart problems. You should talk to your doctor about any heart problems you may have had before taking this medicine.

An MS professional will discuss side effects and how they can be managed with you in more detail.

How might glatiramer acetate affect my life?

Glatiramer needs to be injected under the skin every day. 

An MS specialist nurse can demonstrate how to inject yourself with different injection devices. This will include a demonstration of how to use an automatic injection device (auto injector). Some people find that auto injectors make injecting themselves easier - particularly in hard-to-reach places. With most types of auto injector, it also means that you don't need to see the needle going in, which can be a help if you are not keen on needles.

Most people are keen to know how choosing this treatment will fit around their lifestyle. 

Most people want to know how the various types of interferon beta might fit around their lifestyle. They want to know about how they will manage injections in different situations such as:

  • At work
  • On holiday
  • In social situations, or
  • Doing leisure activities.

Your MS nurse can help you talk through all of these things and help you work out what is the best option for you.

You can then give yourself the injection, or a friend or family member can be trained to give it to you. You don’t need to go hospital for injections.

Things to consider include: 

  • Are you able to inject yourself? If not, is there someone who can help you?
  • The glatiramer acetate injection needs to be stored in a fridge. If it cannot be stored in a fridge, it can be stored at room temperature, but only for one month.
  • You will need to have a secure storage area for your syringes and needles, and a special box to discard needles. 
  • Glatiramer acetate injections are given under the skin, so they use a shorter, thinner needle than some other disease-modifying drugs.

People going on holiday with hypodermic needles are required to take proof of medical need to travel on an aircraft.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT NATALIZUMAB

What is natalizumab?

Natalizumab is a drug that affects how your immune system works. It stops your white blood cells (one of the types of cells your immune system makes to help fight infection) passing from your blood into your brain and nervous system. This prevents the inflammation that causes nerve damage in MS.

You have it as a drip (an IV infusion) into your vein, which can take up to two hours. You may need one dose every four weeks.

Who can have natalizumab?

Natalizumab is only recommended for people with severe relapsing-remitting MS that is getting worse quickly (you may have heard this called 'rapidly evolving' MS). That means:[21]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Natalizumab for the treatment of adults with highly active relapsing-remitting multiple sclerosis. August 2007. Technology Appraisal 127. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/TA127 (accessed 28 September 2012).

  • People who have two or more serious relapses in a year
  • Where an MRI scan of the brain shows nerve damage that is getting worse.

You will probably be advised not to take natalizumab if you are pregnant, planning to become pregnant, or breastfeeding. There is not enough evidence yet that this drug is safe for babies.[5]British National Formulary. Drugs affecting the immune response. Section 8.2. British Medical Association and Royal Pharmaceutical Society of Great Britain. Also available at http://bnf.org (accessed on 28 September 2012).

What are the main effects of natalizumab on relapsing remitting MS?

Natalizumab can slow down how quickly MS gets worse after two years of treatment.[23]Pucci E, Giuliani G, Solari A, et al. Natalizumab for relapsing remitting multiple sclerosis CR (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. In a group of people with MS who were followed up for two years, natalizumab reduced the number of relapses they had on average by about half.[23]Pucci E, Giuliani G, Solari A, et al. Natalizumab for relapsing remitting multiple sclerosis CR (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

Natalizumab may slow down the rate at which disability becomes worse for people with MS within two years.[23]Pucci E, Giuliani G, Solari A, et al. Natalizumab for relapsing remitting multiple sclerosis CR (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

What are the main effects of natalizumab on what you can do and how you feel?

Natalizumab reduces the likelihood that you will have a relapse, and also slows down how quickly disability caused by MS gets worse, so it may help you to feel more in control of your illness.

What are the main side effects of natalizumab?

Natalizumab can cause a serious brain condition. About 2 in 1,000 people taking natalizumab get a brain condition called progressive multifocal leucoencephalopathy (PML).[26]Yousry TA, Major EO, Ryschkewitsch C, et al. Evaluation of patients treated with natalizumab for progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy. New England Journal of Medicine. 2006; 354: 924-933.

This condition only affects some people. There is a blood test that can tell if there is a chance you could develop PML if you start taking natalizumab. It is called the JCV antibody test. Usually, only people who have these antibodies in their blood are at risk of getting PML.

PML is a rare disorder that damages the material (myelin) that covers and protects nerves in part of the brain. PML can be fatal or cause severe disability. By February 2012, 212 of 99,571 people worldwide had PML from taking natalizumab.[27]Bloomgren G, Richman S, Hotermans C, et al. Risk of natalizumab-associated progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy. New England Journal of Medicine. 2012; 366:1870-1880. It is more likely to occur after you've been taking the drug for more than two years.[28]Medicines and Healthcare Products Regulatory Agency. Drug safety update: volume 3, issue 10, March 2010. Available at: http://www.mhra.gov.uk/home/groups/pl-p/documents/publication/con076097.pdf (accessed on 28 September 2012).

Natalizumab can cause liver damage. If you have signs of jaundice (yellowing of the skin or eyes), dark-coloured urine, or unusual tiredness while taking this drug, you should talk to your doctor straight away. This liver damage is not severe and is reversed when treatment is stopped. But if this happens to you, you will probably need to change your treatment.[29]British National Formulary. Natalizumab. Section 8.2.4. British Medical Association and Royal Pharmaceutical Society of Great Britain. Also available at http://bnf.org (accessed on 7 March 2013).

Other side effects include urine infections, tiredness, fever, rash, sinus congestion, swelling, and itching.[23]Pucci E, Giuliani G, Solari A, et al. Natalizumab for relapsing remitting multiple sclerosis CR (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. [31]European Medicines Agency. Tysabri. May 2012. Available at: http://www.ema.europa.eu/ema/index.jsp?curl=pages/medicines/human/medicines/000603/human_med_001119.jsp&mid=WC0b01ac058001d124 (accessed on 28 September 2012). Many of these side effects only last a short time after the infusion.

About 10 in 100 people get an allergic reaction to natalizumab.[32]Polman CH, O'Connor PW, Havrdova E, et al. A randomized, placebo-controlled trial of natalizumab for relapsing multiple sclerosis. New England Journal of Medicine. 2006; 354: 899-910. Less than 1 in 100 people have a serious reaction.[31]European Medicines Agency. Tysabri. May 2012. Available at: http://www.ema.europa.eu/ema/index.jsp?curl=pages/medicines/human/medicines/000603/human_med_001119.jsp&mid=WC0b01ac058001d124 (accessed on 28 September 2012). If you have an allergic reaction you need to change your treatment to a different drug.

An MS professional will discuss side effects with you and how they can be managed in more detail.

How might natalizumab affect my life?

Natalizumab is taken as an intravenous infusion (a 'drip') into a vein, once every four weeks. You have the infusion in hospital. The treatment takes between one and two hours. You need to stay in the hospital for another hour after the infusion is complete in case you have an allergic reaction. If you don't have any reaction, you can leave and carry on with normal activities straight after your treatment.

Animation about Multiple Sclerosis

This decision aid is to help people with multiple sclerosis (MS) and their health care team decide whether a disease modifying drug is suitable for them, and if so which one. This decision aid is only for people with relapsing-remitting MS who have never used disease modifying drugs before.

The four treatment options are:

  • Active monitoring and support: this means people will monitor their symptoms and have regular checks with their health professionals. Although people won't be taking medication to control relapses, they can start taking medication if they have a relapse. All people with multiple sclerosis can have treatment to manage their symptoms including physiotherapy, occupational therapy, psychological support, advice to help them manage their own illness (self-management), and medicine to control symptoms. For the purposes of this decision aid, active monitoring refers to people who choose not to take a disease modifying drug (interferon beta, glatiramer acetate, natalizumab), although they may take medicines to treat their symptoms.
  • Interferon beta: Interferon beta is a drug based on naturally produced proteins that help reduce the inflammation that causes nerve damage. Reducing nerve damage helps reduce the chance of multiple scleroris getting worse or relapsing. Interferon beta is a drug people can inject themselves.
  • Glatiramer acetate: Glatiramer acetate is a mix of substances called amino acids. This drug helps to stop the body's immune system attacking the coating around the nerves. Reducing nerve damage helps reduce the chance of multiple scleroris getting worse or relapsing. Glatiramer acetate is a drug people can inject themselves.
  • Natalizumab: Natalizumab is a drug that affects how the immune system works. It stops the white blood cells (one of the types of cells your immune system makes to help fight infection) passing from the blood into the brain and nervous system. This drug is given as an infusion every four weeks.

Not everyone will be able to choose from all of these options. Some of these medications are not suitable for everyone. For example, natalizumab can only be taken by people who have severe relapsing-remitting multiple sclerosis that is getting worse quickly. MS specialist nurses or neurologists can help people decide which treatments are suitable for them.

Although active monitoring is listed as a distinct treatment option in this decision aid, everyone who has MS, whether they are taking disease modifying drugs or not, will be actively monitored and provided with self-care support.  

The right treatment decision for you may change over time. This decision aid aims to help you make the right choice for you now. You may wish to change your mind if your situation or your life changes. If you choose a disease modifying drug, and then decide to change treatment, you should speak to your healthcare team. This decision aid is only for people who have not taken disease modifying drugs before, so it won't be the right place for you to get help if you have already taken a disease modifying drug.

The decisions people with relapsing-remitting multiple sclerosis who have never used disease modifying drugs are making include:

  • Should I start taking disease modifying drugs, as well as my other treatments, to reduce my chance of relapse?
  • If I start taking a disease modifying drug, is there one that is best for me?
  • If I don't take a disease modifying drug, will my multiple sclerosis do me more harm?

All people with multiple sclerosis can have treatment to manage their symptoms including physiotherapy, occupational therapy, psychological support, advice to help them manage their own illness (self-management), and medicine to control symptoms. Specialist health professionals will work with people with multiple sclerosis to find the best way to manage their symptoms before talking through the disease modifying medications options.

MORE INFORMATION ABOUT MULTIPLE SCLEROSIS

What does the central nervous system do?

To understand what happens when a person get MS, it helps to know about the central nervous system (or CNS for short).

The CNS includes the brain and the spinal cord.[34]Martini FH, Ober WC, Garrison CW, et al. Fundamentals of anatomy and physiology. 5th edition. Prentice Hall, New Jersey, NJ; 2001.

  • The brain controls everything the body does. The brain controls how a person moves, speaks, recognises their family, thinks, and remembers things.
  • The spinal cord is the main highway of the CNS. It is a big bundle of nerves that runs down the back from the brain. It sits inside the bones of the spine and carries nerve signals to and from the brain.

The brain and spinal cord are made up of billions of nerve cells that share information. The information moves from one nerve cell to another as chemical signals. Groups of nerve cells have specific jobs. For example, some let you think, learn, remember, and plan. Others let you see and hear. Others manage the millions of actions that keep the body working.

Many of the nerves in the brain and spinal cord have a coating called myelin, which is made mainly of fat. Myelin helps signals travel quickly and smoothly along the nerves.

What is multiple sclerosis?

Multiple sclerosis is a disease where the body's immune system damages nerves in the brain and spinal cord.

The immune system's job is to help protect the body by fighting off infection. In some people, the immune system goes wrong and attacks the myelin coating around the nerves in the brain and spinal cord.[4]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Management of multiple sclerosis in primary and secondary care. November 2003. Clinical guideline 8. Available at http://publications.nice.org.uk/multiple-sclerosis-cg8 (accessed on 28 September 2012). [36]Hauser SL, Goodkin DE. Multiple sclerosis and other demyelinating diseases. In: Braunwald E, Fauci AS, Kasper DL, et al (editors). Harrison's principles of internal medicine. 15th edition. McGraw Hill, New York, NY; 2001. This causes inflammation (swelling), and the myelin coating may be damaged or destroyed. The damage to the myelin coating around the nerve is what causes the symptoms of MS, as signals travelling along the nerves may slow down, get blocked, speed up, or get mixed up. This can affect different parts of the body. For example, the brain may send a message to the hand to pick up a cup. If the signal gets mixed up, the hand may not move the way a person wants it to, or the movement may be too weak for the person to pick up the cup.

There are three main types of MS:

  • Relapsing-remitting MS. People get bouts of symptoms called relapses. These last for at least 24 hours, and usually for a few weeks. 
  • Secondary progressive MS. Some of the symptoms stay after a relapse and get worse over time.
  • Primary progressive MS. The symptoms never really go away from the start. They slowly get worse but may vary over time. Most people with primary progressive MS do not have relapses. However, relapses can occasionally happen.

In relapsing-remitting MS, most of the damage to the nervous system is to the myelin around nerves, rather than the nerve cells themselves.

Most people with MS get the relapsing-remitting type, then go on to have the secondary progressive type. The information in this decision aid is for people with relapsing-remitting MS.[36]Hauser SL, Goodkin DE. Multiple sclerosis and other demyelinating diseases. In: Braunwald E, Fauci AS, Kasper DL, et al (editors). Harrison's principles of internal medicine. 15th edition. McGraw Hill, New York, NY; 2001.

Here are some definitions of terms that you may find useful when reading this patient decision aid:

  • Relapse: This is a period in which a person with MS suffers has an acute (sudden) worsening of symptoms they have had already, or they have completely new symptoms for at least 24 hours, without a fever or another explanation for the worsening of symptoms. It usually lasts for several days or weeks. After a relapse symptoms often, but not always, improve over a period of weeks or months.
  • Disability: This is when a person with MS loses the ability to perform a physical or mental function.
What are the symptoms of multiple sclerosis?

People get many different symptoms from multiple sclerosis. They include:[38]Noseworthy JH, Lucchinetti C, Rodriguez M, et al. Multiple sclerosis. New England Journal of Medicine. 2000; 343: 938-952.

  • Tiredness and weakness
  • Numbness and odd sensations
  • Problems moving parts of the body
  • Dim or blurred vision
  • Dizziness and vertigo
  • Muscle spasms
  • Bladder problems
  • Sexual problems
  • Bowel problems
  • Emotional upset
  • Feelings of confusion.

The symptoms a person gets depends on where in their brain and spinal cord the inflammation happens. The inflammation that causes MS symptoms can come and go.

People with MS can be affected in different ways.

  • Some people might get only some of these symptoms, others might get most of them. Most people have a few at a time.
  • Some people have certain symptoms at one time and other symptoms at a different time.
  • Symptoms can be mild or severe. They are sometimes so mild that people don't notice them, especially at first.
  • Symptoms can last just a day or two, or for weeks on end.

The pattern of symptoms depends on the type of MS a person has.  Most people who have relapsing-remitting MS have a permanent background of symptoms, which can include any of the symptoms listed here.

Why has this happened to me?

Certain things increase someone's chances of getting MS. 

Family and genes: People with a first degree relative with MS (mother, father, sister, or brother),  have about a 2 in 100 to 3 in 100 chance of getting MS themselves.[36]Hauser SL, Goodkin DE. Multiple sclerosis and other demyelinating diseases. In: Braunwald E, Fauci AS, Kasper DL, et al (editors). Harrison's principles of internal medicine. 15th edition. McGraw Hill, New York, NY; 2001. There isn't any single gene that causes MS, but a combination of many genes.[36]Hauser SL, Goodkin DE. Multiple sclerosis and other demyelinating diseases. In: Braunwald E, Fauci AS, Kasper DL, et al (editors). Harrison's principles of internal medicine. 15th edition. McGraw Hill, New York, NY; 2001.

Where a person lives: MS is more common in colder countries that are further from the equator.

  • In the UK, MS is more common in north-east Scotland and the Orkney and Shetland Islands than in the rest of the country.[41]Compston A. Genetic epidemiology of multiple sclerosis. Journal of Neurology, Neurosurgery and Psychiatry. 1997; 62: 553-561.
  • Below the equator, MS is more common in the southern part of Australia than further north.[38]Noseworthy JH, Lucchinetti C, Rodriguez M, et al. Multiple sclerosis. New England Journal of Medicine. 2000; 343: 938-952.
  • MS is also more common in northern Europe (for example, Norway, Sweden, and Finland) than in southern Europe (for example, Greece, Spain, and Portugal).[41]Compston A. Genetic epidemiology of multiple sclerosis. Journal of Neurology, Neurosurgery and Psychiatry. 1997; 62: 553-561.

No one knows for sure why this is. A person's chance of getting MS seems to depend on where they lived as a child. So if someone moved from a colder country to a warmer one after the age of about 15, it doesn't lower their chance of getting MS.

How do I find out I have multiple sclerosis?

Most people find out they have MS after having symptoms, then seeing their GP. If a person's GP thinks their symptoms could be because of MS, they will be referred to a doctor who specialises in diseases of the brain and spinal cord (a neurologist).[4]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Management of multiple sclerosis in primary and secondary care. November 2003. Clinical guideline 8. Available at http://publications.nice.org.uk/multiple-sclerosis-cg8 (accessed on 28 September 2012). If a person's symptoms are mild, and could be caused by something else, the GP may wait to see if the person's symptoms come back before referring them to a neurologist.[4]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Management of multiple sclerosis in primary and secondary care. November 2003. Clinical guideline 8. Available at http://publications.nice.org.uk/multiple-sclerosis-cg8 (accessed on 28 September 2012).

The neurologist will order an MRI scan to look for areas of nerve damage around the brain and spinal cord. Blood tests can rule out other possible causes of the symptoms. Some people need to have a lumbar puncture, where the doctor takes a sample of spinal fluid to test. Not everyone needs a lumbar puncture.

If a person has MS, these tests will show that the coating of the nerves (called myelin) have been damaged in at least two places in their brain and spinal cord.

How many people have multiple sclerosis?

About 100,000 people in the UK have MS - that's between 1 and 2 in every 1,000 people. It is the most common cause of disability due to nerve damage in young adults.[46]Multiple Sclerosis Society. What is MS? Available at: http://www.mssociety.org.uk/what-is-ms (accessed 22 November 2012). [47]Ford HL, Gerry E, Johnson M, et al. A prospective study of the incidence, prevalence and mortality of multiple sclerosis in Leeds. Journal of Neurology. 2002; 249: 260-265. [48]Sloka JS, Pryse-Phillips WE, Stefanelli M. Incidence and prevalence of multiple sclerosis in Newfoundland and Labrador. The Canadian Journal of Neurological Sciences. 2005; 32: 37-42.

MS is usually diagnosed in people between the ages of 20 and 40 years,[49]Weinshenker BG, Bass B, Rice GP, et al. The natural history of multiple sclerosis: a geographically based study. I. Clinical course and disability. Brain. 1989; 112: 133-146. but it can occur in people younger and older than this. Women are almost twice as likely as men to get MS.[46]Multiple Sclerosis Society. What is MS? Available at: http://www.mssociety.org.uk/what-is-ms (accessed 22 November 2012). But when people develop MS later in life (primary progressive MS often occurs in older people) there is less difference between men and women.

What will happen?

MS affects people in different ways. With relapsing-remitting MS, a person's symptoms may come and go for many years. The relapsing-remitting type of MS usually turns into the secondary progressive type eventually. This may take years.[51]O'Connor P. Key issues in the diagnosis and treatment of multiple sclerosis. An overview. Neurology. 2002: 59 (supplement 3): S1-S33. About 50 in 100 people diagnosed with relapsing-remitting MS have secondary progressive MS 10 years later.[4]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Management of multiple sclerosis in primary and secondary care. November 2003. Clinical guideline 8. Available at http://publications.nice.org.uk/multiple-sclerosis-cg8 (accessed on 28 September 2012).

Disease modifying drugs can help people by:

  • Reducing how many relapses they have
  • Slowing down the disease, and
  • Helping people stay active.

No treatment gets rid of MS altogether.[7]Rice GP, Incorvaia B, Munari L, et al. Interferon in relapsing-remitting multiple sclerosis (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. [18]Johnson KP, Brooks BR, Cohen JA, et al. Copolymer 1 reduces relapse rate and improves disability in relapsing-remitting multiple sclerosis: results of a phase III multicenter, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial. Wiley, Chichester, UK. [32]Polman CH, O'Connor PW, Havrdova E, et al. A randomized, placebo-controlled trial of natalizumab for relapsing multiple sclerosis. New England Journal of Medicine. 2006; 354: 899-910.

The disease modifying drugs we discuss in this decision aid are the ones that are offered to people first. These drugs are to help prevent relapses, or to reduce the number of relapses people have. If they don’t work, or they stop working, people are likely to be offered a combination of drugs.

There are many things to think about if you and the health professionals supporting you decide that a disease-modifying drug is right for you. One thing many people want to know is how the various medicines might work around their lifestyle. So people might want to know things like:

  • Which of the medicines need to be kept in the fridge?
  • Which need to be taken more often than others?
  • Does taking these medicines restrict how much someone can travel?

Most of the medicines need to be injected by that person taking them. Injections can, understandably, be worrying to many people at first. The information on medicines in this decision aid should help people begin to understand how different drug choices might fit into their lives.

Although active monitoring is listed as a distinct treatment option in this decision aid, everyone who has MS - whether they are taking disease modifying drugs or not - will be actively monitored. This means people will monitor their symptoms, and have regular checks with their health professionals to discuss how they are doing.

There are also treatments available for individual symptoms: for example, drugs to relax the muscles for people who often get muscle cramps.

If you have just been diagnosed with multiple sclerosis, there are many places you can get help. About 100,000 people in the UK have MS, so you are far from alone. There are patient support groups where you can meet and talk to people like you. These are easy to find online, or you could ask your doctor where to find them.

Many people find these support groups helpful. You may find that talking with other people who are living with MS can help you make decisions about your treatment. Or you may just find that it feels good to talk about your condition with someone other than a health professional. There are plenty of people willing to listen and to help.
