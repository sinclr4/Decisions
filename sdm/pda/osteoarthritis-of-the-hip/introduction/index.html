Our Decision Support telephone service is here to assist and guide you through the Shared Decision Making process.

The Decision Support team is made of professional Health Coaches who are clinicians, commonly nurses, who have undertaken additional training to become a Health Coach.

The Health Coaches will assist you by:

  • Providing you with information
  • Helping you to understand your condition
  • Recognise what is important to you regarding the outcome of treatment
  • Identifying potential solutions
  • Encourage you in discussing options with your family
  • Transferring skills which will assist you in using the information and resources available to you
  • Support you in building confidence in discussing your choice with your doctor
  • You may find that this can be achieved in one telephone call with a Health Coach however; if further calls are required to support you in reaching your decision the Health Coach will schedule these with you.

Important Things To Note

  • A Health Coach does not know which treatment option is best for you.
  • The Health Coach does not have information about your medical history.
  • You will still need to talk over your decision and reasons for your decision with your doctor or nurse.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT LIFESTYLE CHANGES

What are lifestyle changes?

These are changes that you can make for yourself that may help improve your symptoms. Examples include:

  • Losing weight (if you are overweight)
  • Taking more exercise.

If you want to lose weight, you will need to change your diet to include fewer calories, and take exercise to burn off excess calories. Your doctor or practice nurse may be able to help you make a weight loss plan. You could also try a commercial weight-loss organisation such as Weight Watchers.

Exercise can include swimming, using the gym, walking, gardening, or any activity that keeps you active.

Some people benefit from self-help support programmes, which use talking therapies such as cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT). These programmes can help people feel more in control of their arthritis, by learning to manage their pain, exercise and keep active safely, solve problems, reduce anxiety and keep healthy.

Programmes can be given in groups or individually, by healthcare professionals or by other patients. These programmes can be an addition to other treatments such as pain medication or physical management, or can be tried before other treatments.

Who can choose lifestyle changes?

Anyone can take a look at their lifestyle and decide whether making some changes could be beneficial. Your doctor may also suggest lifestyle changes that may help your arthritis.

What are the effects of lifestyle changes on pain?

We don’t know for sure whether exercising and achieving a healthy weight can reduce pain for people with osteoarthritis of the hip.

If you are overweight, this puts extra strain on your joints and increases the chance that your osteoarthritis will get worse. Being overweight makes it more likely that both your hips will be affected and that you'll develop arthritis in other joints, such as your knees.[1]Lievense AM, Bierman-Zeinstra SM, et al. Influence of obesity on the development of osteoarthritis of the hip: a systematic review. Rheumatology (Oxford). 2002; 41: 1155-1162.[2]Manek NJ, Hart D, Spector TD. The association of body mass index and osteoarthritis of the knee joint: an examination of genetic and environmental influences. Arthritis and Rheumatism. 2003; 48: 1024-1029. 

Regular exercise keeps you active and mobile and builds up muscle, strengthening the joints. This may improve symptoms.[3]Tak E, Staats P, Van Hespen A, et al. The effects of an exercise program for older adults with osteoarthritis of the hip. Journal of Rheumatology. 2005; 32: 1106-1113. Exercise relieves stress and helps you sleep, which may ease pain. Exercise can also help you lose weight.

What are the effects of lifestyle changes on how well you can get around?

We don’t know how much exercise helps improve mobility for hip osteoarthritis. There has been some research on this but the findings of these studies have not provided a clear answer.[4]Fransen M, McConnell S, Bell M. Exercise for osteoarthritis of the hip or knee (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.[5]Ravaud P, Giraudeau B, Logeart I, et al. Management of osteoarthritis (OA) with an unsupervised home based exercise programme and/or patient administered assessment tools: a cluster randomised controlled trial with a 2 x 2 factorial design. Annals of the Rheumatic Diseases. 2004; 63: 703-708.[3]Tak E, Staats P, Van Hespen A, et al. The effects of an exercise program for older adults with osteoarthritis of the hip. Journal of Rheumatology. 2005; 32: 1106-1113.

What are the effects of lifestyle changes on what you can do?

Taking regular exercise and maintaining a healthy weight can improve your general fitness and mean you are able to do physically more than you used to. 

What are the unwanted side effects of lifestyle changes?

If you don't do exercises properly, they may not help you, and you could injure yourself. A doctor or physiotherapist can help advise you on how to exercise. Some people, such as people with heart disease, might need medical clearance from their doctor before they start on an exercise programme.

Exercises that are gentle on your joints include walking, cycling, swimming, or exercising in water (for example, doing aqua-aerobics). An exercise programme may also include stretching exercises and strength training with weights or elastic resistance bands. 

Losing weight means following a lower-calorie diet. Dieting can be harmful if you don’t get the right nutrients. Talk to your doctor or to a dietitian about losing weight safely.

How will lifestyle changes affect my life?

Making changes takes time and commitment and results will not happen overnight. There is no ‘quick fix’ when it comes to exercise and dieting. You need to keep exercising and keep an eye on your diet to see long-term results.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT PHYSICAL MANAGEMENT

What is physical management?

Physical management covers three main areas of treatment:

  • Physiotherapy: advice on exercise and self-management strategies, and manual therapy
  • Occupational therapy: advice on dealing with difficulties in your daily routine, such as washing and dressing, caused by your arthritis
  • Mobility aids: walking aids, special footwear, or shoe inserts.

You have physiotherapy in sessions with physiotherapists. You have occupational therapy in sessions with occupational therapists. Mobility aids may be supplied by either physiotherapists or occupational therapists. 

Some people benefit from self-help support programmes, which use talking therapies such as cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT). These programmes can help people feel more in control of their arthritis, by learning to manage their pain, exercise and keep active safely, solve problems, reduce anxiety and keep healthy.

Programmes can be given in groups or individually, by healthcare professionals or by other patients. These programmes can be an addition to other treatments such as pain medication or physical management, or can be tried before other treatments.

Who can choose physical management?

Your GP or hospital consultant can refer you for these services. Some of these services are also available privately without a GP referral.


What are the effects of physical management on pain?

We don’t know for sure whether physical management can help with pain. There is not enough evidence about this.

A physiotherapist can offer advice about exercise and self-management strategies that may help with your symptoms. For example, something as simple as taking a hot bath might make a stiff joint feel better, or cold packs may help to reduce any swelling, although the evidence for this is limited.

Manual therapy, where a physiotherapist moves your hip joint beyond its normal range of movement, is another form of physical management. This stretches the muscles around the joint, which may have become shortened, and may help to reduce your pain and stiffness and improve your mobility.[7]Hoeksma HL, Dekker J, Ronday HK, et al. Comparison of manual therapy and exercise therapy in osteoarthritis of the hip: a randomized clinical trial. Arthritis and Rheumatism. 2004; 51: 722-729.

Assistive devices, like shoe inserts and walking aids, may also help reduce pain.[8]Hip pain and mobility deficits - hip osteoarthritis: clinical practice guidelines linked to the international classification of functioning, disability, and health from the orthopaedic section of the American Physical Therapy Association. Journal of Orthopaedic & Sports Physical Therapy. 2009; 39: A1-A25. 

What are the effects of physical management on how well you can get around?

Some walking aids such as special shoes or a walking stick may help you keep active and mobile.

You may be advised to have shoes with thick soft soles and sometimes an insole. A walking stick, used in the opposite hand to distribute some of your body weight down the stick, away from your affected hip when you walk, can also often help.[9]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Osteoarthritis: the care and management of osteoarthritis in adults. February 2008. Clinical guideline 59. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/cg59 (accessed on 30 May 2012).

What are the effects of physical management on what you can do?

Walking aids and aids around the house are intended to help increase what you can do. In one group of people, about 30 in 100 people said they find walking aids such as canes helpful. About 26 in 100 people said they find them little or no help. In another group, more than 90 in 100 people said aids helped them to do what they wanted to do, and to be more independent.[10]National Collaborating Centre for Chronic Conditions. Osteoarthritis: national clinical guideline for care and management in adults. London: Royal College of Physicians, 2008.

What are the unwanted side effects of physical management?

Exercising and physiotherapy may cause some pain and discomfort at first. This is because the treatment is stretching and working your muscles. But this should wear off.

It’s important to get professional advice before buying aids for the home or for walking. If you don’t, you could get the wrong type of aids and waste money.

How will physical management affect my life?

Both physiotherapy and occupational therapy will require a number of sessions. You will need to set aside time to do this. Ask your doctor how many sessions you are likely to need, and how often you would need to go to the hospital or clinic. Some physiotherapists and occupational therapists visit people at home.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT TREATMENTS TO MANAGE PAIN

What are treatments to manage pain?

There are two main types of treatment to manage pain: medication and self-help.

Pain medications are drug treatments designed to offer pain relief. Some can be bought from a pharmacy without a prescription, while others need to be prescribed by a doctor. They come in different formulations - tablets, gels or creams, and injections.

Over-the-counter: medicines you can buy without a prescription include paracetamol, paracetamol combined with low-dose codeine, and some non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) such as ibuprofen in a low dose. NSAIDs come as tablets (called oral NSAIDs) and as gels you rub on the skin (called topical NSAIDs).

Prescription medicines: these include higher-dose NSAIDs or cyclo-oxygenase 2 (COX-2) inhibitors and higher-dose opioid medicines, such as codeine.

Corticosteroid injections into the hip joint: injections of corticosteroids into the joint have to be done by doctors, physiotherapists or nurse specialists. Because injections are used as a short-term treatment, you usually shouldn't have more than three injections in a joint per year.[11]British National Formulary 61, Section 10. British Medical Association and Royal Pharmaceutical Society of Great Britain. Also available at http://bnf.org (accessed on 30 May 2012). 

You may hear corticosteroids referred to as steroids, but these are not the anabolic steroids used by some bodybuilders and athletes. They are similar to the natural chemicals your body makes to reduce inflammation. Some examples of corticosteroids include dexamethasone and triamcinolone.

Some people benefit from self-help support programmes, which use talking therapies such as cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT). These programmes can help people feel more in control of their arthritis, by learning to manage their pain, exercise and keep active safely, solve problems, reduce anxiety and keep healthy.

Programmes can be given in groups or individually, by healthcare professionals or by other patients. These programmes can be an addition to other treatments such as pain medication or physical management, or can be tried before other treatments.

Who can choose treatments to manage pain?

Most people can take some types of pain medications. If you have certain medical conditions, including asthma or a stomach ulcer, you may not be able to take some types of pain medication because of the risk they will worsen your condition. You can ask your GP about self-help support programmes for people with osteoarthritis or other long-term conditions.

What are the effects of treatments to manage pain?

Many people find that over-the-counter medicines help with their arthritis pain.

Oral NSAIDs seem to relieve the pain of hip arthritis more than paracetamol, but more studies are needed to confirm this.[12]Verkleij SP, Luijsterburg PA, Bohnen AM, et al NSAIDs vs acetaminophen in knee and hip osteoarthritis: a systematic review regarding heterogeneity inﬂuencing the outcomes. Osteoarthritis and Cartilage. 2011; 19: 921-929.

One study found that injecting corticosteroids into the joint can reduce pain by about half, and the results may last for up to three months.[13]Lambert RG, Hutchings EJ, Grace MG, et al. Steroid injection for osteoarthritis of the hip: a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial. Arthritis and Rheumatism  2007; 56: 2278-2287.

Topical NSAIDs (creams and gels you rub on the skin) are not likely to help with pain from hip osteoarthritis.[9]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Osteoarthritis: the care and management of osteoarthritis in adults. February 2008. Clinical guideline 59. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/cg59 (accessed on 30 May 2012). 

What are the effects of treatments to manage pain on how well you can get around?

If you feel less pain, you are likely to be able to get around more. Studies suggest that oral NSAIDs like ibuprofen can help reduce joint stiffness.

Topical NSAIDs (creams and gels you rub on the skin) are not likely to help you get around better if you have hip osteoarthritis.[9]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Osteoarthritis: the care and management of osteoarthritis in adults. February 2008. Clinical guideline 59. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/cg59 (accessed on 30 May 2012). 

What are the effects of treatments to manage pain on what you can do?

Medication can help reduce the pain and tenderness of osteoarthritis. Reduced pain and stiffness may help you do everyday things, like getting out of a chair or going for a walk.

What are the unwanted side effects of treatments to manage pain?

Most pain medication has some unwanted side effects. The risk of side effects becomes more likely with stronger drugs. You are more likely to have side effects if you have other existing health problems, such as heart disease.

If you are on any other medications it is important to check with your doctor or pharmacist that these will not react with your pain medicine.

Paracetamol is unlikely to cause side effects if you take the correct dose. Taking too much paracetamol can damage your liver. This can be very dangerous.

NSAIDs, like ibuprofen and COX-2 inhibitors, can cause bleeding in the stomach when taken over a long period of time. They can also raise the risk of heart attacks and stroke, although this risk is relatively low. NSAID tablets may not be suitable for people with certain conditions, such as asthma, a peptic ulcer, or angina, or if you have had a heart attack or stroke.

Opioids, like codeine and tramadol, can help relieve severe pain, but they can also cause side effects such as drowsiness, nausea, and constipation. You may need to take a laxative to treat constipation.

Tramadol is not suitable if you have epilepsy, and dihydrocodeine is not recommended for patients with chronic obstructive pulmonary disease. Pregnant women should not take any opioids.

If you take opioid painkillers regularly, you risk becoming addicted to them.

Corticosteroid injections could cause an infection in the joint. Repeated injections could damage the cartilage in your joint. You usually have only two or three injections a year because of this risk. Other side effects include discomfort or swelling at the injection site, a brief worsening of your arthritis pain, and a redness in your face (flushing) soon after the injection.[11]British National Formulary 61, Section 10. British Medical Association and Royal Pharmaceutical Society of Great Britain. Also available at http://bnf.org (accessed on 30 May 2012). [17]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Osteoarthritis: the care and management of osteoarthritis in adults. February 2008. Clinical guideline 59. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/cg59 (accessed on 21 June 2012).

How will treatments to manage pain affect my life?

Some people dislike taking medicines every day. You need to know whether your medicines interact with other herbal medicines, vitamin supplements, or foods. If you take some types of painkillers over a long time, you may also need to take medicines to protect your stomach lining.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT COMPLEMENTARY TREATMENTS

What is complementary treatment?

There are many different types of complementary therapy, including acupuncture, aromatherapy, transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation (TENS), dietary supplements, and massage.

Acupuncture: involves treatment with needles. Usually, about six needles are placed around the painful area and sometimes in other areas as well. A course of treatment usually consists of six or more sessions.

Aromatherapy: uses essential plant oils as a therapy. The oils may be applied directly to the skin or release an aroma that is inhaled.

Transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation (TENS): a type of electrotherapy that can be used at home. With TENS, an electrode is applied to the skin at varying intervals as required.

Dietary supplements: Glucosamine and chondroitin are similar to natural substances that form cartilage in the body. They can be bought from pharmacies in the UK. Glucosamine is available in two forms: glucosamine hydrochloride and glucosamine sulphate. Chondroitin is available in the form of chondroitin sulphate.

Who can choose complementary treatment?

Anyone can try alternative or complementary therapies to relieve their arthritis pain, although these treatments may not be available on the NHS.  

You may be able to borrow a TENS machine from the NHS for a short period. 

What are the effects of complementary treatment on pain?

There is some evidence that acupuncture and glucosamine sulphate can help with pain.

Glucosamine may reduce pain by about 17 points on a scale of 1 to 100.

Acupuncture can provide short to medium-term pain relief for some people. It doesn’t work for everyone and we don’t know in advance who will benefit.

Studies have shown that glucosamine sulphate may be more effective than glucosamine hydrochloride and chondroitin in helping with the symptoms of arthritis.

There’s no evidence that aromatherapy or TENS can help with pain from osteoarthritis of the hip.

What are the effects of complementary treatment on how well you can get around?

We don’t know whether any complementary treatments improve mobility.

What are the effects of complementary treatment on what you can do?

We don’t know whether any complementary treatments improve your ability to carry out everyday activities like washing and dressing.

What are the unwanted side effects of complementary treatment?

Complementary therapies can have side effects and may interact with prescribed drugs. Glucosamine can interfere with the blood-thinning drug warfarin. If you are taking other medicines, check with your pharmacist or doctor to make sure that glucosamine is safe to take with them.

There is a risk of infection from needles used in acupuncture if they are not sterilised properly.

How will complementary treatment affect my life?

Complementary therapies can be expensive and time consuming.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT SURGERY

What is surgery?

There are two main types of hip replacement surgery for osteoarthritis of the hip.

The most common type is total hip replacement, which involves removing the damaged bones and inserting a new hip joint.

A less common type is hip resurfacing, where the damaged surfaces of the hip joint are smoothed and covered with a metal surface. 

Who can choose surgery?

Most people with hip osteoarthritis won’t need surgery and can manage their condition with other treatments. Surgery is usually for people who have severe symptoms and have tried other treatments without success. You may wish to consider surgery if:  

  • You are in constant pain
  • You have severe pain in your hip that stops you from sleeping
  • Your independence is reduced because you can't do everyday things for yourself, like getting in and out of the bath or tying your own shoelaces
  • The pain and problems you have in getting around and doing everyday things are making you depressed
  • You can no longer do things that you used to do, like going out in the car to see friends or family
  • You have a job but the pain in your hip is stopping you from working.

You don’t need to rush into a decision about surgery. Arthritis usually gets worse quite slowly, so you have time to decide what you want to do. Not everyone finds that their pain gets worse, and some people manage to live with it. But if you have symptoms that are stopping you from enjoying life, you may wish to think about having surgery before any disability you may have gets worse.

If you’re considering surgery, you’ll need to see an orthopaedic surgeon for a clinical examination and assessment, along with an x-ray if you’ve not already had one. The surgeon will look at the damage to your joint and consider which type of operation and artificial joint is best, along with discussing what expectations you have of surgery.

The majority of people with hip osteoarthritis will not be suitable for resurfacing. It is most often used for younger people.

You may also have an anaesthetic assessment before surgery. The anaesthetist will ask about your general fitness and consider whether a general anaesthetic (where you are asleep during surgery) or a nerve block (where you are awake during surgery, but don’t feel any pain) is better for you. 

What are the effects of surgery on pain?

Hip replacement usually works very well to relieve pain and stiffness. If you have a hip replacement operation there is a good chance that the pain and stiffness in your hip will get better or go away completely.[18]Ethgen O, Bruyere O, Richy F, et al. Health-related quality of life in total hip and total knee arthroplasty: a qualitative and systematic review of the literature. Journal of Bone and Joint Surgery: American Volume. 2004; 86: 963-974.

But not everyone is happy with the result. Surgery is not guaranteed to completely rid you of your symptoms. You may still have hip pain and stiffness.

In one group of more than 1,500 people who had a hip replacement, between 61 in 100 and 84 in 100 patients rated their satisfaction with the operation as good or very good. People were less satisfied if they needed surgery a second time on the replacement hip. These figures are from an older study so may be a little out of date.[19]Espehaug B, Havelin L, Engesoeter L, et al. Patient satisfaction and function after primary and revision total hip replacement. Clinical Orthopaedics and Related Research. 1998; 351: 135-148. 

In a group of 126 people suitable for hip resurfacing, resurfacing and hip replacement seem to work about as well as each other to improve pain.[20]Costa ML, Achten J, Parsons NR, et al. Total hip arthroplasty versus resurfacing arthroplasty in the treatment of patients with arthritis of the hip joint: single centre, parallel group, assessor blinded, randomised controlled trial. BMJ. 2012; 344: e2147.

What are the effects of surgery on how well you can get around?

Most people can get around much better after surgery than before the operation. You may not move as freely as people who haven’t had hip arthritis.[18]Ethgen O, Bruyere O, Richy F, et al. Health-related quality of life in total hip and total knee arthroplasty: a qualitative and systematic review of the literature. Journal of Bone and Joint Surgery: American Volume. 2004; 86: 963-974. 

Immediately after the surgery you are likely to have pain and stiffness from the operation.

Before the operation, you may have a visit from an occupational therapist to check whether you will need adaptations to your home to help you while you are recovering from surgery. This could include handrails by the stairs.

An artificial hip has some limitations. For example, positions such as squatting aren't recommended because of the risk of dislocation.

If you are suitable for resurfacing, you may have a greater range of movement than if you have hip replacement.

What are the effects of surgery on what you can do?

Hip replacement or resurfacing can improve your ability to do everyday tasks by relieving pain and improving mobility. Everyday things like getting dressed, climbing the stairs, and getting in and out of the bath should be easier.[19]Espehaug B, Havelin L, Engesoeter L, et al. Patient satisfaction and function after primary and revision total hip replacement. Clinical Orthopaedics and Related Research. 1998; 351: 135-148.    

What are the unwanted side effects of surgery?

There are risks associated with all types of surgery, including hip replacement. There can be complications including blood clots, infections, bleeding, and a risk of death.[23]Royal College of Surgeons of England, British Orthopaedic Association. National total hip replacement outcome study: final report to the Department of Health. June 2000. Available at http://www.rcseng.ac.uk (accessed on 12 March 2012).

  • About 3 in 100 people get blood clots in their legs. You will need to take blood-thinning medicine and wear support stockings if this happens. In less than 1 in 100 people, the blood clot reaches the lungs. This can be more dangerous.
  • The nerves in your leg can be damaged during surgery, making your foot weak or floppy. This happens to about 3 in 100 people.
  • Three or four in 1000 people die during an operation to replace a hip because of osteoarthritis. 

Other problems happen after surgery. Sometimes the joint comes apart (dislocates) in the months following surgery. This happens to about 4 in 100 people.[24]Phillips CB, Barrett JA, Losina E, et al. Incidence rates of dislocation, pulmonary embolism, and deep infection during the first six months after elective total hip replacement. Journal of Bone and Joint Surgery, American Volume. 2003; 85: 20-26. Also, the joint can get too loose. This can make you feel unstable, and can be painful. Loosening of the joint usually happens many years after surgery.

After hip resurfacing, about 2 in 100 people will have a fracture in the bone of their leg, where it fits into the joint. About 1 in 100 people have loosening of the joint within 5 years.[25]Amstutz HC, Le Duff MJ, Campbell PA, et al. Complications after metal-on-metal hip resurfacing arthroplasty. Orthop Clin North Am. 2011; 42: 207-230. About 5 in 100 people need a further operation within 5 years because traces of metal worn away from the new surface cause a reaction.[26]National Joint Registry for England and Wales. 9th Annual Report. 2012. Available at: http://www.njrcentre.org.uk/NjrCentre/Portals/0/Documents/England/Reports/9th_annual_report/NJR%209th%20Annual%20Report%202012.pdf (accessed on 11 October 2012).

Complications are more likely if you have other health conditions such as heart or lung disease, or if you smoke or are overweight.

There have been recent cases of one type of hip replacement - metal-on-metal (MoM) replacements - wearing out quicker than would be expected. There are also concerns that they could leak traces of metal into the bloodstream. This type of hip replacement is almost never used now, because of these problems. 

How will surgery affect my life?

Recovering from surgery can take several months and you are likely to need at least six weeks off work. Your hospital will be able to tell you more about what you can and can't do while recovering from surgery.

The rehabilitation process after surgery can be a demanding process and requires commitment.

Some artificial hip joints wear out or go wrong, meaning that you need another operation to repair or replace the joint. This can also happen after resurfacing.

New hips usually last between 10 and 15 years.[27]de Verteuil R, Imamura M, Zhu S, et al. A systematic review of the clinical effectiveness and cost-effectiveness and economic modelling of minimal incision total hip replacement approaches in the management of arthritic disease of the hip. Health Technololgy Assessements. 2008; 12: iii-iv, ix-223. If you have a hip replacement now, you may need another replacement later in life.

Animation about Osteoarthritis of the Hip

This decision aid is to help you decide which treatments you choose for osteoarthritis of the hip. There are several types of treatment available. You can try different treatments, either together or separately. The choice you make may depend on many things, including how much your osteoarthritis bothers you, how active you want to be, how long you've had osteoarthritis and which treatments you have already tried. Not all the options are suitable for everyone. 

You can combine different treatments. The options for treatment are:

  • Lifestyle changes, including weight loss and exercise
  • Physical management, including physiotherapy and occupational therapy
  • Treatments to manage pain, including tablets you take by mouth, injections into the joint and self-help support.
  • Complementary treatments, including acupuncture and nutritional supplements
  • Surgery, including hip resurfacing and total hip replacement. Surgery is usually for people with severe symptoms who have tried other treatments first.

The decisions people with osteoarthritis are making include:

  • I've just been diagnosed with hip osteoarthritis. Will I need medication or physical management, or should I try lifestyle changes first?
  • My hip pain is worsening. Should I try different treatments to the ones I have tried already?
  • I've tried other treatments, but my hip pain still interferes with my life. Is it time to consider having surgery?
  • I don't want to have surgery now. Should I try pain management treatments and physical management?

The right choice for you may change over time. This decision aid aims to help you make the right choice for you now, but you may want to change to a different option in future.

MORE INFORMATION ABOUT OSTEOARTHRITIS OF THE HIP

What does the hip do?

Your hip joint is where your thigh bone (the femur) meets your hip (the pelvis). It allows you to move your leg.

The top part of the thigh bone is shaped like a ball, and the bottom of the hip joint is shaped like a cup or socket. The way these bones fit together allows a lot of movement at your hip joint. This movement allows you to do things like sit cross-legged, run, kick a ball, and climb stairs.

A smooth material called cartilage covers the bones where they meet and rub together. This protective coating absorbs shock. It allows the bones to slide over each other effortlessly when the joint moves.

What is osteoarthritis?

Osteoarthritis is a condition of the joints. As we age, the smooth cartilage at the ends of bones can become rough and damaged. Osteoarthritis happens when the damage is worse than it is with normal wear and tear. This can cause pain and stiffness in the joint. It limits the movement of the hip where the bones meet.

Over time, the cartilage can wear away completely. The bone in your joints grows and tries to repair the damage. This bone growth happens faster than it usually would. In places, the bone forms growths called bone spurs. Bone spurs can change the shape of joints, which means they can't move as well as they should.

The result is that your hip joint may feel stiff and painful to move. You may not be able to move it as freely as you did before.

What are the symptoms of hip osteoarthritis?

Symptoms vary greatly from person to person. The amount of damage to the joints and the severity of symptoms can also vary. For example, a joint may be severely damaged without causing symptoms, or symptoms may be severe without affecting the movement of a joint.

The main symptoms of osteoarthritis are:[28]NHS Choices. Osteoarthritis – symptoms. September 2010. http://www.nhs.uk/Conditions/Osteoarthritis/Pages/Symptoms.aspx (accessed on 30 May 2012).

  • Pain, especially when walking 
  • Stiffness in the morning, which improves soon after you start to move (within half an hour)
  • Difficulty moving your affected joints.

These symptoms mean you may find it difficult to do things you used to take for granted. People with osteoarthritis of the hip often find it hard to put their shoes and socks on or to get in and out of a car.

Most people with hip osteoarthritis feel pain in the groin or outside the hip, which is worse when they move the hip. For some people, the brain will identify pain in the knee and not in the hip, because of the ‘wiring’ that transmits the pain signals. In most cases, pain will be at its worst when you walk, although it can also affect you when you are resting.

Why has this happened to me?

The exact cause of arthritis is often not clear. Arthritis is more common as people age, but not everyone gets arthritis as they get older. It is more common in people aged 50 or over. Women are more likely to develop arthritis. Other people more likely to get arthritis include:

  • People who are overweight or obese
  • People with close relatives with arthritis
  • People who have had a job that involves a lot of physical labour such as squatting or lifting.
How do I know I have osteoarthritis of the hip?

If you have symptoms like pain and stiffness around your hip, your doctor may suspect you have osteoarthritis. You may need an x-ray. Sometimes you need blood tests or scans to rule out other types of arthritis.

An x-ray may show bone spurs, loss of cartilage, and other signs of osteoarthritis.

The signs of arthritis on the x-ray have little to do with how bad your arthritis feels. Some people with little pain have lots of signs of arthritis on the x-ray, while other people with more pain have fewer signs.

That’s why the choices you make about treatment should depend on how you feel about your arthritis rather than the results of an x-ray.

Your other hip, your knees, spine, or arms may have some arthritis in them as well and this can affect which treatment will help you most. When you have tests for arthritis, it’s important to let your doctors know about problems that you might have in other joints, as well as any other health problems.

Many people have osteoarthritis in their joints but do not realise it because they do not have any symptoms. They do not have any pain or any difficulty in moving their joints.

We don't know why some people who have signs of osteoarthritis on x-rays have no symptoms while others with less severe signs have a lot of pain and disability.

What will happen?

Although there is no cure for osteoarthritis there are treatments that can help control the pain and discomfort it causes, and help you move more freely. In most people osteoarthritis does not lead to disability. With the right support, you can lead a healthy, active life.

Osteoarthritis doesn’t always get worse and it doesn’t always lead to disability. Osteoarthritis usually gets worse slowly over time. 

Being overweight tends to make hip arthritis worse, and keeping active (including gentle aerobic exercise) is likely to make it better.[34]Lievense AM, Bierman-Zeinstra SM, Verhagen AP, et al. Influence of obesity on the development of osteoarthritis of the hip: a systematic review. Rheumatology (Oxford). 2002;  41: 1155-1162.

What happens to you will depend on many things, including how old you are, how active your lifestyle is, and what treatments you choose.

Some people benefit from self-help support programmes, which use talking therapies such as cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT). These programmes can help people feel more in control of their arthritis, by learning to manage their pain, exercise and keep active safely, solve problems, reduce anxiety and keep healthy.

Programmes can be given in groups or individually, by healthcare professionals or by other patients. These programmes can be an addition to other treatments such as pain medication or physical management, or can be tried before other treatments.
