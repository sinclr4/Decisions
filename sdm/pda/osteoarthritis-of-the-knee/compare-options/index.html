STEP 5: MY DECISION

Our Decision Support telephone service is here to assist and guide you through the Shared Decision Making process.

The Decision Support team is made of professional Health Coaches who are clinicians, commonly nurses, who have undertaken additional training to become a Health Coach.

The Health Coaches will assist you by:

  • Providing you with information
  • Helping you to understand your condition
  • Recognise what is important to you regarding the outcome of treatment
  • Identifying potential solutions
  • Encourage you in discussing options with your family
  • Transferring skills which will assist you in using the information and resources available to you
  • Support you in building confidence in discussing your choice with your doctor
  • You may find that this can be achieved in one telephone call with a Health Coach however; if further calls are required to support you in reaching your decision the Health Coach will schedule these with you.

Important Things To Note

  • A Health Coach does not know which treatment option is best for you.
  • The Health Coach does not have information about your medical history.
  • You will still need to talk over your decision and reasons for your decision with your doctor or nurse.

MORE INFORMATION ABOUT OSTEOARTHRITIS OF THE KNEE

What does the knee do?

The knee is the hinge joint between your lower leg and upper leg. It allows the leg to bend, so you can do things like walk, run, kneel, sit, and kick.

A smooth material called cartilage covers the bones where they meet and rub together. This protective coating absorbs shock. It allows the bones to slide over each other effortlessly when the joint moves.

What is osteoarthritis of the knee?

Osteoarthritis is a condition that breaks down cartilage and makes it rough and thinner. This can cause pain and stiffness in the joint and can reduce movement of the knee. Osteoarthritis can also cause swelling of the knee.

Sometimes osteoarthritis causes bone spurs (also known as osteophytes) to grow on the inside of the knee. Bone spurs rub against the knee bones. This can be painful.

If you have osteoarthritis in your knees, it is likely that both your knees will be affected over time, unless it happened as the result of an injury or another condition affecting only one knee.

What are the symptoms of knee osteoarthritis?

The main symptoms of knee osteoarthritis are:

  • Pain, especially while walking. It may be worse going up or down hills or stairs.
  • Stiffness, especially in the morning.

Sometimes, your knees may 'give way' beneath you or make it difficult to straighten your legs. You may also hear a soft, grating sound when you move the affected joint.

The symptoms that people get vary. Some people have much more trouble with pain and stiffness than others.

Why has this happened to me?

The exact cause of osteoarthritis is often not clear. Arthritis is more common as people age, but not everyone gets arthritis as they get older. It is more common in people aged 50 or over. Women are more likely to develop osteoarthritis.

Other people more likely to get osteoarthritis include:

  • People who are overweight or obese
  • People with close relatives with arthritis
  • People who’ve had a joint injury before.
How do I find out I have osteoarthritis of the knee?

If you have symptoms like pain and stiffness around your knee, your doctor may suspect you have osteoarthritis. You may need an x-ray to be sure. This can show changes to the bone and damage to cartilage.

Sometimes you need blood tests or scans to rule out other types of arthritis, such as rheumatoid arthritis.

The signs of arthritis on the x-ray have little to do with how bad your arthritis feels. Some people with little pain have lots of signs of arthritis on the x-ray, while other people with more pain have fewer signs.

That’s why the choices you make about treatment depend more on how you feel about your arthritis than the results of the x-ray.

How many people have osteoarthritis of the knee?

Osteoarthritis is the most common type of arthritis in the UK. About 2 million people in the UK visit their doctor because of osteoarthritis each year.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Osteoarthritis: the care and management of osteoarthritis in adults. February 2008. Clinical guideline 59. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/cg59 (accessed on 21 June 2012). Up to 8.5 million people in the UK may have joint pain caused by osteoarthritis. The NHS in England and Wales does more than 60,000 knee replacement operations every year.[2]National Joint Registry. National Joint Registry for England and Wales 4th annual report. Available at http://www.njrcentre.org.uk/NjrCentre/Portals/0/Documents/England/Reports/NJR_AR_4.pdf (accessed on 21 June 2012). 

Many people have osteoarthritis in their joints but do not realise it because they do not have any pain or stiffness.

What will happen?

Osteoarthritis tends to get gradually worse over time. However, there's no way to predict whether your pain will get worse and if it does, how fast. The amount of pain you feel now doesn't predict how you'll feel in the future.

Although there is no cure for knee osteoarthritis, most people can live full and active lives with treatments and support to help manage pain and stiffness.

About 33 in 100 people find their knee osteoarthritis improves over several years. During the same time period, another 33 in 100 stay about the same, and the remaining 33 in 100 get worse. Being overweight is likely to make knee osteoarthritis worse.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Osteoarthritis: the care and management of osteoarthritis in adults. February 2008. Clinical guideline 59. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/cg59 (accessed on 21 June 2012).

Some people benefit from self-help support programmes, which use talking therapies such as cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT). These programmes can help people feel more in control of their arthritis, by learning to manage their pain, exercise and keep active safely, solve problems, reduce anxiety and keep healthy.

Programmes can be given in groups or individually, by healthcare professionals or by other patients. These programmes can be an addition to other treatments such as pain medication or physical management, or can be tried before other treatments.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT LIFESTYLE CHANGES

What are lifestyle changes?

These are changes that you can make yourself that may help improve your symptoms. The main changes are:

  • Losing weight (if you are overweight)
  • Taking more regular exercise.

If you want to lose weight, you will need to change your diet to include fewer calories, and take exercise to burn off excess calories. Your doctor or practice nurse may be able to help you make a weight loss plan. You could also try a commercial weight-loss organisation such as Weight Watchers.

Exercise can include swimming, using the gym, walking, gardening, or any activity that keeps you active. We’re not talking here about specific exercises intended to strengthen your knee, which might be recommended by a physiotherapist. Those are covered under the section on physical management.

Some people benefit from self-help support programmes, which use talking therapies such as cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT). These programmes can help people feel more in control of their arthritis, by learning to manage their pain, exercise and keep active safely, solve problems, reduce anxiety and keep healthy.

Programmes can be given in groups or individually, by healthcare professionals or by other patients. These programmes can be an addition to other treatments such as pain medication or physical management, or can be tried before other treatments.

Who can choose lifestyle changes?

Anyone can take a look at their lifestyle and think about making some changes. Your doctor may also recommend you lose some weight or take more regular exercise, if you need to do so.

What are the effects of lifestyle changes on pain?

Losing weight can help people’s knee pain. A group of people who lost an average of 11 kilograms over a year said their knee pain improved by 7 points on a scale of 1 to 20.[4]Bliddal H, Leeds AR, Stigsgaard L, et al. Weight loss as treatment for knee osteoarthritis symptoms in obese patients: 1-year results from a randomised controlled trial. Annals of the Rheumatic Diseases. 2011; 70: 1798-1803.

Taking regular exercise may also help reduce pain for people with knee arthritis. Research shows that regular exercise may reduce pain by 1 point on a scale of 1 to 20. The reduction in pain may sound small, but that is an average. Some people may get more benefit, and others may get none.[5]Fransen M, S McConnell. Exercise for osteoarthritis of the knee (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

What are the effects of lifestyle changes on how well you can move around?

Taking regular exercise and losing weight if you need to can help you to move around more easily. You may be able to walk further and faster, climb stairs more easily and move your joint more freely.[6]Messier SP, Loeser RF, Miller GD, et al. Exercise and dietary weight loss in overweight and obese older adults with knee osteoarthritis. Arthritis and Rheumatology. 2004; 50: 1501-1510.

What are the effects of lifestyle changes on what you can do?

Taking regular exercise and reaching a healthy weight can improve your general fitness. This may mean you are able to do more everyday activities.[7]Devos-Comby L, Cronan T, Roesch SC. Do exercise and self-management interventions benefit patients with osteoarthritis of the knee? A metaanalytic review. Journal of Rheumatology. 2006; 33: 744-756. [8]Fransen M, McConnell S, Bell M. Exercise for osteoarthritis of the hip or knee (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library.

What are the effects of lifestyle changes on your quality of life?

Regular exercise can help you feel better about your life.[9]Thorstensson CA, Roos EM, Petersson IF, et al. Six-week high-intensity exercise program for middle-aged patients with knee osteoarthritis: a randomized controlled trial. BMC Musculoskeletal Disorders. 2005; 6: 27. Exercise is good for relieving stress and can improve your mood, because it releases chemicals called beta-endorphins, which are natural painkillers and make you feel happier.

What are the unwanted side effects of lifestyle changes?

Some people find exercise can make their knee pain worse at first, especially high-intensity exercise like running. Talk to your doctor or physiotherapist about what sort of exercise is best for you. Some people with other medical conditions such as heart disease need to check with their doctor before starting a new exercise programme.

Losing weight through a very strict diet can mean you don’t get the right nutrients. A dietician can help you find a diet that ensures you get enough nutrients, while not taking in too many calories.

How will lifestyle changes affect my life?

Making changes such as taking regular exercise and losing weight takes time and commitment. You need to make time for exercise and change your diet.  Results will not happen overnight.  You need to make long-term changes to get long-term benefits for your arthritis.

Taking regular exercise and keeping to a healthy weight can reduce your chances of other illnesses, such as heart disease and diabetes, as well as the effect they may have on your knee arthritis.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT PHYSICAL MANAGEMENT

What is physical management?

Physical management includes physiotherapy and occupational therapy.

A physiotherapist can work with you to find physical ways to improve your knee pain and stiffness. This may include hands-on manipulation of your leg, teaching you specific exercises to strengthen your leg muscles around the knee, and showing you how to tape up your knee.

A physiotherapist may provide or recommend other aids like inserts to your shoes, knee braces, or walking canes. The physiotherapist may recommend you use cold packs to reduce swelling of your knee, or massage your knee at home.

An occupational therapist can help you find ways around day-to-day problems caused by your arthritis. That might include grab bars or raised seats around the home, or showing you how to do things like put on your shoes and socks, when it’s hard to bend your knee.

Some people benefit from self-help support programmes, which use talking therapies such as cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT). These programmes can help people feel more in control of their arthritis, by learning to manage their pain, exercise and keep active safely, solve problems, reduce anxiety and keep healthy.

Programmes can be given in groups or individually, by healthcare professionals or by other patients. These programmes can be an addition to other treatments such as pain medication or physical management, or can be tried before other treatments.

Who can choose physical management?

Most people with osteoarthritis of the knee can have physical management. Your GP or hospital consultant can refer you to a physiotherapist or occupational therapist. Some of these services are also available privately without a GP referral.

What are the effects of physical management on pain?

Knee exercises can strengthen muscles around the knee. This supports the joint and can make it less painful.[10]Jamtvedt G, Dahm KT, Christie A, et al. Physical therapy interventions for patients with osteoarthritis of the knee: an overview of systematic reviews. Physical Therapy. 2008; 88: 123-136. Your physiotherapist will show you how to do these exercises.

Shoe inserts, taping up the knee, and wearing a knee brace may also help relieve pain.[11]Hinman RS, Crossley KM, McConnell J, et al. Efficacy of knee tape in the management of osteoarthritis of the knee: blinded randomised controlled trial. BMJ. 2003; 327: 135. [12]Brouwer RW, Jakma TS, Verhagen AP, et al. Braces and orthoses for treating osteoarthritis of the knee (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. [13]Raja K. Efficacy of knee braces and foot orthoses in conservative management of knee osteoarthritis: a systematic review. American Journal of Physical Medicine & Rehabilitation. 2011; 90: 247-262.

Some types of physical management can help you walk further and faster. A comparison of various therapeutic exercises on the functional status of patients with knee osteoarthritis. Seminars in Arthritis and Rheumatism. 2003; 32: 398-406.

What are the unwanted side effects of physical management?

Manipulation of the knee joint and knee exercises may cause some pain and discomfort at first. This is because the treatment is stretching and working the muscle.

How will physical management affect my life?

Physiotherapy and occupational therapy require a number of sessions. You’ll need to set aside time for this.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT TREATMENTS TO MANAGE PAIN

What are treatments to manage pain?

There are two main types of treatment to manage pain: medication and self-help.

Pain medications are drug treatments designed to offer pain relief. Some can be bought from a pharmacy without a prescription, while others need to be prescribed by a doctor. They come in different formulations - tablets, gels or creams, and injections.

Over-the-counter tablets: You can buy paracetamol, paracetamol combined with low-dose codeine, and some non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) such as ibuprofen and diclofenac in a low dose, without a prescription.

Over-the counter gels or creams: You can buy gels and creams containing painkillers such as ibuprofen and diclofenac without a prescription.

Prescription medicines: These include higher-dose and more potent NSAIDs or cyclo-oxygenase 2 (COX-2) inhibitors (often called coxibs). Opioid medicines like codeine, tramadol, and dihydrocodeine may be used if NSAIDs don’t work well enough. Capsaicin cream, which is made from chilli peppers, produces a burning sensation at first but may reduce the pain in the joint.

Steroid injections: Steroids can be injected directly into the knee joint to provide pain relief for people who have a flare-up of bad pain. They are not used as a long-term treatment.

Some people benefit from self-help support programmes, which use talking therapies such as cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT). These programmes can help people feel more in control of their arthritis, by learning to manage their pain, exercise and keep active safely, solve problems, reduce anxiety and keep healthy.

Programmes can be given in groups or individually, by healthcare professionals or by other patients. These programmes can be an addition to other treatments such as pain medication or physical management, or can be tried before other treatments.

Who can choose treatments to manage pain?

Most people can safely take one or more of these medicines. It is important to read the safety information and talk to the pharmacist if you are buying pain medications without a prescription. Some medicines may not be appropriate for you, particularly if you have other health problems or are already taking other medicines.

Steroid injections are not offered to everyone with knee arthritis. They are used for bad flare-ups of pain. People who need them usually have a maximum of four injections a year.

What are the effects of treatments to manage pain on pain?

Over-the-counter medicines can help ease arthritis pain for many people.

Simple painkillers like paracetamol can help reduce pain for some people, although the reduction in pain may be small (4 points more than taking a pretend (placebo) pill on a scale of 1 to 100).[24]Towheed T, Maxwell L, Judd M, et al. Acetaminophen for osteoarthritis (Cochrane Review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. Gels and creams containing NSAIDs can reduce pain in the short term (less than four weeks) but we don’t know if they work after that.[25]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. June 2011. Clinical guideline 59. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/cg59 (accessed on 25 June 2012).

NSAIDs such as ibuprofen may work better than paracetamol.[26]Wegman A, Van Der Windt D, Van Tulder M, et al. Nonsteroidal antiinflammatory drugs or acetaminophen for osteoarthritis of the hip or knee? A systematic review of evidence and guidelines. Journal of Rheumatology. 2004; 31: 344-354.

Some people find that NSAIDs don’t work so well after they have taken them for a few years.[27]Bjordal JM, Ljunggren AE, Klovning A, et al. Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, including cyclo-oxygenase-2 inhibitors, in osteoarthritic knee pain: meta-analysis of randomised placebo controlled trials. BMJ. 2004; 329: 1317.

Capsaicin cream may help about 33 in 100 people with knee arthritis. The effect may not be immediate but may build up over about a week.[28]Deal CL, Schnitzer TJ, Lipstein E, et al. Treatment of arthritis with topical capsaicin: a double-blind trial. Clinical Therapeutics. 1991; 13: 383-395.

Prescription drugs like stronger NSAIDs and COX-2 inhibitors can help reduce pain if over-the-counter treatments don’t work well enough. They reduce pain 10 points more than taking a pretend (placebo) pill, on a scale of 1 to 100.[27]Bjordal JM, Ljunggren AE, Klovning A, et al. Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, including cyclo-oxygenase-2 inhibitors, in osteoarthritic knee pain: meta-analysis of randomised placebo controlled trials. BMJ. 2004; 329: 1317.

Steroid injections can provide temporary pain relief and reduce swelling. They reduce pain 21 points more than having a pretend (placebo) injection, on a scale of 1 to 100. The effect lasts for one to four weeks.[30]Bellamy N, Campbell J, Robinson V, et al. Intraarticular corticosteroid for treatment of osteoarthritis of the knee (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. They are usually one-off treatments and are used for flare-ups if your knee osteoarthritis gets worse.

Many people find self-help programmes help them manage pain better. 

What are the effects of treatments to manage pain on how well you can get around?

If medication can take away some of the pain, this can help with improving mobility. NSAIDs, like ibuprofen, can also help reduce joint stiffness.[27]Bjordal JM, Ljunggren AE, Klovning A, et al. Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, including cyclo-oxygenase-2 inhibitors, in osteoarthritic knee pain: meta-analysis of randomised placebo controlled trials. BMJ. 2004; 329: 1317. A type of gel containing the NSAID diclofenac may help your knees feel less stiff and make it easier to stand up, walk and climb stairs.[32]Bookman AA, Williams KS, Shainhouse JZ. We don’t know if steroid injections will improve how well you can get around. There isn’t enough research looking at this.

What are the effects of treatments to manage pain on what you can do?

Medication can help reduce the pain and tenderness of arthritis. It may help you do everyday things, like getting out of a chair or going for a walk.[27]Bjordal JM, Ljunggren AE, Klovning A, et al. Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, including cyclo-oxygenase-2 inhibitors, in osteoarthritic knee pain: meta-analysis of randomised placebo controlled trials. BMJ. 2004; 329: 1317.

What are the effects of treatments to manage pain on your quality of life?

Long-term pain can have a huge impact on your quality of life. It can upset your sleep and make you feel depressed and anxious. Finding an effective treatment that eases the pain can make a big difference. Feeling more in control of your pain, from taking part in a self-help programme, may also improve your quality of life.

What are the unwanted side effects of treatments to manage pain?

All pain medications can have some side effects. The risk of side effects becomes more likely with stronger drugs and if you are taking other medications.

If you are taking any other medications (including herbal medicines) it is important to check with your doctor or pharmacist that these will not interact dangerously with your pain medicine.

Taking more than the recommended dose of paracetamol can be dangerous and cause liver damage.

NSAID tablets, including ibuprofen, can cause internal bleeding. They may not be suitable for people with certain conditions, such as asthma, a peptic ulcer, or angina, or if you have had a heart attack or stroke.[35]British National Formulary. Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs. Section 10.1.1.  British Medical Association and Royal Pharmaceutical Society of Great Britain. Also available at http://bnf.org (accessed on 26 June 2012). Taking NSAIDs regularly slightly increases the risk of a heart attack or stroke.[36]Kearney PM, Baigent C, Godwin J, et al. Do selective cyclo-oxygenase-2 inhibitors and traditional non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs increase the risk of atherothrombosis? BMJ. 2006; 332: 1302-1308.

Opioids, like codeine, can cause serious side effects. Common side effects include feeling drowsy, getting constipation, feeling sick, and vomiting. More serious effects include breathing problems and low blood pressure.[37]British National Formulary. Opioid analgesics. Section 4.7.2.  British Medical Association and Royal Pharmaceutical Society of Great Britain. Also available at http://bnf.org (accessed on 26 June 2012).

Tramadol is not suitable if you have epilepsy, and dihydrocodeine is not recommended for patients with chronic obstructive pulmonary disease.[38]Electronic Medicines Compendium. Dihydrocodeine summary of product characteristics. 4.3 Contraindications. http://www.medicines.org.uk/EMC/medicine/23620/SPC/Dihydrocodeine+30mg+tablets/#CONTRAINDICATIONS (accessed on 26 June 2012).

There is a very small risk that corticosteroid injections could lead to an infection in the joint. For this reason, steroid injections aren’t recommended for three months prior to planned total knee replacement surgery. Repeated injections could also damage the cartilage in your joint. Minor side effects can include discomfort or swelling at the injection site, a brief worsening of your arthritis pain and a redness in your face (flushing) soon after the injection.[39]British National Formulary. Local corticosteroid injections. Section 7.4.8. British Medical Association and Royal Pharmaceutical Society of Great Britain. Creams or gels with capsaicin often irritate the skin, giving a burning feeling. Nearly half of the people who use them get this irritation.[41]Grace D, Rogers J, Skeith K, et al. Topical diclofenac versus placebo: a double blind, randomized clinical trial in patients with osteoarthritis of the knee. Journal of Rheumatology. 1999; 26: 2659-2663.

How will treatments to manage pain affect my life?

You might need to take medicines every day to manage your pain. When using pain medicines, you should:

  • Be aware that they can interact with other medicines, including herbal medicines, and can cause side effects
  • Talk to your pharmacist or GP before starting a new medicine to discuss your medical history, other health problems and any other medicines or herbal and vitamin supplements you already take
  • Carefully follow the instructions for using them
  • Use them at the lowest dose and frequency that controls your symptoms.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT COMPLEMENTARY TREATMENTS

What are complementary treatments?

There are many different types of complementary therapy. The ones we look at here are acupuncture, aromatherapy, transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation (TENS), and the dietary supplements glucosamine and chondroitin.

Acupuncture: This involves treatment with needles. Usually, about six needles are placed around the painful area and sometimes in other areas as well. Six or more sessions will usually be needed as the effect gradually builds up.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Osteoarthritis: the care and management of osteoarthritis in adults. February 2008. Clinical guideline 59. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/cg59 (accessed on 21 June 2012). [43]Miller E, Maimon Y, Rosenblatt Y, et al. Delayed effect of acupuncture treatment in OA of the knee: a blinded, randomized, controlled trial. Evidence-based Complementary and Alternative Medicine. 2011. Available at http://www.hindawi.com/journals/ecam/2011/792975/ (accessed on 21 June 2012).

Aromatherapy: This treatment uses essential plant oils as a therapy. The oils may be applied directly to the skin or release an aroma that is inhaled.

Transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation (TENS): This is a type of electrotherapy that can be used at home. With TENS, an electrode is applied to the skin to stimulate nerve fibres and kill the pain.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Osteoarthritis: the care and management of osteoarthritis in adults. February 2008. Clinical guideline 59. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/cg59 (accessed on 21 June 2012).

Dietary supplements: Glucosamine and chondroitin (two of the “building blocks” of cartilage) can be bought from pharmacies in the UK. Glucosamine is available as glucosamine hydrochloride or glucosamine sulphate, while chondroitin is available in the form of chondroitin sulphate.

Who can choose complementary treatments?

Anyone can try alternative or complementary therapies to relieve their arthritis pain, although they are not usually available on the NHS.

You may be able to borrow a TENS machine from the NHS for a short period.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Osteoarthritis: the care and management of osteoarthritis in adults. February 2008. Clinical guideline 59. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/cg59 (accessed on 21 June 2012).

What are the effects of complementary treatments on pain?

We don’t know whether most types of complementary treatments improve pain, because there is not enough good-quality research.

Acupuncture can provide short to medium-term pain relief for some people, but it is unclear which people with osteoarthritis will particularly benefit.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Osteoarthritis: the care and management of osteoarthritis in adults. February 2008. Clinical guideline 59. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/cg59 (accessed on 21 June 2012). [47]Witt C, Brinkhaus B, Jena S, et al. Acupuncture in patients with osteoarthritis of the knee: a randomised trial. FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT SURGERY

What is surgery?

During a knee replacement operation, the surgeon removes the rough, damaged surfaces of the knee joint and replaces them with new, smoother surfaces made from metal and plastic. Two or three surfaces can be replaced, such as:

  • The bottom of your thigh bone
  • The top of your shin bone
  • The back of your kneecap.

There are two types of knee replacement. A total knee replacement resurfaces your whole knee. A partial knee replacement or partial knee resurfacing resurfaces only the inner or outer half of your knee, so less of your original bone is removed. Partial knee replacements only work for people with arthritis in one half of the knee (which for most people is the inner half, nearest your other leg).

Knee replacement surgery takes 60 minutes to 90 minutes. You'll be given either a general anaesthetic (a drug that puts you to sleep) or an epidural (an injection into your spine). If you have an epidural, the lower part of your body will be numb. You won't be able to see the surgery but you will be able to hear what's going on.

Arthroscopy can be used to examine and treat the inside of the knee joint. You'll usually be given a general anaesthetic. Your surgeon makes small cuts into the knee joint. A thin tube with a camera is put through one of the cuts to examine the joint. Fluid can be flushed through one of the other cuts to wash out the joint. This is called arthroscopic lavage. Small surgical instruments can remove damaged tissue at the same time. This is called debridement. At the end of the procedure the excess fluid is drained out of the joint and the cuts are closed with stitches.

Who can choose surgery?

A knee replacement is normally only recommended if other treatments, such as lifestyle changes, physical treatment, or pain medications, haven't helped. Surgery is usually for people who have severe symptoms.

You may be offered knee replacement surgery if:[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Osteoarthritis: the care and management of osteoarthritis in adults. February 2008. Clinical guideline 59. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/cg59 (accessed on 21 June 2012).

  • You have severe pain, swelling, and stiffness in your knee joint and you find it hard to get around
  • Your knee pain is so severe that it interferes with your quality of life and sleep
  • Everyday tasks, such as shopping or getting out of the bath, are difficult or impossible
  • You cannot work or have a normal social life.

Guidelines for doctors say that people should be referred for surgery before the knee joint causes severe pain and is very hard to move.

Adults of any age can be considered for a knee replacement. Knee replacements are more likely to last in older people. For younger, active people, the artificial joint is more likely to wear out. Doctors may suggest that younger people wait until they are older for a knee replacement.[53]Johnson VG, Worland RL, Keenan J, et al. Patient demographics as a predictor of the ten-year survival rate in primary total knee replacement. Journal of Bone and Joint Surgery: British Volume. 2003; 85: 52-56.

You need to be physically fit enough to have the operation.

Arthroscopy is only recommended for people with knee problems that are likely to be due to loose pieces of bone and tissue in the joint.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Osteoarthritis: the care and management of osteoarthritis in adults. February 2008. Clinical guideline 59. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/cg59 (accessed on 21 June 2012).

What are the unwanted side effects of surgery?

There are risks associated with all types of surgery, including knee replacement. There can be complications including blood clots, infections, bleeding, and a risk of death.

Most knee joint replacements are problem-free but complications do arise in about 5 in every 100 cases.[68]NHS Choices. Knee replacement - risks of surgery. Available at http://www.nhs.uk/Conditions/Knee-replacement/Pages/Risks.aspx (accessed on 26 June 2012).

Complications are more likely if you have other health conditions such as heart or lung disease, or if you smoke or are overweight or obese. Your doctors will assess whether you are fit for surgery and will discuss these risks with you.

The biggest problem in the first few days after surgery is getting a blood clot in the veins, known as a deep vein thrombosis or DVT. DVT affects one in four people who have knee surgery. You have problems bending the knee.

You can buy simple painkillers, such as paracetamol, from your pharmacy. Your GP may also prescribe pain medication. You are unlikely to need to go to hospital to get pain medications. You might need to take medicines every day to manage your pain. Self-help support programmes vary in length.

Complementary treatments

Often, complementary therapists recommend a course of treatment. These can vary in length and be expensive.

Surgery

You will need to stay in hospital for three to five days after knee replacement surgery. For the first three to six weeks after the operation you will need a walking aid, such as crutches, to help support you. Most people can get back to their normal activities within two to three months.  Some people might need up to six months.

After arthroscopy to wash out the joint and remove damaged tissue you will normally be able to go home once you have recovered from the anaesthetic. You may have to rest with the leg raised for a short period, and you will need crutches at first to help support you when walking. It may take up to three weeks before you can use your knee normally.
