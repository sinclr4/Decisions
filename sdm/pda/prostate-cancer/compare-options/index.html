Our Decision Support telephone service is here to assist and guide you through the Shared Decision Making process.

The Decision Support team is made of professional Health Coaches who are clinicians, commonly nurses, who have undertaken additional training to become a Health Coach.

The Health Coaches will assist you by:

  • Providing you with information
  • Helping you to understand your condition
  • Recognise what is important to you regarding the outcome of treatment
  • Identifying potential solutions
  • Encourage you in discussing options with your family
  • Transferring skills which will assist you in using the information and resources available to you
  • Support you in building confidence in discussing your choice with your doctor
  • You may find that this can be achieved in one telephone call with a Health Coach however; if further calls are required to support you in reaching your decision the Health Coach will schedule these with you.

Important Things To Note

  • A Health Coach does not know which treatment option is best for you.
  • The Health Coach does not have information about your medical history.
  • You will still need to talk over your decision and reasons for your decision with your doctor or nurse.

MORE INFORMATION ABOUT LOCALISED PROSTATE CANCER

What is the prostate?

The prostate is a walnut-sized gland that sits underneath the bladder. It surrounds the tube (urethra) that you urinate through. 

The main role of the prostate is to help make semen. Semen, which contains sperm, comes out of the penis when a man ejaculates.

What is localised prostate cancer?

Localised prostate cancer is a cancer that has not spread outside of your prostate.

If prostate cancer spreads to nearby parts of the body it is called locally advanced prostate cancer. This patient decision aid is only about localised prostate cancer.

When your body's cells are healthy, they grow and divide to form new cells as your body needs them. When cells grow old and die, new cells take their place.

Sometimes this process breaks down. New cells form rapidly, when the body doesn't need them, and old cells don't die when they should.

In prostate cancer, some of the cells in the prostate gland start to grow out of control, invading and destroying healthy cells. The extra cells can form a mass of tissue called a cancer, growth, or tumour.

Some prostate cancers grow slowly and do not need treatment.

How do I find out if I have localised prostate cancer?

Four different tests are commonly used to detect prostate cancer.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Prostate cancer: diagnosis and treatment. Clinical guideline 58. February 2008. Available at: http://www.nice.org.uk/CG58 (accessed on 22 August 2012). If you have been diagnosed with prostate cancer, you will probably have had all of these tests already. They are:

  • Digital rectal examination (DRE), during which your doctor uses a gloved, lubricated finger to examine your prostate via your back passage (rectum).
  • Prostate-specific antigen (PSA) blood test, a measure of the level of PSA circulating in the blood. PSA is a protein that is only produced by the prostate.
  • Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), a scan that produces clear images of your prostate. It uses magnetic waves, not x-rays. 
  • A needle biopsy guided by trans-rectal ultrasound (TRUS), tissue samples taken by inserting small needles into the prostate gland.

The tests might not have been performed in this order. You won't be diagnosed with prostate cancer until you have had at least a PSA test, a digital rectal exam, and a biopsy.

You and your doctor may have decided on these tests because you have a family member who has prostate cancer, or because you were worried that something was wrong with your prostate. 

High PSA levels may suggest you have prostate cancer, but there are other possible explanations. A needle biopsy guided by trans-rectal ultrasound (TRUS) is needed to confirm prostate cancer.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Prostate cancer: diagnosis and treatment. Clinical guideline 58. February 2008. Available at: http://www.nice.org.uk/CG58 (accessed on 22 August 2012).

The results from the needle biopsy are also used to classify your prostate cancer as low risk, intermediate risk, or high risk. This patient decision aid is for men with low-risk or intermediate-risk localised prostate cancer that hasn't spread to other parts of the body. 

How is prostate cancer classified?

Prostate cancers are divided into three groups, depending on the risk of the cancer growing or spreading through the body. Knowing the risk group helps you decide which treatment may be best for you. 

  • Low risk: the cancer is very unlikely to grow or spread within the next ten years.
  • Intermediate risk: the cancer is unlikely to grow or spread for several years.
  • High risk: the cancer may grow or spread within a few years.

The risk group is calculated by combining results from the prostate-specific antigen (PSA) blood test, the Gleason score, and the stage of the cancer (the TNM score). This decision aid is only about low-risk and intermediate-risk localised prostate cancer.

The method used to classify your risk group is not perfect. Some men who at first are thought to have a low-risk localised prostate cancer will later be found to have a higher risk cancer.  

PSA test: The higher the PSA level in your blood, the more likely it is that cancer is present.

Gleason score: This is calculated by looking at samples of prostate cells taken in a biopsy. A pathologist will look for different grades of prostate cancer in the biopsy samples. The lowest grade is usually 3, and the highest is 5. The two main grades are then added together to give the Gleason score. The higher the Gleason score, the more likely the cancer is to spread.

As an example, men with low-risk localised prostate cancer will have a Gleason score of 6 (3 + 3).

Men with intermediate-risk localised prostate cancer have a Gleason score of 7 and might be graded 3 + 4 or 4 + 3. The order is important. The first number refers to the grade of the cancer cells that appear most commonly in your biopsy, and the second number to the cancer cells found less commonly. When the first number - called the primary cancer grade - is 3, it means that the cancer is likely to grow more slowly (be less aggressive) than a primary cancer grade of 4. Ask your doctor for your Gleason score and for a breakdown of the numbers.

TNM score: The TNM score describes the stage of your cancer. It uses results from the digital rectal examination (DRE) and sometimes an MRI (magnetic resonance imaging) scan to classify prostate cancer. The TNM score can tell you and your doctor:

  • How big your cancer is and whether it has spread outside the prostate (T for tumour)
  • Whether your prostate cancer has spread to the lymph nodes (N for nodes) 
  • Whether your prostate cancer has spread to other areas of your body, such as your hip bones (M for metastasis).  

T1 prostate cancers are very small and completely inside the prostate. They cannot be found by digital rectal examination. T2 prostate cancers are larger, but still within the prostate. Your doctor might feel the cancer as a hard area or lump during a digital rectal examination. 

You have a low-risk localised prostate cancer if you have:[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Prostate cancer: diagnosis and treatment. Clinical guideline 58. February 2008. Available at: http://www.nice.org.uk/CG58 (accessed on 22 August 2012).

  • a PSA level of less than 10 nanograms per millilitre, and
  • a Gleason score of 6 or less, and
  • a cancer stage of T1 or T2A. 

You have an intermediate-risk localised prostate cancer if you have:[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Prostate cancer: diagnosis and treatment. Clinical guideline 58. February 2008. Available at: http://www.nice.org.uk/CG58 (accessed on 22 August 2012). 

  • a PSA level of between 10 and 20 nanograms per millilitre, or
  • a Gleason score of 7, or
  • a cancer stage of T2b or T2c. 
What are the symptoms of localised prostate cancer?

Prostate cancer might not cause any symptoms. But if they happen, many of them are likely to be the same as those caused by an enlarged prostate that is not cancerous (BPH or benign prostatic hyperplasia). It's possible to have prostate cancer and BPH at the same time. Larger prostate cancers (like T2 cancers) can cause symptoms on their own.

The symptoms include:

  • Rushing to the toilet to pass urine
  • Having trouble starting to urinate
  • Straining to urinate or taking a long time to finish
  • Having difficulty in passing urine
  • Leaking urine, or not being able to control the flow of urine (urinary incontinence)
  • Passing urine more often than usual, especially at night
  • Pain on passing urine or ejaculating
  • Blood in the urine or semen (this is rare).

Many localised prostate cancers grow slowly. You may never have symptoms or feel ill from the disease.

Why has this happened to me?

For most men, there is no one cause of prostate cancer. But there are things that affect your chances of getting it.

Being older increases your chance of getting prostate cancer. More than 90 in 100 of all cases of prostate cancer are diagnosed in men over 65 years of age.[5]NHS cancer screening programmes. Information about prostate cancer. Available at: http://www.cancerscreening.nhs.uk/prostate/prostate-cancer-information.html (accessed on 22 August 2012).

Men with a father who had prostate cancer are just over twice as likely to get prostate cancer as men with no male relatives affected. Men with a brother with prostate cancer are just over three times more likely to get prostate cancer than men with no male relatives affected. The risk is higher if you have more than one close male relative with prostate cancer, or if your relative was under 60 years of age when they were diagnosed.[6]Burford DC, Kirby M, Austoker J. Prostate Cancer Risk Management Programme information for primary care; PSA testing in asymptomatic men. Evidence document. January 2010. Available at: http://www.cancerscreening.nhs.uk/prostate/pcrmp-guide-2.html (accessed on 22 August 2012).

Black men are three times as likely to get prostate cancer as white men of the same age.[7]Ben-Schlomo Y, Evans S, Ibrahim F, et al. The risk of prostate cancer amongst black men in the United Kingdom. The PROCESS cohort study. European Urology. 2008; 53: 99-105.

How many men have localised prostate cancer?

Prostate cancer is the most common cancer in men in the UK.[8]Cancer Research UK. Prostate cancer: key facts. May 2012. Available at: http://info.cancerresearchuk.org/cancerstats/keyfacts/prostate-cancer (accessed on 22 August 2012). But we don’t know exactly how many men are specifically diagnosed with localised prostate cancer because the figures are not readily available.

For prostate cancer as a whole, the latest figures show that more than 40,000 men find out they have it each year.[9]Cancer Research UK. Prostate cancer – UK incidence statistics. May 2012. Available at: http://info.cancerresearchuk.org/cancerstats/types/prostate/incidence (accessed on 22 August 2012). And about 10,700 men will die from prostate cancer each year.[10]Cancer Research UK. Prostate cancer mortality statistics. June 2012. Available at: http://www.cancerresearchuk.org/cancer-info/cancerstats/types/prostate/mortality/uk-prostate-cancer-mortality-statistics (accessed on 03 December 2012). But many more men survive prostate cancer than die of it. Today, more than 80 in 100 men survive beyond five years, whereas in the 1970s only 30 in 100 men did.[9]Cancer Research UK. Prostate cancer – UK incidence statistics. May 2012. Available at: http://info.cancerresearchuk.org/cancerstats/types/prostate/incidence (accessed on 22 August 2012). 

What may happen?

Your prostate cancer has been diagnosed at an early stage. It’s localised, which means that it hasn’t spread beyond your prostate. If you have low-risk prostate cancer there’s a good chance that you will live just as long as someone who doesn’t have prostate cancer. Even without treatment.

But many men will want to have their prostate cancer treated or managed in some way. There are treatments that aim to cure your cancer (for example, surgery to remove your prostate or radiotherapy) and others that may improve your symptoms (like hormone therapy).

The choices you make will depend on many things. These may include the risk of the cancer growing or spreading, your likely life expectancy, your general health, the benefits and side effects of different treatments, and your personal values and preferences.

If you are older and have other serious medical problems, then you may be more likely to die from one of your other problems than from prostate cancer. If you are younger, there’s more time for the cancer to grow or spread so your chances of dying from prostate cancer are greater. Younger men are more likely to be offered treatments that can cure their cancer.  

Some men may have difficulty passing urine, leak urine (incontinence), or have erection problems (impotence). These are some of the possible side effects of treatment, but they are also more common as men get older. They are unlikely to be caused by prostate cancer. 

Prostate cancer becomes very difficult to cure when it spreads beyond the prostate. Treatments used to control the growth may help your chances of survival.

Sometimes prostate cancer can spread far beyond the prostate (called metastatic disease). It may spread to your bones, causing pain and broken bones. Men with metastatic disease may feel extremely tired, feel weak, and lose weight. About one third of men with prostate cancer that has spread to other parts of the body will be alive after five years.[12]Cancer Research UK. Prostate cancer survival statistics. June 2012. Available at: http://info.cancerresearchuk.org/cancerstats/types/prostate/survival/prostate-cancer-survival-statistics (accessed on 22 August 2012).

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT WATCHFUL WAITING

What is watchful waiting?

Watchful waiting means watching for symptoms that mean your cancer may be growing. Watchful waiting is a long-term approach. 

You may still have occasional doctor’s appointments to attend. You are unlikely to have regular prostate-specific antigen tests or biopsies.

Many men who opt for watchful waiting don’t have symptoms from their prostate cancer. Symptoms can be treated if they develop, but the treatment is not intended to cure the cancer.

Who can have watchful waiting?

Watchful waiting is an option where a person waits to see how the cancer and its symptoms change over time. Watchful waiting will not cure or control the spread of cancer. Men with other serious illnesses or limited life expectancy usually choose the watchful waiting option.

What are the main effects of watchful waiting on localised prostate cancer?

Watchful waiting doesn’t cure or control the spread of your cancer.

Men who choose watchful waiting are much more likely to die from another cause than from their prostate cancer.

What are the main effects of watchful waiting on what you can do and how you feel?

Watchful waiting is unlikely to have much effect on what you can do. You can carry out all of your usual everyday activities while having watchful waiting.

Some men feel relieved after choosing watchful waiting. It means that they can avoid many or all of the tests and investigations that are part and parcel of other prostate cancer treatments.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Prostate cancer: diagnosis and treatment. Clinical guideline 58. February 2008. Available at: http://www.nice.org.uk/CG58 (accessed on 22 August 2012). They are likely to need fewer visits to doctors and hospitals than with other options. 

 

What are the main side effects of watchful waiting?

There are few immediate side effects with watchful waiting.

Difficulty passing urine, leaking urine (incontinence), and erection problems (impotence) can happen in men having watchful waiting. These effects aren't caused by watchful waiting. And they're unlikely to be caused by prostate cancer. They can happen by chance and are more common as men get older.  

Bone pain can happen if the cancer spreads. These side effects aren’t caused by watchful waiting, but by the cancer growing. There aren’t enough good studies to know how often they happen. Many localised prostate cancers never affect men’s health.

How might watchful waiting affect your life?

Watchful waiting is a passive approach to managing prostate cancer. This means that there's no active treatment.

Many men and their partners will find the prospect of erection problems upsetting. Some men find other ways to get sexual satisfaction or try treatments, including pills or vacuum pumps, that may help them to get an erection.

Leakage of urine (incontinence) can affect confidence, relationships, and daily life. Some men will try to make sure that they’re never too far from a toilet. Pads can be used to absorb leaking urine and may be available through the NHS. Other men may have to pay for incontinence pads themselves. 

There are no pills or tablets to remember; nor are there treatment-related side effects. It is easier to get back into your routine compared with other treatments. This might help some men to deal with the physical and emotional aspects of their diagnosis.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT ACTIVE SURVEILLANCE

What is active surveillance?

Active surveillance is a way of closely monitoring localised prostate cancer with regular prostate-specific antigen (PSA tests), and biopsies if they are needed. Usually the monitoring involves:[14]Prostate Cancer UK. Active surveillance. Available at: http://prostatecanceruk.org/information/treatment/treatment-choices/active-surveillance (accessed on 22 August 2012).

  • PSA testing every three months for the first two years, then testing once every six months
  • A digital rectal exam every three to six months for the first two years, then once each year
  • Needle biopsies every few years, depending on your hospital.

Some hospitals use magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) scans every few years to monitor prostate cancer.

You will be offered radiotherapy or surgery with the aim of trying to cure your cancer if:

  • the tests show that your prostate cancer is growing
  • you are in good health and you decide to stop active surveillance at any point or for any reason.
Who can have active surveillance?

Men with low-risk localised prostate cancer are likely to be offered active surveillance first.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Prostate cancer: diagnosis and treatment. Clinical guideline 58. February 2008. Available at: http://www.nice.org.uk/CG58 (accessed on 22 August 2012). They will be well enough to have radiotherapy, low-dose rate brachytherapy or surgery in the event that their cancer gets worse.

Some men with intermediate-risk localised prostate cancer might want to discuss active surveillance with their doctor. They’re likely to have a low PSA level and a Gleason score of 3 + 4. Ask your doctor about your PSA test results and Gleason score if you’re not sure of them.

What are the main effects of active surveillance on localised prostate cancer?

Choosing active surveillance helps to avoid, or delay, the side effects of radiotherapy, low-dose-rate brachytherapy or surgery.

There’s no good research to tell us how well active surveillance works. More is known about men who just visited their doctor once every six months to have their prostate cancer checked. [16]Wilt TJ, Brawer MK, Barry MJ et al.  The Prostate cancer Intervention Versus Observation Trial:VA/NCI/AHRQ Cooperative Studies Program #407 (PIVOT): design and baseline results of a randomized controlled trial comparing radical prostatectomy to watchful waiting for men with clinically localized prostate cancer. Contemp Clin Trials. 2009 Jan;30(1):81-7. [17]Wilt TJ, Brawer MK, Jones KM, et al. Radical prostatectomy versus observation for localized prostate cancer. NEJM. 2012; 367: 203-213.

In the group of men with localised prostate cancer who visited their doctor once every six months: [18]Wilt TJ, Brawer MK, Jones KM, et al. Radical prostatectomy versus observation for localized prostate cancer. New England Journal of Medicine. 2012; 367: 203-213.

  • About 64 in 100 men with low-risk cancer were still alive after 12 years. This compares with 42 in 100 men with intermediate-risk cancer
  • About 3 in 100 men with low-risk cancer died from their prostate cancer. This compares with 11 in 100 men with intermediate-risk cancer who died from their prostate cancer.

Only a small number of men died from their prostate cancer. 

The benefit of regular visits to your doctor seems to differ depending on whether you have been diagnosed with a low-risk or an intermediate-risk localised prostate cancer. Six monthly visits to the doctor seem to make a bigger difference for men with low-risk cancer than for men with intermediate-risk cancer.

Active surveillance involves frequent visits to your doctor and close monitoring with tests. So it’s likely to be at least as good as visiting your doctor once every six months to have your prostate cancer checked.

What are the main effects of active surveillance on what you can do and how you feel?

Active surveillance is unlikely to have much effect on what you can do. You can carry out all your usual activities while having active surveillance.

Some men find it reassuring to have regular tests to confirm that their cancer is not growing. Other men worry about living with an untreated cancer.

Active surveillance allows your doctor to closely monitor your cancer at regular intervals. If your cancer begins to grow, you start treatment with the aim of curing the cancer.

What are the main side effects of active surveillance?

The tests you have during active surveillance have side effects. Some men find needle biopsies painful. Needle biopsies can cause bleeding into your semen. This happens to about 50 in 100 men who have a biopsy. It usually goes away within a few days.[19]Ilic D, O’Connor D, Green S, et al. Screening for prostate cancer: an updated Cochrane systematic review. British Journal of Urology International. 2011; 107: 882-891.

You may feel worried or anxious about living with an untreated cancer. This can put pressure on your life at work, at home, and in your personal relationships.[20]BMJ Best Health. Prostate cancer. July 2012. Available at: http://besthealth.bmj.com/x/topic/392699/essentials.html (accessed on 22 August 2012).

Active surveillance delays treatment. As you get older you may become unable to have certain treatments, especially if your general health is not good.

Difficulty passing urine, leaking urine (incontinence), and erection problems (impotence) can happen in men having active surveillance. These effects aren't caused by active surveillance. And they're unlikely to be caused by prostate cancer. They can happen by chance and are more common as men get older. 

How might active surveillance affect your life?

By closely monitoring your cancer, rather than treating it, you avoid the side effects of early treatment. For many men, this will mean a better quality of life with no difference in the length of their life.

Active surveillance does not suit everyone. It delays treatment until tests show that your cancer is growing. Some men are comfortable with this. But others might not be happy living with an untreated cancer. They are more likely to want a treatment that aims to cure their cancer soon after diagnosis.

Like watchful waiting, there are no pills or tablets to remember. There’s no treatment right away. So active surveillance won’t physically affect you as much as treatments like radiotherapy or surgery. You will still have to visit your doctor regularly. They will want to know your PSA levels and might also recommend magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) scans or needle biopsies. You will need to go to hospital to have an MRI scan or a needle biopsy.

Many men and their partners will find the prospect of erection problems upsetting. Some men find other ways to get sexual satisfaction or try treatments, including pills or vacuum pumps, that may help them to get an erection. If you have treatment, your ability to get an erection may improve with time.

Leakage of urine (incontinence) can affect confidence, relationships, and daily life. Some men will try to make sure that they’re never too far from a toilet. Pads can be used to absorb leaking urine and may be available through the NHS. Other men may have to pay for incontinence pads themselves.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT EXTERNAL BEAM RADIOTHERAPY

What is external beam radiotherapy?

External beam radiotherapy uses radiation (x-rays) to destroy cancer cells. It’s often just called radiotherapy.

Radiotherapy is the most common treatment in the UK for men with localised prostate cancer.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Prostate cancer: diagnosis and treatment. Clinical guideline 58. February 2008. Available at: http://www.nice.org.uk/CG58 (accessed on 22 August 2012).

A machine positioned above the body directs the radiation through the abdomen and into the cells in the prostate. The aim of the treatment is to kill the cancer cells in the prostate so that the cancer can’t grow and spread to other parts of the body. The radiation dose needed to kill the cancer cells is much higher than the dose used for x-ray images.

Men having radiotherapy are outpatients, and are treated with 20 to 38 doses of radiation, called fractions. Each session lasts a few minutes. Treatment takes place once a day, for five days a week. A course of treatment lasts between four and seven weeks.

You will have a PSA test about six or 12 weeks after your radiotherapy finishes.[22]Prostate Cancer UK. External beam radiotherapy. Available at: http://prostatecanceruk.org/information/treatment/treatment-choices/radiotherapy (accessed on 22 August 2012). For the next two years, you will have a PSA test about once every six months to monitor the effects of the radiotherapy. Your PSA level should drop if radiotherapy has worked.

Who can have radiotherapy?

Radiotherapy is an option for men with low-risk or intermediate-risk localised prostate cancer.

It’s usually offered to men with intermediate-risk prostate cancer who have a life expectancy of more than 10 years.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Prostate cancer: diagnosis and treatment. Clinical guideline 58. February 2008. Available at: http://www.nice.org.uk/CG58 (accessed on 22 August 2012).[24]Heidenreich A, Bellmunt J, Bolla M, et al. EAU guidelines on prostate cancer. European Association of Urology. 2012. Available at: http://www.uroweb.org/guidelines/online-guidelines/ (accessed on 22 August 2012). It may also be an option for men who do not want, or cannot have, a radical prostatectomy.[24]Heidenreich A, Bellmunt J, Bolla M, et al. EAU guidelines on prostate cancer. European Association of Urology. 2012. Available at: http://www.uroweb.org/guidelines/online-guidelines/ (accessed on 22 August 2012).

What are the main effects of radiotherapy on localised prostate cancer?

In a group of men who had radiotherapy and were followed for 10 years:[26]Jones CU, Hunt D, McGowan DG, et al. Radiotherapy and short-term androgen deprivation for localized prostate cancer. New England Journal of Medicine. 2011; 365: 107-118.

  • About 64 in 100 men with low-risk prostate cancer were still alive after 10 years. This compares with 54 in 100 men with intermediate-risk prostate cancer.
  • About 1 in 100 men with low-risk prostate cancer died from their prostate cancer. This compares with 10 in 100 men with intermediate-risk prostate cancer.

Men who are at risk of their prostate cancer coming back may be offered hormone therapy (androgen deprivation therapy) in addition to radiotherapy.[27]Cancer Research UK. About hormone therapy for prostate cancer. July 2012. Available at: http://cancerhelp.cancerresearchuk.org/type/prostate-cancer/treatment/hormone/about-hormone-therapy-for-prostate-cancer (accessed on 22 August 2012). It works by lowering the level of testosterone. Testosterone causes prostate cancer cells to grow more rapidly.

Most men who are given hormone therapy start before their radiotherapy.[28]The Prostate Cancer Charity. Recurrent prostate cancer. December 2009. Available at: http://prostatecanceruk.org/media/41608/recurrence.pdf (accessed on 22 August 2012). It can help to shrink the prostate tumour before radiotherapy begins.

In a group of men who had a total of four months of hormone therapy starting two months before and finishing two months after their radiotherapy:[26]Jones CU, Hunt D, McGowan DG, et al. Radiotherapy and short-term androgen deprivation for localized prostate cancer. New England Journal of Medicine. 2011; 365: 107-118.

  • About 67 in 100 men with low-risk prostate cancer were still alive after 10 years. This compares with 64 in 100 men with low-risk prostate cancer who had radiotherapy alone
  • About 61 in 100 men with intermediate-risk prostate cancer were still alive after 10 years. This compares with 54 in 100 men with intermediate-risk prostate cancer who had radiotherapy alone.

Adding hormone therapy to radiotherapy improves life expectancy compared with radiotherapy alone in men with low-risk and intermediate-risk localised prostate cancer.  

What are the main effects of radiotherapy on what you can do and how you feel?

The aim of radiotherapy is to cure your cancer. You may feel relieved to have a treatment that can reduce the risk of cancer spreading and growing. 

Radiotherapy involves many trips to the hospital while you are having treatment. This can take up to seven weeks. 

What are the main side effects of radiotherapy?

Radiotherapy can cause temporary side effects and longer term side effects. Some longer term side effects can be permanent.

Temporary side effects may begin a few weeks after starting treatment and last for a few weeks after finishing.[27]Cancer Research UK. About hormone therapy for prostate cancer. July 2012. Available at: http://cancerhelp.cancerresearchuk.org/type/prostate-cancer/treatment/hormone/about-hormone-therapy-for-prostate-cancer (accessed on 22 August 2012). They include:

  • Wanting to urinate more often than usual
  • Diarrhoea and wind
  • Sore skin around the lower abdomen
  • Pubic hair falling out.

The radiotherapy treatment itself is painless and does not make you radioactive.

Longer term side effects, including urinary and bowel problems, happen when radiotherapy injures the bladder or back passage (rectum). Bowel problems include painful, frequent, or urgent bowel movements.

Radiotherapy may cause erection problems. We don't know how common this is. Men who had good erections before radiotherapy may have fewer erection problems after treatment. Men who have radiotherapy may have a dry climax (retrograde ejaculation). Dry climax may not affect how much you enjoy sex.

Hormone therapy (before or after radiotherapy) may cause breasts to grow, pain in the breast area, erection problems, decreased sex drive, hot flushes, tiredness, and liver problems.[31]Kumar S, Shelley M, Harrison C, et al. Neo-adjuvant and adjuvant hormone therapy for localised and locally advanced cancer (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.[32]Shelley MD, Kumar S, Coles B, et al. Adjuvant hormone therapy for localised and locally advanced prostate carcinoma: a systematic review and meta-analysis of randomised trials. Cancer Treatment Reviews. 2009; 35: 540-546. Long-term treatment with hormone therapy might increase your risk of bone thinning (osteoporosis) and heart attack.

How might radiotherapy affect your life?

During treatment, you will need to go to hospital every working day for up to seven weeks (a possible total of 38 treatments). You need to arrange transport to and from sessions. You may find it difficult to fit work around treatments. Many men also feel too tired to work while having radiotherapy. 

Long-term problems include erection difficulties, leakage of urine (incontinence) and an irritable, windy bowel. They may have a big effect on your life.

Many men and their partners will find the prospect of erection problems upsetting. Some men find other ways to get sexual satisfaction or try treatments, including pills or vacuum pumps, that may help them to get an erection.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT LOW-DOSE RATE BRACHYTHERAPY

What is low-dose rate brachytherapy?

It’s a type of radiotherapy. It’s sometimes called internal radiotherapy.

Low-dose rate brachytherapy involves planting very small radioactive ‘seeds’ directly in or near the cancer in your prostate. The aim is to target and destroy the cancer cells while sparing the healthy cells.

The seeds are planted when you are under a general anaesthetic or a spinal anaesthetic (an injection into the small of your back that numbs nerves from the waist down for several hours). The procedure takes between one and one-and-a-half hours. Most men can go home on the same day.

You will not feel the seeds once they have been planted in your prostate. The radiation is released slowly by the seeds and fades away after a few months. The seeds will remain in your body for the rest of your life, even after the radiation has worn off.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT RADICAL PROSTATECTOMY

What is a radical prostatectomy?

Radical prostatectomy is an operation to remove the whole prostate gland. Removing the prostate takes away the tumour so that it can’t grow or spread. It's sometimes just called surgery.

The surgery removes the prostate through a single cut in your abdomen (retropubic surgery) or through a cut in the skin between the anus and testicles (called perineal surgery). This type of operation is called open prostatectomy.

Keyhole surgery (laparoscopic surgery) can also be used to remove the prostate. This operation uses a series of small cuts (about 4 or 5) to reach and remove your prostate. Not all hospitals offer laparoscopic surgery.

You will have a general anaesthetic and will need a catheter after the operation. The catheter is inserted into your penis through the urethra and drains urine from the bladder. It’s normally taken out one to two weeks after surgery.

The aim of radical prostatectomy is to cure you of cancer. In addition to removing the prostate, the surgeon will remove some of the healthy tissue around it and the tubes that carry semen (seminal vesicles).

Sometimes the edge of the healthy tissue contains some cancer cells (called a positive margin). This could mean - but not always - that a few cancer cells were left behind during surgery. You may be offered radiotherapy to destroy any remaining cancer cells and help stop the cancer coming back.

You’re likely to have a prostate-specific antigen (PSA) test about six weeks after surgery.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Prostate cancer: diagnosis and treatment. Clinical guideline 58. February 2008. Available at: http://www.nice.org.uk/CG58 (accessed on 22 August 2012). Your PSA level should drop to - and stay at - very low levels (less than 0.02 nanograms per millilitre) if the surgery has worked. For the next two years, you will have a PSA test every three to six months to monitor the effects of the surgery. [1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Prostate cancer: diagnosis and treatment. Clinical guideline 58. February 2008. Available at: http://www.nice.org.uk/CG58 (accessed on 22 August 2012).[53]The Prostate Cancer Charity. Surgery: radical prostatectomy. Available at: http://prostatecanceruk.org/media/11611/surgery.pdf (accessed on 22 August 2012).

If your doctor thinks that not all the cancer was successfully removed during surgery, or if you are at risk of the prostate cancer coming back, you may be offered radiotherapy after your radical prostatectomy.[22]Prostate Cancer UK. External beam radiotherapy. Available at: http://prostatecanceruk.org/information/treatment/treatment-choices/radiotherapy (accessed on 22 August 2012).

Who can have a radical prostatectomy?

Surgery is an option for men with low-risk or intermediate-risk localised prostate cancer.

It’s a major operation. It is usually offered to men with intermediate-risk prostate cancer who are in good general health and have a life expectancy of more than 10 years.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Prostate cancer: diagnosis and treatment. Clinical guideline 58. February 2008. Available at: http://www.nice.org.uk/CG58 (accessed on 22 August 2012).[24]Heidenreich A, Bellmunt J, Bolla M, et al. How might radical prostatectomy affect your life?

Long-term problems after radical prostatectomy, such as erection problems and leakage of urine (incontinence), may have a big effect on your life.

Some men will always wonder whether they really needed surgery. Before your operation, no one can say for sure how your prostate cancer might behave in the future. Many prostate cancers grow slowly and never cause symptoms or health problems, while others grow quickly and can spread to other parts of the body.

It may take six to eight weeks before you are ready to return to work after open surgery. [81]Prostate Cancer UK. Surgery: radical prostatectomy. Available at http://prostatecanceruk.org/info/prostate_cancer/treatment_surgery.asp (accessed on 30 August 2012). Men who have keyhole surgery may feel able to return between two and six weeks after surgery.
