MORE INFORMATION ABOUT PROSTATE SPECIFIC ANTIGEN (PSA) TESTING

What is the prostate gland?

The prostate is a walnut-sized gland that sits underneath the bladder. It surrounds the tube (urethra) that you urinate through. 

The main role of the prostate is to help make semen. Semen, which contains sperm, comes out of the penis when a man ejaculates.

What are the symptoms of prostate cancer?

Prostate cancer might not cause any symptoms. But if they happen, many of them are likely to be the same as those caused by an enlarged prostate that is not cancerous (benign prostatic hyperplasia).

Symptoms of prostate cancer include:

  • Problems urinating, including having trouble starting to urinate, having difficulty in passing urine, or taking a long time to finish
  • Passing urine more often than usual, especially at night
  • Pain on passing urine or ejaculating
  • Pain or stiffness in the lower back, hips, or the bones in the thighs. This usually only happens if the cancer has spread to nearby bones
  • Blood in urine or semen. This is rare
  • Tiredness and anaemia. Advanced cancer can cause a shortage of red blood cells (anaemia). This causes extreme tiredness
  • Loss of appetite.
What increases the chances of getting prostate cancer?

For most men, there is no one cause of prostate cancer. But there are things that affect your chances of getting it.[1]NHS Choices. Causes of prostate cancer. February 2011. Available at: http://www.nhs.uk/Conditions/Cancer-of-the-prostate/Pages/Causes.aspx (accessed on 08 August 2012).

Being older increases your chance of getting prostate cancer. More than 90 in 100 of all cases of prostate cancer are diagnosed in men over 65 years of age.[2]NHS cancer screening programmes. Information about prostate cancer. 2012. Available at: http://www.cancerscreening.nhs.uk/prostate/prostate-cancer-information.html (accessed on 26 July 2012).

Men with a father who had prostate cancer are just over twice as likely to get prostate cancer as men with no male relatives affected. Men with a brother with prostate cancer are just over three times as likely to get prostate cancer as men with no male relatives affected. The risk is higher if you have more than one close male relative with prostate cancer, or if your relative was under 60 years of age when they were diagnosed.[3]Burford DC, Kirby M, Austoker J. Prostate Cancer Risk Management Programme information for primary care; PSA testing in asymptomatic men. Evidence document. January 2010. Available at: http://www.cancerscreening.nhs.uk/prostate/pcrmp-guide-2.html (accessed 13 August 2012).

Black men are three times as likely to get prostate cancer as white men of the same age.[4]Ben-Schlomo Y, Evans S, Ibrahim F, et al. The risk of prostate cancer amongst black men in the United Kingdom. The PROCESS cohort study. European Urology. 2008; 53: 99-105.

There may be a link between the chance of getting prostate cancer and having close female relatives who have breast cancer. But this link is still uncertain.[3]Burford DC, Kirby M, Austoker J. Prostate Cancer Risk Management Programme information for primary care; PSA testing in asymptomatic men. Evidence document. January 2010. Available at: http://www.cancerscreening.nhs.uk/prostate/pcrmp-guide-2.html (accessed 13 August 2012).

How do I know if I have prostate cancer?

Doctors use four different tests to diagnose prostate cancer. You won't be diagnosed with prostate cancer until you have had a rectal ultrasound scan and biopsy.[6]NICE. Prostate cancer. Diagnosis and treatment. Clinical Guideline 58. February 2008. Available at: http://www.nice.org.uk/nicemedia/pdf/CG58NICEGuideline.pdf (accessed 15 August 2012).

  • Prostate specific antigen (PSA) test. This is a blood test that measures the amount of PSA circulating in the blood. PSA is a protein that is only produced by the prostate. If there is cancer in the prostate, more PSA gets into your blood from your prostate. Your PSA level is measured in units called nanograms (ng) per millilitre (ml) of blood. There are other causes of a raised PSA level. That’s why a PSA test on its own cannot tell you for certain whether you have prostate cancer. It can only tell you if you have a raised risk of prostate cancer. If you have a raised PSA level you will need to have other tests to find out if this is caused by prostate cancer.
  • Digital rectal examination (DRE). A doctor will insert a gloved, lubricated finger into your rectum (back passage) to feel the prostate gland. He or she may be able to tell if the prostate gland feels abnormal. This is more likely if the prostate has a tumour that is bigger than one inch, or three centimetres in size. Some men find this test uncomfortable.
  • Rectal ultrasound scan. A small device is inserted into your back passage. It uses sound waves to create a clear picture of the prostate gland that a doctor can see on a computer screen. This can help to tell if there is a tumour in your prostate gland. Some men find this test uncomfortable.
  • Biopsy. The aim of a biopsy is to find prostate cancers that could cause you health problems.[7]NICE. Prostate cancer. Diagnosis and treatment. Clinical Guideline 58. February 2008. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/nicemedia/pdf/CG58NICEGuideline.pdf (accessed 15 August 2012). During a biopsy a doctor uses a needle to remove very small pieces of tissue from your prostate to look at under a microscope. This can show changes in the cells that are caused by prostate cancer. The needle will be either gently pushed through the wall of your rectum into the prostate or inserted into the skin between your scrotum (the sac that holds your testicles) and your anus (the opening through which you empty your bowels). The needle will be guided by a device called an ultrasound probe. Some men find this test uncomfortable or painful.
How many people have prostate cancer?

Prostate cancer is the most common cancer among men in the UK. More than 40,000 men find out they have prostate cancer each year.[8]Cancer Research UK. Prostate cancer statistics. May 2012. Available at: http://info.cancerresearchuk.org/cancerstats/types/prostate (accessed 08 August 2012).

What will happen after the PSA test?

If you have a PSA test, you will also probably have a digital rectal examination (where the doctor inserts a gloved, lubricated finger into your back passage) to feel the prostate gland. Your doctor will ask some questions about your family history of prostate cancer, your ethnic background, and the results from any previous PSA test results you may have had.

If your PSA test shows you have normal PSA levels

If your PSA level is healthy for your age, you are less likely to have prostate cancer. There is a chance that your PSA level will go up in future. If you have a normal PSA test result, doctors recommend that you do not need to have another test for at least two years. 

The PSA test doesn’t always pick up prostate cancer. Around 15 in 100 men with a normal PSA level will have prostate cancer. [9]Thompson IM, Pauler DK, Goodman PJ, et al. Prevalence of prostate cancer among men with a prostate-specific antigen level. New England Journal of Medicine. 2004; 350: 2239-2246. [10]NHS Clinical Knowledge Summaries. Benefits and harms of PSA testing. 2011. Available at: http://www.cks.nhs.uk/prostate_cancer/management/scenario_diagnosis/psa_testing#-480150 (accessed 29 August 2012). Many of these prostate cancers might never cause problems during the man's lifetime.

If your PSA test shows you have raised PSA levels

Men with PSA levels above what is healthy for their age have a higher chance of being diagnosed with prostate cancer during the next 20 to 25 years.[11]Loeb S, Ballentine CH, Catalona WJ, et al. Baseline prostate-specific antigen testing at a young age. European Urology. 2012; 61: 1-7.

If your PSA level is only slightly above what is considered normal for your age, you are less likely to have prostate cancer. There are other illnesses that can cause a slight increase in PSA levels, such as an enlarged prostate, prostatitis, or urinary infections. Your GP might recommend that you have a repeat PSA test in one to three months to see if the level is going up or is stable.[12]Cancer Research UK. Screening for prostate cancer. July 2012. Available at: http://cancerhelp.cancerresearchuk.org/type/prostate-cancer/about/screening-for-prostate-cancer (accessed 20 August 2012).

If your PSA level is much higher than what is considered normal for your age, it is more likely that this is a sign of cancer. But a PSA test on its own cannot tell you for certain whether you have prostate cancer. You will still need more tests to be certain.

Your GP will usually refer you to a specialist doctor called a urologist. The specialist will probably repeat the tests that your GP did, to check for any changes. They will discuss whether you should have another test, called a biopsy. This is the main test doctors use to tell if you have prostate cancer.

Treatments for prostate cancer

These are the main treatments for men diagnosed with prostate cancer:

Watchful waiting

Some men decide not to have any further treatment for prostate cancer unless they start to have symptoms, or their symptoms start to get worse.

Active surveillance

This means having regular PSA tests and prostate biopsies to check how quickly the cancer is growing, or if it is causing other health problems. Men see a doctor for tests every three or six months. If the cancer starts to grow they can choose to have treatment.

Hormone treatment (with drugs)

Hormone treatment drugs block the action of the male sex hormone testosterone, which helps the prostate cancer to grow. This treatment is also called androgen deprivation and androgen suppression. 

Hormone treatment (with surgery)

This involves an operation to remove your testicles. This permanently gets rid of your body’s main source of testosterone. The aim is to stop the prostate cancer cells from growing and spreading. 

Surgery

This involves an operation to remove the prostate gland. The aim is to cure the cancer. 

External beam radiotherapy 

This is when a machine beams radiation into the tumour to kill the cancer cells. The aim is to cure the cancer.[13]NHS Cancer Screening Programmes. Prostate cancer risk management programme. 2012. Available at: http://www.cancerscreening.nhs.uk/prostate/index.html (accessed 09 August 2012).

Brachytherapy

Brachytherapy uses radioactive metal 'seeds' or pellets that are placed directly into your prostate. The seeds contain a radioactive substance that breaks down in the cells, giving off rays of energy called radiation. This is designed to kill cancer cells.[14]NHS Cancer Screening Programmes. Prostate cancer risk management programme. 2012. Available at: http://www.cancerscreening.nhs.uk/prostate/index.html (accessed 09 August 2012).

What will happen if I have prostate cancer?

Most prostate cancers grow slowly, so you have a good chance of surviving your cancer whether you have treatment or not. Even if your cancer has spread to tissue nearby, your chances of surviving are still good.

Because prostate cancer can grow slowly, some men live with prostate cancer for many years without having any trouble from their cancer. Many older men with prostate cancer die of other causes, without even knowing they had prostate cancer. 

Other prostate cancers grow much faster. Unfortunately, it is difficult to tell whether your prostate cancer will grow quickly or slowly.

Doctors usually talk about the percentage of men who are likely to be alive in five or 10 years. Here we talk about the average survival rates for men with different types of prostate cancer. It’s not possible to say exactly what will happen to you.

Almost all men whose cancer has not spread beyond their prostate will be alive five years after diagnosis.[15]Majeed A, Babb P, Jones J, et al. Trends in prostate cancer incidence, mortality and survival in England and Wales 1971-1998. BJU International. 2000; 85: 1058-1062.

About 60 in 100 men whose cancer has spread into tissues or organs near the prostate will live for at least five years.[15]Majeed A, Babb P, Jones J, et al. Trends in prostate cancer incidence, mortality and survival in England and Wales 1971-1998. BJU International. 2000; 85: 1058-1062.

If your cancer has spread to your bones and other distant parts of your body doctors call this metastatic disease. About 33 in 100 men whose cancer has spread to other places in their body will live for five years or more.[17]Cancer Research UK. Prostate cancer statistics. May 2012. Available at http://info.cancerresearchuk.org/cancerstats/types/prostate (accessed 08 August 2012). In about 30 in 100 men the cancer will have spread from the prostate to other organs and bones (called metastatic disease) within 10 years of finding out they have prostate cancer.

What is prostate cancer?

When your body's cells are healthy, they grow and divide to form new cells as your body needs them. When cells grow old and die, new cells take their place.

Sometimes this process breaks down. New cells form too rapidly, when the body doesn't need them, and old cells don't die when they should.

In prostate cancer some of the cells in the prostate gland start to grow out of control, invading and destroying healthy cells. The extra cells can form a mass of tissue called a growth or tumour.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT HAVING THE PSA TEST

What is the PSA test?

The PSA blood test can help to diagnose prostate cancer. It measures the amount of a protein called prostate-specific antigen (or PSA) in your blood. PSA is made by the prostate gland. It is normally found in semen, the fluid which contains your sperm. If there is cancer in the prostate, more PSA leaks into the blood.

Your GP can take a blood sample to be tested for PSA. Your test result will show the level of PSA in a measurement of nanograms (ng) per millilitre (mL) of blood. The higher the level of PSA, the more likely it is to be a sign of cancer.

A PSA test on its own cannot tell you for certain whether you have prostate cancer. It can only tell you if you have a raised risk of prostate cancer. Other illnesses which are not cancer (such as an enlarged prostate, prostatitis, and urinary infections) can also cause a rise in PSA. If you have a raised PSA level you will need to have other tests to find out if this is caused by prostate cancer. 

PSA levels can vary between men of the same age. Doctors use the following cut-offs to help decide what is a healthy PSA level:

  • A PSA level below 3.0ng/ml is considered healthy if you are aged between 50 and 59 
  • A PSA level below 4.0ng/ml is considered healthy if you are aged between 60 and 69 
  • A PSA level below 5.0ng/ml is considered healthy if you are aged 70 or older.

If you have a PSA level higher than is considered healthy for your age, you will need other tests to find out if this is caused by prostate cancer.

Who can have a PSA Test?

Any man over 50 can choose to have a free PSA test on the NHS, if they have discussed the benefits and risks with their GP. In England, the Department of Health recommends that doctors do not advise men to have the test unless there are reasons to think they have a higher chance of having prostate cancer. 

There are some things that make it more likely your PSA test result will not be accurate. PSA testing should not be done within:

  • 6 weeks of a prostate biopsy
  • 4 weeks of a urinary infection
  • 1 week of digital rectal examination
  • 48 hours of vigorous exercise
  • 48 hours of ejaculation.
What effect does the PSA test have on your chances of being diagnosed with prostate cancer?

Having a PSA test increases your chances of being diagnosed with prostate cancer. In big groups of men who have had the PSA test, between 6 in 100[18]Djulbegovic M, Beyth RJ, Neuberger MM, et al. Screening for prostate cancer: systematic review and meta-analysis of randomised controlled trials. BMJ. 2010; 341: c4543. and 12 in 100[19]Hugosson J, Carlsson S, Aus G, et al. Mortality results from the Göteborg randomised population-based prostate-cancer screening trial. Lancet Oncology. 2010; 11: 725-732. men who have had a PSA test were diagnosed with prostate cancer during the next 10 to 15 years.

A PSA test can’t tell you what stage a cancer is, or whether it is likely to grow slowly or quickly. You can’t tell from a PSA test how likely a cancer is to cause you health problems or to need treatment. Not all prostate cancers grow or spread quickly enough to cause health problems or need treatment.

What is the effect of having a PSA test on when you find out you have prostate cancer?

Men who have a PSA test are more likely to be diagnosed soon after the cancer has started. Early diagnosis may not be an advantage. It may mean that you live longer knowing you have prostate cancer. It may not mean you live longer than you would have done if you hadn't known about the prostate cancer.

In one group of men who had a PSA test, prostate cancer was diagnosed between seven and eight years earlier than it would have been otherwise.[20]Finne P, Fallah M, Hakama M, et al. Lead-time in the European randomised study of screening for prostate cancer. European Journal of Urology. 2010; 46: 3102-3108.

Men who are diagnosed with prostate cancer after having a PSA test are likely to have cancer that has not spread beyond the prostate gland. They seem less likely to have cancer that has spread outside the prostate or to other parts of the body.[18]Djulbegovic M, Beyth RJ, Neuberger MM, et al. Screening for prostate cancer: systematic review and meta-analysis of randomised controlled trials. BMJ. 2010; 341: c4543. [22]Cancer Research UK. The stages of prostate cancer. July 2012. Available at: http://cancerhelp.cancerresearchuk.org/type/prostate-cancer/treatment/the-stages-of-prostate-cancer (accessed on 4 October 2012).

In one review of studies of men who had a PSA test:[18]Djulbegovic M, Beyth RJ, Neuberger MM, et al. Screening for prostate cancer: systematic review and meta-analysis of randomised controlled trials. BMJ. 2010; 341: c4543.

  • Two in 100 had prostate cancer that was small and completely inside the prostate gland
  • Three in 100 had prostate cancer that was larger, but still inside the prostate gland
  • Less than 1 in 100 had prostate cancer that had broken through the covering of the prostate or spread to other parts of the body.
What is the effect of a PSA test on your chances of dying from prostate cancer?

Having a PSA test may decrease your chances of dying from prostate cancer by about 1 in 1000.[24]Schröder FH, Hugosson J, Roobol MJ, et al. Prostate cancer mortality at 11 years of follow-up. New England Journal of Medicine. 2012; 366: 981-990.

Some studies have looked at how likely men are to die of prostate cancer if they have a PSA test, compared to if they don't have one. The research says:

  • PSA testing prevented one death from prostate cancer for every 1000 men who took a test.[24]Schröder FH, Hugosson J, Roobol MJ, et al. Prostate cancer mortality at 11 years of follow-up. New England Journal of Medicine. 2012; 366: 981-990.
  • For every 1,000 men who have a PSA test, seven will eventually die of prostate cancer.[18]Djulbegovic M, Beyth RJ, Neuberger MM, et al. Screening for prostate cancer: systematic review and meta-analysis of randomised controlled trials. BMJ. 2010; 341: c4543.
  • Having a PSA test doesn’t seem to make much difference to how likely you are to die of prostate cancer if you have other serious health problems.[27]Crawford ED, Grubb R, Black A, et al. Comorbidity and mortality results from a randomized prostate cancer screening trial. Journal of Clinical Oncology. 2011; 29: 355-361.
What is the effect of having a PSA test on how long you are likely to live?

On average, men who have PSA tests do not live longer than other men. We don't know whether having a PSA test will affect the length of your life, because there are so many factors that could affect that. 

Research looking at groups of men in studies shows:

  • Around 17 in 100 men who had a PSA test died during the studies, for any reason
  • Around 20 in 100 men who didn’t have a PSA test died during the studies.

The studies lasted between four and 20 years. This difference was so small it could have been down to chance.[28]Lumen N, Fonteyne V, De Meerleert G, et al. Population screening for prostate cancer: an overview of available studies and meta-analysis. International Journal of Urology. 2012; 19: 100-108.

What is the effect of having a PSA test on your quality of life?

We don’t know if having a PSA test makes much difference to your quality of life, because there is not enough research to be sure.[29]Chou R, Croswell JM, Dana T, et al. Screening for prostate cancer: a review of the evidence for the U.S. Preventive Services Task Force. Annals of Internal Medicine. 2011; 155: 762-771. 

The test itself is unlikely to have an effect, but the results of the test could affect your quality of life. If you have a positive result, your quality of life could be affected by worry about cancer, and by the tests and treatments you may need as a result. Or, if you have cancer diagnosed and treated, your quality of life may improve compared to if you did not have your cancer diagnosed until later.

In one study, researchers said for every 100 men who had a PSA test, seven would gain an extra year of improved enjoyment of life.[30]Heijnsdijk EAM, Wever EM, Auvinen A. Quality-of-life effects of prostate-specific antigen screening. New England Journal of Medicine. 2012; 367: 595-605. 

Some studies have looked at the effect of having a PSA test on how likely you are to feel anxious or worried. 

  • Having a PSA test doesn’t seem to make much difference to how likely you are to feel anxious or depressed.[31]Lane JA, Hamdy FC, Martin RM, et al. Latest results from the UK trials evaluating prostate cancer screening and treatment: the CAP and ProtecT studies. European Journal of Cancer. 2010; 46: 3095-3101.
  • Men who have a raised PSA level, but who don’t have prostate cancer, are more likely to worry about prostate cancer than men who don’t have a PSA test. They are also more likely to have sexual problems than men who don’t have a PSA test. Both these effects lasted for up to a year after having the test.[32]Lin K, Lipsitz R, Miller T, et al. Benefits and harms of prostate-specific antigen screening for prostate cancer: an evidence update for the U.S. Preventive Services Task Force. Annals of Internal Medicine. 2008; 149: 192-199.
What effect might a raised PSA test result have on your life?

If you have a PSA test that shows you have a higher PSA level than is normal for your age, your doctor will probably suggest you have more tests to find out if it is caused by prostate cancer. About 17 in 100 men who have a PSA test have a result showing a higher level than is normal for their age.[24]Schröder FH, Hugosson J, Roobol MJ, et al. Prostate cancer mortality at 11 years of follow-up. New England Journal of Medicine. 2012; 366: 981-990.

Your doctor will recommend you have another PSA test, a digital rectal examination, or a biopsy. A PSA test and a digital rectal examination can both be done by your GP. You will need to go to hospital to have a biopsy. Most men don’t need to stay in hospital overnight.

Your doctor may also suggest you have a transrectal ultrasound. If so, this can often be done at the same time as a biopsy. You will need to go to hospital. Most men don’t need to stay in hospital overnight.

If you have a raised PSA level, and further tests show this is caused by prostate cancer, you will need to decide whether to have treatment. It’s likely that you will need to spend time in hospital if you choose to have treatment. (For more, see the Patient Decision Aid on localised prostate cancer.) Treatments for prostate cancer are likely to have side effects. 

If your PSA test shows you have a raised PSA level that is caused by prostate cancer, but you choose not to have treatment, you may find that this causes some worry or anxiety.

What are the side effects of the PSA test and other prostate cancer tests?

About 7 in every 1000 men who has a PSA test have some kind of problem from a PSA test, or from a biopsy or other prostate cancer test. The most common problems are bleeding, blood clots and problems going to the toilet.[18]Djulbegovic M, Beyth RJ, Neuberger MM, et al. Screening for prostate cancer: systematic review and meta-analysis of randomised controlled trials. BMJ. 2010; 341: c4543.

Some men have complications after having a PSA blood test. 

  • Around 26 in every 10,000 men who have a PSA test will have some problems. The most common are dizziness, bruising, or swelling and bleeding.[35]Ilic D, O’Connor D, Green S, et al. Screening for prostate cancer: an updated Cochrane systematic review. British Journal of Urology International. 2011; 107: 882-891.
  • Around 3 in every 10,000 men who have a PSA test will faint or pass out.[18]Djulbegovic M, Beyth RJ, Neuberger MM, et al. Screening for prostate cancer: systematic review and meta-analysis of randomised controlled trials. BMJ. 2010; 341: c4543.

Some men have complications or problems after having a digital rectal exam. Around 3 in 100,000 men who have a digital rectal exam have either pain or some bleeding from the back passage afterwards.[18]Djulbegovic M, Beyth RJ, Neuberger MM, et al. Screening for prostate cancer: systematic review and meta-analysis of randomised controlled trials. BMJ. 2010; 341: c4543.

Some men have complications or problems after having a biopsy. 

  • Around 1 in 100 men will have urinary infections or problems going to the toilet after having a biopsy.[29]Chou R, Croswell JM, Dana T, et al. Screening for prostate cancer: a review of the evidence for the U.S. Preventive Services Task Force. Annals of Internal Medicine. 2011; 155: 762-771.
  • Around 50 in 100 men who have a biopsy will have blood in their sperm. For every 100 men who had blood in their sperm, 77 found this went away within three days.[35]Ilic D, O’Connor D, Green S, et al. Screening for prostate cancer: an updated Cochrane systematic review. British Journal of Urology International. 2011; 107: 882-891.
  • Around 8 in 100 men who have a biopsy find it painful. Around 4 in 100 had a high temperature.[35]Ilic D, O’Connor D, Green S, et al. Screening for prostate cancer: an updated Cochrane systematic review. British Journal of Urology International. 2011; 107: 882-891.

Some men who have treatment for prostate cancer have complications or problems from the treatment. These can include erection problems, urinary incontinence (inability to control your bladder), heart problems, and bowel problems.

One review of studies looked at the risks from having treatment for prostate cancer after having a PSA test.[29]Chou R, Croswell JM, Dana T, et al. Screening for prostate cancer: a review of the evidence for the U.S. Preventive Services Task Force. Annals of Internal Medicine. 2011; 155: 762-771. Here's what it found:

  • Compared to men who had prostate cancer who chose watchful waiting, treating three men with surgery or seven men with radiation therapy would mean one more man would have erection problems.
  • For every five men who had an operation to remove their prostate gland, one more man would have urinary incontinence compared to men who chose watchful waiting.
  • Around 1 man in 100 who has surgery to remove his prostate gland is at risk of dying. Between 1 and 3 men in 100 who have surgery will be at risk of serious heart problems.
  • Compared to men who had prostate cancer who chose watchful waiting, men who had radiation therapy (external beam or brachytherapy) were more likely to have bowel problems.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT NOT HAVING THE PSA TEST

What is choosing not having a PSA test?

If you choose not to have a PSA test, you don't have a blood test to find out whether you have a raised risk of prostate cancer.

Who can choose not to have a PSA test?

Nobody needs to have a PSA test if they don't want to have one.

What effect does not having a PSA test have on your chances of being diagnosed with prostate cancer?

Not having a PSA test reduces your chances of being diagnosed with prostate cancer.

In studies looking at big groups of men who don't have a PSA test, between 4 and 10 in 100 will be diagnosed with prostate cancer during the next 10 to 15 years.[18]Djulbegovic M, Beyth RJ, Neuberger MM, et al. Screening for prostate cancer: systematic review and meta-analysis of randomised controlled trials. BMJ. 2010; 341: c4543.

What is the effect of not having a PSA test on when you find out you have prostate cancer?

Men who don't have a PSA test are less likely to be diagnosed soon after the cancer has started. Later diagnosis may not be a disadvantage. It may mean that you live longer without knowing you have prostate cancer. It may not mean your life is shorter than it would have been if you had known about the prostate cancer.

In one group of men who didn't have a PSA test, aged between 65 and 74, prostate cancer was diagnosed around eight years later than in men who did have a PSA test.[43]Finne P, Fallah M, Hakama M, et al. Lead-time in the European randomised study of screening for prostate cancer. European Journal of urology. 2010; 46: 3102-3108.

Men who find out they have prostate cancer without having a PSA test are likely to have cancer that has not spread beyond the prostate gland. They seem less likely to have cancer that has spread outside the prostate or to other parts of the body.[18]Djulbegovic M, Beyth RJ, Neuberger MM, et al. Screening for prostate cancer: systematic review and meta-analysis of randomised controlled trials. BMJ. 2010; 341: c4543. [28]Lumen N, Fonteyne V, De Meerleert G, et al. What is the effect of not having a PSA test on your chances of dying from prostate cancer?

Not having a PSA test may mean your chances of dying from prostate cancer are about 1 in 1000 higher than someone who does have a PSA test.

Some studies have looked at how likely men are to die of prostate cancer if they have a PSA test, compared to if they don't have one. The research says:[24]Schröder FH, Hugosson J, Roobol MJ, et al. Prostate cancer mortality at 11 years of follow-up. New England Journal of Medicine. 2012; 366: 981-990.

  • For every 1,000 men who don't have a PSA test, 8 will eventually die of prostate cancer. That is one more than dies if they do have a PSA test.[18]Djulbegovic M, Beyth RJ, Neuberger MM, et al. Screening for prostate cancer: systematic review and meta-analysis of randomised controlled trials. BMJ. 2010; 341: c4543.
  • Not having a PSA test doesn’t seem to make much difference to how likely you are to die of prostate cancer if you have other serious health problems.[30]Heijnsdijk EAM, Wever EM, Auvinen A. Quality-of-life effects of prostate-specific antigen screening. New England Journal of Medicine. 2012; 367: 595-605.
What is the effect of not having a PSA test on how long you are likely to live?

On average, men who don't have a PSA test do not live shorter lives than other men. We don't know whether not having a PSA test will affect the length of your life, because there are so many factors that could affect that. 

Research looking at groups of men in studies shows:

  • Around 20 in 100 men who didn’t have a PSA test died during the studies, for any reason
  • Around 17 in 100 men who had a PSA test died during the studies.

The studies lasted between four and twenty years. This difference was so small it could have been down to chance.[28]Lumen N, Fonteyne V, De Meerleert G, et al. Population screening for prostate cancer: an overview of available studies and meta-analysis. International Journal of Urology. 2012; 19: 100-108.

What is the effect of not having a PSA test on your quality of life?

We don’t know if deciding not to have a PSA test makes much difference to your quality of life, because there is not enough research to be sure.[31]Lane JA, Hamdy FC, Martin RM, et al. Latest results from the UK trials evaluating prostate cancer screening and treatment: the CAP and ProtecT studies. European Journal of Cancer. 2010; 46: 3095-3101.

Some studies have looked at the effect of not having a PSA test on how likely you are to feel anxious or worried. 

  • Not having a PSA test doesn’t seem to make much difference to how likely you are to feel anxious or depressed.[53]Schröder FH, Hugosson J, Roobol MJ, et al. Screening and prostate-cancer mortality in a randomized European study. New England Journal of Medicine. 2009; 360: 1320-1328.
  • Men who don’t have a PSA test are less likely to worry about prostate cancer than men who have a raised PSA level, but who don’t have prostate cancer. Men who don't have a PSA test are less likely to have sexual problems than men who have a PSA test. 
What are the main side effects of choosing not to have a PSA test?

If you choose not to have a PSA test, you will not know if you have a raised risk of prostate cancer. You won’t need to have further tests or treatments for prostate cancer unless you get symptoms of prostate cancer. This means you won't have side effects from the PSA test or the other prostate cancer tests.

How might choosing not to have PSA test affect your life?

If you choose not to have a PSA test, you will not know if you have a raised risk of prostate cancer. Some men who choose not to have a PSA test feel worried and anxious about their risk of prostate cancer.

EFFECT ON CHANCES OF HAVING COMPLICATIONS FROM BIOPSY OR TREATMENT

Having the PSA test

If you have a raised PSA test result, your doctor will suggest you have a biopsy. If you are diagnosed with prostate cancer, you will need to decide about treatment. Some men have problems or complications after a biopsy or treatment for prostate cancer. Problems from prostate cancer treatment include urinary incontinence and difficulty getting an erection.
