Our Decision Support telephone service is here to assist and guide you through the Shared Decision Making process.

The Decision Support team is made of professional Health Coaches who are clinicians, commonly nurses, who have undertaken additional training to become a Health Coach.

The Health Coaches will assist you by:

  • Providing you with information
  • Helping you to understand your condition
  • Recognise what is important to you regarding the outcome of treatment
  • Identifying potential solutions
  • Encourage you in discussing options with your family
  • Transferring skills which will assist you in using the information and resources available to you
  • Support you in building confidence in discussing your choice with your doctor
  • You may find that this can be achieved in one telephone call with a Health Coach however; if further calls are required to support you in reaching your decision the Health Coach will schedule these with you.

Important Things To Note

  • A Health Coach does not know which treatment option is best for you.
  • The Health Coach does not have information about your medical history.
  • You will still need to talk over your decision and reasons for your decision with your doctor or nurse.

MORE INFORMATION ABOUT RECTAL CANCER (WITHOUT DISTANT SPREAD)

What is the rectum?

The rectum is the last 15 centimetres (6 inches) of the large bowel. The large bowel (rectum) and the small bowel (colon) are parts of the digestive system, a group of organs that help break down food.

The walls of the colon and rectum are made up of layers. The inner layer is called the mucosa. This is the layer next to where food waste passes. It is from cells in the mucosa that cancer usually starts.

The waste from food passes through the colon into the rectum. The colon and rectum are covered with muscles. They automatically tighten and relax to push food from the stomach through to the rectum. The food waste leaves the body through an opening called the anus.

What is rectal cancer?

Rectal cancer is cancer that starts in the rectum (the end of the colon furthest from the stomach).

The inner lining of the bowel is a thin layer of cells called the mucosa. Cells in this lining have a short life. They grow, divide in two, then die and get replaced. Your body controls this activity so that cells are replaced every five days or so.

But sometimes things go wrong. Cells keep dividing when they don't need to, and old ones don't die. If this happens, you can get a cluster of extra cells called a tumour. Some tumours are made up of normal cells. These are not dangerous. Doctors call them benign tumours or polyps. They stay put and don't cause any problems. But other tumours are made up of cancer cells. Doctors call these malignant tumours. Rectal cancer happens when a malignant tumour starts to grow in the rectum.

Cells in rectal tumours can break away and move to other parts of your body. These breakaway cells are carried in your bloodstream or in your lymphatic system. This is a network of tubes and small collections of cells called lymph nodes that helps the body fight off disease.

When the cancer cells arrive at a different part of your body, they can start growing into another tumour. When cancer spreads like this, it's called metastasis. This Patient Decision Aid is for people who have rectal cancer that hasn't spread to any other part of the body, other than the rectum or nearby lymph nodes.

If you have rectal cancer, your doctor will need to find out how big your cancer is and whether it has spread (metastasised). This is called staging your cancer and helps help doctors recommend which treatment might be most suitable for you. Your specialist is likely to arrange for you to have one or more additional tests:[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. CG131. The diagnosis and management of colorectal cancer. November 2011. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/nicemedia/live/13597/56998/56998.pdf (accessed on 9 January 2013).

  • CT scan: this uses computerised x-rays to build a three-dimensional picture of the organs and tissues inside your abdomen (tummy). It can show how big the tumour is and whether it has spread outside your bowel, for example to your liver or lungs.
  • MRI scan: this uses a magnetic field to get very detailed pictures of the inside of your body and show whether the cancer has spread. It can help show what stage the cancer is and whether you might benefit from treatment such as radiotherapy.[2]MERCURY study group. Diagnostic accuracy of preoperative magnetic resonance imaging in predicting curative resection of rectal cancer: prospective observational study. BMJ 2006;333;779. [3]Beets-Tan RGH, Beets GL, Vliegen RFA, et al. Accuracy of magnetic resonance imaging in prediction of tumour-free resection margin in rectal cancer surgery. The Lancet, Volume 357, Issue 9255, Pages 497-504, 17 February 2001.

Rectal cancer is numbered in four stages:[4]National Cancer Institute. National Institutes of Health. Rectal cancer physician data query. Stages of rectal cancer. Available at http://www.cancer.gov/cancertopics/pdq/treatment/rectal/Patient/page2 (accessed 11 February 2013).

  • A stage 0 cancer has abnormal cells in a layer of the wall of the rectum called the mucosa. The abnormal cells are in the mucosa only. These abnormal cells are not a cancer, but may become cancer and spread to other parts of the body. Stage 0 cancer is also called carcinoma in situ.
  • A stage 1 cancer has moved through the inner layers of the bowel and may also have spread to the thick muscle layer of the bowel wall, but not to the lymph nodes or to other parts of the body.
  • A stage 2 cancer has spread through the thick muscle layer of the bowel wall, but not to the lymph nodes or to other parts of the body.
  • A stage 3 cancer has spread through the thick muscle layer of the bowel wall and also to the lymph nodes.
  • A stage 4 cancer has spread through the thick muscle layer of the bowel wall to other parts of the body and possibly to the lymph nodes.
What are the symptoms of rectal cancer?

Many people who have rectal cancer don't have symptoms. Some of the symptoms of bowel cancer are also symptoms of other illnesses that affect the digestive system. 

These symptoms may suggest rectal cancer:[5]US National Library of Medicine. Colon cancer. Available at http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmedhealth/PMH0001308/ (accessed on 9 January 2013).

  • Blood in the stools
  • Diarrhoea or other changes in bowel habits that last for more than six weeks
  • Feeling as if your bowels don't empty properly
  • Stomach pain, cramps, or bloating and wind in the lower abdomen
  • Tenderness in the lower abdomen
  • Losing weight for no known reason, or losing your appetite.

There are tests people need to have to be sure they have rectal cancer.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. CG131. The diagnosis and management of colorectal cancer. November 2011. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/nicemedia/live/13597/56998/56998.pdf (accessed on 9 January 2013).

What causes rectal cancer?

There is no single cause of rectal cancer. Smoking cigarettes and drinking alcohol increase the chances of getting many different cancers, including rectal cancer. Almost all rectal cancers start in glands in the lining of the rectum. Nearly all rectal cancers begin as non-cancerous (benign) polyps, which slowly develop into cancer.

People have an increased risk of rectal cancer if they:

  • Are older than 60. Your chances of getting rectal cancer increase as you get older. About 83 in 100 people who have colon and rectal cancer are aged 60 or over.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. CG131. The diagnosis and management of colorectal cancer. November 2011. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/nicemedia/live/13597/56998/56998.pdf (accessed on 9 January 2013).
  • Have colorectal polyps. A benign polyp is a small, non-cancerous growth on the inside of the bowel wall. Benign polyps are not harmful in themselves, but can develop into a tumour.
  • Have an inflammatory bowel disease (such as ulcerative colitis or Crohn's disease).
  • Have a close relative who has had rectal or colon cancer. If no one in your close family has had colon or rectal cancer, your chances of getting this type of cancer are about 2 in 100. But if one of your parents, a brother, a sister, or a child has had colon or rectal cancer, your risk increases to about 6 in 100. If you have a close relative who was younger than 45 when diagnosed, your risk increases to roughly 10 in 100.[8]Iskowitz SH, Kim YS. Colonic polyps and polyposis syndromes. In: Feldman M, Sleisenger MH, Scharschmidt BF. Sleisenger and Fordtran’s gastrointestinal and liver disease: pathophysiology, diagnosis, management. 9th edition. Saunders, Philadelphia, PA; 2010.
  • Have certain inherited diseases. Two of the most common are familial adenomatous polyposis (FAP) and hereditary nonpolyposis colorectal cancer (HNPCC, also known as Lynch syndrome). About 1 in 100 people with colon or rectal cancer have FAP. Between 3 and 8 in 100 people with colon or rectal cancer have HNPCC.[9]Mayer RJ. Gastrointestinal tract cancer. In: Braunwald E, Hauser SL, Fauci AS, et al. Harrison's principles of internal medicine. McGraw-Hill, New York, NY; 2011. 18th edition
  • Are very overweight. At least 10 in 100 people with rectal cancer in the UK are obese.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. CG131. The diagnosis and management of colorectal cancer. November 2011. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/nicemedia/live/13597/56998/56998.pdf (accessed on 9 January 2013). Doctors usually consider adults obese if they have a body mass index (BMI) of 30 or more.
  • Rectal cancer may be linked to a high-fat, low-fibre diet and to a high intake of red meat. But the evidence about this is not clear.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. CG131. The diagnosis and management of colorectal cancer. November 2011. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/nicemedia/live/13597/56998/56998.pdf (accessed on 9 January 2013).
How do I find out I have rectal cancer?

It's best to see your GP if you have any bowel problems or symptoms that are worrying you. Don't wait to see if your symptoms go away. They may not be cancer, but if so it's important to rule out cancer as a cause. If you delay getting symptoms checked out, and you do have cancer, the cancer might be more advanced by the time it is diagnosed. This makes it harder to cure.

If you have symptoms that your GP suspects are rectal cancer, he or she is likely to ask you some questions about your symptoms, such as how long they have bothered you and when you notice them, and some questions about your general health. The GP is also likely to give you a physical exam to feel gently for swelling around your stomach or in your abdomen, and putting a gloved finger into your back passage (rectum) to feel for any lumps that could be cancerous.

Your GP may also give you a blood test to check for anaemia. If you are bleeding inside from a tumour or for another reason, you can become anaemic. This is when you feel tired and look pale. It happens because you don't have enough red blood cells in your body.

If your GP thinks that you could have cancer, he or she should refer you urgently to a hospital consultant or specialist clinic. You should get an appointment within two weeks of going to the GP.[12]Department of Health. Referral guidelines for suspected cancer. March 2000. Available at http://www.dh.gov.uk (accessed on 31 May 2012). If your GP doesn't think it could be cancer, you may still be referred to a specialist, but the appointment won't be so urgent. Most people who see a bowel specialist, including many of those who see one urgently, don't have cancer. Around 10 in 100 people who see a bowel specialist urgently have rectal cancer.[13]Scott MA, Knight A, Brown K, et al. A single common urgent pathway for all colorectal referrals reduces time to diagnosis and treatment. Colorectal Dis. 2006 Nov;8(9):766.71.

The specialist will arrange an endoscopy to look inside your bowel.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. CG131. The diagnosis and management of colorectal cancer. November 2011. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/nicemedia/live/13597/56998/56998.pdf (accessed on 9 January 2013). The endoscopy involves putting a flexible tube with a light and a viewing lens or camera into your anus to see inside your bowel.

There are different types of endoscopy. It's most likely you will have an endoscopy called a colonoscopy, which examines your entire large bowel.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. CG131. The diagnosis and management of colorectal cancer. November 2011. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/nicemedia/live/13597/56998/56998.pdf (accessed on 9 January 2013).

If you are not able to have a colonoscopy, your specialist is likely to suggest you have a different type of endoscopy to look at the rest of your bowel. This is usually a test called flexible sigmoidoscopy, which examines just the last third of your large bowel.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. CG131. The diagnosis and management of colorectal cancer. November 2011. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/nicemedia/live/13597/56998/56998.pdf (accessed on 9 January 2013).

If you have a flexible sigmoidoscopy, you will also need to have another test to show the rest of your rectum. This may be a type of scan called a CT scan or a type of x-ray called a barium enema.

During the endoscopy, your doctor is also likely to take a small sample of any growths or other abnormal tissue that he or she finds in your colon or rectum. This is called a biopsy. The tissue can be examined in the laboratory to see if it is cancer.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. CG131. The diagnosis and management of colorectal cancer. November 2011. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/nicemedia/live/13597/56998/56998.pdf (accessed on 9 January 2013). 

How many people have rectal cancer?

Around 14,000 people are diagnosed with rectal cancer every year in the UK. That's about two in every 10,000 people. Rectal cancer is more common in older people. Each year around 6,000 people die from rectal cancer.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. CG131. The diagnosis and management of colorectal cancer. November 2011. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/nicemedia/live/13597/56998/56998.pdf (accessed on 9 January 2013).

What support is available to me?

Rectal cancer can be a complicated illness to manage. It can cause bowel and sexual problems and other symptoms. All of this can affect the way you feel.[19]Engel J, Kerr J, Schlesinger-Raab A, et al. Quality of life in rectal cancer patients. A four-year prospective study. Annals of Surgery. 2003; 238: 203-213. Some people may feel it is helpful to have psychological support. 

If you decide to have hospital treatment you should be offered a package of care and support called enhanced recovery. This is a programme that aims to speed up the time you spend in hospital after having an operation to treat rectal cancer, and help you go home feeling better sooner. It can be extended to include things like counselling, advice about your diet and staying healthy, and exercises and rehabilitation after you leave hospital. Many people who follow an enhanced recovery programme find it helpful.[20]Blazeby JM, Soulsby M, Winstone K, et al. A qualitative evaluation of patients' experiences of an enhanced recovery programme for colorectal cancer. Colorectal Disease. 2010; 12: 236-242.

You can talk to a specialist nurse about how to manage some of the effects of certain treatments. For example, should you need a stoma you can talk to a stoma nurse for advice and support before and after your operation about how to look after your stoma, and what kinds of things to do to stay generally healthy during and after treatment.

What will happen?

No one can say for sure what will happen to you. You are not a statistic, and no two people respond to treatment or to cancer in exactly the same way. We know that, in general, the outlook for your disease depends mainly on:

  • How early your cancer was detected
  • The stage of your cancer
  • Whether any of your lymph nodes are involved and where these nodes are
  • Whether your cancer has spread beyond your lymph nodes
  • What the tumour looks like under a microscope (the more your tumour cells look like normal cells, the better your outlook may be).

These things can also affect how likely a treatment is to cure your cancer, or stop it from spreading.

Here's what we know:

  • Around 50 in 100 people diagnosed with rectal cancer live for at least five years after diagnosis.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. CG131. The diagnosis and management of colorectal cancer. November 2011. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/nicemedia/live/13597/56998/56998.pdf (accessed on 9 January 2013). Some people live for longer than this, others live for less long.
  • You're more likely to live for longer if you are younger.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. CG131. The diagnosis and management of colorectal cancer. November 2011. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/nicemedia/live/13597/56998/56998.pdf (accessed on 9 January 2013).
  • The number of people with rectal cancer who live for at least 10 years is only a little lower than those who live for at least five years. This suggests that if your cancer doesn't come back within five years, it probably won't come back.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. CG131. The diagnosis and management of colorectal cancer. November 2011. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/nicemedia/live/13597/56998/56998.pdf (accessed on 9 January 2013).

People are less likely to survive for at least five years if they have later stage rectal cancer. Around 83 in 100 people with stage 1 rectal cancer live for at least five years. Around 64 in 100 people with stage 2 cancer, 38 in 100 with stage 3 cancer, and 3 in 100 with stage 4 cancer live for this long.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. CG131. The diagnosis and management of colorectal cancer. November 2011. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/nicemedia/live/13597/56998/56998.pdf (accessed on 9 January 2013).

The number of people who survive for at least five years after being told they have rectal cancer has doubled between the early 1970s and early 2000s.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. CG131. The diagnosis and management of colorectal cancer. November 2011. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/nicemedia/live/13597/56998/56998.pdf (accessed on 9 January 2013). This is probably due to better treatment, such as major surgery, and better adjuvant treatments, such as radiotherapy and chemotherapy.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT MAJOR SURGERY

What is major surgery for rectal cancer?

This means having an operation to cut out the cancer from your rectum by removing it through a cut in your abdomen. During the operation the surgeon will also remove a large section of your rectum around the cancer.

The two types of major surgery for rectal cancer included in this Patient Decision Aid are anterior resection and abdominoperineal resection.

In anterior resection, a surgeon makes cuts in your abdomen and then removes the part of your rectum containing the tumour, along with nearby lymph nodes and tissue on either side of the tumour. He or she will then join your colon to the remaining part of the rectum so that, after the operation, you will pass stools in the usual way. Sometimes, to give the area time to heal, you may need to have a loop ileostomy. This is where the last part of the small intestine, the ileum, is brought out through an opening in the skin of your abdomen. Usually this can be reversed, and the intestines reconnected, by having a small procedure around eight weeks later. In the meantime, you wear a stoma bag over the stoma to collect your stools.

In abdominoperineal resection the surgeon makes cuts in the abdomen, and another in the area around the anus. This allows the surgeon to remove the part of the rectum around the anus (back passage) and the tissues surrounding it, including the sphincter muscle (a ring of muscle around the end of your rectum). After you have your rectum removed in this way, you will need a way to pass your stools out of the body. This is called a colostomy. A surgeon stitches the end of your colon to an opening in the skin of your abdomen called a stoma. Your stools then pass through the stoma into an ileostomy bag. This is permanent.

Both anterior resection and abdominoperineal resection can be done in one of two ways. Keyhole surgery (also called laparoscopy) removes the tumour by making several small cuts and one slightly larger cut in your abdomen. Open surgery involves taking out the tumour through one large cut in your abdomen. Both types of operation work equally well at stopping rectal cancer from returning.[26]Jackson TD, Kaplan GG, Arena G, et al. Laparoscopic versus open resection for colorectal cancer: a metaanalysis of oncologic outcomes. Journal of the American College of Surgeons. 2007; 204: 439-446.

Before your operation, you may also have radiotherapy or a combination of radiotherapy with chemotherapy. This is done to shrink the cancer before removing it. It is called neo-adjuvant treatment. Whether you need neo-adjuvant treatment depends on whether there is a high chance that your cancer might come back after your operation.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. CG131. The diagnosis and management of colorectal cancer. November 2011. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/nicemedia/live/13597/56998/56998.pdf (accessed on 9 January 2013).

Who can have major surgery for rectal cancer?

Major surgery is usually used on later stage tumours. Major surgery may not be suitable for everyone or every type of tumour. The type of major surgery that is right for you depends on where your tumour is, how big it is, what type of rectal cancer you have, and how advanced the cancer is. It may also depend on your general health.

If a later stage rectal cancer is very close to the anus or the sphincter muscles (a ring of muscle surrounding the rectum and the anus) then it is usually not possible to do an anterior resection.

What are the main effects of major surgery on rectal cancer?

The effect of major surgery on your rectal cancer can depend on what stage rectal cancer you have. Generally, later stage rectal cancers that are treated with major surgery are less likely to be cured or stopped from spreading than earlier stage cancers. For most people with rectal cancer, major surgery is the only treatment that has a chance of permanently stopping rectal cancer from returning.

In one group of people who had major surgery to remove a stage 1 tumour from their rectum, around 81 in 100 were still alive after five years.[28]Wu Y, Wu YY, Li S, et al. TEM and conventional rectal surgery for T1 rectal cancer: a meta-analysis. Hepato-Gastroenterology. 2011; 58: 364-368. Not everyone with rectal cancer dies of rectal cancer. The number of people who survive for at least five or 10 years has increased in recent years.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. CG131. The diagnosis and management of colorectal cancer. November 2011. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/nicemedia/live/13597/56998/56998.pdf (accessed on 9 January 2013).

Having surgery to remove rectal cancer can affect how likely it is that your cancer comes back. In one group of people with early rectal cancer who had major surgery, between 2 and 9 in 100 had their tumour come back, either in the same place or elsewhere in the body, within five years.[30]Chang A, Nahas C, Araujo S, et al. Early rectal cancer: local excision or radical surgery? Journal of Surgical Education. 2008; 65: 67-72.

Some studies have looked at whether neo-adjuvant treatment before major surgery improves your chances of living for longer or having better results than if you have surgery on its own. Here's what we know:

  • People who have radiotherapy before having major surgery for rectal cancer are less likely to have their cancer come back in the same place as people who don't have preoperative radiotherapy.[31]Folkesson J, Birgisson H, Pahlman L. Swedish Rectal Cancer Trial: long lasting benefits from radiotherapy on survival and local recurrence rate. J Clin Oncol. 2005 Aug 20;23(24):5644-50.
  • Around 9 in 100 people who have preoperative radiotherapy and then major surgery have their cancer come back in the same place. Around 26 in 100 people who have major surgery on its own have their cancer come back in the same place.[31]Folkesson J, Birgisson H, Pahlman L. Swedish Rectal Cancer Trial: long lasting benefits from radiotherapy on survival and local recurrence rate. J Clin Oncol. 2005 Aug 20;23(24):5644-50.
  • People who have preoperative radiotherapy for rectal cancer are more likely to live for longer after having treatment than people who don't have preoperative radiotherapy.
  • Around 38 in 100 people who had preoperative radiotherapy and then major surgery were still alive 13 years after having treatment.[31]Folkesson J, Birgisson H, Pahlman L. Swedish Rectal Cancer Trial: long lasting benefits from radiotherapy on survival and local recurrence rate. J Clin Oncol. 2005 Aug 20;23(24):5644-50. Around 30 in 100 people who had major surgery on its own were still alive 13 years after having treatment.[31]Folkesson J, Birgisson H, Pahlman L. Swedish Rectal Cancer Trial: long lasting benefits from radiotherapy on survival and local recurrence rate. J Clin Oncol. 2005 Aug 20;23(24):5644-50.
What are the main effects of major surgery for rectal cancer on what you can do and how you feel?

The type of major surgery you have for rectal cancer can affect what you do and how you feel. Here's what we know from studies:[35]Cornish JA, Tilney HS, Heriot AG, et al. A meta-analysis of quality of life for abdominoperineal excision of rectum versus anterior resection for rectal cancer. Annals of Surgical Oncology. 2007; 14: 2056-2068.

  • Both anterior resection and abdominoperineal resection affect your overall quality of life, your health, and how your health makes you feel, by about the same amount.
  • After their operation, people who had anterior resection were more able to do physical things than people who had abdominoperineal resection. This may be because of the nature of their cancer and the operation. Abdominoperineal resection is a more complicated operation than anterior resection and may have been used for cancers that were more complicated and had more of an effect on people's physical health.
  • People who had anterior resection had more energy and had fewer problems having sex compared to before their operation.
  • People who had abdominoperineal resection were less bothered by thinking problems and felt better emotionally compared to how they felt before their operation.

We're not sure if having a permanent colostomy after abdominoperineal resection affects how you feel compared to not having a colostomy at all, or having a temporary colostomy after anterior resection. There haven't been enough good-quality studies for us to know for sure.[36]Pachler J, Wille-Jørgensen P. Quality of life after rectal resection for cancer, with or without permanent colostomy. 2012. (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. One group of people who had a stoma for 12 months after having anterior resection scored the same on a questionnaire about their health as people who didn't have a stoma.[37]Lindgren R, Hallbook O, Rutegard J, et al. What is the risk for a permanent stoma after low anterior resection of the rectum for cancer? A six-year follow-up of a multicenter trial. Diseases of the Colon & Rectum. 2011; 54: 41-47.

What are the main complications of major surgery?

Having a major operation can cause heart or breathing problems after surgery, especially if you already have an illness affecting the heart or lungs.

Both types of major surgery for rectal cancer can cause complications. How likely you are to have complications depends on things like your age and your general health. In one group of people who had major surgery for stage 1 rectal cancer, around 47 in 100 had complications after treatment.[28]Wu Y, Wu YY, Li S, et al. TEM and conventional rectal surgery for T1 rectal cancer: a meta-analysis. Hepato-Gastroenterology. 2011; 58: 364-368.

Here's what we know from studies of people who had either anterior resection or abdominoperineal resection:[39]Paun BC, Cassie S, MacLean AR et al. Postoperative complications following surgery for rectal cancer. Annals of Surgery. 2010; 251: 807-818.

  • Around 11 in 100 had some faeces leak from the join where the two pieces of bowel were reconnected (anastomotic leakage). Faeces are toxic to the body and a leak can cause an infection in the inside lining of the abdomen (the peritoneum) called peritonitis. This needs medical treatment.
  • Around 7 in 100 people get an infection in the incisions the surgeons make to remove the cancer.
  • Around 12 in 100 people get an infection in their pelvis (the area between your hips).
  • There is a risk of dying during the operation. This depends on things including your age and your general health. Overall the chance of dying during or after major surgery for rectal cancer is around 2 in 100.

Major surgery for rectal cancer can cause bowel problems such as diarrhoea. This can affect around 35 in 100 people who have anterior resection. This is more likely if you have radiotherapy before having major surgery.[40]Scheer AS, Boushey RP, Liang S, et al. The long-term gastrointestinal functional outcomes following curative anterior resection in adults with rectal cancer: a systematic review and meta-analysis. Diseases of the Colon & Rectum. 2011; 54: 1589-1597.

Having a stoma doesn't seem to make a difference to how likely you are to have diarrhoea once the stoma has been reversed. One questionnaire of people who had a stoma for 12 months after an anterior resection found that, after the stoma was reversed, they were no more likely to have problems with diarrhoea or incontinence than people who didn't have a stoma.[37]Lindgren R, Hallbook O, Rutegard J, et al. What is the risk for a permanent stoma after low anterior resection of the rectum for cancer? A six-year follow-up of a multicenter trial. Diseases of the Colon & Rectum. 2011; 54: 41-47.

Having chemotherapy or radiotherapy may affect how likely you are to have complications after major surgery for rectal cancer. Here's what we know from studies:

  • People who have radiotherapy before having major surgery (called preoperative radiotherapy) for rectal cancer have more side effects later on in life than people who don't have preoperative radiotherapy.[42]Birgisson H, Pahlman L, Gunnarsson U, et al. Late gastrointestinal disorders after rectal cancer surgery with and without preoperative radiation therapy. British Journal of Surgery. 2008; 95: 206-213.
  • In one group of people, an average of 14 years after their treatment, 14 in 100 people who had preoperative radiotherapy had a small bowel obstruction and 6 in 100 people who didn't have preoperative radiotherapy had a bowel obstruction. They were also more likely to need an operation to treat the bowel obstruction.[42]Birgisson H, Pahlman L, Gunnarsson U, et al. Late gastrointestinal disorders after rectal cancer surgery with and without preoperative radiation therapy. British Journal of Surgery. 2008; 95: 206-213.
  • People who had preoperative radiotherapy were more likely to have infections and to go back to hospital after their treatment than people who didn't have preoperative radiotherapy.[42]Birgisson H, Pahlman L, Gunnarsson U, et al. Late gastrointestinal disorders after rectal cancer surgery with and without preoperative radiation therapy. How might having major surgery affect my life?

If you need neo-adjuvant treatment before having major surgery, you may need to take time off work, or ask someone to help you travel to and from hospital.

Depending on the operation you've had, you will probably be ready to go home after about two weeks. You will usually have an appointment between three and six weeks after your operation. Taking part in an enhanced recovery programme after major surgery for rectal cancer can reduce the time spent in hospital compared to people who aren't part of a programme.[20]Blazeby JM, Soulsby M, Winstone K, et al. A qualitative evaluation of patients' experiences of an enhanced recovery programme for colorectal cancer. Colorectal Disease. 2010; 12: 236-242.

If you have a colostomy, a specialist stoma nurse may arrange to see you at home to check your stoma. It can take around six weeks to recover from your operation. You will need to take time off work to have treatment and to recover from treatment. You may also need to take time off work, and ask someone to help you travel to and from hospital, if you need neo-adjuvant treatment before your operation.

Having major surgery for rectal cancer can mean you need a colostomy or a loop ileostomy so that your stools can be collected in a colostomy bag. If you have a colostomy or a loop ileostomy you will need to have a stoma (an opening in the skin of your abdomen that stools can pass through into a ileostomy bag). A colostomy or a loop ileostomy can either be temporary or permanent.

If your tumour is in the lower part of your rectum it's more likely that you will need to have your whole rectum and anus removed, and that you will need a permanent stoma.[37]Lindgren R, Hallbook O, Rutegard J, et al. What is the risk for a permanent stoma after low anterior resection of the rectum for cancer? A six-year follow-up of a multicenter trial. Diseases of the Colon & Rectum. 2011; 54: 41-47. Available at http://www.hqip.org.uk/assets/NCAPOP-Library/NCAPOP-2012-13/Bowel-Cancer-Audit-National-Report-pub-2012.pdf. (Accessed 13 March 2013).

Having a colostomy should not stop you doing anything you used to do. But you will have to change your routine and get used to changing the bag that collects your bowel movements. You'll get plenty of help adjusting to having a colostomy from your local hospital team who are looking after you, a stoma nurse, or a local colostomy support group. Having a colostomy should not make any difference to your job unless your work involves heavy digging, or other kinds of manual work. If you are not sure about this, ask your stoma nurse for advice.

Major surgery for rectal cancer can affect your sex life. If you are a man, abdominoperineal resection may stop your erections, affect your ability to reach orgasm, or make your orgasms less pleasurable. If you already had some sexual problems before having treatment, abdominoperineal resection may make these worse. If you are a woman, major surgery for rectal cancer can cause scar tissue in the abdomen that may sometimes cause pain or discomfort during sex.

In men, abdominoperineal resection can damage the nerves that control ejaculation leading to dry orgasms (orgasms without semen). Sometimes this surgery causes retrograde ejaculation, which means the semen goes backward into the bladder during an orgasm. This can affect your fertility and whether you can father a child.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT LOCAL REMOVAL

What is local removal of rectal cancer?

This is an operation to cut out the cancer from your rectum. Your surgeon will also cut out some healthy tissue around the tumour to try to get rid of any cancer cells that are left.

Local removal is also known as local resection. During the operation, your surgeon inserts instruments into your anus (back passage) to remove the cancer in your rectum.

The two types of local resection we consider in this Patient Decision Aid are called transanal endoscopic microsurgery (TEM or TEMS) and local transanal resection.

Transanal resection (also known as transanal excision) is done with instruments inserted through the anus. Your surgeon will examine your bowel, and find the exact location of the tumour. During this operation a surgeon cuts through all layers of the rectum to remove cancer as well as some surrounding normal rectal tissue, and then closes the opening in the rectal wall. It is usually done with general anaesthetic, which means you will be asleep during the operation. Occasionally a surgeon will use a local anaesthetic, which numbs the area being operated on so you don't feel pain.

In transanal endoscopic microsurgery a surgeon inserts a specially designed magnifying scope through the anus and into your rectum, and uses it to remove the tumour from the wall of your rectum. This can also be done with a general or local anaesthetic.

Who can have local removal?

Whether you can have local removal will depend on what stage rectal cancer you have.

Local removal is usually used on early stage tumours. It is not suitable for everyone with rectal cancer. The type of local removal that is right for you will depend on where your tumour is, how big it is, what type of rectal cancer you have, and how advanced the cancer is.

If your rectal cancer was found at a very early stage, it is often possible for it to be removed using local removal, through the anus. If local removal is used on a later stage tumour, there is a chance the surgeon will not be able to spot or remove tumours that have spread to the lymph nodes.

Local removal may be an option for people who have other serious illnesses, that may make a major operation less safe for them.

What are the main effects of local removal on rectal cancer?

What effect local removal will have on your rectal cancer can depend on what stage rectal cancer you have. Generally, local removal is more likely to cure early stage rectal cancer, or stop it from spreading.

Between 69 and 83 in 100 people live for at least five years after having local removal for early stage rectal cancer.[30]Chang A, Nahas C, Araujo S, et al. But you are more likely to live for longer without cancer coming back if you have surgery than if you have one type of local removal (transanal endoscopic microsurgery).[52]Sgourakis G, Lanitis S, Gockel I, et al. What are the main effects of local removal on what you can do and how you feel?

We don't know if having local removal for rectal cancer has an effect on what you are able to do. Some people may find that their symptoms improve after having local removal for rectal cancer. This can mean they are able to do more everyday things.

What are the main side effects of local removal?

Having local removal for rectal cancer can cause complications. Your chances of having complications depend in part on your age and your general health.

How might local removal affect my life?

If you have local removal, you may be able to leave hospital either the same day or the next day. You may have to stay in hospital for longer than this if you have other health problems. After this you may need up to six weeks to recover at home. You will need to take time off work to have treatment and to recover from treatment.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT MONITORING

What is monitoring?

This means monitoring the cancer to see if it is growing and, if needed, treatment to help the symptoms and pain. This option doesn't remove or shrink the cancer, and doesn't treat or cure the cancer.

Monitoring does not cure your cancer or stop it from spreading, unlike most other treatments for rectal cancer. 

A part of monitoring may mean having regular checks to see what is happening to your cancer. Monitoring is an option if you can't have other treatments, or don't want to have other treatments for rectal cancer.

Not everyone who has rectal cancer wants to have treatment to stop the cancer growing or spreading. Some people decide not to have treatments for their cancer, if they feel this will have little effect on how long they live. These people may feel that they don't want to spend time in their later years having surgery or other treatments.

Doctors recommend that if you decide not to have treatment, you still have regular appointments to see if your condition has changed or the cancer is growing.

Monitoring also involves treating your symptoms, like laser therapy to stop any bleeding inside your bowel, or blood transfusions if you have lost a lot of blood. Monitoring also involves treatments to help you manage your pain (palliative treatments).

You can discuss with your doctor exactly what kinds of treatments might be available to help you. You can also see a specialist nurse who can advise you on what types of foods to eat and ways to stay as healthy as you can for as long as possible.

Who can have monitoring?

Monitoring may be offered to people who either can't have other treatments, or decide not to have other treatments, that cure rectal cancer or stop it from spreading.

Your doctor may suggest you have treatment if your cancer grows or spreads to other parts of your body. The decision to have treatment or not is yours.

What are the main effects of monitoring on rectal cancer?

Monitoring has no effect on rectal cancer. It will not cure your cancer or stop it from spreading. 

Monitoring can help tell if your cancer is changing or growing. You may decide to have no treatment unless something changes. You can change your mind about treatment at any time.

Deciding to have monitoring, or to wait before having treatment, may mean that rectal cancer is no longer able to be treated or less likely to be cured.

What are the main effects of monitoring on what you can do and how you feel?

Rectal cancer that is not treated will continue to grow or spread to other parts of the body. This can affect what you can do and how you feel.

What are the main side effects of monitoring?

Monitoring is not a treatment, so there are no side effects or complications.

If you decide not to have treatment for your rectal cancer, there is a chance your cancer may continue to grow or spread to other parts of your body.

Rectal cancer that is not treated may cause other problems, including bleeding, pain, and bowel obstruction.

How might monitoring affect my life?

There are no effects on your life from having no treatment, other than the effects of the cancer itself.

You may have regular hospital appointments and tests to monitor your cancer and see if it is spreading to other parts of your body. Some people may prefer to have appointments with their GP or at a local clinic, in a hospice, or at home.

If you decide to have other treatments to help your symptoms, like bleeding in your stomach, this may require some time in hospital or having to take pain medication.
