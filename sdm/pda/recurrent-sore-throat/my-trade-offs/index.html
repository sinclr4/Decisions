Our Decision Support telephone service is here to assist and guide you through the Shared Decision Making process.

The Decision Support team is made of professional Health Coaches who are clinicians, commonly nurses, who have undertaken additional training to become a Health Coach.

The Health Coaches will assist you by:

  • Providing you with information
  • Helping you to understand your condition
  • Recognise what is important to you regarding the outcome of treatment
  • Identifying potential solutions
  • Encourage you in discussing options with your family
  • Transferring skills which will assist you in using the information and resources available to you
  • Support you in building confidence in discussing your choice with your doctor
  • You may find that this can be achieved in one telephone call with a Health Coach however; if further calls are required to support you in reaching your decision the Health Coach will schedule these with you.

Important Things To Note

  • A Health Coach does not know which treatment option is best for you.
  • The Health Coach does not have information about your medical history.
  • You will still need to talk over your decision and reasons for your decision with your doctor or nurse.

MORE INFORMATION ABOUT RECURRENT SORE THROAT

What is recurrent sore throat?

Recurrent sore throat is when you keep getting sore throats. Another name for sore throat is pharyngitis.

Doctors say you have recurrent sore throat if you have had a sore throat: [1]Scottish Intercollegiate Guidelines Network. Management of sore throat and indications for tonsillectomy. National clinical guideline 117. April 2010. Available at: http://www.sign.ac.uk/pdf/sign117.pdf (accessed 15 October 2012).

  • At least seven times in the past year, or
  • At least five times in each of the past two years, or
  • At least three times in each of the past three years.

Recurrent sore throat may stop you from doing everyday things like going to school.

A sore throat is usually caused by an infection from a virus or bacteria. It is often a symptom of another illness, such as a cold, flu, or tonsillitis.

Doctors usually say children with a sore throat caused by an infection should be kept away from school for two days to prevent the infection spreading to other children. Sore throat infections are easily passed from one child to another at school. This can happen if a child breathes in droplets in the air, after another child with the infection sneezes or coughs. Sore throat infections can also be passed on in saliva, or on a child’s hands.

What is tonsillitis?

Tonsillitis is when you get swelling and inflammation (redness) in your tonsils. This is usually caused by an infection from a virus or bacteria. Your adenoids may become infected when you have tonsillitis.

Tonsillitis will cause a sore throat. You may also get fever and headache. Your recurrent sore throat may be caused by your tonsils and adenoids being infected.

What are tonsils and adenoids, and what do they do?

Tonsils are two small round glands at the back of your throat behind your tongue. They are part of your immune system. Doctors think tonsils act like a barrier to stop infections from spreading further into your body. By the age of about 4 years old, the rest of the immune system takes over this function.

Adenoids are small lumps of tissue at the back of your throat, just above your tonsils. They are part of your immune system. They help to protect your body from infections from viruses and bacteria. 

You only have adenoids during childhood. They start to grow from birth and they reach their maximum size after around three to five years. They then start to shrink. By adulthood they completely disappear. This is because your immune system is stronger when you're an adult and you won't need them anymore.

What are the symptoms of recurrent sore throat?

The main symptoms of recurrent sore throat are:[2]NHS Choices. Sore throat – symptoms. May 2012. Available at: http://www.nhs.uk/Conditions/Sore-throat/Pages/Symptoms.aspx (accessed on 25 October 2012).

  • Painful, itchy, or scratchy throat, especially when you swallow
  • Redness or tenderness in your throat
  • Swollen tonsils.

You may have other symptoms of an infection, such as:[2]NHS Choices. Sore throat – symptoms. May 2012. Available at: http://www.nhs.uk/Conditions/Sore-throat/Pages/Symptoms.aspx (accessed on 25 October 2012).

  • Headache
  • Stiff, aching muscles
  • Tiredness
  • Cough
  • Runny or blocked nose
  • Fever/high temperature of over 38°C (or 100.4°F). If you have a high temperature that doesn’t go down, even after taking painkillers such as paracetamol or ibuprofen, you may have a more serious illness. In this case you should see your doctor.

Your symptoms will be the same if your sore throat is caused by a virus or bacteria. Sore throat caused by a virus won't be any worse or last any longer than sore throat caused by bacteria.[1]Scottish Intercollegiate Guidelines Network. Management of sore throat and indications for tonsillectomy. National clinical guideline 117. April 2010. Available at: http://www.sign.ac.uk/pdf/sign117.pdf (accessed 15 October 2012).

Why has this happened to me?

The most common cause of sore throats are viruses, such as the ones that cause colds. Between 50 and 80 in 100 children with sore throat have it because they've caught a virus.[1]Scottish Intercollegiate Guidelines Network. Management of sore throat and indications for tonsillectomy. National clinical guideline 117. April 2010. Available at: http://www.sign.ac.uk/pdf/sign117.pdf (accessed 15 October 2012).

Viruses spread easily from one person to another. You can catch a virus from someone by breathing in the tiny droplets that come out of their nose and mouth after they sneeze or cough.[6]NHS Choices. Sore throat – causes. May 2012. Available at: http://www.nhs.uk/Conditions/Sore-throat/Pages/Causes.aspx (accessed on 25 October 2012).

Bacteria can also cause sore throats. The most common type of bacteria that causes sore throats is called Group A streptococcal bacteria.[7]Drug and Therapeutics Bulletin. Diagnosis and treatment of streptococcal sore throat. Drugs and Therapeutics Bulletin. 1995; 33: 9-12. This causes around 30 in 100 sore throats in children.[6]NHS Choices. Sore throat – causes. May 2012. Available at: http://www.nhs.uk/Conditions/Sore-throat/Pages/Causes.aspx (accessed on 25 October 2012).

Sore throats can be caused by things other than the usual bacterial and viral infections. These things are much less common. They include:[6]NHS Choices. Sore throat – causes. May 2012. Available at: http://www.nhs.uk/Conditions/Sore-throat/Pages/Causes.aspx (accessed on 25 October 2012).

  • Hay fever. This is an allergic reaction to pollen or spores.
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux. This is a condition that causes stomach acid to leak upwards into your gullet (the tube that goes from your mouth to your stomach).
  • Oral mucositis. This is inflammation of the layer of tissue that lines your mouth. It is caused by some cancer medications.
  • Irritation from having a tube passed down your nose and into your stomach to provide liquid food, which you may have if you're in hospital and cannot eat solid foods.  
  • Stevens-Johnson syndrome. This is a very severe allergic reaction to medications.
  • Kawasaki disease. This is a rare condition that affects children aged under 5 years.
  • Some blood disorders, such as leukaemia (bone marrow cancer) or aplastic anaemia (when the bone marrow does not produce enough blood cells).
How do I find out I have recurrent sore throat?

If you feel pain around your tonsils and the back of your throat, particularly when you swallow, you probably have a sore throat. You don't usually need to see your doctor to find out you have a sore throat.

You should see your doctor if your sore throat is bad, if it stops you from doing everyday things, or if it lasts longer than three days. You doctor will want to look at your mouth and throat. He or she may feel your neck to check for swelling. Your doctor will also ask you about your symptoms and how often you have them.

Your doctor can't tell just from your symptoms whether a sore throat is caused by bacteria or a virus.[10]Shaikh N, Swaminathan N, Hooper EG, et al. Accuracy and precision of the signs and symptoms of streptococcal pharyngitis in children: a systematic review. Journal of Pediatrics. 2012; 160: 487-493. Doctors can do tests to check if your sore throat is caused by bacteria. However, these tests are not always accurate.[1]Scottish Intercollegiate Guidelines Network. Management of sore throat and indications for tonsillectomy. National clinical guideline 117. April 2010. Available at: http://www.sign.ac.uk/pdf/sign117.pdf (accessed 15 October 2012).

  • At least seven times in the past year, or
  • At least five times in each of the past two years, or
  • At least three times in each of the past three years.
How many people have recurrent sore throat?

Recurrent sore throat is common, particularly among children.[13]MeReC. Managing sore throats. MeReC Bulletin. 1999; 10: 41-44. Each year about 10 in 100 people see their doctor because of recurrent sore throat.[14]Shvartzman P. Are antibiotics appropriate for sore throats? Careful prescribing is beneficial. BMJ. 1994; 309: 1011-1012.

What will happen?

A sore throat will usually clear up on its own. Most children get better within a week.[15]Spinks A, Glasziou PP, Del Mar CB. Antibiotics for sore throat (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

Sometimes the infection that is causing your sore throat spreads. If this happens, it may cause:

  • Change in your voice
  • Loss of voice (laryngitis)
  • Ear infection (otitis media)
  • Sinus infection (sinusitis). Your sinuses are small spaces in your skull that are filled with air. Sinusitis can cause pain in your face.

Some people can get serious complications from a sore throat, such as an infection (or abscess) on the tonsils. Serious complications from a sore throat are not common.

In the past, around 17 in 1,000 people with sore throat got rheumatic fever.[16]Spinks A, Glasziou PP, Del Mar CB. Antibiotics for sore throat (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. This is a serious complication that can develop following an untreated throat infection caused by a type of bacteria called streptococcus. It is less common nowadays.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT ACTIVE OBSERVATION

What is active observation for recurrent sore throat?

Active observation is where your doctor regularly monitors your recurrent sore throat to check if it gets better or worse, and how often it comes back.

If your sore throat is caused by bacteria, your doctor may prescribe you antibiotics. These are tablets or drops that you swallow which can help kill off bacteria. You will need to take them several times a day for around 10 days.[1]Scottish Intercollegiate Guidelines Network. Management of sore throat and indications for tonsillectomy. National clinical guideline 117. April 2010. Available at: http://www.sign.ac.uk/pdf/sign117.pdf (accessed 15 October 2012). Antibiotics won’t help if your sore throat is caused by a virus.

It's possible to do a test on your sore throat to check whether it is caused by bacteria. But your doctor may not test you, as these tests are not always accurate.[1]Scottish Intercollegiate Guidelines Network. Management of sore throat and indications for tonsillectomy. National clinical guideline 117. April 2010. Available at: http://www.sign.ac.uk/pdf/sign117.pdf (accessed 15 October 2012).

Who can have active observation for recurrent sore throat?

Anyone with recurrent sore throat can choose to have active observation.

If your sore throat is caused by bacteria, such as Group A streptococcus, you can have antibiotics. [1]Scottish Intercollegiate Guidelines Network. Management of sore throat and indications for tonsillectomy. National clinical guideline 117. April 2010. Available at: http://www.sign.ac.uk/pdf/sign117.pdf (accessed 15 October 2012). Antibiotics will only help if your sore throat is caused by bacteria. Antibiotics won't work if it's caused by a virus.

What are the main effects of active observation on recurrent sore throat?

A sore throat is likely to clear up by itself without treatment.

In a group of children with sore throat who took dummy pills (called a placebo):[16]Spinks A, Glasziou PP, Del Mar CB. Antibiotics for sore throat (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

  • Around 40 in 100 didn’t have a painful throat after three days
  • Around 85 in 100 didn’t have a fever after three days
  • Around 82 in 100 had no symptoms of sore throat after one week.

In one group of children who had active observation for two years, the average number of sore throats fell from eight in a year to four in a year.[21]Lock C, Wilson J, Steen N, et al. North of England and Scotland Study of Tonsillectomy and Adeno-tonsillectomy in Children (NESSTAC): a pragmatic randomised controlled trial with a parallel non-randomised preference study. Health Technology Assessment. 2010; 14: 13.

Taking antibiotics for your sore throat may help your symptoms. A group of children who took antibiotics for their sore throat had half the amount of pain and fever after three days of taking them, compared to before.[16]Spinks A, Glasziou PP, Del Mar CB. Antibiotics for sore throat (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

Your sore throat may last for less time if you have active observation and take antibiotics. One group of people who took antibiotics for their sore throat had symptoms that lasted 16 fewer hours, on average, than those who took dummy pills.[16]Spinks A, Glasziou PP, Del Mar CB. Antibiotics for sore throat (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

Having active observation may mean you are less likely to have another sore throat.[24]Lock C, Wilson J, Steen N, et al. North of England and Scotland Study of Tonsillectomy and Adeno-tonsillectomy in Children (NESSTAC): a pragmatic randomised controlled trial with a parallel non-randomised preference study. Health Technology Assessment. 2010; 14: 13. Doctors think this is because a recurrent sore throat is likely to clear up by itself without treatment.

What are the main effects of active observation on what you can do and how you feel?

Having active observation without antibiotics probably won’t affect what you can do.

Taking antibiotics for your sore throat probably won’t reduce the number of days you're off school. A group of children who took antibiotics for their sore throat had, on average, around two to three days off school.[25]Zwart S, Rovers MM, de Melker RA, et al. Penicillin for acute sore throat in children: randomised, double blind trial. BMJ. 2003; 327: 1324-1326. This was similar to a group who didn't take antibiotics.[25]Zwart S, Rovers MM, de Melker RA, et al. Penicillin for acute sore throat in children: randomised, double blind trial. BMJ. 2003; 327: 1324-1326.

If your sore throat is caused by bacteria, taking antibiotics may make your sore throat last for less time compared with not taking them.[16]Spinks A, Glasziou PP, Del Mar CB. Antibiotics for sore throat (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. This may mean you're able to return more quickly to doing the things that you weren't able to when you had a sore throat, such as eating certain foods. Taking antibiotics won't help if your sore throat is caused by a virus.

We don’t know if active observation affects how you feel. Research hasn't really looked at this.

What are the main side effects of active observation?

You're unlikely to get any side effects if you have active observation without antibiotics.

Some people who take antibiotics have side effects, such as an upset stomach, diarrhoea, or a rash. If you take an antibiotic too often and when you don't need it, this can make the bacteria become resistant to the antibiotic, so that it doesn't work any more.

How might active observation affect my life?

If you have active observation, you may have to spend time visiting your doctor. This may mean you need to take time off school. For parents and carers, this may mean taking time off work to take your child to the doctor.

You won't have to spend time having treatment if you choose to have active observation without antibiotics.

Some people find it hard to remember to take their tablets. If you are taking antibiotics you need to take them several times a day, for around 10 days. Otherwise they may not work.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT SURGERY

What is surgery for recurrent sore throat?

Surgery for recurrent sore throat is having an operation to remove your tonsils. This is called a tonsillectomy. You may also have an operation to remove your adenoids. This is called an adenoidectomy. An operation to remove both your tonsils and your adenoids is called an adenotonsillectomy.

You may get recurrent sore throat because your tonsils or adenoids keep getting infected. Having surgery to remove your tonsils or adenoids can stop them from getting infected. This may help to stop you from getting recurrent sore throat. Children who also have problems breathing when sleeping (such as those with a condition called obstructive sleep apnoea) may have an adenotonsillectomy.

If you choose to have surgery you will need to go to hospital. The operation to remove your tonsils is done by a specialist surgeon. It takes around 45 minutes. You may have to spend a night in hospital afterwards. The operation is done under general anaesthetic, which means you will be asleep during it.

If you have an operation to remove your adenoids, this will be done at the same time as having your tonsils removed. The operation to remove your tonsils and adenoids takes around 1 hour and 15 minutes. The operation is done under general anaesthetic, which means you will be asleep during it.

Who can have surgery for recurrent sore throat?

Surgery is for children who have a sore throat caused by tonsillitis that stops them from doing normal everyday things like going to school. You can have this operation if you've had these sore throats:[1]Scottish Intercollegiate Guidelines Network. Management of sore throat and indications for tonsillectomy. National clinical guideline 117. April 2010. Available at: http://www.sign.ac.uk/pdf/sign117.pdf (accessed 15 October 2012).

  • At least seven times in the past year, or
  • At least five times in each of the previous two years, or
  • At least three times in each of the previous three years.

Children who also have problems breathing when sleeping (such as those with a condition called obstructive sleep apnoea) may have an operation to remove both their tonsils and adenoids. This is called an adenotonsillectomy.

 

What are the main effects of surgery on recurrent sore throat?

Having surgery may help your sore throat symptoms in the future, such as throat pain and difficulty swallowing.[29]Burton MJ, Glasziou PP. Tonsillectomy or adeno-tonsillectomy versus non-surgical treatment for chronic/recurrent acute tonsillitis (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. It is more likely to help if the symptoms of your recurrent sore throat are severe.

Children who have surgery for severe sore throat don't get symptoms such as fever as many times in the year after surgery as those who don't have surgery.[30]Buskens E, van Staaij B, van den Akker J, et al. Adenotonsillectomy or watchful waiting in patients with mild to moderate symptoms of throat infections or adenotonsillar hypertrophy. Archives of Otolaryngology - Head & Neck Surgery. 2007; 133: 1083-1088.

Having surgery for severe recurrent sore throat may reduce the number of times you get throat infections and a fever in a year compared with not having surgery.[30]Buskens E, van Staaij B, van den Akker J, et al. Adenotonsillectomy or watchful waiting in patients with mild to moderate symptoms of throat infections or adenotonsillar hypertrophy. Archives of Otolaryngology - Head & Neck Surgery. 2007; 133: 1083-1088. Having surgery for mild recurrent sore throat probably won’t affect how many times a year you get a fever.[32]Van Staaij BK, van den Akker EH, Rovers MM, et al. Effectiveness of adenotonsillectomy in children with mild symptoms of throat infections or adenotonsillar hypertrophy: open, randomised controlled trial. BMJ. 2004; 329: 651.

Children who have surgery for severe recurrent sore throat have, on average, around three fewer sore throats in the year after surgery than in the year before.[29]Burton MJ, Glasziou PP. Tonsillectomy or adeno-tonsillectomy versus non-surgical treatment for chronic/recurrent acute tonsillitis (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. Those who have surgery for less-severe sore throat have around one fewer sore throat in the year after surgery than in the year before.[29]Burton MJ, Glasziou PP. Tonsillectomy or adeno-tonsillectomy versus non-surgical treatment for chronic/recurrent acute tonsillitis (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

Children who have surgery for recurrent sore throat have, on average, about 17 days with a sore throat in the year after surgery.[29]Burton MJ, Glasziou PP. Tonsillectomy or adeno-tonsillectomy versus non-surgical treatment for chronic/recurrent acute tonsillitis (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. Those who don’t have surgery have, on average, about 22 days with a sore throat in a year.[29]Burton MJ, Glasziou PP. Tonsillectomy or adeno-tonsillectomy versus non-surgical treatment for chronic/recurrent acute tonsillitis (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

Children who have surgery for recurrent sore throat are less likely to need to see their doctor because of a sore throat than those who have other treatments.[24]Lock C, Wilson J, Steen N, et al. North of England and Scotland Study of Tonsillectomy and Adeno-tonsillectomy in Children (NESSTAC): a pragmatic randomised controlled trial with a parallel non-randomised preference study. Health Technology Assessment. 2010; 14: 13.

What are the main effects of surgery on what you can do and how you feel?

Having surgery for recurrent sore throat may reduce the amount of time you’re off school. In a group of children who had surgery for recurrent sore throat, 89 in 100 children didn’t miss any school because of a sore throat for at least five months after surgery. Some of the children had not missed a school day with a sore throat after 18 months. [38]Robb PJ, Gowrinath K, Agyeman K, et al. Paediatric tonsillectomy: parental experience and outcomes. Laryngology & Otology. 2009; 123: 103-107.

Having surgery for recurrent sore throat may make you feel better.[24]Lock C, Wilson J, Steen N, et al. North of England and Scotland Study of Tonsillectomy and Adeno-tonsillectomy in Children (NESSTAC): a pragmatic randomised controlled trial with a parallel non-randomised preference study. Health Technology Assessment. 2010; 14: 13. In a group of 47 parents with children who had surgery for recurrent sore throat, 42 parents said their child felt either better or much better after surgery than before.[38]Robb PJ, Gowrinath K, Agyeman K, et al. Paediatric tonsillectomy: parental experience and outcomes. Laryngology & Otology. 2009; 123: 103-107.

What are the main side effects of surgery?

Having general anaesthetic may make you feel a bit wobbly and dizzy when you wake up after surgery. You may feel sick. You may feel pain in your neck and throat after having your tonsils and adenoids removed. This can last for up to two weeks after the surgery.

Surgery for recurrent sore throat can cause side effects, such as pain and bleeding. In one group of children who had surgery for recurrent sore throat, around 14 in 100 got side effects after the operation. About 3 in 100 had problems with bleeding after surgery.[29]Burton MJ, Glasziou PP. Tonsillectomy or adeno-tonsillectomy versus non-surgical treatment for chronic/recurrent acute tonsillitis (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

In another group of children who had surgery for recurrent sore throat, around 6 in 100 had problems because of the operation.[30]Buskens E, van Staaij B, van den Akker J, et al. Adenotonsillectomy or watchful waiting in patients with mild to moderate symptoms of throat infections or adenotonsillar hypertrophy. Archives of Otolaryngology - Head & Neck Surgery. 2007; 133: 1083-1088. The most common problems were bleeding and feeling sick or unwell. Around 5 in 100 children in this group needed further treatment for problems following surgery.[30]Buskens E, van Staaij B, van den Akker J, et al. Adenotonsillectomy or watchful waiting in patients with mild to moderate symptoms of throat infections or adenotonsillar hypertrophy. Archives of Otolaryngology - Head & Neck Surgery. 2007; 133: 1083-1088.

 

How might surgery affect my life?

You will need to spend time in hospital if you choose to have surgery. This may mean you have to miss school. For parents and carers, this may mean taking time off work to look after your child. You can usually go home the same day as having surgery, or the day after.

You may find it painful to swallow straight after surgery. This may affect what you can eat and drink, and it may affect your speech. It can take up to two weeks before your throat feels normal again so that you can go back to school and do normal everyday things.

Taking painkillers like paracetamol and ibuprofen may help if you have pain or fever after surgery.[1]Scottish Intercollegiate Guidelines Network. Management of sore throat and indications for tonsillectomy. National clinical guideline 117. April 2010. Available at: http://www.sign.ac.uk/pdf/sign117.pdf (accessed 15 October 2012).

If you have complications after surgery you may need to go back to hospital. This may mean you have to take time off school again. For parents and carers, this may mean you have to take time off work again.

  • Around 4 in 100 people who have surgery for recurrent sore throat will need to go back to hospital again within 28 days of having surgery.[1]Scottish Intercollegiate Guidelines Network.   • Of those children who go back to hospital after surgery, around 73 in 100 have to go back because of bleeding.[1]Scottish Intercollegiate Guidelines Network. April 2010. Available at: http://www.sign.ac.uk/pdf/sign117.pdf (accessed 15 October 2012).

You can choose to have surgery if you keep getting sore throats that stop you from doing everyday things like going to school. You may have fewer sore throats after surgery compared to before. If you'd rather wait and see if your sore throats get better over time, you can try active observation. You may have antibiotics during active observation to help your sore throat if it’s caused by bacteria.
