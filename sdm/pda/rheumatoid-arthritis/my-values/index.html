Our Decision Support telephone service is here to assist and guide you through the Shared Decision Making process.

The Decision Support team is made of professional Health Coaches who are clinicians, commonly nurses, who have undertaken additional training to become a Health Coach.

The Health Coaches will assist you by:

  • Providing you with information
  • Helping you to understand your condition
  • Recognise what is important to you regarding the outcome of treatment
  • Identifying potential solutions
  • Encourage you in discussing options with your family
  • Transferring skills which will assist you in using the information and resources available to you
  • Support you in building confidence in discussing your choice with your doctor
  • You may find that this can be achieved in one telephone call with a Health Coach however; if further calls are required to support you in reaching your decision the Health Coach will schedule these with you.

Important Things To Note

  • A Health Coach does not know which treatment option is best for you.
  • The Health Coach does not have information about your medical history.

MORE INFORMATION ABOUT RHEUMATOID ARTHRITIS

How do my joints work?

A joint is where two bones meet. Joints hold your bones together and allow your bones to move. There are many different types of joints in your body.

On the outside of a joint are ligaments. These are strong fibres that keep your bones in place. The ligaments form an envelope that surrounds the joint. This envelope is called a joint capsule.

Inside the joint capsule is the lining of the joint. This is known as the synovial membrane. This lining makes a fluid called synovial fluid that keeps the joint moving smoothly, much like oil in a car engine.

The ends of the bones that meet in a joint are covered with a hard, slippery material called cartilage. The cartilage makes the ends of the bones smooth so that bones can move easily. Cartilage also protects the ends of the bones from rubbing against each other.

What is rheumatoid arthritis?

Rheumatoid arthritis is an autoimmune disease. It makes your joints painful, tender, stiff, and swollen. An autoimmune disease is when your own immune system attacks your body. Your immune system normally protects you against infections and diseases. When you have rheumatoid arthritis, your immune system attacks the joints in your body. We don’t know for certain why people get rheumatoid arthritis.

Rheumatoid arthritis can affect different joints in different people. It usually affects similar joints on both sides of your body. The small joints of your hands and feet are usually affected first. It can also affect other parts of your body, such as your heart and kidneys.

People with rheumatoid arthritis usually have it for the rest of their life. Over time it can make your joints lose their shape and stop them from working properly. Treatment can help slow or stop the progress of the disease for some people. This doesn't mean you stop having rheumatoid arthritis, but treatment may mean you have periods when you don't have symptoms and the joints don't get worse.

What are the symptoms of rheumatoid arthritis?

The symptoms of rheumatoid arthritis include painful, tender, stiff, and swollen joints. Joints affected by rheumatoid arthritis are especially stiff in the morning and they may feel hot when you touch them. Joints affected by rheumatoid arthritis may not work as well as they should and this can make everyday tasks, like getting dressed, difficult.

When you get swelling in your joints, this is called synovitis. People with severe synovitis may feel unwell and tired, and have a high temperature. They may lose weight and feel depressed.

Some people with rheumatoid arthritis have symptoms that come and go (flare-ups of symptoms). Other people have symptoms that get worse over time.

People with rheumatoid arthritis are more likely to have vascular diseases (problems with blood vessels and circulation) and heart problems than people who don't have rheumatoid arthritis. This may be because the inflammation that affects your joints might also affect other parts of the body, including your heart.[1]National Collaborating Centre for Chronic Conditions. Rheumatoid arthritis: national clinical guideline for management and treatment in adults. Royal College of Physicians, London, UK; 2009.

Why has this happened to me?

We don't know why some people get rheumatoid arthritis. There are some things that may increase your chance of getting it. These include:

  • Family history and genes: Rheumatoid arthritis can sometimes run in families[2]Winchester R, Dwyer E, Rose S. The genetic basis of rheumatoid arthritis: the shared epitope hypothesis. Rheumatic Diseases Clinics of North America. 1992; 18: 761-783.
  • Being a woman: Rheumatoid arthritis is between two and four times as common in women as in men[1]National Collaborating Centre for Chronic Conditions. Rheumatoid arthritis: national clinical guideline for management and treatment in adults. Royal College of Physicians, London, UK; 2009.
  • Being middle-aged: Rheumatoid arthritis can start at any age, but for most people it starts in their 40s[4]Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. Prevalence of arthritis: United States, 1997. Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report. 2001; 50: 334-336.
  • Smoking: People who smoke tobacco are more likely to get rheumatoid arthritis. Rheumatoid arthritis is more severe in smokers[5]Deighton C. Smoke gets in your joints? Annals of Rheumatic Diseases. 1997; 56: 453-454.
  • Drinking alcohol heavily: If you drink large amounts of alcohol regularly, you may be more likely to get rheumatoid arthritis than if you don't drink. There is some evidence that drinking a small amount of alcohol (a few drinks per week) might lower your chance of getting rheumatoid arthritis more than if you don't drink alcohol.[6]Di Giuseppe D, Alfredsson L, Bottai M, et al. Long term alcohol intake and risk of rheumatoid arthritis in women. BMJ. 2012; 345: e4230.
How do I find out if I have rheumatoid arthritis?

You will need to see your doctor and possibly a specialist to find out if you have rheumatoid arthritis. It can be difficult to diagnose because:[1]National Collaborating Centre for Chronic Conditions. Rheumatoid arthritis: national clinical guideline for management and treatment in adults. Royal College of Physicians, London, UK; 2009.

  • Many people see their doctor for aches, pains, and swelling in their bones and muscles, but most don't have rheumatoid arthritis
  • There isn't a test that can tell for certain if someone has rheumatoid arthritis (although a number of results from different tests can help your doctor make a diagnosis).

Before making a diagnosis, your doctor or specialist will check your joints for swelling and to see how well they move. You might have a blood test to check for a chemical called 'rheumatoid factor'. People with rheumatoid arthritis are more likely to have this chemical in their blood, although not having it doesn't mean you won't have rheumatoid arthritis. You might need to have an x-ray.

Your doctor or specialist is likely to talk to you about measuring the severity of your symptoms using the Disease Activity Score (or DAS for short). It's sometimes called DAS28 because 28 of your joints may be affected by rheumatoid arthritis.

When working out your DAS28, your doctor or specialist will look at how many tender and swollen joints you have, at your blood test results, and at your 'global assessment of health' score. The global assessment of health is a tool used by your doctor or specialist to score how well you feel on a one to 10 scale. Your doctor or specialist will use all of these measurements to work out your overall DAS28.

  • A DAS28 of more than 5.1 means your disease is not well controlled. This is called having active disease.
  • A DAS28 between 3.2 and 5.1 means the disease is moderately active.
  • A DAS28 of less than 3.2 means your disease is well controlled.
  • A DAS28 of less than 2.6 means your disease is not getting any worse, or you're improving. This is called being in remission.

The DAS28 is one useful way of checking how well you are doing, but it can't calculate how well you yourself feel. You can always talk to your doctor about how well you feel and about your treatment.

Your specialist will check your DAS28 regularly. The advantage of this is that, if your disease score increases, your specialist can suggest doing something to try to lower it. This may mean suggesting different medications or combinations of medications.

How many people have rheumatoid arthritis?

About 580,000 people in England have rheumatoid arthritis. That's about 1 in 100 people.[8]National Audit Office. Services for People with Rheumatoid Arthritis. The Stationery Office. London. 2009. The disease is two to four times more common in women than in men.[1]National Collaborating Centre for Chronic Conditions. Rheumatoid arthritis: national clinical guideline for management and treatment in adults. Royal College of Physicians, London, UK; 2009.

About 26,000 people are diagnosed with rheumatoid arthritis each year in the England.[8]National Audit Office. Services for People with Rheumatoid Arthritis. The Stationery Office. London. 2009.

What will happen?

If you are diagnosed with rheumatoid arthritis you can expect to be treated quickly. The earlier you get treatment, the more likely it is to help. Your doctor will refer you straight away to a specialist rheumatologist who is used to treating people with the condition. Treatment for rheumatoid arthritis almost always includes some medication.

Your doctor or specialist will ask you about all your symptoms and examine your joints. You might also be offered help from an occupational therapist (OT for short). They can help you to:

  • Look after your joints
  • Do everyday things more easily with less pain
  • Learn about pacing yourself and not getting over-tired
  • Prioritise the things in life that matter to you, so you can use your energy and the time when you feel well doing those things.

Rheumatoid arthritis can affect people in different ways. We don't know for sure what will happen to you.

  • About 20 in 100 people have symptoms that come and go, which don't bother them very much.[11]Arthritis Research Campaign. Arthritis information. 2012. Available at: http://www.arthritisresearchuk.org/arthritis-information.aspx (accessed on 17 October 2012). This is called having mild rheumatoid arthritis.
  • About 75 in 100 people have a more serious form of the disease where their symptoms come and go, but the pain is severe and gets worse.[11]Arthritis Research Campaign. Arthritis information. 2012. Available at: http://www.arthritisresearchuk.org/arthritis-information.aspx (accessed on 17 October 2012). When your symptoms suddenly get worse, this is called having a 'flare-up'. You might have a flare-up if you've been doing more physical activities than usual, or if you're stressed or upset. There may be no clear reason why your symptoms suddenly get worse.
  • About 5 in 100 people are partly disabled by the effect of rheumatoid arthritis on their joints.[11]Arthritis Research Campaign. Arthritis information. 2012. Available at: http://www.arthritisresearchuk.org/arthritis-information.aspx (accessed on 17 October 2012). These people's joints become so damaged that they can't work or do normal everyday things like using a tin-opener or getting dressed.[11]Arthritis Research Campaign. Arthritis information. 2012. Available at: http://www.arthritisresearchuk.org/arthritis-information.aspx (accessed on 17 October 2012).
  • People with rheumatoid arthritis have a higher chance of getting heart problems than people who don't have rheumatoid arthritis. This may be because the inflammation that affects your joints also affects other organs in your body, including your heart.[1]National Collaborating Centre for Chronic Conditions. Rheumatoid arthritis: national clinical guideline for management and treatment in adults. Royal College of Physicians, London, UK; 2009.

Your rheumatoid arthritis may get worse over time.[1]National Collaborating Centre for Chronic Conditions. Rheumatoid arthritis: national clinical guideline for management and treatment in adults. Royal College of Physicians, London, UK; 2009. If you have severe symptoms at the time you are first diagnosed, that often means your disease will get worse over time.

Rheumatoid arthritis specialists are experts in tailoring treatment to fit individuals, and in reacting to changes in your condition so that they can give you the best possible treatment. This is why it's important to talk to your specialist and tell them about your symptoms and how you feel. They can't tell everything from tests, and your input is crucial.

Keeping a close eye on your disease activity in this way can help reduce long-term damage to your joints.

Many people with rheumatoid arthritis cope better when they learn more about their disease and how to manage it themselves. Having the support of friends and family can also be helpful in coping with the disease.[17]Griffith J, Carr A. What is the impact of early rheumatoid arthritis on the individual? Best Practice and Research in Clinical Rheumatology. 2001; 15: 77-90.

Treatment for rheumatoid arthritis almost always includes some medications (drugs). Some people with rheumatoid arthritis find that none of the available drug treatments help them enough. These are usually people with severely damaged joints. Some of these people choose to have surgery. Surgery for rheumatoid arthritis may be able to:

  • Remove inflamed joint tissue
  • Correct joint problems
  • Replace damaged joints completely.

Most people with rheumatoid arthritis find drug treatments helpful and do not need surgery. The results of surgery may depend on whether you can also get good control over the disease using drugs. Surgery does not slow down the progress of rheumatoid arthritis.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT SINGLE DRUG TREATMENT

What is single drug treatment for rheumatoid arthritis?

Single drug treatment for rheumatoid arthritis means taking a medication to help treat your symptoms and to help slow any damage to your joints. Early treatment can stop some, but not all, joint damage.

The main drugs for treating rheumatoid arthritis are called disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drugs (or DMARDs for short). These drugs can help reduce the pain and swelling in your joints, and help make your joints less stiff. They can also help reduce the damage to your joints.

The drugs you are likely to be offered first (these are called first-line treatments) are:

  • methotrexate
  • sulfasalazine

Most people in the UK are offered methotrexate as the first-line treatment. Doctors here consider it the most effective and safe medicine for most people with rheumatoid arthritis.

There are other DMARDs, including leflunomide and hydroxychloroquine. These are not usually offered as first-line treatments. They are usually used as part of a combination of drugs, when someone is taking more than one DMARD to treat their disease.

If the first-line treatments for rheumatoid arthritis don't work for you, there are other drugs, called second-line treatments, that you may be able to have. You can discuss these with your doctor.

You may have heard of methotrexate being used to treat some cancers. This can worry some people, because they link the drug in their mind with cancer. But methotrexate is just one of many medicines that can treat more than one condition (for example, aspirin can help with heart problems as well as being a painkiller).

It's likely that your doctor will advise you to start taking one or more DMARDs as soon as you're diagnosed. Some doctors prefer to start patients on more than one drug (especially if symptoms are severe), but many doctors prefer starting with one first, and keeping a close eye on how well you do.

Which DMARD your doctor recommends might depend on how severe your symptoms are. You should be offered medication that matches how mild or severe your symptoms are. Your specialist will be able to tell from tests when you might benefit from changing your treatment. This might mean taking a different drug to get better results.

Rheumatoid arthritis specialists are experts in reacting to changes in your condition so that they can give you the best possible treatment. This is why it's important to talk to your specialist and tell them about your symptoms and how you feel. They can't tell everything from tests, and your input is crucial.

Who can have single drug treatment for rheumatoid arthritis?

Most people with rheumatoid arthritis can take drugs to treat their disease.

If you're a woman, you won't be able to take methotrexate if you're pregnant, planning to become pregnant, or breastfeeding. If you're a man, most disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drugs (DMARDs) can affect your sperm and fertility. This is temporary and lasts for as long as you take the drug. You can discuss with your doctor which DMARD best suits you.

You can take drugs for your rheumatoid arthritis at the same time as doing things to help yourself stay healthy, such as doing exercise and eating healthily.

What are the main effects of single drug treatment on rheumatoid arthritis?

The main effects of disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drugs (DMARDs) are to relieve the symptoms of rheumatoid arthritis and to slow down the damage to your joints.[18]Gaujoux-Viala C, Smolen JS, Landewé R, et al. Current evidence for the management of rheumatoid arthritis with synthetic disease-modifying antirheumatic drugs: a systematic literature review informing the EULAR recommendations for the management of rheumatoid arthritis. Annals of the Rheumatic Diseases. 2010; 69: 1004-1009.

In some people, DMARDs can stop the disease from causing further damage to the joints. If taking DMARDs means the damage to your joints has slowed or stopped, this is called being in remission. Between 30 in 100 and 55 in 100 people with rheumatoid arthritis who take a DMARD, and who get carefully monitored treatment, are in remission after 18 months.[19]Sokka T, Makinen H. Remission Makes Its Way to Rheumatology. Arthritis Research & Therapy 2010, 12:129.

The positive effects of taking one or more DMARDs in improving symptoms and reducing joint damage can last for at least five years.[1]National Collaborating Centre for Chronic Conditions. Rheumatoid arthritis: national clinical guideline for management and treatment in adults. Royal College of Physicians, London, UK; 2009.

Taking drugs for your rheumatoid arthritis as soon as possible after your diagnosis is likely to work better than if you wait until your symptoms get worse before taking them.[1]National Collaborating Centre for Chronic Conditions. Rheumatoid arthritis: national clinical guideline for management and treatment in adults. Royal College of Physicians, London, UK; 2009.

What are the main effects of single drug treatment on what you can do and how you feel?

If taking a disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drug (DMARD) for your rheumatoid arthritis reduces the pain, swelling, and stiffness in your joints, you will be able to move more easily. You're likely to feel better and be able to do more everyday activities. You may also feel better about doing things like socialising, travelling, and working.

Some people with rheumatoid arthritis feel depressed about their disease. Taking DMARDs can help improve the symptoms of rheumatoid arthritis and this may help people feel less depressed.[1]National Collaborating Centre for Chronic Conditions. Rheumatoid arthritis: national clinical guideline for management and treatment in adults. Royal College of Physicians, London, UK; 2009.

 

What are the main side effects of single drug treatment?

Drugs for rheumatoid arthritis can cause side effects in some people.

There are some drugs you shouldn't take if you have certain medical problems, such as liver and kidney problems. You can discuss with your doctor which drugs are best for you.

About 20 in 100 people with rheumatoid arthritis stop taking some disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drugs (DMARDs) because of side effects.[23]Suarez-Almazor ME, Belseck E, Shea B, et al. Sulfasalazine for rheumatoid arthritis (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. Methotrexate may cause fewer side effect problems than the other DMARDs.[1]National Collaborating Centre for Chronic Conditions. Rheumatoid arthritis: national clinical guideline for management and treatment in adults. Royal College of Physicians, London, UK; 2009.

If a particular medicine causes side effects that make you want to stop taking it, there are others you can try. Side effects don't affect everyone who takes these drugs.

The most common side effect of methotrexate is nausea straight after you take it. You only need to take methotrexate once a week, so the nausea some people feel is less of a problem than if they had to take it every day. If it makes you feel very sick you can have a weekly injection instead.

Methotrexate may cause other side effects for some people, including blisters on the skin or in the mouth, loss of appetite, diarrhoea, and weight loss.[25]Maetzel A, Wong A, Strand V, et al. Meta-analysis of treatment termination rates among rheumatoid arthritis patients receiving disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drugs. Rheumatology. 2000; 39: 975-981. About 3 in 100 people have some hair loss.[26]National Rheumatiod Arthritis Society. Methotrexate in rheumatoid arthritis. November 2011. Available at http://www.nras.org.uk/about_rheumatoid_arthritis/newly_diagnosed/which_drugs_are_used/methotrexate_in_rheumatoid_arthritis.aspx (accessed on 24 January 2013).

Sulfasalazine may cause diarrhoea, stomach pain, loss of appetite, vomiting, skin rash, and itching.[27]Dougados M, Combe B, Cantagrel A, et al. Combination therapy in early rheumatoid arthritis: a randomised, controlled, double blind 52 week clinical trial of sulphasalazine and methotrexate compared with the single components. Annals of the Rheumatic Diseases. 1999; 58: 220-225.

How might single drug treatment affect my life?

If you are a woman, you won't be able to take methotrexate if you are pregnant, planning on becoming pregnant, or breastfeeding. You can talk to your doctor about how to change your treatment if you are planning a pregnancy.

If you're a man, most DMARDs can affect your sperm and fertility. This is temporary and lasts for as long as you take the drug. You can discuss with your doctor which DMARD is best for you.

You may need to see your doctor or specialist regularly to check how well your treatment is working and for side effects. This may affect your work and your normal daily activities.

If your drug treatment is not helping you enough or if it is giving you side effects, your doctor or specialist might advise you to take a different type of drug. Taking a different type of drug may mean you have to change your usual daily routine.

If you're in remission, you may be able to reduce how much medication you take. This may mean you're able to do more things and enjoy life better.

If you take a DMARD you will need to have regular blood tests, probably every three months for the first year, then once a year after that. Checking your blood like this means your doctor can spot any problems early, and can change your treatment to one that suits you better.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT MULTIPLE DRUG TREATMENT

What is multiple drug treatment for rheumatoid arthritis?

Multiple drug treatment for rheumatoid arthritis means taking two or more different medications to treat your symptoms and to try to slow or stop any joint damage.

The main drugs for treating rheumatoid arthritis are disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drugs (or DMARDs for short).

These drugs can help reduce the pain and swelling in your joints, and help make your joints less stiff. They can also reduce joint damage. Your doctor might advise you to start taking a combination of DMARDs as soon as you're diagnosed. You will be able to discuss with your doctor the combination of drugs that will help you get the best out of your treatment.

Different types of DMARDs that your doctor may suggest you take in combination for your rheumatoid arthritis include:

  • methotrexate
  • sulfasalazine
  • leflunomide
  • hydroxychloroquine. 

Doctors in the UK consider methotrexate to be the most effective and safe drug for treating rheumatoid arthritis. For most people taking DMARDs for rheumatoid arthritis, multiple drug treatment will include methotrexate.

You may have heard of methotrexate being used to treat some cancers. This can worry some people, because they link the drug in their mind with cancer. But methotrexate is just one of many medicines that can treat more than one condition (for example, aspirin can help with heart problems as well as being a painkiller).

If your symptoms are mild, your doctor may advise you to start by taking one DMARD. If this doesn't control your symptoms enough, you may need to take more than one DMARD, or other types of drugs as well as a DMARD.

Which DMARDs your doctor recommends might depend on how severe your symptoms are. You should be offered medication that matches how mild or severe your symptoms are. And your specialist will be able to tell from tests when you might benefit from changing your treatment. This might mean taking different drugs or combinations of drugs to get better results.

Rheumatoid arthritis specialists are experts in reacting to changes in your condition so that they can give you the best possible treatment. This is why it's important to talk to your specialist and tell them about your symptoms and how you feel. They can't tell everything from tests, and your input is crucial.

As well as DMARDs, your doctor might suggest that you take a low dose of a type of drug called a corticosteroid. Corticosteroids are anti-inflammatory drugs that can help reduce the inflammation (pain, swelling, redness, and heat) in your joints. They may also help reduce joint damage if you take them with a DMARD.

People with rheumatoid arthritis might be able to use corticosteroids in two ways:

  • For a short period to treat the symptoms of rheumatoid arthritis, either as tablets, or as injections into the joints.
  • In a low dose, as part of multiple drug treatment, to help treat the disease itself. Some people may be able to take corticosteroids for longer if they take them in this way.

People usually take corticosteroids over a short period of time when they're first diagnosed and when they're just starting to take DMARDs. This is because it can take weeks or months before you will be able to notice the effects of DMARDs on your symptoms. Corticosteroids work quickly so they can help your symptoms while the DMARDs start to work.

Many people use corticosteroids when they have a 'flare-up' of their symptoms, because they work quickly to reduce symptoms and get them through the flare-up.

Corticosteroids are not usually suitable as a regular long-term treatment, because they can cause serious side effects if taken for a long time or in high doses. Your doctor will want to make sure you don't use corticosteroids too often. If you find you need to use corticosteroids often, it could be a sign that you need to change your treatment.

The group of drugs listed here are called 'first-line' treatments. This means they are the first drugs your doctor will suggest you take for your rheumatoid arthritis when you're diagnosed. These first-line treatments help most people with rheumatoid arthritis. If they don't work for you, there is another group of drugs called 'second-line' treatments that may help. You can discuss these second-line treatments with your doctor if you need to.

Your doctor may suggest that you take a combination of these first-line drugs to treat your rheumatoid arthritis if one drug is not helping your symptoms as well as you would like.

Who can have multiple drug treatment for rheumatoid arthritis?

For many people with rheumatoid arthritis, a combination of disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drugs (DMARDs) is the most helpful treatment. This combination will usually include methotrexate.

  • Can reduce your disease activity score (DAS28) for up to three months[1]National Collaborating Centre for Chronic Conditions. Rheumatoid arthritis: national clinical guideline for management and treatment in adults. Royal College of Physicians, London, UK; 2009.
  • May be better at reducing joint damage than taking one DMARD by itself.[1]National Collaborating Centre for Chronic Conditions. Rheumatoid arthritis: national clinical guideline for management and treatment in adults. Royal College of Physicians, London, UK; 2009.

Taking drugs for your rheumatoid arthritis as soon as possible after you are diagnosed is likely to work better than if you wait until getting worse before taking them.[1]National Collaborating Centre for Chronic Conditions. Rheumatoid arthritis: national clinical guideline for management and treatment in adults. Royal College of Physicians, London, UK; 2009.

What are the main effects of multiple drug treatment on what you can do and how you feel?

If taking a combination of drugs for your rheumatoid arthritis reduces the pain, swelling, and stiffness in your joints, you may be able to move your joints better. You're likely to feel better and be able to do more everyday activities if you have better movement in your joints. You may also feel better about doing things like socialising, travelling, and working.

Some people with rheumatoid arthritis feel depressed about their disease. Taking disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drugs (DMARDs) can help improve the symptoms of rheumatoid arthritis and this may help people feel less depressed about their disease.[1]National Collaborating Centre for Chronic Conditions. Rheumatoid arthritis: national clinical guideline for management and treatment in adults. Royal College of Physicians, London, UK; 2009.

Taking a combination of DMARDs can reduce the number of work days missed by about 20 a year compared with taking one DMARD.[1]National Collaborating Centre for Chronic Conditions. Rheumatoid arthritis: national clinical guideline for management and treatment in adults. Royal College of Physicians, London, UK; 2009.

 

What are the main side effects of multiple drug treatment?

Most of the drugs you can take for rheumatoid arthritis can cause side effects in some people. You may not be able to take some drugs in combination if you have certain medical problems, such as liver and kidney problems. You can discuss with your doctor which drugs are best for you.

About 20 in 100 people with rheumatoid arthritis stop taking some disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drugs (DMARDs) because of the side effects they get with these drugs.[23]Suarez-Almazor ME, Belseck E, Shea B, et al. Sulfasalazine for rheumatoid arthritis (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. Methotrexate may cause fewer side effects than the other DMARDs.[1]National Collaborating Centre for Chronic Conditions. Rheumatoid arthritis: national clinical guideline for management and treatment in adults. Royal College of Physicians, London, UK; 2009.

The more drugs that you take in combination for your rheumatoid arthritis, the more likely you are to have problems with side effects.[1]National Collaborating Centre for Chronic Conditions. Rheumatoid arthritis: national clinical guideline for management and treatment in adults. Royal College of Physicians, London, UK; 2009. This doesn't mean that if you take two drugs you'll have twice as many side effects. You may have no extra side effects, or very few.

Side effects of taking DMARDs

If a particular medicine causes side effects that make you want to stop taking it, there are others you can try. Side effects don't affect everyone who takes these drugs.

The most common side effect of methotrexate is nausea straight after you take it. You only need to take methotrexate once a week, so the nausea some people feel is less of a problem than if they had to take it every day. If it makes you feel very sick you can have a weekly injection instead.

Methotrexate may cause other side effects in some people, including blisters on the skin or in the mouth, loss of appetite, diarrhoea, and weight loss.[25]Maetzel A, Wong A, Strand V, et al. Meta-analysis of treatment termination rates among rheumatoid arthritis patients receiving disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drugs. Rheumatology. 2000; 39: 975-981. About 3 in 100 people have some hair loss.[26]National Rheumatiod Arthritis Society. Methotrexate in rheumatoid arthritis. November 2011. Available at http://www.nras.org.uk/about_rheumatoid_arthritis/newly_diagnosed/which_drugs_are_used/methotrexate_in_rheumatoid_arthritis.aspx (accessed on 24 January 2013).

Sulfasalizine may cause diarrhoea, stomach pain, loss of appetite, vomiting, skin rash, and itching.[27]Dougados M, Combe B, Cantagrel A, et al. Leflunomide may cause diarrhoea, nausea, hair loss, dizziness, loss of appetite, mouth ulcers, skin rash, and increased blood pressure.[53]Hewitson PJ, Debroe S, McBride A, et al. Leflunomide and rheumatoid arthritis: a systematic review of effectiveness, safety and cost implications. Journal of Clinical Pharmacy and Therapeutics. 2000; 25: 295-302. 

Hydroxychloroquine may cause nausea, diarrhoea, constipation, headaches, dizziness, loss of appetite, skin rash, and blurred vision.[25]Maetzel A, Wong A, Strand V, et al. Meta-analysis of treatment termination rates among rheumatoid arthritis patients receiving disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drugs. Rheumatology. 2000; 39: 975-981.

Side effects of taking corticosteroids

Corticosteroids are usually given in low doses and over a short period of time. Doctors don't recommend that you take high doses of corticosteroids for a long period of time because they may cause serious side effects, including:[55]Verhoeven AC, Boers M. Limited bone loss due to corticosteroids: a systematic review of prospective studies in rheumatoid arthritis and other diseases. Journal of Rheumatology. 1997; 24: 1495-1503. 

  • High blood pressure (hypertension)
  • Diabetes
  • Weak bones that are more likely to break
  • Stomach ulcers
  • Obesity
  • Eye problems, such as cataracts or glaucoma
  • Problems with your adrenal glands (these are glands on top of your kidneys that produce important hormones that you need to stay alive)
  • A weaker immune system.
How might multiple drug treatment affect my life?

If you are a woman, you won't be able to take methotrexate or leflunomide if you are pregnant, planning to become pregnant, or breastfeeding. You can talk to your doctor about how to change your treatment if you are planning a pregnancy.

If you're a man, most disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drugs (DMARDs) can affect your sperm and fertility. This is temporary and lasts for as long as you take the drug. You can discuss with your doctor which DMARD is best for you.

You may need to see your doctor or specialist regularly to check how well your combination of drugs is working and for side effects. This may affect your work and your normal daily activities.

If your drugs are not helping you enough or if they're giving you side effects, your doctor or specialist might advise you to take a different combination of drugs. Taking a different combination of drugs may mean you have to change your usual daily routine.

If you're in remission you may be able to reduce the number of drugs you're taking in your combination. Taking fewer drugs may mean you're able to do more things and enjoy life better.

If you take DMARDs you will need to have regular blood tests, probably every month for the first year or so, then about every two to three months after that. Checking your blood like this means your doctor can spot any problems early, and can change your treatment to one that suits you better.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT SUPPORTIVE THERAPY AND SYMPTOMATIC TREATMENT

What is supportive therapy and symptomatic treatment for rheumatoid arthritis?

Supportive therapy and symptomatic treatment includes many things you can do to help relieve the symptoms of rheumatoid arthritis that don't include the usual drug treatment with disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drugs (DMARDs).

These treatments will not treat the disease itself - they can't reduce the damage to your joints - but they can help make you more mobile and reduce your pain.

Supportive therapy and symptomatic treatment could include:

  • Occasionally taking some extra medications, such as painkillers
  • Getting extra help, for example, from a foot specialist
  • Taking self-care measures.

Supportive therapy and symptomatic treatment is not something that people with rheumatoid arthritis do instead of taking DMARDs, and it's not something that you don't have to do if you are taking DMARDs. Everyone with rheumatoid arthritis can do things to help themselves.

Self-care means taking some responsibility for looking after yourself. This is important because:

  • Although you will get a lot of help from your doctor and other specialists, most of the time you will be taking care of yourself, in your home or at work
  • No one knows your symptoms better than you do
  • There are some important things you can do to help yourself that medicines can't do.

The specialists who care for you will guide you through a self-management programme that best suits you and your symptoms. You may be able to get help from an occupational therapist (OT for short). They can help you with some of the things listed below, such as learning to pace yourself and looking after your joints.

They should also be able to arrange a workplace assessment for you, so that your working environment is as comfortable for you as it can be.

Things people with rheumatoid arthritis can do to help themselves may involve:

  • Taking more exercise
  • Having physiotherapy
  • Giving up smoking
  • Eating a healthier diet
  • Looking after their joints
  • Pacing their activities
  • Getting specialist foot care
  • Making sure they take their medicines when they should. Not taking drugs properly can cause a 'flare-up' of symptoms
  • Making sure they turn up for appointments. Regular check-ups and blood tests are very important in making sure you get the best treatment.

Exercise and physiotherapy

Exercise is something that you can do yourself to get fitter and stronger. You can do different types of exercises to improve your overall fitness. Strength exercises like lifting small weights will help improve the strength of your muscles. Exercises that make your heart beat faster will help improve your fitness and make your lungs and heart stronger. Movement exercises, like swimming, will help improve the movement in your joints.

Doing an exercise programme with a physiotherapist is another way of doing exercise. This may also include learning to look after your joints and about rheumatoid arthritis and how to deal with flare-ups and pain.

Giving up smoking

  • People who don't smoke are less likely to get rheumatoid arthritis.[56]Källberg H, Ding B, Padyukov L, et al. Smoking is a major preventable risk factor for rheumatoid arthritis: estimations of risks after various exposures to cigarette smoke. Annals of Rheumatic Diseases. 2011; 70: 508-511.
  • People with rheumatoid arthritis who give up smoking may have less severe symptoms than people who continue to smoke.[57]Saaga KG, Cerhanc JR, Kolluric S, et al. Cigarette smoking and rheumatoid arthritis severity. Annals of Rheumatic Diseases. 1997; 56: 463-469.
  • People with rheumatoid arthritis have a higher chance of getting heart problems.[1]National Collaborating Centre for Chronic Conditions. Rheumatoid arthritis: national clinical guideline for management and treatment in adults. Royal College of Physicians, London, UK; 2009. If you're a smoker, giving up smoking may help reduce your chance of getting heart problems.

Eating a healthier diet

Changing to a healthier diet may help your rheumatoid arthritis because:

  • Losing excess weight, if you are overweight, puts less strain on your joints.
  • Being too thin and light may mean that your joints, bones, and muscles get weaker. A healthy diet can help you keep to a healthy weight.

Some foods make the symptoms of rheumatoid arthritis worse. A dietician can give you advice about this, but not everyone with rheumatoid arthritis reacts the same way to the same foods. Finding out what foods cause problems for you as an individual may take some trial and error.

Also, people with rheumatoid arthritis have a higher chance of getting vascular diseases (problems with blood vessels and circulation) and heart problems. A healthy diet can help keep your heart and blood vessels healthy.

Looking after your joints

There are many ways that you can look after your joints, such as:

  • Avoiding activities that cause pain in your joints, or doing them in a different way. For example, you may feel less pain in your joints doing a particular task sitting down instead of standing.
  • Using devices to help take the strain from your joints, such as using a jar opener when opening a jar.
  • Having a good posture when standing or sitting down.
  • Using the largest and strongest joints and muscles in your body for doing tasks such as lifting.
  • Splinting. Occasionally some people need to wear splints on painful joints for a while, to help take the pressure off them. An occupational therapist can help with this.

Pacing your activities

Pacing your activities means learning how much of an activity you can do before it causes you pain. It may be a process of trial and error. Paying attention to what your body is telling you may help reduce the pain that you get from doing certain activities.

Looking after your feet

About 75 in 100 people with rheumatoid arthritis have foot pain or some difficulty walking.[1]National Collaborating Centre for Chronic Conditions. Rheumatoid arthritis: national clinical guideline for management and treatment in adults. Royal College of Physicians, London, UK; 2009.

Many of these people use specialist foot-care products such as special insoles to help ease the pain in their feet and to help them with walking.[1]National Collaborating Centre for Chronic Conditions. Rheumatoid arthritis: national clinical guideline for management and treatment in adults. Royal College of Physicians, London, UK; 2009. Comfortable shoes, often fitted with insoles, can be helpful. Some people find they need slightly bigger shoes that can fit the insoles.

Taking extra medicines

As well as the DMARDs your doctor may prescribe, there are other medicines that can help when your symptoms are particularly bad. These include:

  • corticosteroids
  • NSAIDs (non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs)
  • analgesics (painkillers).

Corticosteroids are anti-inflammatory drugs that can help reduce the inflammation (pain, swelling, redness, and heat) in your joints. They may also help reduce damage to your joints caused by your rheumatoid arthritis.

People with rheumatoid arthritis might be able to use corticosteroids in two ways:

  • For a short period to treat the symptoms of rheumatoid arthritis, either as tablets, or as injections into the joints.
  • In a low dose, as part of multiple drug treatment, to help treat the disease itself. Some people may be able to take corticosteroids for a longer spell if they take them in this way.

Most people with rheumatoid arthritis who take corticosteroids use them over a short period when they are first diagnosed and just starting to take DMARDs. This is because it can take weeks or months before you will be able to notice the effects of DMARDs on your symptoms. Corticosteroids work quickly so they may help your symptoms while the DMARDs start to work.

Many people use corticosteroids when they have a 'flare-up' of their symptoms, because they work quite quickly. If you find that your symptoms flare up, causing you a lot of pain, you can talk to your doctor about corticosteroids. Corticosteroids can be given as tablets or as injections directly into the joints.

Corticosteroids are not usually suitable as a regular, long-term treatment, because they can cause serious side effects if taken for a long time. Your doctor will want to make sure you don't use corticosteroids too often. If you find you need to use corticosteroids often, it could be a sign that you need to change your treatment.

NSAIDs are anti-inflammatory drugs that can help reduce the inflammation in your joints. NSAIDs won't reduce the damage to your joints. Some commonly used NSAIDs are ibuprofen, naproxen, and diclofenac. NSAIDs are also used as painkillers.

Analgesics are usually called painkillers. Some commonly used analgesics are paracetamol and codeine. These drugs can help reduce the pain you get from rheumatoid arthritis.

 


Who can use supportive therapy and symptomatic treatment for rheumatoid arthritis?

Everyone with rheumatoid arthritis can do things to help themselves. Your doctor can't be with you all the time, and there are many things you can do to look after yourself. Caring for your joints and your health when you have rheumatoid arthritis isn't just about taking medication.

Before you try things like doing more exercise or changing your diet, you may want to speak with your doctor to discuss how best to do this.

If you have foot pain and are having problems walking, you can ask your doctor to refer you to a foot specialist.

Your doctor can refer you to a physiotherapist or an occupational therapist.

You may not be able to take corticosteroids if you have heart problems, diabetes, or liver problems.

You may not be able to take non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) if you have heart problems, stomach problems, or severe liver or kidney problems.

What are the main effects of supportive therapy and symptomatic treatment on rheumatoid arthritis?

Self-care cannot slow down the damage to your joints, in the way some drugs can. It can help you to look after your joints and your health, get stronger, and avoid pain.

If you're overweight, changing your diet to lose excess weight may put less pressure on your joints. What are the main effects of supportive therapy and symptomatic treatment on what you can do and how you feel?

Exercise can improve your fitness, strength, and joint movement. Exercise can also make your heart healthier. This may mean you're able to get around better without feeling tired or in pain.

Physiotherapy can help improve the strength of your grip. How might supportive therapy and symptomatic treatment affect my life?

Exercising regularly may mean changing your normal daily routine so that you can fit it in.

Taking part in a physiotherapy programme may mean attending regular appointments with a physiotherapist. Your appointments may be at a specialist rheumatology clinic, which may be further from where you live than your doctor's surgery. You may have to spend time travelling to appointments.

Giving up smoking can be difficult and requires commitment. Some people find it easier to give up if they avoid being around people who smoke. 

If you decide to try a special diet to help your symptoms or to lose or maintain weight, you may have to plan your meals carefully.

Using specialist foot-care products such as custom-made shoes or insoles may involve having to see a foot specialist regularly so that they can check your feet. 

You don't need to do all these things at once. You could plan to introduce changes gradually into your life to see what helps most.
