Our Decision Support telephone service is here to assist and guide you through the Shared Decision Making process.

The Decision Support team is made of professional Health Coaches who are clinicians, commonly nurses, who have undertaken additional training to become a Health Coach.

The Health Coaches will assist you by:

  • Providing you with information
  • Helping you to understand your condition
  • Recognise what is important to you regarding the outcome of treatment
  • Identifying potential solutions
  • Encourage you in discussing options with your family
  • Transferring skills which will assist you in using the information and resources available to you

  • A Health Coach does not know which treatment option is best for you.
  • The Health Coach does not have information about your medical history.

MORE INFORMATION ABOUT SMOKING CESSATION

What will happen if I keep smoking?

We can't say for certain what will happen to you as an individual if you keep smoking. Everyone is different. We do know that smoking is a major cause of poor health and early death for many people. 

  • About half of all smokers die of a disease caused by smoking. The diseases most likely to kill smokers are lung cancer, heart disease, and stroke.[1]Department of Health. Smoking kills: a white paper on tobacco. November 1998. Available at http://www.archive.official-documents.co.uk/document/cm41/4177/4177.htm (accessed on 23 October 2012).
  • On average, the life of someone who smokes is 16 years shorter than the life of someone who doesn't smoke.[1]Department of Health. Smoking kills: a white paper on tobacco. November 1998. Available at http://www.archive.official-documents.co.uk/document/cm41/4177/4177.htm (accessed on 23 October 2012).
  • If you smoke, you're more likely to get cancer in your lungs, intestine, throat, pancreas, kidneys, bladder, or cervix.[1]Department of Health. Smoking kills: a white paper on tobacco. November 1998. Available at http://www.archive.official-documents.co.uk/document/cm41/4177/4177.htm (accessed on 23 October 2012).
  • Smoking is the most important cause of the lung disease called chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD).[1]Department of Health. Smoking kills: a white paper on tobacco. November 1998. Available at http://www.archive.official-documents.co.uk/document/cm41/4177/4177.htm (accessed on 23 October 2012). COPD is the fifth biggest cause of death worldwide.[5]World Health Organization. World Health Report 1999. Making a difference. Available at http://www.who.int/whr/1999/en/index.html (accessed on 23 October 2012).
  • Smoking causes more than a quarter of cancer deaths in the UK.[6]Cancer Research UK. Tobacco and cancer risk. December 2011. Available at http://info.cancerresearchuk.org/cancerstats/causes/lifestyle/tobacco/index.htm (accessed on 23 October 2012).

There are more than 4,000 chemicals in tobacco smoke. Many of these are poisons. At least 43 of these chemicals cause cancer.[7]Burns DM. Nicotine addiction. In: Braunwald E, Fauci AS, Kasper DL (editors). Harrison's Principles of Internal Medicine. 15th edition. McGraw-Hill, London, UK; 2001. Smoke also contains tar and harmful gases, such as carbon monoxide.

Some particles in smoke seem to make 'bad' cholesterol in your blood stick to your blood vessels. This cholesterol build-up makes your blood vessels narrower and increases your chances of having a heart attack or stroke.

If you smoke when you're pregnant, smoke can damage your baby's health. The chemicals in smoke keep the baby from getting all the oxygen and nutrients it needs to grow. If you smoke when pregnant:

  • Your baby is likely to weigh less than if you hadn't smoked - infants who don't weigh enough are likely to have breathing problems
  • Your baby may be born early and need special care
  • Your child is more likely to get asthma
  • Up to 1 in 10 infant deaths may be related to the mother smoking while she was pregnant.

Breathing in smoke from other people's cigarettes can cause health problems in people who don't smoke. Second-hand smoke can:[1]Department of Health. Smoking kills: a white paper on tobacco. November 1998. Available at http://www.archive.official-documents.co.uk/document/cm41/4177/4177.htm (accessed on 23 October 2012).

  • Cause lung cancer and heart problems (such as heart attacks and stroke) in people who don't smoke.
  • Make children's asthma worse.
  • Cause pneumonia, ear infections, bronchitis, coughing, and wheezing in young children.
  • Lead to lung infections in children. Up to 17,000 children in the UK need hospital treatment each year because of breathing problems.

Smoking 'light' or 'mild' cigarettes is no less harmful to your health.[9]National Cancer Institute. "Light" cigarettes and cancer risk. Available at http://www.cancer.gov/cancertopics/factsheet/Tobacco/light-cigarettes (accessed on 8 November 2012). Cigar smokers are just as likely to get mouth and throat cancer as cigarette smokers. They're also much more likely to get lung cancer and other diseases linked to smoking than people who don't smoke.

What will happen if I stop smoking?

Almost as soon as you stop smoking, your body starts to clear itself of the poisonous chemicals found in smoke.[10]Dresler C, Leon M (editors). Tobacco control reversal of risk after quitting smoking. IARC handbooks of cancer prevention. Volume 11. International Agency for Research on Cancer, Lyon, France; 2007.

  • Within hours. The amount of carbon monoxide in your blood will return to normal within 48 hours. (Carbon monoxide reduces the amount of oxygen that can get into your blood and makes it harder for you to breathe.)
  • Within days. Your sense of taste and smell will get better.
  • In one to two months. If you have a long-term cough, symptoms like coughing, producing phlegm, and wheezing will improve.
  • In one year. If you have chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD, a condition that makes it hard for you to breathe), your breathing will get easier.
  • In five years. For women, your chances of getting cervical cancer drop to the same as someone who's never smoked.
  • In 10 to 15 years. Your chances of getting coronary heart disease (which causes heart attacks) are about the same as someone who's never smoked.
  • In 10 to 20 years. Your chances of getting lung cancer fall to about two thirds of what they would have been if you'd carried on smoking.

If you stop smoking before the age of 35, you avoid 90 percent of the health problems linked with smoking.[11]Peto R, Darby S, Deo H, et al. Smoking, smoking cessation, and lung cancer in the UK since 1950: combination of national statistics with two case-control studies. BMJ. 2000; 321: 323-329. But even if you stop after the age of 50, you reduce your chances of dying from a disease linked to smoking.

Stopping smoking increases your chances of living longer. On average, if you give up smoking:[12]Jha P, Ramasundarahettige C, Landsman V, et al. 21st-century hazards of smoking and benefits of cessation in the United States. N Engl J Med 2013;368:341-50.

  • Between age 25 and 34, you'll gain an extra 10 years of life
  • Between age 35 and 44, you'll gain an extra nine years of life
  • Between age 45 and 54, you'll gain an extra six years of life
  • Between age 55 and 64, you'll gain an extra four years of life.

If you don't feel ready to give up smoking completely, or if you feel it would be too hard to give up straight away, you could try reducing (cutting down) the amount you smoke. If you cut the number of cigarettes you smoke by half, it makes a big difference to the amounts of carbon monoxide and dangerous chemicals in your body.[13]Stead LF, Lancaster T. Interventions to reduce harm from continued tobacco use (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

Many people who give up smoking put on some weight. This is because smoking can affect your appetite and make you want to eat less. When you stop smoking, your appetite returns to normal.

The average amount that people put on is 3.6 kilograms to 5.9 kilograms (8.0 pounds to 13 pounds).[14]Ockene JK, Emmons KM, Mermelstein RJ, et al. Relapse and maintenance issues for smoking cessation. Health Psychology. 2000; 19 (supplement 1): S17-S31.

What makes it hard to stop smoking?

Smoking is both a habit and an addiction to a chemical (nicotine). To stop smoking, you need to break the habit and get over the addiction to nicotine. People do stop smoking, even if it takes several attempts. 

Nicotine is a chemical found in all tobacco products, whether it's cigarettes, cigars or a pipe, or chewing tobacco. It's the nicotine that gives you a 'hit'. 

  • Nicotine reaches your brain about seven seconds after you puff on a cigarette.[15]Balfour DJ. The neurobiology of tobacco dependence: a commentary. Respiration. 2002; 69: 7-11.
  • It helps your brain make a chemical called dopamine. Dopamine plays a part in making us feel pleasure.[16]National Institute on Drug Abuse. NIDA infofacts: cigarettes and other nicotine products. August 2010. Available at http://www.drugabuse.gov/Infofacts/tobacco.html (accessed on 23 October 2012).
  • People who smoke say it makes them feel relaxed. 
  • If you're addicted, from time to time your body craves nicotine and you feel you need to have a cigarette.

Some of the difficulty in giving up smoking is about breaking a habit, rather than actually being addicted to nicotine. Seeing other people smoking, especially if you live with someone who smokes, can make you want a cigarette. Many people link smoking to particular activities, such as having a coffee break or going for a drink. 

Treatments such as nicotine replacement therapy and drugs can help you overcome nicotine cravings. Counselling or self-help strategies may help you make changes and break your smoking habits. There is a lot of help available if you want to give yourself the best chance of stopping smoking.

What are withdrawal symptoms from nicotine?

Withdrawal symptoms are when you get unpleasant physical or mental symptoms because you stop taking a drug you were addicted to (in this case nicotine).

If you give up smoking you might have nicotine withdrawal symptoms for several weeks. You might:[17]Stapleton J. Cigarette smoking prevalence, cessation and relapse. Statistical Methods in Medical Research. 1998; 7: 187-203.

  • Feel sad and depressed
  • Feel irritable
  • Feel anxious
  • Find it hard to concentrate
  • Feel restless
  • Want to eat
  • Crave a cigarette.
How many people smoke?

Around 13 million adults in the UK smoke cigarettes. That's 22 in every 100 people aged 16 and over.

Many young people also smoke. Five in 100 children aged 11 to 15 are regular smokers.[18]NHS. The Information Centre for Health and Social Care. Statistics on Smoking - England 2012. August 2012. Available at http://www.ic.nhs.uk/pubs/smoking12 (accessed on 23 October 2012).

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT CONTINUING TO SMOKE

What is continuing to smoke?

Continuing to smoke means that:

  • You haven't tried to stop smoking, or
  • You've tried to stop but haven't managed it yet.
Who can continue to smoke?

Everyone who smokes would be well advised to stop, but no one can force you to do it. If you decide to continue smoking, it's your choice.

Pregnant women are strongly advised not to smoke. This is because smoking while pregnant can harm your baby.

  • If you smoke while pregnant, your baby is likely to weigh less than if you hadn't smoked; infants who don't weigh enough are likely to have breathing problems.
  • Your baby may be born early and need special care.
  • Your child is more likely to get asthma.
  • Up to 1 in 10 infant deaths may be caused by the mother smoking while she was pregnant.

Many people continue to smoke because:

  • They want to give up but are worried about withdrawal symptoms
  • They want to give up but feel that they need help
  • They have tried to give up but found it too hard.

Most people don't manage to give up the first time they try. If you try to stop a few times and it doesn't work, you might want to think about other treatments that may help you.

What are the main effects of continuing to smoke?

Smoking is bad for your health in many ways. Smoking can cause many diseases and shortens lives. These are just a few of the health problems caused by smoking:[1]Department of Health. Smoking kills: a white paper on tobacco. November 1998. Available at http://www.archive.official-documents.co.uk/document/cm41/4177/4177.htm (accessed on 23 October 2012).

  • Cancer in your lungs, intestine, throat, pancreas, kidneys, bladder, or cervix
  • Heart disease
  • Breathing problems, including chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD)
  • Stroke
  • Weak bones
  • Low sperm count
  • Early death.

If you continue to smoke, your chances of getting these and other problems will increase.

About half of all smokers die of a disease caused by smoking.[1]Department of Health. Smoking kills: a white paper on tobacco. November 1998. Available at http://www.archive.official-documents.co.uk/document/cm41/4177/4177.htm (accessed on 23 October 2012). On average, people who smoke die 16 years earlier than those who don't.[1]Department of Health. Smoking kills: a white paper on tobacco. November 1998. Available at http://www.archive.official-documents.co.uk/document/cm41/4177/4177.htm (accessed on 23 October 2012).

What are the main effects of continuing to smoke on what you can do and how you feel?

Smoking causes breathing problems, meaning that smokers are often less able to do exercise, get out of breath more quickly, and feel more tired than people who don't smoke. 

Breathing problems can include wheezing, coughing, and getting more chest infections.[22]Tillmann M, Silcock J. A comparison of smokers' and ex-smokers' health-related quality of life. Journal of Public Health Medicine. 1997; 19: 268-273.

  • Five years after stopping smoking, 25 in 100 people still sometimes have attacks of wheezing. This compares with 40 in 100 people who still smoke.
  • Five years after stopping smoking, 12 in 100 people still have regular coughing problems. This compares with 31 in 100 who still smoke.
What are the main side effects of continuing to smoke?

The main side effects of smoking are the diseases and health problems that it causes.

Smoking is also expensive and seen as antisocial. It is now banned in most public buildings and in many public spaces.

Smoking can harm the people around you. If you smoke in your home or in your car, any adults and children around you will have to breathe the smoke. This can cause them the same health problems that smokers have, even though they don't smoke.[9]National Cancer Institute. "Light" cigarettes and cancer risk. Available at http://www.cancer.gov/cancertopics/factsheet/Tobacco/light-cigarettes (accessed on 8 November 2012).

Smoking affects your sense of taste and smell, so that you may not enjoy food as much as if you stopped smoking.

How might continuing to smoke affect my life?

If you carry on smoking you are more likely to suffer from a serious illness. This may mean that you have to take more time off work, or that you may not be able to work at all.

Smoking causes breathing problems, such as coughing, wheezing, and shortness of breath. These breathing problems can stop you from exercising and doing things that you like. Serious breathing problems, such as COPD, can make it hard for you to walk.

Smokers spend more time visiting their doctor, and more time in hospital. Five in every 100 hospital beds in the UK are filled by someone with a disease caused by smoking.[18]NHS. The Information Centre for Health and Social Care. Statistics on Smoking - England 2012. August 2012. Available at http://www.ic.nhs.uk/pubs/smoking12 (accessed on 23 October 2012).

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT SELF-MANAGED QUITTING OR REDUCTION

What is self-managed quitting or reduction?

If you find it hard to stop smoking, it may be because you are addicted to nicotine.

Self-managed quitting or reduction means trying to stop smoking or to reduce (cut down) how much you smoke on your own, without help from a doctor or other health professional. This could mean:

  • Using nicotine replacement therapy products that you can buy over the counter from a pharmacy
  • Using self-help materials, such as books or websites, which can give you information and help about reducing or quitting
  • Rewarding yourself for smoking less or for stopping altogether
  • Socialising with other people who are trying to stop smoking
  • Using electronic cigarettes (so-called e-cigarettes) instead of smoking
  • Just using your willpower to cut down or stop smoking.

You don't have to try just one of these methods. You could try any combination of them that suits you. Below is some information on the various options in more detail.

Nicotine replacement therapy (NRT) means getting nicotine from something other than tobacco. This means that you can continue to take in nicotine for a while, without all the health problems you get from smoking tobacco.

Most people use nicotine replacement therapy for about three months after they stop smoking, but it can be used for longer.

You can get various kinds of nicotine replacement therapy products, including:

  • Patches that you put on your skin, which look a bit like sticking plasters
  • Chewing gum
  • Inhalers - some look a bit like the inhalers some people use for asthma; others look a bit like cigarettes, but they are not the same as electronic cigarettes (e-cigarettes)
  • Tablets that you put under your tongue
  • Lozenges that melt slowly in the mouth
  • Mouth or nasal (nose) sprays.

You can buy them from chemists or supermarkets. You can use just one type of product, or any combination of products.

It’s best not to smoke when using nicotine replacement therapy, but some people use it while they cut down gradually before stopping.

You could choose to try nicotine replacement therapy on its own, or you could try it at the same time as some other treatments.

Self-help guides

These are things you can use to help you stop smoking without medicines or other treatments. They may contain information about the harm smoking can do, and encouragement to help you stop. They include:

  • Books
  • CDs
  • DVDs
  • Websites.

Willpower

Using your willpower to stop smoking means stopping without using any treatments, just your determination to stop.

Electronic cigarettes (e-cigarettes)

Electronic cigarettes are a fairly new kind of treatment. They are plastic tubes that look a bit like real cigarettes. You puff on them in the same way you would a real cigarette, but they don't burn like tobacco does. They contain nicotine that you breathe in every time you puff on them.

Although e-cigarettes contain nicotine, they can't really be classed as nicotine replacement therapy as they haven't been tested as thoroughly as other products. It's not certain how much nicotine they actually contain. It may not be enough to replace the nicotine you would get from a cigarette.[25]U.S. Food and Drug Administration. News & Events - electronic cigarettes (e-cigarettes). Available at http://www.fda.gov/newsevents/publichealthfocus/ucm172906.htm (accessed on 23 October 2012).

Rewards

Some people reward themselves for not smoking - after all, stopping can be hard, and you may feel you deserve a treat for managing to stop or cut down. This might mean buying yourself a present, or spending time doing something you enjoy, when you achieve no-smoking 'milestones'.

For example, you might give yourself a reward for quitting for the first week, then for getting through the first month, then for every month or few months after that. If you think about how expensive it is to smoke, you could pay for your rewards using some or all of the money you save when you stop.

Some people can get involved in no-smoking reward schemes where they work. They may be able to win prizes for not smoking for a period of time.[26]Cahill K, Perera R. Competitions and incentives for smoking cessation. Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews 2011, Issue 4

Socialising with other people who are quitting

Some people find it harder to stop smoking if they socialise with other people who smoke. If you usually socialise with other people who smoke, you might be able to find a stop-smoking group in your area, perhaps through the internet. Meeting socially with other people who are trying to quit may help motivate you.

Or it could just mean arranging to meet friends who don't smoke, at least until you feel you can be around others who smoke without feeling tempted to join in.

Who can try self-managed quitting or reduction?

Anyone can try self-managed quitting or reduction.

It's best not to use nicotine replacement therapy or to smoke when you're pregnant as nicotine may cause problems for your baby.[27]Morales-Suárez-Varela MM, Bille C, Christensen K, et al. Smoking habits, nicotine use, and congenital malformations. Obstetrics & Gynecology. 2006; 107: 51-57. However, using nicotine replacement therapy when pregnant is less harmful to your baby than smoking when pregnant. If using nicotine replacement therapy means you don't smoke while you're pregnant, that is a better choice for your baby.[28]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. PH10: Smoking cessation services. February 2008. Available at http://publications.nice.org.uk/smoking-cessation-services-ph10 (accessed on 23 October 2012).

We can't say for sure how well over-the-counter nicotine replacement therapy works in helping people cut down or stop smoking. There hasn't been much research. What we do know is that using nicotine replacement therapy bought over the counter doesn't seem to help as much as using nicotine replacement therapy with support from a health professional.[29]Stead LF, Perera R, Bullen C, et al. We also know that:

  • The different kinds of nicotine replacement therapy all seem to work about as well as each other.
  • Gums and patches that have higher doses of nicotine seem to work better than those with lower doses.   • Some people find it helps to use more than one product: for example, using gum and patches together.[29]Stead LF, Perera R, Bullen C, et al. Nicotine replacement therapy for smoking cessation (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

  • Using self-help materials (such as books and CDs) might help some people stop smoking. About 7 in 100 people who use self-help materials managed to stop smoking for at least six months. This compares with about 5 in 100 who just use willpower.[32]Lancaster T, Stead LF. Self-help interventions for smoking cessation (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.
  • It's hard to say how effective willpower is in helping you stop smoking. Different numbers of people in different groups manage to stop smoking using willpower.
  • Looking at all the evidence, it seems that between 5 in 100 and 12 in 100 people who just use willpower manage to stop smoking for at least six months. The number who manage to stop smoking for at least a year is slightly lower.[32]Lancaster T, Stead LF. Self-help interventions for smoking cessation (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK. [34]Hughes JR, Stead LF, Lancaster T. Antidepressants for smoking cessation (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.
  • There is no good evidence to show whether electronic cigarettes (e-cigarettes) can help you stop smoking.
  • We don't know whether socialising with people who don't smoke can help you stop smoking or cut down. There hasn't been enough research.
  • We don't know whether giving yourself rewards for not smoking can help. There hasn't been much research. Workplace schemes to reward people for not smoking don't seem to help much in the long term.[35]Cahill K, Perera R. Competitions and incentives for smoking cessation (Cochrane). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.
What are the main effects of self-managed quitting or reduction on what you can do and how you feel?

The unpleasant mental and physical feelings you can get when you stop smoking are called withdrawal symptoms. When you stop smoking your body still craves nicotine. Withdrawal symptoms from stopping smoking can last for several weeks. You might:[17]Stapleton J. Cigarette smoking prevalence, cessation and relapse. Statistical Methods in Medical Research. 1998; 7: 187-203.

  • Feel sad and depressed
  • Feel irritable
  • Feel anxious
  • Find it hard to concentrate
  • Feel restless
  • Want to eat
  • Crave a cigarette.

Nicotine replacement therapy should help ease your withdrawal symptoms. You might find it easier to get on with everyday life while you try to stop smoking, if you use nicotine replacement therapy.

Self-help materials such as books and CDs are a good way of learning about the harm that smoking does. This may make you more determined to stop smoking in the long term.

Socialising with other non-smokers, or rewarding yourself, may help keep you motivated to quit.

What are the main side effects of self-managed quitting or reduction?

Nicotine patches cause a mild skin rash in up to 50 in 100 people. You may be able to avoid rashes by putting the patches in a different place each time you use them.[37]Mallin R. Smoking cessation: integration of behavioral and drug therapies. American Family Physician. 2002; 65: 1107-1114.

Chewing nicotine gum too fast can make you feel sick. You don't chew nicotine gum the way you would with ordinary chewing gum. The best way is to leave it in your cheek, and just bite on it every now and then. That way you don't get too much nicotine too quickly, and you're less likely to feel sick.[37]Mallin R. Smoking cessation: integration of behavioral and drug therapies. American Family Physician. 2002; 65: 1107-1114.

Nicotine sprays and inhalers can irritate the nose or throat.[39]Fant RV, Owen LL, Henningfield JE. Nicotine replacement therapy. Primary Care; Clinics in Office Practice. 1999; 26: 633-652.

Some people don't like the taste of some nicotine replacement products.

Many people gain some weight when they stop smoking. The average amount that people put on is 3.6 kilograms to 5.9 kilograms (8.0 pounds to 13 pounds).[14]Ockene JK, Emmons KM, Mermelstein RJ, et al. Relapse and maintenance issues for smoking cessation. Health Psychology. 2000; 19 (supplement 1): S17-S31.

There is some evidence that nicotine replacement therapy can reduce the amount of weight people gain. This only works as long as people use the nicotine replacement therapy. When they stop using it, they are as likely to put on weight as people who stop smoking using other treatments.[41]Farley AC, Hajek P, Lycett D, et al. Interventions for preventing weight gain after smoking cessation (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

We don't know what the side effects of electronic cigarettes (e-cigarettes) are.[25]U.S. Food and Drug Administration. News & Events - electronic cigarettes (e-cigarettes). Available at http://www.fda.gov/newsevents/publichealthfocus/ucm172906.htm (accessed on 23 October 2012).

  • They haven't been tested as thoroughly as most other treatments.
  • Some health authorities have questioned whether they are safe to use.
  • We don't know how many harmful chemicals they contain.
  • Some health authorities have suggested that e-cigarettes might encourage young people who don't smoke to start smoking.
How might self-managed quitting or reduction affect my life?

The different nicotine replacement products need to be used in different ways. You'll need to follow the instructions for each different product.

  • You chew the gum gently and slowly.
  • You use the mouth spray in your mouth whenever you want a cigarette.
  • You put the patches on your skin, like sticking plasters, and change them every day.
  • You let the lozenges dissolve in your mouth.
  • You let the tablets dissolve under your tongue.
  • You use the nasal spray in your nose.

Using self-help materials can be time consuming and can mean taking on board a lot of information. You may have to set aside time to read, watch DVDs, or listen to CDs or tapes.

Socialising with other non-smokers may mean changing your normal routines and travelling to new places.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT HEALTH PROFESSIONAL-ASSISTED QUITTING OR REDUCTION

What is health professional-assisted quitting or reduction?

Health professional-assisted quitting and reduction means getting help to stop or cut down smoking from your doctor, or from another health professional. This could be a nurse, counsellor, or someone who is trained to help people stop smoking.

The kind of help you can get from health professionals includes:

  • Medicines to help you stop smoking
  • Nicotine replacement therapy products (such as gum and patches), with support and advice to help you cut down or stop
  • Counselling to help you stop smoking
  • Different combinations of these treatments.

There are two medicines that you can get with a prescription from your doctor, which may help you stop smoking. They are called bupropion and varenicline.

If you take varenicline tablets you usually start taking one a day, then increase to taken two a day. One week after you have started taking varenicline, you set a date to stop smoking. You continue taking the tablets for 12 weeks, while you try to stop smoking. If you stop smoking, you can continue taking it for an additional 12 weeks.

You may need to start taking varenicline at a low dose, then build up to a higher dose, so your body has time to adjust to the drug.

If you take buproprion tablets you usually start taking one a day, then increase to two a day. You usually continue taking the tablets for between seven and nine weeks.

Doctors usually only prescribe bupropion or varenicline as part of a programme to stop smoking. This means that if you want to take these drugs you will need to agree to have counselling. You will need to have at least one appointment with a nurse or doctor to discuss smoking and how to stop.

Nicotine replacement therapy (NRT) means getting nicotine from something other than tobacco. This means that you can continue to take in nicotine for a while, without all the health problems you get from smoking tobacco.

You can buy nicotine replacement therapy products over the counter from a pharmacy. Or you could get them on prescription through your doctor. If you choose to get them through your doctor, or to talk to your doctor about using nicotine replacement therapy, they can give you advice on how best to use them.

You may be able to get support when using these products from a health professional who is not a doctor. This could be a nurse or someone who is trained to help people stop smoking. You may find that talking to a health professional about your progress from time to time helps you not to smoke.

Most people use nicotine replacement therapy for about three months after they stop smoking, but it can be used for longer.

You can get various kinds of nicotine replacement therapy products, including:

  • Patches that you put on your skin, which look a bit like sticking plasters
  • Chewing gum
  • Inhalers - some look a bit like the inhalers some people use for asthma; others look a bit like cigarettes, but they are not the same as electronic cigarettes (e-cigarettes)
  • Tablets that you put under your tongue
  • Lozenges that melt slowly in the mouth
  • Mouth or nasal (nose) sprays.

You can use just one type of product, or any combination of products.

It's best not to smoke when using nicotine replacement therapy, but some people use it while they cut down gradually before stopping.

You could choose to try nicotine replacement therapy on its own, or you could try it at the same time as some other treatments, such as counselling or some alternative treatments.

Counselling is a 'talking treatment'. When you have counselling you talk with someone who is trained to help you with a particular problem.

If you have counselling to help you stop smoking, you can do it:

  • In person, one to one with your counsellor
  • Over the phone, one to one with your counsellor
  • With a group of people who want to stop smoking and a counsellor.

Your counsellor may be:

  • A nurse
  • A doctor
  • Someone who is not a nurse or a doctor, but who is trained to help people stop smoking.

You can choose to try counselling by itself to stop smoking, or you could use it at the same time as trying other treatments, such as prescription drugs, nicotine replacement therapy, or some alternative treatments.

If you want to take prescription drugs to help you stop smoking, you will usually have to have counselling as well.

What is alternative therapy quitting or reduction?

Alternative treatments are things that your doctor probably won't prescribe, but that you're free to try if you think they might help you. They may not have been studied as thoroughly as other options we look at here. This means there is less evidence about how well they work.

These options include:

Acupuncture

This is a traditional Chinese treatment. If you have acupuncture, a trained acupuncturist puts sterile needles into your skin.

Some people have acupuncture to try to help reduce the withdrawal symptoms they get when they stop smoking. Usually, treatment starts some days before the person actually stops smoking. The acupuncturist inserts needles in the outer ear (or in other parts of the body) for 15 to 20 minutes. The treatment may be repeated over the following days.[70]White AR, Rampes H, Liu JP, et al. Acupuncture and related interventions for smoking cessation (Cochrane review). In: The Cochrane Library. Wiley, Chichester, UK.

Hypnosis

Some people go to a hypnotherapist when trying to stop smoking. This kind of therapist uses hypnosis to make you feel very deeply relaxed. This kind of relaxation is sometimes called a hypnotic trance. The hypnotherapist then gives you suggestions to help you change the way you behave.

If you're trying to stop smoking, your hypnotherapist may suggest that you don't need cigarettes or that you don't like smoking anymore.

Homeopathy

The idea of homeopathy is that a small amount of what makes someone ill can also help cure them. So homeopathic medicines are highly diluted. Practitioners believe these diluted substances can help the body heal itself. There are many homeopathic products that are aimed at helping people stop smoking, including Nux vomica and Lobelia.

Herbal remedies

Several herbal remedies are aimed at helping people stop smoking, including Lobelia, St. John's Wort, and Ginseng. The idea of herbal remedies is that they either reduce withdrawal symptoms, or interfere with chemicals in the brain that give people pleasure from smoking.

Reflexology

Reflexology is a type of massage, involving the hands, feet, and ears. Reflexologists believe that the different areas of the body are somehow mirrored on different parts of the hands and feet. They believe that putting pressure on the parts of your hands or feet that are somehow connected to your lungs can help you stop smoking.

You can try any of these treatments on their own, or you might want to think about using some of them alongside drugs, nicotine replacement therapy, or counselling.
