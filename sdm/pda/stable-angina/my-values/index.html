Our Decision Support telephone service is here to assist and guide you through the Shared Decision Making process.

The Decision Support team is made of professional Health Coaches who are clinicians, commonly nurses, who have undertaken additional training to become a Health Coach.

The Health Coaches will assist you by:

  • Providing you with information
  • Helping you to understand your condition
  • Recognise what is important to you regarding the outcome of treatment
  • Identifying potential solutions
  • Encourage you in discussing options with your family
  • Transferring skills which will assist you in using the information and resources available to you
  • Support you in building confidence in discussing your choice with your doctor
  • You may find that this can be achieved in one telephone call with a Health Coach however; if further calls are required to support you in reaching your decision the Health Coach will schedule these with you.

Important Things To Note

  • A Health Coach does not know which treatment option is best for you.
  • The Health Coach does not have information about your medical history.
  • You will still need to talk over your decision and reasons for your decision with your doctor or nurse.

MORE INFORMATION ABOUT STABLE ANGINA

How does my heart work?

Your heart is surrounded by a thick muscular wall. It is divided into four parts called chambers. There is a top chamber and bottom chamber on the right side of the heart and the same on the left side of the heart.

The two chambers on the right side of your heart pump blood to your lungs, where the blood picks up oxygen. Blood and oxygen from your lungs return to the two chambers on the left side of your heart, which then pump it to the rest of your body.

Your heart also needs its own supply of blood so that it can work. Your heart gets this blood from the coronary arteries. You have a left coronary artery and a right coronary artery. They supply the heart's muscular wall with blood that has a lot of oxygen in it.

What is stable angina?

Angina is the temporary pain or discomfort you get when your heart doesn’t get enough oxygen. It is not a heart attack. The pain does not harm the heart.

Angina is usually caused by coronary artery disease. This is when bits of abnormal tissue (called plaques) develop on the inside of your coronary arteries. Over time your coronary arteries get narrower, stiffer, and rougher. This means less blood and oxygen can get to your heart, especially when you exercise. This lack of blood and oxygen to the heart causes the symptoms of angina.

Any of your coronary arteries can be affected in angina, and more than one artery may be affected.

Angina can be called ‘stable’ or ‘unstable’. Stable angina is when you have a regular pattern to your angina symptoms. You get symptoms when your heart is working harder than usual, but they go away after you rest for a few minutes or after you take angina drugs. Unstable angina is when you have no regular pattern to your angina symptoms.

When you have had angina for a while, you will be able to learn to recognise the pattern of symptoms and know when it will happen. You have stable angina when you've had a regular pattern of symptoms for at least two months.

Things that can bring on angina are walking up a hill or stairs, eating a heavy meal, or going out in cold weather. It can also happen when you get angry, upset, or excited.

When you are at rest, or feeling calm, your heart doesn't work harder than normal. Your heart will usually have enough oxygen for its needs, and you shouldn’t feel any pain or discomfort.

What are the symptoms of stable angina?

Stable angina symptoms can feel different for different people. You might feel stabbing pains, tightness and pressure in your chest, as well as general discomfort or tingling. Some people feel these symptoms in their back, shoulder, throat, arm, or jaw.

Pain or discomfort from stable angina should go when you stop doing what it is that is causing your heart to work harder. If exercise brings on your angina symptoms, they should go away soon after you stop exercising. You can exercise providing you don't get symptoms. If you get angina symptoms, you should stop exercising.

If your symptoms don't go away within 15 minutes you should call an ambulance straight away.

If you take drugs called short-acting nitrates to help with angina attacks, you should call an ambulance straight away if your symptoms don't go away after taking two doses of the drugs.

Why has this happened to me?

Angina is usually caused by coronary artery disease. This is when bits of fatty tissue (called plaques) develop on the inside of your coronary arteries.

There are a number of things that can increase your chance of getting angina and coronary artery disease. They include:

  • Being a man
  • Being middle-aged or older
  • Having close family members who had heart attacks at an early age (before about 55)
  • Smoking tobacco
  • Having high cholesterol, high blood pressure, chronic kidney disease or diabetes
  • Being overweight or obese, or not doing enough exercise.

If you feel under stress, you might eat or smoke more than you would normally. We don't know if stress by itself makes it more likely that you'll get angina.

How do I find out if I have stable angina?

Seeing your doctor if you are getting pain or discomfort in your chest is usually the first step. Your doctor will ask you about your symptoms and general health because there are many possible causes of chest pain. Your doctor will ask you what the pain or discomfort feels like, what brings it on, how long it lasts, and what makes it go away. Your doctor might also ask about risk factors, such as whether you smoke, your diet, and if there is anyone in your family with heart problems.

Your doctor may check your weight, breathing, pulse, and blood pressure. They may also check your skin for bumps, which can be a sign of high cholesterol.

Your doctor may suggest other tests, such as an electrocardiogram (ECG), which can tell if your heart is working properly. They may also do blood tests to check for anaemia, diabetes, overactive thyroid, and high cholesterol. Anaemia and overactive thyroid can give you symptoms that are like angina symptoms. Your doctor may also suggest a chest x-ray or stress test. A stress test can check how much exercise your heart can cope with.

If you’ve already been told by your doctor that you have stable angina you may have had some of these tests already.

How many people have stable angina?

Stable angina affects about 2 million people in the UK. About 8 in 100 men and 3 in 100 women aged 55 to 64 years have (or have had) stable angina. For people aged 65 to 74 years, these numbers go up to 14 in 100 men and 8 in 100 women.

There are a number of support groups for people with angina. Some of them have websites where you can read about other people’s experiences of angina. Some also allow you to write about your own experiences, or ask questions and get answers from other people with angina.

What will happen?

People with angina may have a better quality of life and better health once they learn more about their condition. Having angina does not mean people should stop exercising or living a normal life. There are things people can do to make their heart healthier, such as not smoking, eating more fruit and vegetables, and avoiding foods that are high in fat and sugar.

If you have angina you probably have coronary artery disease. This means you have a higher chance of having a heart attack than someone without coronary artery disease. It might help to think of angina as a warning that you might have a heart attack in the future.

People with stable angina who have treatment can live for many years. Your doctor won’t be able to tell you how long you will live if you have stable angina.

People with angina may be offered two types of drug to help them. These are:

  • Anti-anginal drugs to help prevent angina symptoms. These drugs may also help treat the symptoms when they occur.
  • Drugs to help prevent heart attacks, strokes, or other heart problems. These are called secondary prevention drugs, and include medicines such as aspirin and statins.

Your doctor may also talk to you about changes you can make to your lifestyle to help prevent heart problems, or having revascularisation to control your symptoms.

If you have other health problems such as high blood pressure or diabetes, you should ask your doctor about separate treatment for these.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT LIFESTYLE CHANGES

What are lifestyle changes?

Lifestyle changes are things you can do yourself to improve your general health. There are some lifestyle changes that you can make to help improve your stable angina. These are:

  • Learning more about angina
  • Learning more about healthy living
  • Stopping smoking
  • Losing weight if you need to
  • Exercising more
  • Eating more fruit and vegetables, and avoiding foods that are high in fat and sugar.

Learning about angina and living healthily

People with angina feel better and stay healthier when they learn more about their condition. [1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Stable angina. July 2011. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG126 (accessed on 10 September 2012). Many people believe things about angina that are not true. For example, some people think that angina is having a ‘mini’ heart attack, or they think having angina means they should stop exercising. These things are not true. Many people live for many years with stable angina.

There are various ways people can learn more about their condition, including reading and listening to recorded information about angina, and speaking with their doctor or nurse. There are also educational programmes that can help people manage their angina while they learn about their condition at the same time.

One of these educational programmes is called the Angina Management Programme. This programme involves:

  • Education about angina, what it is and what it isn’t
  • Managing stress
  • Setting goals for the future
  • Relaxation and breathing techniques
  • Finding the amount of exercise that’s right for you.

The Angina Plan is another programme that is similar to the Angina Management Programme. It uses a workbook to help you learn about your condition.

Stopping smoking

Smoking can cause heart disease. Stopping smoking can help improve your cardiovascular (heart and blood vessels) system and help you live longer.

Exercising more

People with angina should not stop exercising. You can exercise providing you don't get symptoms. Exercise is an important part of looking after your heart. Your doctor can help you decide on a reasonable and safe amount of exercise for you to take. They can also advise you on the best type of exercise to take. If you get angina symptoms, you should stop exercising.

Thinking about what you eat

Your diet can affect the health of your heart and coronary arteries. Cutting down on certain fats and on sugary foods, and eating more fish, fruit, and vegetables will help keep your heart and arteries in better shape. Eating a healthier diet may also help you lose weight.                         

Who can make lifestyle changes?

Anyone can make lifestyle changes. You may want to talk to your doctor or do some research before making big changes to how much you exercise or your diet.

What are the main effects of lifestyle changes on stable angina?

There is little research about how lifestyle changes can directly affect angina symptoms. Most lifestyle changes are more important for what doctors call ‘secondary prevention’. That means making your heart healthier in the long term so that you are less likely to get a heart attack, stroke, or other heart problems.

What are the main side effects of lifestyle changes?

Side effects from lifestyle changes are usually temporary. Your body will just need some time to get used to any changes to your lifestyle.

Stopping smoking may give you short-term side effects such as dizziness, insomnia, irritability, and headaches. Smoking can make you eat less so you may put on weight as your appetite returns when you stop smoking.

Your doctor can give you advice about how to manage any side effects from lifestyle changes.

How might lifestyle changes affect my life?

Smoking is an addiction so giving up can be difficult. Your doctor can give you further advice on how to stop and how to deal with your nicotine cravings.

Changing your lifestyle habits such as changing your diet or taking more exercise may be difficult and may take some time to get used to. Taking responsibility for your own health and wellbeing may require planning and adapting to difficult changes.

You may need to attend regular sessions if you are on an angina education programme. With the Angina Plan you will also need to fill in a workbook on a regular basis.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT MEDICINE (DRUGS)

What is medicine for stable angina?

Medicine means taking drugs to treat stable angina. You can combine medicine with healthy lifestyle changes. 

There are two groups of drugs for stable angina. These are:

  • Anti-anginal drugs, which help prevent angina symptoms and also treat the symptoms when they occur
  • Secondary prevention drugs, which reduce your chances of having a heart attack, a stroke or other cardiovascular diseases. They do not prevent or treat your angina symptoms.

The types of anti-anginal drugs people are usually offered are:

  • Shorter-acting nitrates
  • Beta-blockers
  • Calcium-channel blockers
  • Longer-acting nitrates

There are different types of beta-blockers and calcium-channel blockers. If one doesn’t work for you or gives you side effects, you and your doctor can look at trying another type.

Your doctor will usually suggest you try beta-blockers and calcium-channel blockers first. If these do not work for you, your doctor may suggest you try longer-acting nitrates.

There are different kinds of nitrates: longer-acting nitrates and shorter-acting nitrates. Shorter-acting nitrates are used to treat symptoms when they happen. Shorter-acting nitrates start working quickly when you have symptoms, but their effects don't last long. These can be tablets that melt in your mouth or a spray that you put under your tongue. Longer-acting nitrate drugs have effects that last longer. They come as tablets. They are usually used to prevent angina symptoms.

You may be offered a combination of these anti-anginal drugs. If these drugs do not work for you, or if there is any reason you can’t take them, you may be offered other drugs. These will probably be:

  • Nicorandil
  • Ranolazine
  • Ivabradine.

The main secondary prevention drugs for angina are statins and aspirin, or other drugs that work like aspirin, called anti-platelet drugs.

Statins help prevent heart attacks and strokes by lowering levels of harmful cholesterol in your blood. Aspirin and other anti-platelet drugs help prevent heart attacks and strokes by stopping your blood from clotting too easily.  This is something that you should discuss with your doctor. Your doctor may suggest you start taking statins, and possibly aspirin, right away. 

Who can have medicine for stable angina?

Most people with stable angina can take drugs to treat it. You can take drugs for angina at the same time as making lifestyle changes. If you’ve had revascularisation for angina your doctor may advise you to keep taking some drugs to help prevent angina symptoms coming back.

Most people can take statins. Aspirin is not suitable for everyone, because it can cause bleeding. If you have a high chance of bleeding your doctor will probably advise you not to take aspirin.[1]National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence. Stable angina. July 2011. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/CG126 (accessed on 10 September 2012).

You can talk with your doctor to decide which drugs are right for you.

How might medicine affect my life?

Some types of angina drug need to be taken regularly (every day) while you take other types only when you have symptoms. 

People may be prescribed two different types of angina drugs - one for angina symptoms and one to prevent heart problems. If you are prescribed both types of drugs, you may have to take several drugs at different times during the day. 

Nitrate tablets don’t last long when they are exposed to air. You may need to get regular prescriptions for these angina drugs from your doctor. This doesn't apply to the type of nitrates you use as a spray or put under your tongue when you have symptoms.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT REVASCULARISATION

What is revascularisation for stable angina?

Revascularisation is a procedure to restore blood flow to the heart. The two main types of revascularisation are:

  • Percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI)
  • Coronary artery bypass graft (CABG). CABG is a major operation.

There are different types of PCI and CABG. Your doctor will be able to tell you more about which type of procedure is best for you. The main purpose of both procedures is to treat angina symptoms. 

People who have either PCI or CABG still need to carry on taking some drugs, probably for life.

PCI is a procedure that widens the arteries to make it easier for blood to flow to the heart. This should improve angina symptoms. It is usually carried out by a heart doctor (cardiologist).

During the procedure, a doctor uses a tiny deflated balloon attached to the end of a thin tube. This tube is inserted into your body, usually near your groin. It is then fed through your blood vessels until it reaches the part of your artery that is blocked. The doctor then inflates and deflates the balloon several times, widening the artery and getting blood flowing through it again.

Most people who have a PCI also have a stent put into the artery. This is a small tube inserted into the artery to keep it open. It acts like a kind of scaffold, to stop the artery closing up again.

CABG (pronounced cabbage) is a major operation carried out by a surgeon. The aim is to improve the blood flow to your heart, which should help your angina symptoms. In a bypass operation, a surgeon takes parts of healthy blood vessels from another part of your body (such as your leg) and uses them to take over from the narrowed arteries in your heart.

Before people are offered PCI or CABG, they have a test called an angiogram. This is a type of x-ray that helps the surgeon see the coronary arteries.

During the test, the surgeon will put a thin tube (called a catheter) into an artery in your arm or thigh, and carefully feed this through to the coronary arteries. The surgeon will then inject a dye through the tube into your coronary arteries. This dye will show up on the x-ray and the surgeon can tell how much your coronary arteries are narrowed. Before the test you may be given drugs to help you relax so that the surgeon can carry out the test without causing you too much discomfort.

Angina is usually caused by coronary artery disease. Doctors and surgeons can use angiograms to classify coronary artery disease based on which blood vessels are narrowed.

Who can have revascularisation for stable angina?

Revascularisation is usually offered to people with angina symptoms that don’t improve much with drugs.

Not everyone can have either type of revascularisation. The type of operation that is suitable for one person may depend on the type of heart disease they have.

Some people may only be able to have one type of revascularisation, because of their health or because of the type of heart disease they have.

To have revascularisation you have to be well enough to go through the operation without too high a risk to your health. This especially applies to CABG surgery, which is a major operation.

Things that may affect whether you are well enough for revascularisation include:

  • Other health problems you may have
  • Whether you can tolerate an anaesthetic.
What are the main effects of revascularisation on stable angina?

The main effect of revascularisation on stable angina is to improve symptoms.

Most of the research on revascularisation is more than 25 years old. Since then, new medicines have been used to treat heart disease. There is a risk of infection with revascularisation. After CABG surgery, some people may get an infection in the area where healthy blood vessels are taken from, such as in their leg. This can usually be treated with antibiotics.

There is a low risk of death from revascularisation. This is something that you can talk about with your doctor.

How might revascularisation affect my life?

PCI is done in hospital. The PCI procedure takes about two hours, and is usually carried out by a heart doctor (cardiologist). People usually need to stay in hospital overnight. It will take about a week before you return to your normal activities after PCI.

Coronary artery bypass graft (CABG) surgery is done in hospital. The operation takes between three and five hours, and is carried out by a surgeon. People who have this operation will need to stay in hospital for about a week. Full recovery from CABG surgery takes up to eight weeks. This will include some time off work, and time when you are not able to do much at all.

After PCI or CABG you will need follow-up appointments with your doctor or surgeon so that they can check your progress.

Your doctor or surgeon is likely to advise you to make lifestyle changes if you haven’t already done so. These may include stopping smoking if you're a smoker, exercising more, and eating a healthier diet.

If you have either PCI or CABG you will still need to carry on taking some drugs, probably for life.
