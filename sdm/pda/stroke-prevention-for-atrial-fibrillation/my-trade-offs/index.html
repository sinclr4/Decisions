MORE INFORMATION ABOUT STROKE PREVENTION FOR ATRIAL FIBRILLATION OR ATRIAL FLUTTER

How does the heart work?

The heart has four main parts (called chambers). The two smaller chambers at the top are called the left atrium and the right atrium. Together they are called the atria. Blood enters the heart by flowing into the atria. The atria then pump the blood into the two bigger, lower chambers.

The two lower chambers are called the left ventricle and the right ventricle. Their job is to pump blood out of the heart and around the body.

The right ventricle pumps blood to the lungs, and the left ventricle pumps blood to the rest of the body.

The top right chamber of the heart (right atrium) contains a natural 'pacemaker' which sets the rate at which the heart beats. The pacemaker sends out an electrical signal to make the muscles in the heart contract in a regular way. The contraction pushes blood through the heart and around the body.

What are atrial fibrillation and atrial flutter?

Atrial fibrillation and atrial flutter are conditions that affect how your heart beats. Usually, your heart beats regularly. If you have atrial fibrillation, it beats irregularly, and usually faster. If you have atrial flutter, it beats faster, but still regularly.

People with atrial fibrillation can also have atrial flutter at times. The treatments available for atrial flutter and atrial fibrillation are the same, which is why we've included both of them in this decision aid. Anything we say about atrial fibrillation in this decision aid applies to atrial flutter too, unless we say it does not.

Atrial fibrillation happens because the pacemaker that controls the muscles of the heart doesn't work properly. It sends out too many electrical signals at once. This makes the muscles of the heart contract too fast, which means that blood is pumped out in an irregular way.

There are several kinds of irregular heartbeats. Atrial fibrillation is the most common.[1]National Collaborating Centre for Chronic Conditions. Atrial fibrillation: national clinical guideline for management in primary and secondary care. 2006. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/nicemedia/live/10982/30055/30055.pdf (accessed on 7 January 2013).

Most people with atrial fibrillation don't get symptoms.[1]National Collaborating Centre for Chronic Conditions. Atrial fibrillation: national clinical guideline for management in primary and secondary care. 2006. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/nicemedia/live/10982/30055/30055.pdf (accessed on 7 January 2013). People who do have symptoms may notice their heart beating irregularly and probably fast.  

If you've had atrial fibrillation or atrial flutter for some time, you may not realise that your heart is beating irregularly or fast because you have become used to it. 

Some of the other symptoms people with atrial fibrillation experience are:

  • Chest pain
  • Shortness of breath
  • Dizziness
  • Feeling tired.
What causes atrial fibrillation?

Atrial fibrillation happens when the natural 'pacemaker' in the heart doesn't work properly. We don't know exactly why some people get atrial fibrillation. The chance of having atrial fibrillation increases as you grow older. Most people with atrial fibrillation are over the age of 45.

Other things that increase your chance of getting atrial fibrillation are:

  • High blood pressure
  • Heart disease
  • Problems with the heart's valves
  • Diabetes
  • Problems affecting the thyroid gland
  • Having had a heart operation. Around 33 in 100 people develop atrial fibrillation after having a heart operation.[1]National Collaborating Centre for Chronic Conditions. Atrial fibrillation: national clinical guideline for management in primary and secondary care. 2006. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/nicemedia/live/10982/30055/30055.pdf (accessed on 7 January 2013).
  • Alcohol or drug abuse
  • Lung cancer
  • Pneumonia
  • Pulmonary embolism (a blood clot on the lung).

Your doctor will be able to tell you if these apply for you. For some people there is no clear explanation of why they have atrial fibrillation.

How do I find out I have atrial fibrillation?

If your GP thinks that you have atrial fibrillation or atrial flutter, you will need a test called an electrocardiogram (ECG). You'll probably have this at your local hospital or clinic. Some GPs do the test in their own surgery.

An electrocardiogram is a painless test that records the electrical activity of your heart, while you wear a monitor attached to your chest.

Because atrial fibrillation and atrial flutter come and go, the ECG might not pick up any problems during your appointment.If that happens you may need to wear an ECG monitor for 24 hours. (Your doctor may call this an ambulatory monitor or a Holter monitor.) 

If your ECG shows atrial fibrillation, you may also have other tests done to find out the cause of your irregular heartbeat. These tests might include: 

  • Echocardiogram. An echocardiogram is an ultrasound scan of your heart. It is a painless test that can help identify possible causes of atrial fibrillation, such as heart disease or problems with the heart's valves.
  • X-ray. A chest x-ray can help doctors see if problems with your lungs are causing your irregular heartbeats. Lung cancer and pulmonary embolisms (blood clots on the lung) can cause atrial fibrillation.
  • Blood test. A blood test can help identify if you have too much of a hormone produced by the thyroid gland, called thyroxine, which can cause atrial fibrillation.
How many people have atrial fibrillation?
  • About 13 in 1,000 men in the UK have atrial fibrillation
  • About 12 in 1,000 women in the UK have atrial fibrillation.[4]DeWilde S, Carey IM, Emmas C, et al. Trends in the prevalence of diagnosed atrial fibrillation, its treatment with anticoagulation and predictors of such treatment in UK primary care. Heart. 2006; 92: 1064-1070. 

The chance of having atrial fibrillation rises as you grow older. In people aged 85 or older:

  • About 132 in 1,000 men in the UK have atrial fibrillation
  • About 110 in 1,000 women in the UK have atrial fibrillation.[4]DeWilde S, Carey IM, Emmas C, et al. Trends in the prevalence of diagnosed atrial fibrillation, its treatment with anticoagulation and predictors of such treatment in UK primary care. Heart. 2006; 92: 1064-1070. 

These numbers are based on patient records so they only include people who have had a diagnosis or treatment for atrial fibrillation. Because there are often no symptoms with atrial fibrillation, the true number of people with the condition is not known.[1]National Collaborating Centre for Chronic Conditions. Atrial fibrillation: national clinical guideline for management in primary and secondary care. 2006. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/nicemedia/live/10982/30055/30055.pdf (accessed on 7 January 2013).

How can atrial fibrillation affect my health?

The main problem with atrial fibrillation is that it increases your chances of having a stroke. That happens because the heart isn't beating properly. The upper chambers of the heart (the atria) beat irregularly and are unable to pump all the blood out. If blood stays in one place for too long, it starts to clot. Blood clots increase your chances of having a stroke.

Sometimes, the heart pumps out one or more of these clots (an embolus). If the clot travels through the blood vessels, it can block an artery to the brain and cause a stroke. This type of stroke, where the brain is deprived of blood and oxygen, is called an ischaemic stroke. 

Overall, people with atrial fibrillation are five times more likely to have a stroke than people without atrial fibrillation. [1]National Collaborating Centre for Chronic Conditions. Atrial fibrillation: national clinical guideline for management in primary and secondary care. 2006. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/nicemedia/live/10982/30055/30055.pdf (accessed on 7 January 2013). Your individual chance of having a stroke during one year ranges from from 19 in 1,000 to 182 in 1,000, depending on your age and medical problems. [8]Gage BF, Waterman AD, Shannon W, et al. Validation of clinical classification schemes for predicting stroke: results from the National Registry of Atrial Fibrillation. Journal of the American Medical Association. 2001; 285: 2864-2870. The number of strokes that are caused by atrial fibrillation increases with age: 

  • 15 in 1,000 strokes in people aged 50 to 59 are caused by atrial fibrillation
  • 235 in 1,000 strokes in people aged 80 to 89 are caused by atrial fibrillation.[1]National Collaborating Centre for Chronic Conditions. Atrial fibrillation: national clinical guideline for management in primary and secondary care. 2006. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/nicemedia/live/10982/30055/30055.pdf (accessed on 7 January 2013).

Strokes caused by atrial fibrillation are more likely to be fatal than strokes caused by other things. 

  • About 25 in 100 people with atrial fibrillation die within 30 days of having a stroke
  • About 14 in 100 people without atrial fibrillation die within 30 days of having a stroke.[10]Lin HJ, Wolf PA, Kelly-Hayes M, et al. Ischemic stroke with accompanying atrial fibrillation was associated with reduced survival and functional status. Stroke. 1996; 27: 1760-1764.

People who have a stroke as a result of atrial fibrillation are more likely to have a second stroke than other people.

  • About 23 in 100 people with atrial fibrillation have another stroke within one year of their first stroke
  • About 8 in 100 people without atrial fibrillation have another stroke within one year of their first stroke.[10]Lin HJ, Wolf PA, Kelly-Hayes M, et al. Ischemic stroke with accompanying atrial fibrillation was associated with reduced survival and functional status. Stroke. 1996; 27: 1760-1764.

People with atrial fibrillation are likely to be more disabled after a stroke than people with no history of atrial fibrillation.[10]Lin HJ, Wolf PA, Kelly-Hayes M, et al. Ischemic stroke with accompanying atrial fibrillation was associated with reduced survival and functional status. Stroke. 1996; 27: 1760-1764.

What is a stroke?

A stroke happens when the blood supply to part of the brain is cut off. This can cause brain damage. The earlier people are treated, the better the chance of making a good recovery.

Strokes affect people in different ways. Some people can't move one side of their body, or have difficulty speaking or swallowing. Some people black out (lose consciousness) for a while. Others feel dizzy and have blurred vision for a few minutes. People having a stroke need medical help straight away.

In most people who have a stroke, the blood supply to their brain is blocked by a blood clot (an ischaemic stroke). A stroke can also happen when a blood vessel in the brain bursts (a haemorrhagic stroke).

Strokes can cause serious long-term disability.

If you would like to find out more about stroke you can visit the Stroke Association website or the AF Association website.

What is the chance that I will have a stroke?

Doctors have developed a scoring system for working out a person's chance of having a stroke if they have atrial fibrillation. It is based on factors that can increase your chances of stroke, such as whether you have certain illnesses and how old you are. Your doctor can use this system to work out how likely you are to have a stroke. That can help you and your doctor decide whether you want treatment to reduce your chance of stroke.

The system is called CHADS2. CHADS2 stands for:
Congestive heart failure (a condition where the heart does not pump effectively)
Hypertension (high blood pressure)
Age greater than 75
Diabetes, and
Having had a previous Stroke or transient ischaemic attack (a mini stroke).[8]Gage BF, Waterman AD, Shannon W, et al. Validation of clinical classification schemes for predicting stroke: results from the National Registry of Atrial Fibrillation. Journal of the American Medical Association. 2001; 285: 2864-2870.

For each thing that is relevant to you, you score 1 point, except if you have had a previous stroke, which scores 2 points. For example, if you have diabetes (1 point) and you previously had a stroke (2 points), your CHADS2 score will be 3. 

CHADS2 scores range from 0 to 6 points. The score helps predict your chance of having a stroke over the course of a year. The annual chance of having a stroke for each of the CHADS2 scores is as follows:[8]Gage BF, Waterman AD, Shannon W, et al. Validation of clinical classification schemes for predicting stroke: results from the National Registry of Atrial Fibrillation. Journal of the American Medical Association. 2001; 285: 2864-2870.

There is a newer system for predicting the chance of having a stroke in patients with atrial fibrillation called CHA2DS2-VASc. In this decision aid, we will be using the more widely used CHADS2 system.

If you are unsure about your individual chance of having a stroke, you can talk to your GP. Your doctor can advise you whether to have treatment based on your individual chance of having a stroke and of the chance of treatment complications. But they can't make you have treatment you don't want: the decision is yours.

Treatments for atrial fibrillation or flutter not included in this decision aid

Some treatments for atrial fibrillation or flutter are not included in this decision aid because they are mainly used to treat symptoms of atrial fibrillation, rather than to reduce your chance of having a stroke. They may return your heartbeat to normal. These treatments include:

  • Cardioversion. This is a procedure where your heart is given a controlled electric shock to encourage it to return to its normal rhythm. There is also chemical cardioversion where medicines called anti-arrhythmics are used to return your heartbeat to normal.
  • Catheter ablation. This is an operation to burn or freeze a tiny part of your heart that is sending abnormal electrical signals to the rest of your heart. 
  • Beta-blockers. These are medicines that can help slow your heart rate down if your heart is beating too fast.

We have not included aspirin in this decision aid. Aspirin used to be given to people with atrial fibrillation to reduce their chance of having a stroke. Aspirin is an anti-platelet medicine. It makes the blood less able to form clots. 

If you take aspirin regularly there is a chance that it may cause bleeding in the stomach or inside the skull (intracranial haemorrhage).[15]Camm JA, Lip GYH, De Caterina R, et al. 2012 focused update of the ESC Guidelines. European Heart Journal. 2012; 33, 2719-2747. So doctors no longer use aspirin much for stroke prevention in people with atrial fibrillation or atrial flutter.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT MEDICINE (ANTICOAGULANTS)

What are anticoagulants?

Anticoagulants are medicines that reduce the chance of having a stroke. They work by 'thinning' the blood, making it less able to form clots. They also work by stopping existing clots from getting bigger. 

There are three main types of anticoagulant used to treat atrial fibrillation and atrial flutter (the names of the medicines are in brackets): 

  • Vitamin K antagonists (warfarin)
  • Xa inhibitors (apixaban, rivaroxaban)
  • Thrombin inhibitors (dabigatran).

All anticoagulant medicines come in the form of tablets.

Warfarin is the main anticoagulant prescribed in the UK. It has been used for many years and a lot is known about it. Apixaban, dabigatran, and rivaroxaban are newer medicines.

One big difference between these medicines for patients is that people taking warfarin need to have regular blood tests, usually at a clinic, to make sure the dose is right for them. People taking the newer drugs don't need to have these blood tests. Taking these newer drugs may give people more freedom than taking warfarin.

Blood clots are formed when a protein called fibrinogen is changed into another protein called fibrin. The process that changes fibrinogen to fibrin is called the clotting cascade. All anticoagulants act on some part of the clotting cascade to stop blood clotting too easily. 

Medicines that prevent the blood from clotting can increase the chance of bleeding (haemorrhage). This can happen with all anticoagulants.

Who can have anticoagulants?

People under the age of 60 without any health problems other than atrial fibrillation probably don't need to take anticoagulants. 

Most people can take anticoagulants. But people may not be able to take anticoagulants if they:

  • Are pregnant or plan on becoming pregnant
  • Have recently had surgery or are due to have surgery
  • Have liver disease
  • Have kidney disease
  • Have oesophageal varices (abnormally enlarged veins in the lower part of the gullet)
  • Have a low platelet count (thrombocytopenia).

If you are unsure whether this applies to you, you can speak to your doctor. Your doctor will also want to know if you are taking other medication. Some medicines, such as non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (e.g., aspirin, ibuprofen) or drugs for breathing problems called oral corticosteroids (e.g., asthma inhalers) increase the chance of bleeding.

Just as there is a scoring system to assess someone's chance of having a stroke (CHADS2), there is another that assesses people's chance of bleeding if they take anticoagulants. This is called the HAS-BLED tool. It works in much the same way as CHADS2, by using a points score. You and your doctor can use this tool to see if there is a chance that anticoagulants might not be safe for you.

What are the main effects of anticoagulants on reducing the chance of a stroke?

Taking anticoagulants can reduce the chance of a stroke.

People with a CHADS2 score of 2 have about a 40 in 1,000 chance of having a stroke in the course of a year. People with a CHADS2 score of 2 who take warfarin have about a 14 in 1,000 chance of having a stroke in the course of a year.[16]Granger CB, Alexander JH, McMurray JJ, et al. Apixaban versus warfarin in patients with atrial fibrillation. New England Journal of Medicine. 2011; 365: 981-992. 

The newer anticoagulants (apixaban, rivaroxaban, and dabigatran) are as effective as warfarin at reducing the chance of a stroke, but they are much easier to manage than warfarin.[15]Camm JA, Lip GYH, De Caterina R, et al. 2012 focused update of the ESC Guidelines. European Heart Journal. 2012; 33, 2719-2747.

Dabigatran (150 milligrams dose) may be more effective than warfarin at reducing the chance of having an ischaemic stroke. In one group of people, 17 in 1,000 people taking warfarin had a stroke or a blood clot in the course of a year, compared with 11 in 1,000 people taking dabigatran (150 milligrams).[18]Connolly SJ, Ezekowitz MD, Yusuf S, et al. Newly identified events in the RE-LY trial. New England Journal of Medicine. 2010; 363: 1875-1876.

What are the main effects of anticoagulants on what you can do and how you feel?

Anticoagulants are designed to reduce the chance of having a stroke. They do not affect the symptoms of atrial fibrillation or atrial flutter. 

We don't know if taking anticoagulants affects how you feel. There haven't been many studies that have looked at this.[1]National Collaborating Centre for Chronic Conditions. Atrial fibrillation: national clinical guideline for management in primary and secondary care. 2006. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/nicemedia/live/10982/30055/30055.pdf (accessed on 7 January 2013).

Taking anticoagulants can affect what you can do. There are some foods, drinks, and medicines that can interfere with anticoagulants, particularly warfarin. Some people find this affects what they can do.

Foods that contain vitamin K interfere with warfarin (broccoli, brussels sprouts, chard, kale, spinach, etc.) as does grapefruit. Alcohol can also affect how warfarin works. Medicines including non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (such as ibuprofen) and antibiotics, and herbal medicines, can interfere with warfarin. If you have atrial fibrillation, your GP or nurse can help you identify what things to try and avoid or use in moderation.

How might taking anticoagulants affect my life?

People who take warfarin need to have their blood tested on a regular basis.

If you take the newer anticoagulants, you won't need to have your blood tested. Taking these newer drugs may give people more freedom than taking warfarin.

People taking warfarin have their blood tested to ensure that:

  • it is not too thin, which increases the chance of bleeding, or
  • too thick, which increases the chance of blood forming clots that may lead to a stroke. 

The effect of warfarin on your blood is measured by the international normalised ratio (INR). 

If you take warfarin, you have your blood tested once or twice a week until your dose has been adjusted to the right INR level. After your blood has reached the right INR level, you still need to have a blood test once every few weeks to ensure your blood has stayed in the right INR range. Blood-testing clinics can be busy places and people may find it hard to fit these visits into their schedule.

Changing diet can change your INR. If your INR changes, you may need to visit the clinic more often until your INR returns to the recommended range. 

Not everyone who takes warfarin has their INR monitored at a clinic. You can check your own INR levels (self-monitoring). This involves pricking a finger to get a drop of blood. The drop of blood is placed on a handheld electronic device which gives an INR reading. If you choose to self-monitor, you will have continuous support from health care professionals.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT NO TREATMENT

What is no treatment?

Not everyone with atrial fibrillation or atrial flutter chooses to have treatment or needs to have treatment. People who have a small chance of having a stroke may choose to have their condition monitored by their doctor instead. This is called active monitoring or active surveillance.

If you choose not to have treatment, your doctor will probably suggest you have a check-up about once a year to see if your chance of having a stroke has changed. If you develop more factors that increase your chance of stroke (conditions such as diabetes or high blood pressure), your doctor will probably suggest you start treatment. 

Who can have no treatment?

Anyone can choose not to have treatment. People who have a CHADS2 score of 1 or above are usually offered treatment. People aged under 60 with no health problems affecting their chance of having a stroke, apart from atrial fibrillation or atrial flutter, may not need treatment.

What are the main effects of no treatment on atrial fibrillation?

Deciding not to have treatment will mean the chances of having a stroke stay the same, and may increase. People with a low chance of having a stroke may find their chance of stroke increases over time. Having no treatment will not affect the symptoms of atrial fibrillation.

What are the main effects of no treatment on what you can do and how you feel?

Deciding not to have treatment will not change what you can do, but we don't know if it will change how you feel. Having atrial fibrillation can affect how much you are able to exert yourself or exercise, and can affect your ability to think clearly.[1]National Collaborating Centre for Chronic Conditions. Atrial fibrillation: national clinical guideline for management in primary and secondary care. 2006. Available at http://www.nice.org.uk/nicemedia/live/10982/30055/30055.pdf (accessed on 7 January 2013). This may affect what you can do or how you feel.

Some people don't have any symptoms from atrial flutter or atrial fibrillation. Others may suffer from dizziness or fainting during attacks of atrial fibrillation or atrial flutter, and if this is the case they might need to stop driving. Not having treatment to reduce the chance of a stroke is unlikely to affect these symptoms. 

What are the main side effects of no treatment?

There are no side effects from not having treatment.

How might no treatment affect my life?

Deciding not to have treatment to reduce the chance of having a stroke is unlikely to directly affect the life of a person who has atrial fibrillation, unless they have a stroke. Deciding not to have treatment means that there is a greater chance that you will have a stroke than if you had been treated. Having a stroke does have a big effect on people's lives.

FURTHER INFORMATION ABOUT SURGERY (LEFT ATRIAL APPENDAGE OCCLUSION)

What is left atrial appendage occlusion?

This is an operation to close off a small pouch in the heart. 

The left atrial appendage (LAA) is a small finger-shaped pouch attached to the left atrium of the heart. Nobody knows exactly what it is for. For people with atrial fibrillation, blood clots can form in the left atrium. These blood clots can escape from the heart and block blood vessels in the brain, causing a stroke. It is common for blood clots in the left atrium to form in this pouch. For every 100 people who get blood clots in the left atrium, 90 of them have clots found in this pouch.[28]Healey JS, Crystal E, Lamy A, et al. Left atrial appendage occlusion study (LAAOS): results of a randomised controlled pilot study of left atrial appendange occlusion during coronary bypass surgery in patients at risk for stroke. American Hearth Journal. 2005; 150: 288-293. 

Occlusion means to block or close. Left atrial appendage occlusion is an operation that blocks the entrance to this pouch by implanting a small device. At first the device doesn't completely stop blood flowing in and out of the left atrial appendage. Within a few weeks the inner surface of the heart grows over the device, cutting it off from the rest of the heart.

The device consists of a metal frame covered with a plastic mesh. It is collapsible, like an umbrella. During the operation, the collapsed device is placed in the heart through a hollow tube. It is then expanded when it reaches the opening of the left atrial appendage.

The operation is usually done with a general anaesthetic. This means you will be asleep throughout the operation. The cardiologist (a doctor who specialises in the heart and blood vessels) inserts a small tube in your groin and feeds another thin, flexible tube up a vein until it gets to the right atrium of your heart. Using a long needle, a small hole is made in the tissue that separates the right and left atrium. The device is moved through the hollow tube to the opening of the left atrial appendage. The device is then expanded in the opening of the left atrial appendage.

The cardiologist can see what's going on inside the person's body using a type of x-ray machine called a fluoroscope. They may also use an ultrasound scanner that is fed into your oesophagus (gullet). The ultrasound gives a clear picture of the left atrial appendage and helps the surgeon decide the size of device that is needed.

Who can have left atrial appendage occlusion?

This operation is not widely available in the UK and has only been performed in a small number of patients. Not many people are likely to be offered this operation.

People may be unsuitable for this operation if:

  • They have had a heart attack in the past three months
  • They have had a stroke or mini-stroke (transient ischaemic attack) in the past 30 days
  • They have congestive heart failure
  • Their resting heart rate is greater than 110 beats per minute
  • They have had only one episode of atrial fibrillation
  • They are pregnant or planning on becoming pregnant.[29]Fountain RB, Holmes DR, Chandrasekaran K, et al. The PROTECT AF (WATCHMAN left atrial appendage system for embolic PROTECTion in patients with atrial fibrillation) trial. American Heart Journal. 2006; 151: 956-961. 

People having this operation as part of research took warfarin for several weeks before the operation and six weeks afterwards.[30]Holmes DR, Reddy VY, Turi ZG, et al. Percutaneous closure of the left atrial appendage versus warfarin therapy for prevention of stroke in patients with atrial fibrillation: a randomised non-inferiority trial. Lancet. 2009; 374: 534-542.

Some people are unable to take warfarin or any other type of anticoagulant medicine, because they have had a serious side effect such as dangerous bleeding in the stomach. In this situation, surgeons have done the operation without using warfarin, although there isn't any research to show how likely this is to cause problems.

What are the main effects of left atrial appendage occlusion on atrial fibrillation and on stroke prevention?

This operation is to lower the chance of having a stroke by preventing blood clots forming in the left atrial appendage. The operation has been shown to work as well as warfarin at preventing stroke.[30]Holmes DR, Reddy VY, Turi ZG, et al. Percutaneous closure of the left atrial appendage versus warfarin therapy for prevention of stroke in patients with atrial fibrillation: a randomised non-inferiority trial. Lancet. 2009; 374: 534-542. The operation has not been tested against the newer anticoagulants (apixaban, dabigatran, rivaroxaban), so we don't know how it compares with these medicines.

People who had the operation also took warfarin before the operation and for 45 days after the operation. After 45 days, they were prescribed two antiplatelet medicines (clopidogrel and aspirin), which they continued taking for six months. After six months, they were prescribed just aspirin, which they will take for the rest of their lives.

Not all strokes that happen in people with atrial fibrillation or atrial flutter are caused by a blood clot from the left atrial appendage, so not all strokes will be prevented by this operation.

The operation itself can cause a stroke. This happens to fewer than 1 in 100 people having the procedure. In about 4 in 100 people, a blot clot (thrombus) can be seen on the implanted device.[32]Reddy, VY, Holmes D, Doshi SK, et al. Safety of percutaneous left atrial appendage closure: results from the Watchman left atrial appendage system for embolic protection in patients with AF clinical trial and the continued access registry. Circulation. 2011; 123:417-424. If this happens, the person needs to carry on taking anticoagulant medicine, such as warfarin.

In one group of people who had this operation, blood continued to flow around the device in 32 in 100 people, one year after the device had been implanted. The device is supposed to block the pouch to prevent blood clots from getting out. However, these leaks did not cause extra strokes. [33]Viles-Gonzalez JF, Kar S, Douglas P, et al. The clinical impact of incomplete left atrial appendage closure with the Watchman device in patients with atrial fibrillation. Journal of the American College of Cardiology. 2012; 59: 923-929. About 14 in 100 people have to carry on taking warfarin for more than 45 days after the operation because some blood is still flowing around the device. About 8 in 100 people still have to take warfarin six months after the operation because some blood is still flowing around the device.[30]Holmes DR, Reddy VY, Turi ZG, et al. Percutaneous closure of the left atrial appendage versus warfarin therapy for prevention of stroke in patients with atrial fibrillation: a randomised non-inferiority trial. Lancet. 2009; 374: 534-542.

What are the main effects of left atrial appendage occlusion surgery on what you can do and how you feel?

This operation is designed to reduce the chance of having a stroke. It does not treat the symptoms of atrial fibrillation or atrial flutter. 

If you have this operation, you will need to take warfarin before and for about six weeks after the operation. After that, you will have to take antiplatelet medicine for the rest of your life. This may affect what you can do and how you feel.

We don't know if this operation will affect your quality of life. There haven't been many studies that have looked at this.

What are the main side effects of left atrial appendage occlusion?

This operation can cause side effects or complications. Some people have a reaction to the anaesthetic, a wound infection, or bleeding.

The main complications of left atrial appendage occlusion are:[32]Reddy, VY, Holmes D, Doshi SK, et al. Safety of percutaneous left atrial appendage closure: results from the Watchman left atrial appendage system for embolic protection in patients with AF clinical trial and the continued access registry. Circulation. 2011; 123:417-424. 

  • The operation fails. This means the device can't be implanted or has to be taken out. This happens to around 10 in 100 people who have the operation.
  • The heart is perforated and blood builds up around the heart. This affects between 2 and 5 in 100 people who have the operation. If a lot of fluid builds up in the sac that surrounds the heart (the pericardium), it can put pressure on the heart and stop it beating properly. This is called cardiac tamponade. The fluid is usually drained by inserting a needle into the chest (between the ribs) and into the pericardial sac. If left untreated, cardiac tamponade can cause death. 
  • Stroke. This can be caused by air bubbles getting into the heart during surgery or by a blood clot being dislodged in the heart.  This affects fewer than 1 in 100 people who have the operation.
  • The device becomes dislodged after the operation and drifts into another area of the heart or into an artery. This affects fewer than 1 in 100 people who have the operation.[36]National Institute of Health and Clinical Excellence. Percutaneous occlusion of the left atrial appendage in non-valvular atrial fibrillation for the prevention of thromboembolism. June 2012. Interventional procedure 349. Available at: http://www.nice.org.uk/guidance/IPG349 (accessed on 18 December 2012). If the device becomes dislodged then an operation is needed to remove it.

Left atrial appendage occlusion is a new operation. As surgeons become more experienced at performing the operation, the number of times these problems occur may become less.

People who have had this surgery have been advised by their cardiologist to take antiplatelet medications, to reduce blood clotting, for the rest of their lives. These medicines can have side effects, including major bleeding (haemorrhage).

How might left atrial appendage occlusion affect my life?

If you have left atrial appendage occlusion you will not be able to do anything strenuous, such as exercise, for about one month after the operation. You  will also need to be available for appointments about one to two months after the operation, six months after the operation, and one year after the operation. 

People who have this operation usually need to stay in hospital for one day following the procedure. If there are problems during the operation you may need to spend more time in hospital. 

It is usually necessary to take warfarin before the operation and for around six weeks following the operation. After that, you will need to take an antiplatelet medicine called clopidogrel until your first six-month check-up. After that, you will need to take aspirin for the rest of your life.
